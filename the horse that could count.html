Clever Hans

A famous horse from early 1900s Germany

People thought Hans could do maths and answer questions. He tapped his hoof to show the answer.

The trainer

Hans was owned by Wilhelm von Osten, a maths teacher who showed the horse to large crowds across Germany.

What Hans seemed to do

He tapped for addition, subtraction and dates. Questions were asked out loud or written on a board.

Public interest

Many thought this proved that animals could think like humans. Newspapers wrote about the clever horse.

The investigation

Scientists formed a commission. Psychologist Oskar Pfungst tested Hans with careful controls.

What the tests changed

Hans did well only when he could see a person who knew the answer. When the person did not know, Hans was mostly wrong.

The real reason

Hans was not doing maths. He was reading tiny body signals, like a small head lift when the right number was reached.

The Clever Hans effect

This effect happens when an animal or person picks up on hidden cues from the tester. Modern science uses blind and double-blind tests to avoid this.

Why it still matters

Today handlers can send signals without meaning to. Studies show detection dogs may false alert when handlers expect a find.

Takeaway for young scientists

Good experiments control for hidden cues. If a subject can see your face or you know the answer, your body may give hints.
