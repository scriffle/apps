PN Junction & LED Simulator

An interactive visualisation of how semiconductor diodes and LEDs work

What you're looking at

This simulation shows electrons flowing through a copper wire into a thin PN semiconductor junction. The two halves of the junction are doped with different elements:

  • N-type (left) — Silicon doped with Phosphorus (5 valence electrons). The extra electron is free to conduct.
  • P-type (right) — Silicon doped with Boron (3 valence electrons). The missing electron creates a “hole” in the valence shell.

How to use

  • Voltage slider — drag to apply forward (+V) or reverse (−V) bias
  • Forward bias: electrons flow through the junction, fill holes, and emit light (LED)
  • Reverse bias: the junction blocks electron flow completely
  • Click on the N or P region to open a zoomed modal showing valence shell wave functions and electron capture

What to look for

  • Phosphorus atoms (P) have 5 wave lobes; Boron atoms (B) have 3
  • Silicon atoms in the P-type region show a suppressed lobe — the hole
  • When an electron fills a hole, the atom glows yellow (photon emission)
  • Electrons repel each other and bounce off lattice atoms as they flow
This simulation is for pedagogical purposes only. It uses simplified models to illustrate the core concepts of PN junctions, doping, and LED light emission. Real semiconductor physics involves quantum mechanics, band theory, and effects not captured here. Particle counts, speeds, and proportions are not to scale.
