Atomic Structure & Notation

Nucleus = protons (p+, mass ≈ 1 u, charge +1) + neutrons (n0, mass ≈ 1 u, charge 0). Electrons orbit in shells.

Nuclide notation AZX    A = mass number (p + n)    Z = atomic number (p)    N = A − Z

Isotope: same Z, different A (different N). Same chemistry, different nuclear behaviour.

Radioisotope: unstable isotope that spontaneously decays, emitting radiation.

1 atomic mass unit: 1 u = 1.661 × 10−27 kg = 931.5 MeV/c2.

A nucleus is STABLE when attractive strong force balances p–p electrostatic repulsion. Too many protons or too unbalanced a p:n ratio → unstable → decays.

Stability band (N vs Z plot): low Z → stable when N ≈ Z. Higher Z → more neutrons needed (N > Z) to dilute repulsion. No stable isotope with Z > 83 (bismuth).

Decay Equations — Conservation Rules

Balance A (top) AND Z (bottom) on both sides. Nucleon number and charge are conserved.
Alpha decay (heavy nuclei; Z ↓ 2, A ↓ 4) AZX → A−4Z−2Y + 42He Example 23892U → 23490Th + 42He
Beta− decay (too many neutrons: n → p + e− + ν̄) AZX → AZ+1Y + 0−1e + ν̄e Example 146C → 147N + 0−1e + ν̄e
Beta+ decay (too many protons: p → n + e+ + ν) AZX → AZ−1Y + 0+1e + νe Example 2211Na → 2210Ne + 0+1e + νe
Gamma emission (excited nucleus sheds energy; A & Z unchanged) AZX* → AZX + γ

Neutrinos (ν, ν̄): carry away "missing" energy in β decay — explains why β particles have a continuous energy spectrum up to a maximum.

Half-Life & Activity

Radioactive decay is RANDOM — cannot predict when a single nucleus decays. Over many nuclei, the rate is statistically predictable.

Half-life (t½): time for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. Constant for a given isotope; unaffected by T, P or chemical state.

Whole half-lives (n = t / t½) N = N0 × (½)n     A = A0 × (½)n N = nuclei remaining; A = activity (Bq = decays/s)

Half-life: (1) n = time elapsed / t½. (2) Remaining = N0 × (½)n. (3) State answer with unit. Example: 80 g of t½ = 6 h isotope after 24 h → n = 4 → 80 × (½)4 = 5 g remaining.

Decay Series

A decay series (chain) is a sequence of decays from an unstable parent through daughter isotopes to a final stable nuclide.

  • α decay: moves DOWN-LEFT on N–Z chart (Z↓2, N↓2)
  • β− decay: moves UP-LEFT (Z↑1, N↓1)
  • β+ decay: moves DOWN-RIGHT (Z↓1, N↑1)

Example: 238U decay chain — 8 α + 6 β− → ends at stable 206Pb.

Biological Effects on Humans

Ionising radiation knocks electrons off atoms in tissue → damages DNA, proteins, cell membranes.

TypeIonising abilityCell damage
αHighestSevere damage along short path — very dangerous INSIDE body (inhaled/ingested). Harmless outside skin.
βModeratePenetrates skin a few mm → burns & tissue damage. Still dangerous internally.
γLowestDangerous both outside AND inside — deep penetration reaches organs.
Inside the body α is MOST dangerous (all energy deposited in small volume). Outside the body γ is MOST dangerous (penetrates to organs).

Short vs long-term effects

  • High dose (short-term): radiation sickness, burns, organ failure, death — effects appear within hours to weeks.
  • Low dose (long-term / stochastic): increased cancer risk, genetic mutations. No safe threshold — probability rises with dose.

Dose problem: (1) D = E/m (Gy). (2) H = D × wR (Sv). (3) Eeff = H × wT. State each with unit. Example: 0.2 J of α absorbed in 50 kg person, gonads → D = 0.004 Gy → H = 0.004 × 20 = 0.08 Sv → Eeff = 0.08 × 0.08 = 6.4 × 10−3 Sv.

Mass-Energy Equivalence

Energy released in any nuclear reaction comes from a tiny loss of mass: products have LESS total mass than reactants. The "missing" mass (Δm) has been converted to energy.
Einstein's mass-energy equivalence E = mc²     ΔE = Δm × c² c = 3.00 × 108 m/s; 1 u of mass = 931.5 MeV of energy

Because c² is enormous (9 × 1016 m²/s²), even a tiny mass defect releases huge energy. 1 g of mass → 9 × 1013 J ≈ 25 GWh.

Mass-to-energy calc: (1) Δm = (mass of reactants) − (mass of products). Keep Δm in kg. (2) ΔE = Δm c². (3) Convert to MeV if needed: divide by 1.6 × 10−13. Example: Δm = 3.0 × 10−29 kg → ΔE = 3.0 × 10−29 × (3 × 108)² = 2.7 × 10−12 J ≈ 17 MeV.

Binding Energy & the Binding Energy Curve

Mass defect (Δm): total mass of separate nucleons − mass of the assembled nucleus. Always positive.

Binding energy (BE): energy required to pull a nucleus apart into its nucleons — equivalently, the energy released when it formed. BE = Δm × c².

BE per nucleon: BE ÷ A. A measure of how tightly each nucleon is bound. Higher value → more stable nucleus.

BE/nucleon curve: rises sharply for light nuclei, PEAKS at 56Fe (~8.8 MeV/nucleon), then gently falls. Iron is the most tightly bound nucleus.

Why both fission AND fusion release energy: Reactions that move TOWARD Fe-56 increase BE/nucleon → products are more tightly bound → mass defect → energy released. FUSION: light nuclei (left of Fe) → heavier product; BE/nucleon rises. FISSION: heavy nuclei (right of Fe) → two mid-mass products; BE/nucleon rises. Both convert mass → energy.

Nuclear Fission

Fission: a heavy nucleus (typically 235U or 239Pu) absorbs a slow neutron → splits into two mid-mass fragments + 2–3 neutrons + γ + energy (~200 MeV per event).

Typical U-235 fission 23592U + 10n → 14156Ba + 9236Kr + 310n + energy
Chain reaction: neutrons released by one fission trigger further fissions in nearby U-235 nuclei → self-sustaining.

Criticality — Mass & Shape

StateAvg neutrons causing next fissionOutcome
Subcritical< 1Reaction dies out
Critical= 1Steady, self-sustaining (reactor)
Supercritical> 1Runaway — exponential growth (bomb)

Critical mass = minimum mass needed to sustain the chain. Too little mass → too many neutrons escape the surface before causing a fission. Shape matters: a sphere has minimum surface-to-volume ratio → lower critical mass than a flat slab of the same mass.

Inside a Fission Reactor

ComponentRole
Fuel rodsEnriched 235U (~3–5%) — the fissile material
Moderator (water, graphite, D2O)Slows fast neutrons to thermal speeds — slow neutrons are far more likely to be absorbed by U-235 and cause fission
Control rods (boron, cadmium)ABSORB neutrons — lowered to slow reaction, raised to speed it up. Keeps reactor critical (k = 1).
Coolant (water, liquid metal, gas)Carries thermal energy from core to heat exchanger
ShieldingThick concrete + steel — absorbs γ and neutrons to protect workers & environment
Energy transfer chain in a reactor: Mass defect in fission → kinetic energy of fragments + γ → collisions heat the fuel & moderator (thermal energy) → coolant transfers heat to water in heat exchanger → steam → spins turbine (mechanical KE) → generator produces electricity. Each stage has losses (mostly waste heat).

Nuclear Fusion

Fusion: two light nuclei combine into a heavier nucleus, releasing energy. Powers the Sun and all stars.

D–T fusion (main research reaction — ITER) 21H + 31H → 42He + 10n + 17.6 MeV

Conditions required: extreme temperature (~108 K) and pressure — both nuclei have +charge so strong electrostatic repulsion must be overcome for strong force to take over.

Fusion releases MORE energy per kg of fuel than fission, uses abundant fuel (hydrogen isotopes from seawater), and leaves no long-lived radioactive waste. But sustained, net-positive fusion is not yet commercially achieved.

Viability of Nuclear Energy for Australia

Arguments FOR

  • Very low operational CO₂ — helps meet emissions targets
  • High energy density — 1 kg U ≈ 2,000,000 kg coal
  • Reliable baseload power (24/7, weather-independent)
  • Australia holds ~30% of world uranium reserves
  • Small land footprint per MW compared to solar/wind

Arguments AGAINST

  • High capital cost & long build time (10–15 years)
  • Long-lived radioactive waste — unresolved storage
  • Accident risk (Chernobyl 1986, Fukushima 2011) — low probability, high consequence
  • Weapons proliferation concerns from enrichment/plutonium
  • Currently prohibited by federal law in Australia
  • Renewables (solar, wind + storage) now cheaper per MWh and faster to deploy
Viability evaluation: weigh emissions benefit & reliability against cost, waste, accident risk, timeline, and public acceptance. Compare against alternatives (solar + wind + batteries, pumped hydro, gas peakers). Consider the grid's needs (baseload vs peaking) and the time horizon for decarbonisation. Australia's sun & wind resources make renewables + storage a strong competitor; nuclear's role is contested.

Decay-Equation Balancing Checklist

  • Sum of A on LHS = sum of A on RHS
  • Sum of Z on LHS = sum of Z on RHS
  • α: −4 to A, −2 to Z (daughter two places LEFT on periodic table)
  • β−: A unchanged, +1 to Z (daughter one place RIGHT)
  • β+: A unchanged, −1 to Z (daughter one place LEFT)
  • γ: A & Z unchanged — just releases a photon
  • Always include antineutrino (β−) or neutrino (β+) for full marks
