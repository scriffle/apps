The Field Model

A field is a region in which an object experiences a force without contact. Field strength = force per unit (mass / charge / on a test object). Field lines point in the direction of the force on a positive test object; closer lines = stronger field.

Static vs changing: a field is static if it does not change with time. Uniform vs non-uniform: uniform = constant strength & direction (parallel, evenly spaced lines, e.g. between charged plates); non-uniform = varies with position (radial point-charge/mass fields).

Monopoles & dipoles: gravity and electricity can have single sources (mass; + or − charge = monopoles). Magnetism only ever occurs as dipoles (N and S together) — no magnetic monopole has been found.

Gravitational Fields

Radial field pointing inward toward the mass. Strength g obeys an inverse-square law with distance r from the centre.

Field & force (point mass) g = GM/r2
Fg = Gm1m2/r2 M source mass, r centre-to-centre distance
g is both the field strength (N kg−1) and the free-fall acceleration (m s−2). Near a surface the field is approximately uniform (g ≈ 9.8). Double r → quarter g (inverse square).
Field strength at 2 Earth radii from Earth's centre: g = gsurface/2² = 9.8/4 = 2.45 N kg−1.

Electric Fields

Around a point charge the field is radial: away from a positive charge, toward a negative charge. Same inverse-square form as gravity.

Field & force (point charge) E = kQ/r2
F = kq1q2/r2   (Coulomb) E in N C−1 (= V m−1)
Gravity and the electric force share the inverse-square form, BUT gravity only attracts (one sign of mass) while electric forces attract OR repel (two signs of charge).
Two charges q1 = +2.0 μC and q2 = −3.0 μC, 0.10 m apart: F = kq1q2/r² = 8.99×109 × (2.0×10−6)(3.0×10−6)/(0.10)² = 5.4 N (attractive).

Uniform Electric Field (parallel plates)

Between two parallel charged plates the field is uniform: evenly spaced parallel lines from + plate to − plate.

Uniform field, force & work E = V/d
F = qE
W = qV = ΔEk V plate voltage, d plate separation
Electron accelerated through V = 200 V: Ek = qV = 1.6×10−19 × 200 = 3.2×10−17 J. Speed: v = √(2Ek/m) = √(2×3.2×10−17/9.1×10−31) = 8.4×106 m s−1.

Potential Energy in Fields (qualitative)

Moving an object against the field stores potential energy; moving with the field releases it. A mass gains GPE moving away from a planet; a positive charge gains EPE moving toward another positive charge.

Energy change = area under a force–distance graph. For gravity, ΔEg = (area under field–distance graph) × mass.
Energy-from-graph method: read the area under the force vs distance graph between the two radii (counts squares × value per square). That area is the work done = the change in potential energy. For a field–distance (g vs r) graph, multiply the area by the mass.
A 2.0 kg mass moves out where the field–distance graph area between r1 and r2 is 6.0 N kg−1 m. ΔEg = area × m = 6.0 × 2.0 = 12 J gained.

Charge in a Magnetic Field

A charge moving through a magnetic field feels a force perpendicular to BOTH its velocity and the field (max when v ⊥ B; zero when v ∥ B).

Force on a moving charge F = qvB   (v ⊥ B)
Direction (conventional current / + charge): right-hand rule — fingers point along v, curl toward B, thumb/palm gives F. For an ELECTRON (negative), the force is in the OPPOSITE direction.
A magnetic force is always ⊥ to velocity → it does NO work → speed is constant. It only changes direction, producing circular motion.
Radius of circular path (v ≪ c) qvB = mv2/r  →  r = mv/(qB)
Electron (q = 1.6×10−19, m = 9.1×10−31) at v = 3.0×106 m s−1 in B = 0.020 T: r = mv/qB = (9.1×10−31×3.0×106)/(1.6×10−19×0.020) = 8.5×10−4 m.
Charged-particle method: in an E field the force qE does work → use W = qV = ½mv² for the speed gained. In a B field the force qvB does NO work → set qvB = mv²/r for the circular radius. Combine both for accelerator/velocity-selector problems.

Particle Accelerators

Model the motion as uniform circular motion. A uniform electric field does work to speed the particle up (linear acceleration); a uniform magnetic field changes its direction without changing speed. Synchrotrons combine both.

Proton (q = 1.6×10−19 C) at v = 2.0×106 m s−1 in B = 0.50 T: F = qvB = 1.6×10−19×2.0×106×0.50 = 1.6×10−13 N.

Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

A wire carrying current in an external magnetic field experiences a force (the motor effect).

Force on a conductor F = nIlB   (I ⊥ B) n loops, I current (A), l length in field (m), B field (T)
Direction: right-hand rule — fingers point along conventional current I, curl toward B, palm pushes in the direction of F. Force is zero when I is parallel to B.

Simple DC Motor

A current loop in a magnetic field: the two sides carry current in opposite directions → forces are opposite → a torque (couple) spins the coil.

The split-ring commutator reverses the current every half-turn, so the torque always drives the coil the same way → continuous rotation. (AC generators use slip rings instead.)

Torque increases with: larger current I, stronger field B, and more loops n. Torque is zero when the coil plane is ⊥ to the field (coil parallel to field gives maximum torque).

Satellites in Orbit

Gravity is the only force and provides the centripetal force, so the orbit is modelled as uniform circular motion.

Orbital motion a = v2/r = 4π2r/T2
GM/r2 = v2/r  →  T2 = 4π2r3/GM
For a given orbit radius the satellite's speed and period are fixed by the central mass M only — they do NOT depend on the satellite's own mass.

Common Exam Traps

  • Magnetic force does no work — it changes direction, not speed.
  • For electrons, reverse the right-hand rule result (or use the left hand).
  • g and E both follow inverse-square laws; gravity only attracts.
  • Satellite speed/period are independent of the satellite's mass.
  • Magnetism has no monopoles — only N–S dipoles.
