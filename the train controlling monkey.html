Jack the Baboon

A true story from 19th century South Africa

Jack worked with railway signalman James Wide. People came to see the unusual team at Uitenhage near Port Elizabeth.

Who was James Wide

James was a skilled rail worker. After a fall between moving cars, he lost both legs below the knee and used a wheelchair to do his job.

Meeting Jack

In 1881 James bought Jack, a chacma baboon. He trained Jack to push the wheelchair and help with daily tasks under supervision.

Learning the job

Jack learned the steps for railway signals. He watched, waited for the order, and pulled the right lever while James checked each move.

Official checks

People reported a baboon working the signals. Railway officials tested Jack. After seeing his accuracy under supervision, they approved his work.

On the payroll

Jack was officially employed. He was paid a small daily wage and a weekly beer. Stories say he worked nine years without a recorded mistake.

End of the story

Jack died in 1890 from tuberculosis. His skull is kept at the Albany Museum in Grahamstown, South Africa.

Why we remember Jack

Jack shows how animals can learn complex routines and work with humans. His story also reminds us to check facts and look for careful records.

Think like a scientist

What tasks did Jack do with supervision. How would you test his accuracy and safety. What records would you keep to show the results.
