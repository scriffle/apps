Motion in two dimensions

In Unit 2 motion ran along a single line, and Newton's laws kept it honest. Now the line breaks open: a thrown ball arcs through the air, a car leans into a bend, a satellite curves forever around the Earth — motion in two dimensions at last. Yet not one of Newton's laws is repealed; they still govern every instant. And beneath the arcs and circles two unbreakable accounts — momentum and energy — balance through every collision, bounce and near-miss. Read it once, in one sitting. Nothing here needs solving, only seeing.

Breaking out of the straight line

Throw a ball across a room and watch its path: not a straight line, but a smooth arch — a parabola. In Unit 2 you could always point along the direction of motion and call it "forwards". A projectile takes that luxury away. It climbs and it travels at once, and to understand it you have to watch two things happening at the same time.

Here is the secret that makes the whole of projectile motion simple, and it is worth holding onto: the two motions do not talk to each other. The sideways motion and the up-and-down motion are independent. Sideways, nothing pushes the ball along once it has left your hand — so it covers equal horizontal distances in equal times, drifting on at a steady speed. Up and down, gravity is the only force acting, pulling the ball toward the ground with the same constant acceleration g the whole way.

That last point is where almost everyone slips, so plant it now: on the way up, the force on the ball is still downward. Gravity does not pause at the top, switch off, or reverse. A ball thrown straight up is decelerating because the downward pull is fighting its upward motion — but the pull itself never changes. All the way up, all the way down, and at the very peak where the ball is briefly motionless, the acceleration is the same constant g, pointing at the centre of the Earth. There is no such thing as a moment with no force; the only force a projectile ever feels (ignoring the air) is its own weight.

Key idea Treat the horizontal and vertical motions separately. Horizontally the ball coasts at constant velocity; vertically it accelerates downward at a constant g the entire flight — rising, falling and at the peak alike. The two stories share only one thing: the clock.

The most beautiful demonstration of this independence costs nothing. Hold two identical balls at the same height. Drop one straight down; at the same instant, flick the other horizontally off the table so it sails out across the room. They part company sideways — one lands at your feet, one lands metres away — but listen for the two thuds. They land at the same moment. The sideways shove the second ball got did nothing to its falling; gravity worked on both verticals identically and brought them down together. A bullet fired level across a field and a bullet simply dropped from the same height reach the ground at the same time.

Real air spoils the clean parabola a little, and it is worth knowing how. Air resistance opposes the motion — it always points backward along the direction the ball is travelling. So it shaves energy off the flight: the ball does not climb as high, and it does not travel as far. The path becomes lopsided, falling more steeply than it rose. The physics of the ideal parabola still runs underneath; air resistance is the correction the real world adds on top, and for now it is enough to know which way it pushes and what it costs.

Trap "At the top of its arc the ball has no force on it because it has stopped." Wrong twice over: a projectile at its peak has stopped moving upward but is still sailing sideways, and gravity is pulling on it as hard as ever. Velocity can be zero for an instant; the force never is.

The force that points inward

Now bend the straight line right around into a circle. Whirl a ball on a string above your head, drive a car around a roundabout, ride a fairground rotor — in each case something travels a circular path at steady speed. This is uniform circular motion, and it hides a subtlety that has fooled people for centuries.

Moving at constant speed sounds like nothing is happening — no speeding up, no slowing down. But velocity is not just speed; it is speed with a direction, and on a circle the direction is changing every instant. The ball is always heading straight ahead along the curve — its velocity points along the tangent, the way it would fly off if the string snapped. To keep bending that velocity around the circle instead of letting it run straight, something must pull the object constantly toward the centre. The object is accelerating — not because it is getting faster, but because it is forever turning.

That inward pull has a name, the centripetal ("centre-seeking") force, and a single relationship governs it: Fnet = mv²r. Go faster, or turn a tighter circle, and the inward force needed grows. The acceleration points straight at the centre; the velocity points along the tangent, at right angles to it. They are always perpendicular.

Key idea In uniform circular motion the velocity is tangential and the net force and acceleration are radial — pointing to the centre. The centripetal force is not a new kind of force; it is the job done by some real, ordinary force that happens to point inward.

Which brings us to the great illusion. Sit in a car that swings hard around a bend and you feel flung against the door — thrown outward, surely, by some "centrifugal force". There is no such force. Nothing is pushing you outward. What is really happening is that your body, obeying Newton's first law, wants to keep going straight ahead — and it is the door, curving inward across your path, that shoves you toward the centre and drags you around the bend with the car. The pressure you feel against the door is the door pushing inward on you; the "flung outward" sensation is just your inertia trying to escape the turn. Every real force in a circle points inward.

So in any circular motion, the honest question is always: which real force is doing the pulling? Whirl a ball on a string and the string's tension hauls it inward. Drive a car around a flat bend and it is friction between the tyres and the road — lose that friction on ice and the car slides straight on, exactly as the first law promises. Build a banked track, tilting the road inward like a velodrome or a freeway off-ramp, and the road's own push — the normal force, angled inward by the tilt — can supply the turn. At the right "design" speed a banked track needs no friction at all: the sideways lean of the normal force alone bends the car around, while its upward part carries the car's weight. That is why race tracks and high-speed curves are banked.

Circles can stand up out of the ground, too. Swing a bucket of water in a vertical loop, or take a car over a humped bridge and down into a dip, and gravity now helps or fights the turn depending on where you are. At the very top of a vertical loop both the support force and gravity point downward — both toward the centre — so they add together to bend the path. There is a slowest speed you can manage up there: the speed at which gravity alone supplies exactly the inward pull the circle needs, the support force having dropped to nothing. Go any slower and gravity is more than the circle calls for, so it curves the object inward off the path and it falls away. At the bottom of the loop the support must overcome gravity and supply the inward turn, so it is largest there — which is exactly why you feel heaviest at the bottom of a dip and lightest, even floating, over a crest.

Trap There is no outward "centrifugal force". The net force in circular motion always points to the centre, and it is supplied by a real, identifiable force — tension, friction, a normal force, or gravity. Name that force, and the problem is half solved. Blame an imaginary outward push, and you will get the direction of everything wrong.

Lift the circle off the planet entirely and you have the grandest example of all: a satellite. A satellite — the Moon, or the spacecraft that carry your phone's location and the weather maps — is simply an object falling toward the Earth and missing, over and over, forever. Gravity is the inward force, the string you cannot see, and it bends the satellite's straight-ahead motion into a closed loop. Here we treat that loop purely as circular motion: an object curving around at steady speed because a centre-pointing force holds it there. Why that force is gravity, how its strength sets the orbit, and why a higher orbit means a slower satellite — that is the gravitation story, and it belongs to the Fields chapter, where the field doing the pulling is mapped properly.

The unbreakable accounts

Watch two cars collide, two pool balls click apart, a spacecraft fire its thrusters. The motion looks chaotic — but underneath, the universe keeps two ledgers that never fail to balance. They are momentum and energy, and learning to read them is learning to predict what comes out of a collision without watching the collision itself.

The first ledger is momentum, the quantity p = mv — how much motion an object carries, mass times velocity. The crucial word is velocity: momentum is a vector, it has a direction. In an isolated system — one where outside forces do not interfere — the total momentum before any interaction exactly equals the total momentum after. Two skaters push apart and drift in opposite directions; a rifle kicks back as the bullet flies forward; a rocket throws gas one way and climbs the other. In every case, add up the momentum with its directions kept straight — one way counted positive, the other negative — and the books balance perfectly. Momentum is conserved in every collision, no exceptions.

Key idea Momentum is a vector. Pick a positive direction, keep the signs consistent, and the total momentum of an isolated system is the same before and after any collision — always, in every kind of collision.

Closely related is impulse: a force acting over a time, FΔt, which is exactly the change in an object's momentum, mΔv. This is why a long, soft stop is gentler than a short, hard one. A car's crumple zone, an airbag, a cricketer's hands giving with the ball, a high-jumper landing on a thick mat — each stretches the stopping time, and for the same change in momentum a longer time means a smaller force. The same idea explains the punch of a struck ball: a big force over a brief contact delivers a big change in momentum.

The second ledger is energy, and it comes in several currencies that trade into one another. There is kinetic energy, Ek = ½mv², the energy of motion. There is gravitational potential energy, Eg = mgΔh, the energy stored by lifting something up, ready to be cashed back as the object falls. And there is elastic (spring) potential energy, Es = ½kx², stored in anything stretched or compressed — a bow, a trampoline, a bumper. On a roller-coaster these trade back and forth all day: height converts to speed down the first drop, speed converts back to height up the next rise, and a launch spring dumps its stored energy into motion at the start.

Notice the shape of that spring formula — energy depends on x², the square of how far you stretch it. Pull a spring twice as far and you store not twice but four times the energy. That steep, quadratic climb is why a slightly longer draw on a bow sends the arrow so much faster, and why a small extra compression of a bumper matters so much in a crash.

Here the two ledgers part ways, and the difference is the whole point. Momentum is always conserved in a collision. Kinetic energy is not. When two objects bounce apart cleanly — billiard balls, or two superballs — almost no kinetic energy is lost; physicists call this an elastic collision, the only kind where the kinetic-energy books also balance. But when two cars crumple together and move off as one wreck, or a ball of putty splats and sticks, kinetic energy goes missing. That is an inelastic collision.

Missing — but not destroyed. Energy is never destroyed; it only changes form. The kinetic energy that vanishes from a crash has been spent bending metal, in the heat of the crumpling, and in the bang you hear. Add up the heat, the sound and the energy of permanent deformation, and the books balance after all. So the practised eye reads a collision like an accountant: momentum, always equal before and after; kinetic energy, equal only if the collision was elastic — and if it fell, the shortfall is precisely the heat, sound and crumpling carried away.

Trap "Energy was lost in the crash, so conservation failed." Energy is never lost — it is converted. In an inelastic collision the missing kinetic energy reappears as heat, sound and deformation; the total energy is conserved. And momentum is conserved either way. Do not confuse "kinetic energy not conserved" with "energy disappeared".

How to use the next two passes

You have now seen the whole Area of Study once: a straight line broken open into arcs and circles, with Newton's laws unbroken beneath every curve, and two ledgers — momentum and energy — that the universe never lets slip. A projectile that feels only gravity and falls in two independent motions at once; a circle held together by a real inward force and never an imaginary outward one; satellites falling around the world forever; and collisions read off two balancing accounts. Everything from here is the same story at higher resolution.
