Motion in two dimensions

In Unit 2 motion occurred along a single line, governed by Newton's laws. That restriction is now removed: a thrown ball arcs through the air, a car turns through a bend, a satellite curves continuously around the Earth. This is motion in two dimensions. None of Newton's laws is repealed; they continue to govern every instant. Beneath the arcs and circles, two conserved quantities, momentum and energy, balance through every collision and interaction. This overview is intended to be read once, in a single sitting. Nothing here requires solving.

Breaking out of the straight line

A ball thrown across a room follows a path that is not a straight line but a smooth arch, a parabola. In Unit 2 a single direction of motion could be designated "forwards". A projectile does not permit this. It rises and travels horizontally at the same time, and understanding it requires considering two motions occurring simultaneously.

The principle that makes projectile motion tractable is the following: the two motions are not coupled. The horizontal motion and the vertical motion are independent. Horizontally, no force acts on the ball once it has left the hand, so it covers equal horizontal distances in equal times, continuing at a constant speed. Vertically, gravity is the only force acting, producing the same constant acceleration g toward the ground throughout the flight.

The following point is a common source of error: on the way up, the force on the ball is still downward. Gravity does not pause at the top, switch off, or reverse. A ball thrown straight up decelerates because the downward force opposes its upward motion, but the force itself does not change. Throughout the ascent, throughout the descent, and at the peak where the ball is momentarily motionless, the acceleration is the same constant g, directed toward the centre of the Earth. There is no instant during which no force acts; the only force on a projectile, neglecting air resistance, is its own weight.

Key idea The horizontal and vertical motions are treated separately. Horizontally the ball moves at constant velocity; vertically it accelerates downward at a constant g throughout the flight, during rising, falling and at the peak alike. The two motions share only the elapsed time.

This independence is demonstrated simply. Two identical balls are held at the same height. One is dropped straight down; at the same instant the other is projected horizontally off the table. They separate horizontally, one landing directly below the release point and one landing some distance away, but they reach the ground at the same moment. The horizontal velocity given to the second ball has no effect on its vertical fall; gravity acts identically on both vertical motions and brings them down together. A bullet fired horizontally across a field and a bullet dropped from the same height reach the ground at the same time.

Air resistance alters the ideal parabola. Air resistance opposes the motion, always acting backward along the direction of travel. It removes energy from the flight: the ball does not rise as high and does not travel as far. The path becomes asymmetrical, falling more steeply than it rose. The ideal parabolic motion remains the underlying behaviour; air resistance is a correction added by real conditions. At this stage it is sufficient to know its direction and its effect.

Trap "At the top of its arc the ball has no force on it because it has stopped." This is incorrect in two respects: a projectile at its peak has stopped moving upward but is still moving horizontally, and gravity continues to act on it unchanged. The velocity can be zero for an instant; the force is never zero.

The force that points inward

The straight line is now bent into a circle. A ball whirled on a string overhead, a car driven around a roundabout, and a fairground rotor all involve an object travelling a circular path at constant speed. This is uniform circular motion, and it contains a subtlety that has long been misunderstood.

Constant speed suggests that nothing is changing, with no speeding up and no slowing down. However, velocity is not only speed; it is speed with a direction, and on a circle the direction changes at every instant. The object always moves along the curve, with its velocity directed along the tangent, the direction it would travel if the string were cut. To bend that velocity around the circle rather than allowing it to continue straight, a force must act continuously toward the centre. The object is accelerating, not because its speed increases, but because its direction is continuously changing.

This inward force is the centripetal ("centre-seeking") force, and a single relationship governs it: Fnet = mv²r. A greater speed, or a tighter circle, requires a greater inward force. The acceleration is directed toward the centre; the velocity is directed along the tangent, at right angles to it. The two are always perpendicular.

Key idea In uniform circular motion the velocity is tangential and the net force and acceleration are radial, directed toward the centre. The centripetal force is not a new kind of force; it is the role performed by some real, ordinary force that is directed inward.

A common misconception concerns this inward force. A person in a car turning sharply around a bend feels pressed against the door, apparently thrown outward by a "centrifugal force". No such force exists. Nothing pushes the person outward. In fact, the body, in accordance with Newton's first law, tends to continue in a straight line, and the door, curving inward across that path, pushes the body toward the centre and carries it around the bend with the car. The pressure felt against the door is the door pushing inward on the body; the sensation of being thrown outward is the body's inertia tending to continue straight. Every real force in circular motion is directed inward.

In any circular motion the analysis turns on identifying which real force provides the inward pull. For a ball whirled on a string, the string's tension acts inward. For a car on a flat bend, friction between the tyres and the road provides the force; if that friction is lost on ice, the car continues straight ahead, as the first law predicts. On a banked track, tilted inward like a velodrome or a freeway off-ramp, the road's normal force, angled inward by the tilt, can supply the turn. At the appropriate "design" speed a banked track requires no friction at all: the inward component of the normal force alone bends the car around, while its upward component supports the car's weight. This is why race tracks and high-speed curves are banked.

Circular motion can also occur in a vertical plane. For a bucket of water swung in a vertical loop, or a car passing over a humped bridge and through a dip, gravity assists or opposes the turn depending on position. At the top of a vertical loop both the support force and gravity are directed downward, both toward the centre, so they add together to bend the path. There is a minimum speed at the top: the speed at which gravity alone supplies exactly the required inward force, with the support force reduced to zero. At any lower speed gravity exceeds the required inward force, so the object curves inward off the path and falls away. At the bottom of the loop the support force must overcome gravity and supply the inward force, so it is greatest there. This is why a person feels heaviest at the bottom of a dip and lightest, even weightless, at the top of a crest.

Trap There is no outward "centrifugal force". The net force in circular motion always points toward the centre, and it is supplied by a real, identifiable force: tension, friction, a normal force, or gravity. Identifying that force is most of the analysis. Attributing the motion to an imaginary outward push leads to incorrect directions throughout the problem.

Circular motion also applies beyond the Earth's surface, in the case of a satellite. A satellite, such as the Moon or a spacecraft providing location data and weather imagery, is an object continuously falling toward the Earth and continuously missing. Gravity is the inward force, an unseen constraint, and it bends the satellite's straight-ahead motion into a closed loop. Here that loop is treated purely as circular motion: an object curving at constant speed because a centre-directed force holds it in the path. The reason that force is gravity, how its magnitude determines the orbit, and why a higher orbit corresponds to a slower satellite, are part of the gravitation treatment, which belongs to the Fields chapter, where the field providing the force is described in full.

The unbreakable accounts

Two cars colliding, two pool balls separating, and a spacecraft firing its thrusters appear to involve disordered motion. Underlying these events, however, are two quantities that are always conserved: momentum and energy. Applying them allows the outcome of a collision to be predicted without analysing the collision itself.

The first conserved quantity is momentum, defined as p = mv, the product of mass and velocity. The relevant term is velocity: momentum is a vector and has a direction. In an isolated system, one in which no external forces act, the total momentum before any interaction equals the total momentum after it. Two skaters push apart and move in opposite directions; a rifle recoils as the bullet moves forward; a rocket expels gas one way and accelerates the other. In each case, when the momentum is summed with directions accounted for, one direction taken as positive and the other as negative, the total is unchanged. Momentum is conserved in every collision, without exception.

Key idea Momentum is a vector. With a chosen positive direction and consistent signs, the total momentum of an isolated system is the same before and after any collision, in every kind of collision.

Closely related is impulse: a force acting over a time, FΔt, which equals the change in an object's momentum, mΔv. This is why a longer, gentler stop produces a smaller force than a short, sudden one. A car's crumple zone, an airbag, a fielder's hands moving with the ball, and a high jumper landing on a thick mat each extend the stopping time, and for the same change in momentum a longer time produces a smaller force. The same relationship explains the effect of a struck ball: a large force over a brief contact delivers a large change in momentum.

The second conserved quantity is energy, which occurs in several forms that convert into one another. Kinetic energy, Ek = ½mv², is the energy of motion. Gravitational potential energy, Eg = mgΔh, is the energy stored by raising an object, available again as the object falls. Elastic (spring) potential energy, Es = ½kx², is stored in anything stretched or compressed, such as a bow, a trampoline, or a bumper. On a roller-coaster these forms convert back and forth: height converts to speed on a drop, speed converts back to height on a rise, and a launch spring transfers its stored energy into motion at the start.

The spring formula depends on x², the square of the extension. Stretching a spring twice as far stores not twice but four times the energy. This quadratic dependence is why a slightly longer draw on a bow gives the arrow much greater speed, and why a small additional compression of a bumper has a large effect in a collision.

At this point the two conserved quantities differ, and the distinction is essential. Momentum is always conserved in a collision. Kinetic energy is not. When two objects rebound cleanly, as with billiard balls or two superballs, almost no kinetic energy is lost; this is called an elastic collision, the only type in which kinetic energy is also conserved. When two cars crumple together and move off as one mass, or a ball of putty deforms and sticks, kinetic energy is lost. This is an inelastic collision.

The kinetic energy is lost but not destroyed. Energy is never destroyed; it only changes form. The kinetic energy lost in a collision is transferred to deforming material, to heat in the crumpling, and to sound. When the heat, the sound and the energy of permanent deformation are included, the total energy is conserved. A collision is therefore analysed as follows: momentum is always equal before and after; kinetic energy is equal only if the collision was elastic, and any shortfall equals the heat, sound and deformation produced.

Trap "Energy was lost in the crash, so conservation failed." Energy is never lost; it is converted. In an inelastic collision the missing kinetic energy reappears as heat, sound and deformation; the total energy is conserved. Momentum is conserved in either case. The statement "kinetic energy not conserved" must not be confused with "energy disappeared".

How to use the next two passes

The whole Area of Study has now been surveyed once: a straight line extended into arcs and circles, with Newton's laws holding beneath every curve, and two conserved quantities, momentum and energy, maintained throughout. A projectile experiences only gravity and falls in two independent motions at once; a circle is maintained by a real inward force and never an imaginary outward one; satellites fall around the Earth continuously; and collisions are analysed using two conserved quantities. The remainder of the material develops this same content at greater resolution.

Pass 2 presents this material in three chapters, each intended to be read before the topic is covered in class: projectile motion, with components and air resistance; circular motion, horizontal, banked and vertical; and momentum and energy, with collisions and the full energy treatment. Each chapter contains every examinable equation with its formula-sheet status, the standard graphs, and checks to confirm each idea has been understood.

Pass 3 is intended for exam preparation: the key-knowledge dot points in study-design order, with the common traps identified, the assessors' advice, and exam-style questions with full marking solutions.
