The scientific investigation

Unit 4 Area of Study 2 is a student-designed practical investigation that generates primary data on a question drawn from fields, motion or light, with one continuous independent variable. The investigation is conducted, recorded in a logbook and analysed by the student, and is reported as a scientific poster of no more than 600 words. This page sets out, in order, what an investigation is, how to measure well, how data becomes evidence, and how the investigation is concluded and communicated.

Connects The investigation may be conducted on any of the three content areas of the course. A fields question draws on Action without contact and Electricity from fields; a light question draws on Light, matter, relativity; a motion question draws on the Unit 3 study of motion. The physics is not new. What is new is the discipline of generating and defending evidence.

What an investigation is

An investigation begins with an aim or a question. The question must be answerable by measurement and must isolate a single relationship: how one continuous quantity depends on another. The quantity the experimenter deliberately changes is the independent variable; the quantity that is measured in response is the dependent variable. Every other quantity that could affect the result is a controlled variable and is held constant throughout. The independent variable in this Area of Study is required to be continuous, meaning it can take any value across a range rather than only fixed categories.

A hypothesis is a testable prediction of the relationship between the independent and dependent variables, expressed before data are collected and based on relevant physics. The investigation does not set out to confirm the hypothesis. It sets out to test it, and the data may support, partially support or fail to support it.

The methodology is the overall type of investigation chosen — here, a controlled practical experiment that generates primary data. The method is the specific procedure: the equipment, the measurements taken, the range of the independent variable, the number of repeated readings and the way the data are analysed. Primary data are data generated by the experimenter, and may be quantitative, in the form of measured numerical values, or qualitative, in the form of recorded observations.

Key idea A valid experiment investigates what it claims to investigate. Validity is threatened when a controlled variable is allowed to change, so that the dependent variable responds to more than one cause, or when observer bias influences the readings.

Measuring well

No measurement is exact. The true value of a quantity is the value that would be found if the quantity could be measured perfectly. A real reading differs from the true value, and the difference between a measurement result and the true value is the measurement error.

Accuracy and precision are distinct, and they are not synonyms. Accuracy is qualitative: a measurement is accurate if it is close to the true value, and the experimenter writes "more accurate" or "less accurate", never an accuracy as a number. Precision is how close repeated measurements are to each other — the spread of the readings — and gives no indication of closeness to the true value. A set of readings can be precise yet inaccurate, or accurate on average yet imprecise.

The behaviour of an instrument is described by its resolution and its uncertainty, which are also distinct. Resolution is the smallest change in the measured quantity that produces a perceptible change in the reading: a millimetre ruler has a resolution of 1 mm and a stopwatch reading to hundredths a resolution of 0.01 s. The uncertainty states the interval in which the true value is expected to lie. A result written as 20 ± 1 mm means the true value is expected to lie in the interval 19–21 mm. By VCE convention the uncertainty of a measuring instrument is half its smallest scale increment, so a millimetre ruler reads to ±0.5 mm and a 0.01 s stopwatch to ±0.005 s. Resolution and uncertainty are not the same quantity and must not be reported as though they were.

Trap The uncertainty of an instrument is half its smallest scale increment, not the increment itself. A millimetre ruler has a resolution of 1 mm but an uncertainty of ±0.5 mm.

Errors are classified by how they act on the readings. A random error is an unpredictable variation that causes a spread of readings about the mean; it affects precision and is reduced by repeating the measurement and averaging. A systematic error causes readings to differ from the true value in a consistent direction by a similar amount each time; it arises from calibration, from the instrument, or from the method, such as parallax or a zero error, and it affects accuracy. Averaging reduces random error only. Averaging does not reduce systematic error, because every reading is shifted in the same direction by the same fault.

Trap Repeating a measurement and averaging reduces random error and improves precision. It does not reduce systematic error: a consistent offset survives the average unchanged. The fault must instead be found and removed.

Repeating measurements supports two related judgements. Repeatability is the closeness of agreement between successive measurements taken under the same conditions — the same observer, instrument, method and location, over a short time — and evaluates precision. Reproducibility is the closeness of agreement when the conditions are changed — a different observer, instrument, method or location — and so checks a claimed precision and can uncover systematic error, linking the result back to accuracy.

Before any data are collected, the experimenter applies the relevant health, safety and ethical guidelines: identifying hazards, controlling risks, and conducting the investigation responsibly.

From data to evidence

Evidence is data that bear on a hypothesis, a model or a theory, and that may support or refute it. Primary data acquire their authority from the way they are recorded. The logbook is a dated, contemporaneous record of every reading, observation, instrument setting and change of plan, written as the investigation proceeds. It authenticates the primary data as the experimenter's own and makes the work able to be checked.

When numerical values are reported, the conventions of significant figures apply. In a sum or difference the result keeps the fewest decimal places of any value used. In a product or quotient the result keeps the fewest significant figures. Rounding is performed only at the final step, never on intermediate values, so that rounding error does not accumulate. Quantities are reported with their correct symbols, units and standard abbreviations, written in the accepted notation, such as m s⁻¹.

Patterns are revealed by graphing. The independent variable is plotted on the horizontal axis and the dependent variable on the vertical axis. Each plotted point carries uncertainty bars: the vertical bar shows the uncertainty in the dependent variable and the horizontal bar the uncertainty in the independent variable, with the true value expected to lie within the bars. A line of best fit is drawn to follow the trend of all the points, passing within the uncertainty bars; it is not drawn through the first and last points only, is not forced through any chosen point or through the origin, and is not extended beyond the range of the data. A line expected to pass through the origin that does not pass through it indicates a systematic error.

Key idea Linearisation transforms a curved relationship into a straight line so that the gradient equals a physical quantity. The variable is transformed and its axis units are transformed with it — for example, plotting t2 against distance with the axis labelled "time2 (s2)". The gradient is then read from two points lying on the line and chosen far apart, its units are the vertical-axis units divided by the horizontal-axis units, and any ×10n axis scale factor is included.

Once a pattern is established it is evaluated. The experimenter identifies the causes of uncertainty in the readings, states the assumptions made and the limitations of the data and the method, and considers whether these could account for any departure of the data from the expected relationship.

Conclusion and poster

A conclusion answers the question and states whether the evidence supports, partially supports or does not support the hypothesis. The words "proves" and "disproves" are not used: a single investigation establishes evidence, not proof. The conclusion also states the limitations of the investigation and its implications, and may indicate further work, but introduces no new information that was not presented earlier.

Before the conclusion is settled, the data and method are evaluated for limitations — sources of random and systematic error, restrictions on the range or number of readings, and assumptions that may not hold — and realistic improvements are proposed for each.

Key idea The investigation is assessed as a scientific poster of no more than 600 words. The prescribed sections are: the title, which is the investigation question; the student's name; an introduction stating the aim, the hypothesis or prediction and the background physics; the methodology and methods; the results, shown so as to reveal the trend; a discussion that interprets and evaluates the data, states limitations and improvements, relates the result to the physics and links back to the hypothesis; a conclusion that answers the question; and the references and acknowledgements.

At the centre of the poster sits a single Communication statement of one sentence that answers the investigation question. It occupies between 20 and 25 per cent of the poster area, so that a reader takes the central finding at a glance. The poster as a whole follows the conventions of science communication — correct terminology, symbols, equations, standard abbreviations, significant figures and units — and acknowledges its references succinctly.

Connects The question may be set in any content area studied. A fields investigation might measure how magnetic field strength varies with distance from a wire, drawing on Action without contact; a motion investigation might measure how a projectile's range depends on launch angle; a light investigation might measure how fringe spacing depends on slit separation, drawing on Light, matter, relativity. In every case the discipline of this Area of Study — one continuous independent variable, controlled conditions, honest uncertainty — is the same.
