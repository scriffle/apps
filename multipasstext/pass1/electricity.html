Pass 1 · Big picture — read at the start of the year

The accident that lit the world

Area of Study 3 examines how fields produce mains electricity; the explanation descends from a single deflection of a meter needle in 1831. The account extends from Faraday's bench to the 500 kV lines leaving the Latrobe Valley; nothing is solved here, only traced.

The flick of a switch

On a winter evening, Melbourne draws its electrical load: kettles, trams, lighting and domestic switches. The wire behind a switch can be traced backwards through the wall, beneath the street, through a suburban substation to a terminal station on the city's edge, then along the 500 kV lines running east across Gippsland. Approximately 170 km from a Melbourne kitchen, the wire terminates at a machine in the Latrobe Valley.

The machine is a coil of wire rotating in a magnetic field. In the Latrobe Valley, steam raised by burning brown coal provides the rotation; in the Snowy Mountains, falling water; on the plains west of Melbourne, moving air. Apart from the boilers and blades, almost every large generator is the same device: a coil of wire turning between magnets.

This Area of Study, titled How are fields used in electricity generation?, explains that device and the network built around it. It begins where the previous page concluded: with the observation that a field can be static or changing, and that the changing kind is the kind that produces electricity.

Connects The previous page identified static versus changing as the consequential field classification. This Area of Study applies that idea to the grid; Unit 4 applies it again, to light itself.

Faraday's accident

London, August 1831: Michael Faraday, formerly a bookbinder's apprentice and originator of the field concept, had pursued one conviction for a decade. An electric current creates a magnetic field, as Hans Christian Oersted had shown in 1820. It followed that a magnetic field should create a current. Faraday wound two separate coils onto an iron ring, drove a steady current through the first, and observed a galvanometer, a sensitive current meter, connected to the second, expecting the steady current that symmetry appeared to predict.

The needle registered nothing, or almost nothing: at the instant the battery was connected, it deflected, then returned to zero and remained there, regardless of how strong and steady the current in the first coil was. At the instant of disconnection, it deflected again, in the opposite direction. The anticipated effect was absent; the observed effect occurred only in the two brief moments when the magnetic field was changing.

By October the experiment had been reduced to its clearest form. Moving a bar magnet into a coil of wire causes the needle to deflect. Holding the magnet stationary inside the coil, for any duration and with any strength of magnet, produces no current. Withdrawing it causes the needle to deflect in the opposite direction.

Motion inward produces current in one direction; motion outward produces current in the other; no motion produces no current. The field does not drive charges through the wire. The change in the field does.

The action a changing field exerts on charges is called an induced emf, short for electromotive force, a misleading historical name: it is not a force, and it is measured in volts. The phenomenon as a whole is electromagnetic induction, and every generator on this page reproduces Faraday's effect deliberately and repeatedly. The statement that the field changes is not precise enough to base a grid on. The question is which quantity must change.

Connects The magnet-and-coil experiment, with its full set of rules for what registers on the meter and what does not, opens Pass 2 at Faraday's law.

The field through a window

The wire loop can be regarded as a window frame and the magnetic field lines as threads passing through the opening. The quantity on which Faraday's discovery depends is the magnetic flux: the amount of field passing through the loop. When the loop is square-on to the field the flux is greatest; when it is tilted less passes through; when it is edge-on the lines pass without entering, giving zero flux. The formula is named ΦB = B⊥A on formula sheet, the field strength multiplied by the area held perpendicular to it, and flux has its own unit, the weber (Wb).

Since induction requires changing flux, the formula lists the quantities that can be changed. B can be changed, by strengthening or weakening the field or by moving the loop nearer to or further from the magnet. A, the area within the field, can be changed by sliding the loop in or out. The angle can be changed, by rotating the loop so that it faces the field, then presents its edge, then faces it again: three quantities, namely field, area and angle.

Faraday's law states that the induced emf equals the rate at which the flux changes, multiplied by the number of turns on the coil: ε = −N ΔΦB/Δt on formula sheet. Expressed as a statement, more flux change in less time produces a larger emf, and a coil of N turns is driven N times over. The magnitude of the flux is irrelevant; only its rate of change is relevant. The strongest field, held steady, induces nothing.

Of the three quantities, the grid uses the third. When a coil rotates in a steady field its flux swings from maximum, through zero, to maximum in the other direction, and back, producing change that does not cease. This rotating coil is the basis of the remainder of the account, with one exception at the end.

Connects Flux, the weber and the three ways to change them are made precise in Magnetic flux — calculation for the perpendicular case, qualitative reasoning for every angle in between.

Nature pushes back

Faraday's law includes a minus sign, and that minus sign has its own name: Lenz's law, after Heinrich Lenz, who stated it in 1834. The induced current flows in the direction that opposes the change creating it. Moving a magnet's north pole toward a coil produces an induced current that circulates so the coil presents a north pole in return, resisting the approach; withdrawing the magnet reverses the current, which then opposes the withdrawal. The wording is precise: the current opposes the change in flux, not the field itself.

The necessity of the opposition can be shown by considering the alternative. If the induced current aided the change, a displaced magnet would be drawn in faster, changing the flux faster, inducing a larger current and drawing it harder still, a runaway process producing energy without input, initiated by a slight push. Energy conservation prohibits this, and that is the content of Lenz's law: it is a consequence of energy conservation. Every joule of electrical energy induced must be supplied as work done against the opposition.

This is why a generator resists rotation. A generator connected to nothing rotates freely; when a lamp is connected the rotation becomes harder, reflecting the energy the lamp consumes. When a suburb is connected, a power station's output of steam is required to keep the coil turning. A braking electric car reverses the arrangement: its motor acts as a generator, and the Lenz opposition becomes the braking force, storing energy in the battery rather than dissipating it in the brake pads.

The motor, run backwards

A previously studied machine can now be reconsidered. Area of Study 2 introduced the DC motor: a coil on an axle between magnet poles; with current supplied, the two sides of the coil are pushed in opposite directions, producing rotation, while a split-ring commutator reverses the current every half-turn so that the rotation does not change direction.

If the battery is disconnected and the coil is rotated, the rotation changes the flux through the coil; the changing flux induces an emf; the emf drives a current out. The same hardware now functions as a generator, the reverse of the motor: a motor consumes electrical energy to produce motion; a generator consumes motion to produce electrical energy. Every label on the motor diagram remains unchanged; only the direction of energy flow is reversed.

One design choice remains, namely how the rotating coil transfers its current to the stationary external circuit, and it determines the form of the output. Slip rings, two complete rings each joined to one end of the coil, maintain continuous contact and deliver the emf as it is produced, reversing direction every half-turn. That output is alternating current, AC, and the machine is called an alternator. The motor's own fitting, the split-ring commutator, does the opposite: by exchanging the connections every half-turn it cancels each reversal as it occurs, so that the external current always flows in one direction, namely direct current, DC, rising and falling but not reversing. One fitting yields two different outputs.

The grid's one trick

When the coil rotates at a perfectly steady rate the induced emf traces a sine wave, swinging positive then negative once per turn as the flux rises and falls. Australia's grid is tuned so that the swing completes 50 cycles every second, mains electricity at 50 Hz. Its magnitude is quoted as 230 V, but that figure conceals a subtlety: the voltage at the wall actually swings between approximately +325 V and −325 V, and 230 V is the steady DC voltage that would deliver the same power. That equivalent average is the root-mean-square (rms) value; Pass 2 explains the name.

Between the Latrobe Valley and the wall, however, a physics problem arises. Wire has resistance, and current flowing through resistance converts electrical energy into heat, a loss given by Ploss = Iline² Rline on formula sheet. The quantity that is squared is the current.

Power equals voltage multiplied by current, so the same power can be transmitted as a low voltage with a large current, or as a high voltage with a small current. Raising the voltage tenfold reduces the current to a tenth and the heating loss to a hundredth. Over 170 km of line, high voltage is not a preference but a necessity.

The device that makes voltage adjustable is a developed form of Faraday's iron ring: the transformer. Two coils share an iron core; no part moves. Alternating current in the primary coil produces a changing flux; the core carries it through the secondary coil; Faraday's law induces an emf there, stepped up or down in exact proportion to the turns on each coil: N1/N2 = V1/V2 = I2/I1 on formula sheet. When the voltage is stepped up the current steps down by the same factor, the power ideally unchanged.

This also explains why the grid operates on AC: a transformer functions only with changing flux. With steady DC the flux remains constant; nothing is induced, and nothing is produced at the output.

The journey shown in Figure 1.1 is therefore Faraday's law applied repeatedly. A coil in the Latrobe Valley induces approximately 20 kV; a step-up transformer raises it to 500 kV for transmission to Melbourne; terminal stations and suburban substations step it back down in stages; and a final transformer near the house delivers 230 V to the wall, 50 times a second.

Electricity without motion

One generator type on the grid does not follow the rotating-coil template. More than four million Australian rooftops now carry photovoltaic cells, panels that remain motionless in the sun while current flows out of them. There is no coil, no magnet and no rotation; only electrons and light move.

Inside each cell is a thin layer of two slices of silicon, each treated to conduct slightly differently. Incident light displaces electrons, and an electric field, built permanently into the junction between the two slices, allows the displaced electrons to cross in one direction only. A one-way action produces a one-way current: photovoltaic cells produce DC. A field still performs the work, in this case the electric field, located at a junction rather than passing a coil.

Houses and the grid operate on AC, so every rooftop system includes one further component: the inverter, which converts the panels' DC into 230 V AC. The study design requires only what an inverter does, not how it does it; its electronic circuitry, like the detailed operation of the silicon, is beyond the course.

One question remains: why light should be able to displace electrons from silicon at all. The answer requires a new idea about the nature of light, namely that it arrives in discrete packets of energy, and it is the opening topic of Unit 4, where the packets are named photons.
