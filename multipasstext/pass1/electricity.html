Pass 1 · Big picture — read at the start of the year

The accident that lit the world

Area of Study 3 asks how fields make the electricity in your wall; the whole answer descends from one twitch of a meter needle in 1831. Read this in one sitting — Faraday's bench to the 500 kV lines leaving the Latrobe Valley — nothing solved, only followed.

The flick of a switch

Some winter evening this year, Melbourne will switch itself on — kettles, trams, footy training under lights, and you, flicking a switch without a thought. Follow the wire behind that switch backwards: through the wall, under the street, through a suburban substation to a terminal station on the city's edge, then along the 500 kV lines that stride east across Gippsland. About 170 km from your kitchen, the wire ends at a machine in the Latrobe Valley.

The machine is a coil of wire spinning in a magnetic field. That is the whole secret. In the Valley, steam raised by burning brown coal does the spinning; in the Snowy Mountains, falling water; on the windy plains west of Melbourne, moving air. Strip away the boilers and blades and almost every large generator on Earth is the same device: a coil of wire, turning between magnets.

This Area of Study — its official title is How are fields used in electricity generation? — explains that one device and the network built around it. And it begins exactly where the last page left off: with the observation that a field can be static or changing, and that the changing kind is the kind that pays.

Connects The last page flagged static versus changing as the consequential field classification. This Area of Study spends that idea on the grid; Unit 4 spends it again, on light itself.

Faraday's accident

London, August 1831: Michael Faraday — the bookbinder's apprentice who gave us the field — had been circling one conviction for a decade. An electric current creates a magnetic field; Hans Christian Oersted had shown that in 1820. Surely, then, a magnetic field should create a current. Faraday wound two separate coils onto an iron ring, drove a steady current through the first, and watched a galvanometer — a sensitive current meter — connected to the second, waiting for the steady current that symmetry seemed to promise.

The needle gave him nothing. Or almost nothing: at the instant he connected the battery, it twitched, then fell back to zero and stayed there, however strong and steady the current in the first coil. At the instant he disconnected, it twitched again — the other way. The effect he had planned for was absent; the one he had not lived entirely at the edges, in the two brief moments when the magnetic field was changing.

By October he had the clean version of the experiment. Push a bar magnet into a coil of wire and the needle kicks. Hold the magnet still inside the coil — for as long as you like, with as strong a magnet as you like — and nothing flows. Pull it out and the needle kicks the opposite way.

Motion in, current one way; motion out, current the other; no motion, no current. The field does not drive charges through the wire. The change in the field does.

The push that a changing field gives to charges is called an induced emf — short for electromotive force, a misleading old name: it is not a force, and it is measured in volts. The phenomenon as a whole is electromagnetic induction, and every generator on this page commits Faraday's accident deliberately, over and over. But "the field changes" is not precise enough to build a grid on. Exactly which quantity has to change?

The field through a window

Picture the wire loop as a window frame, and the magnetic field lines as threads passing through the opening. The quantity Faraday's discovery hinges on is the magnetic flux: how much field passes through the window. Hold the window square-on to the field and the flux is at its greatest; tilt it and less passes through; turn it edge-on and the lines slide past without entering at all — zero flux. The formula is named ΦB = B⊥A on formula sheet — field strength multiplied by the area held perpendicular to it — and flux earns its own unit, the weber (Wb).

If induction needs changing flux, the formula is a list of handles. You can change B, by strengthening or weakening the field or moving the loop nearer the magnet or further away. You can change A, the area sitting inside the field, by sliding the loop in or out. Or you can change the angle, by rotating the loop so it shows the field its face, then its edge, then its face again — three handles: field, area, angle.

Faraday's law says the induced emf equals the rate at which the flux changes, multiplied by the number of turns on the coil: ε = −N ΔΦB/Δt on formula sheet. Read it as a sentence rather than solving it: more flux change in less time means a bigger push, and a coil of N turns is pushed N times over. The size of the flux is irrelevant; only its rate of change counts. The strongest field in the world, held steady, induces precisely nothing.

Of the three handles, the grid grips the third. Spin a coil in a steady field and its flux swings from maximum, through zero, to maximum the other way, and back — change that never runs out. Hold that picture: it is the rest of this story, with one sunlit exception at the end.

Connects Flux, the weber and the three ways to change them are made precise in Magnetic flux — calculation for the perpendicular case, qualitative reasoning for every angle in between.

Nature pushes back

Faraday's law arrives wearing a minus sign, and the minus sign has its own name: Lenz's law, after Heinrich Lenz, who stated it in 1834. The induced current flows in the direction that opposes the change creating it. Push a magnet's north pole toward a coil and the induced current circulates so that the coil presents a north pole back, resisting the approach; pull the magnet away and the current reverses, now pulling the magnet back. Note the precise wording — the current opposes the change in flux, never the field itself.

To see why it must oppose, run the alternative. If the induced current aided the change, a nudged magnet would be drawn in faster, changing the flux faster, inducing a larger current, pulling harder still — a runaway spiral handing out energy for free, started by a fingertip. Energy conservation forbids it, and that is all Lenz's law is: energy conservation wearing a disguise. Every joule of electrical energy you induce must be paid for as work done against the opposition.

This is why a generator resists being turned. Spin one connected to nothing and it turns freely; connect a lamp and the crank stiffens — you can feel, in your wrist, the energy the lamp is spending. Connect a suburb and it takes a power station's worth of steam to keep the coil turning. A braking electric car runs the bargain in reverse: its motor acts as a generator, and the Lenz opposition becomes the braking force — banking energy in the battery instead of burning it in the brake pads.

The motor, run backwards

Now revisit a machine you already own. Area of Study 2 built the DC motor: a coil on an axle between magnet poles; current in, the two sides of the coil pushed in opposite directions, rotation out — with a split-ring commutator reversing the current every half-turn so that the rotation never changes direction.

Unhook the battery and spin the coil yourself. Rotation changes the flux through the coil; changing flux induces an emf; the emf drives a current out. The same hardware is now a generator — the film of the motor run backwards: a motor spends electrical energy to make motion; a generator spends motion to make electrical energy. Every label on the motor diagram survives unchanged; only the energy flow turns around.

One design choice remains — how the spinning coil hands its current to the stationary outside world — and it decides everything about the output. Slip rings, two complete rings with one joined to each end of the coil, keep continuous contact and deliver the emf exactly as it is made: reversing direction every half-turn. That output is alternating current, AC, and the machine is called an alternator. The motor's own fitting, the split-ring commutator, does the opposite: by swapping the connections every half-turn it cancels each reversal as it happens, so the outside current always flows one way — direct current, DC — rising and falling in bumps, but never reversing — one small fitting, two different products.

The grid's one trick

Spin the coil at a perfectly steady rate and the induced emf traces the smoothest curve in physics: a sine wave, swinging positive then negative once per turn as the flux rises and falls. Australia's grid is tuned so that the swing completes 50 cycles every second — mains electricity at 50 Hz. Its size is quoted as 230 V, but that number hides a subtlety: the voltage at your wall actually swings between about +325 V and −325 V, and 230 V is the steady DC voltage that would deliver the same power. That honest average is the root-mean-square (rms) value; Pass 2 unpacks the name.

Between the Latrobe Valley and that wall, though, stands a physics problem. Wire has resistance, and current flowing through resistance turns precious electrical energy into useless heat — a loss named Ploss = Iline² Rline on formula sheet. Notice what is squared: the current.

Power is voltage multiplied by current, so the same power can travel as a low voltage with a large current, or as an enormous voltage with a tiny current. Raise the voltage ten times and the current falls to a tenth — and the heating loss to a hundredth. Over 170 km of line, high voltage is not a preference; it is the entire game.

The machine that makes voltage adjustable is Faraday's iron ring grown up: the transformer. Two coils share an iron core; nothing moves. Alternating current in the primary coil makes a changing flux; the core carries it through the secondary coil; Faraday's law induces an emf there, stepped up or down in exact proportion to the turns on each coil: N1/N2 = V1/V2 = I2/I1 on formula sheet. Step the voltage up and the current steps down by the same factor, the power ideally untouched.

Here, too, is why the grid runs on AC at all: a transformer works only with changing flux. Give it steady DC and the flux freezes — nothing is induced, and nothing comes out.

So the journey in Figure 1.1 is Faraday's law applied over and over. A coil in the Valley induces about 20 kV; a step-up transformer lifts it to 500 kV for the long march to Melbourne; terminal stations and suburban substations step it back down in stages; and a final transformer near your house delivers 230 V to the wall, 50 times a second, ready for the flick of a switch.

Electricity without motion

One generator on the grid breaks the spinning-coil template entirely. More than four million Australian rooftops now carry photovoltaic cells — panels that sit silent and motionless in the sun while current flows out of them. No coil, no magnet, no rotation: nothing moves but electrons and light.

Inside each cell is a thin sandwich of two slices of silicon, each treated to conduct slightly differently. Arriving light knocks electrons loose, and an electric field, built permanently into the junction between the two slices, lets the loosened electrons cross in one direction only. A one-way push makes a one-way current: photovoltaic cells produce DC. A field is still doing all the work — the electric field this time, standing at a junction rather than sweeping past a coil.

Your house and the grid speak AC, so every rooftop system includes one more box: the inverter, which converts the panels' DC into 230 V AC. The study design asks only what an inverter does, not how — its electronic circuitry, like the fine detail of the silicon sandwich, is officially beyond the course.

One question is left deliberately hanging: why should light be able to knock electrons out of silicon at all? The answer needs a new idea about what light is — that it arrives in discrete packets of energy — and it is the opening move of Unit 4, where the packets get their name: photons.
