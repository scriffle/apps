Action without contact

Everything in Unit 3 Area of Study 2 — and most of the year beyond it — grows from one question: how can an object move another object it never touches? Read this story in one sitting. Nothing here needs to be solved, only seen.

The oldest magic trick

Let go of your phone and it falls. Nothing visible reaches up to take it — no string, no hand, no puff of air — yet the instant your fingers open, something accelerates it toward the floor at 9.8 m s⁻², every time.

A compass needle, sealed inside its plastic case, swings to the north wherever in Victoria you stand. A balloon rubbed on a jumper lifts your hair from a handsbreadth away. Three small mysteries, one shared puzzle: in each case, motion arrives with no contact at all.

This puzzle has bothered better minds than ours. Newton wrote the law of gravitation that still guides spacecraft, yet he privately called the notion of bodies acting on one another across empty space an absurdity, and declined to guess how the trick was done. The whole of this Area of Study — its official title is How do things move without contact? — is the modern reply.

The reply is a single idea, the field, plus a year of consequences. This page tells the story end to end, so that when the detail arrives in Pass 2 it arrives in a landscape you already know.

Connects You have been using a field strength since Year 11: the 9.8 you multiply masses by carries the unit N kg⁻¹ — newtons of force for every kilogram placed at that point. Pass 2 chapter 1 makes that unit the centre of Unit 3.

The invention of the field

The answer arrived from an unlikely direction. Michael Faraday — a bookbinder's apprentice who taught himself science between jobs — spent the 1830s watching iron filings arrange themselves into arcs around magnets. Where others saw a pretty pattern, Faraday saw something bolder: the magnet alters the space around it, and that altered condition of space — he called the region a field — is real, present whether or not anything is there to be acted on.

The modern definition keeps his idea and tightens it: a field is a physical quantity that has a value at every point in space. Some fields store a single number at each point — a sports analyst's heat map, recording how often a player occupied each patch of the MCG, is exactly this kind of field.

The fields in this Area of Study store something richer at every point: an arrow. The arrow's direction tells you which way the force exerted on a suitable test object placed there would act, and its length tells you how strongly. A field diagram, with its smooth lines and arrowheads, is a map of those arrows — direction along the line, strength shown by how closely the lines crowd together.

One drawing rule does real work: field lines never cross. If two lines met, the field at that point would have two directions at once — and no force can act in two directions at once.

Connects A field diagram is your Unit 2 force diagram scaled up: instead of drawing the arrows acting on one object, you pre-compute the arrow for every point in space. The precise definition, test objects and field-line rules open the core at What is a field?

Three fields, one grammar

Unit 3 hands you three of these maps. The gravitational field is sourced by mass and acts on mass. The electric field is sourced by charge and acts on charge. The magnetic field is sourced by moving charge — a current in a wire, or the orbiting and spinning electrons inside a bar magnet — and acts on moving charge. Figure 1.1 draws the three side by side, and the family resemblance is the point: one grammar, three dialects.

Now the differences, because the differences carry the physics. Mass comes in one kind only — there is no negative mass — so gravity only ever attracts, and every gravitational field line points inward. In the language of Figure 1.1, a mass is purely a sink: lines flow in, never out.

Electric charge comes in two signs. Field lines flow out of positive charges (sources) and into negative charges (sinks), and either kind can exist on its own — an isolated charge is called a monopole. Two signs means electric forces can attract or repel.

Magnetism follows the strangest rule of the three. Nobody has ever found an isolated north or south pole: cut a bar magnet in half and you hold two complete magnets, each with both poles, all the way down to subatomic particles. Magnetic sources are dipoles only — a source and a sink permanently paired — which is why the magnet's lines in Figure 1.1 leave the north face and loop around to the south.

Hold that three-way contrast — gravity: monopoles of one sign; electricity: monopoles of both signs; magnetism: dipoles only — and the three maps stop being three things to memorise and become one idea with three settings. Simulation 1.1 lets you test all three for yourself.

Position is stored energy

Here is the first of four threads to carry through the year. Lift your schoolbag from the floor to the desk and you have done work against the gravitational field. That energy has not vanished: it is stored in the bag's raised position, and the moment the bag slides off the desk it is returned as kinetic energy, in full.

Every field in this Area of Study runs the same bank. Separate a positive and a negative charge and you bank energy in their separation; release them and the account empties back into motion. Position in a field is stored energy — potential energy — and the whole topic is double-entry bookkeeping: energy out of one account, into another, total unchanged.

The accounting unit is the detail to watch. Gravitational stores are tallied per kilogram of mass; electrical stores are tallied per coulomb of charge — and "energy per coulomb" is a quantity you have used since Unit 1 under its everyday name, voltage. One formula, W = qV not on sheet, says exactly this: the energy transformed when a charge q moves through a potential difference V — the formula sheet carries the same statement folded into ½mv² = qV. Nothing to calculate yet — only notice that it is Unit 1's definition of voltage read backwards.

And when a field is not uniform, the store is read graphically, as the area under a force–distance graph — the same trick Units 1&2 used for springs. One ledger, new deposit types.

Connects Gravitational storage is banked in Gravity and orbits and electrical storage in Electric fields. Area of Study 1 runs the identical ledger for projectiles and collisions, and in Unit 4 the photoelectric effect will use voltage as a measuring instrument for single electrons.

The circle that keeps returning

Thread two is a shape. Newton imagined a cannon on a mountaintop above the atmosphere, fired horizontally. A slow cannonball arcs to the ground nearby; a faster one lands further away; and at about 7.9 km s⁻¹ something remarkable happens — the ball falls toward Earth exactly as fast as Earth's surface curves away beneath it. It is falling continuously, and missing continuously: that is all an orbit is.

The Moon is Newton's cannonball, fired long ago. In every second it falls about 1.4 mm toward Earth — the distance its curved path drops below the straight line it would otherwise follow — and in that same second its sideways velocity carries it far enough that the drop never brings it closer. A satellite needs no engine to stay up; it needs sideways speed and a single inward force: gravity.

This rewrites what you may believe about astronauts. At the International Space Station's altitude the gravitational field strength is still about 8.7 N kg⁻¹ — close to 90 per cent of its surface value — so "there is no gravity in space" is wrong. Astronauts float because the station and everyone inside it are falling together: no surface exerts a normal force on anyone, and the normal force is what is felt as weight. Physicists call the state apparent weightlessness, and the word "apparent" is doing honest work.

Now the costume change. A charged particle moving across a magnetic field experiences a force, named by F = qvB on formula sheet, that points at right angles to the particle's velocity — always. A sideways force of constant size changes a path's direction but never its speed, so the particle is steered into a circle, exactly as the satellite is. Whether the inward role is played by gravity on a satellite or by a magnetic field on an electron in a synchrotron ring, it is one piece of circular-motion mathematics in two costumes — constant speed, never constant velocity, because the direction never stops changing.

Connects Orbits are built properly in Satellites and circling charges in Circular paths. Area of Study 1 supplies the circular-motion rule both obey — learn to spot which force is playing the inward, centripetal role and half the year's hardest problems collapse into one.

Fields put to work

Thread three: fields are not only for explaining nature — you can build with them. Start from the workhorse fact of this Area of Study: a current-carrying wire placed across a magnetic field experiences a force, named by F = nIlB on formula sheet. Reverse the current and the force reverses too.

Now bend the wire into a rectangular coil on an axle between two magnet poles. Current travels up one side of the coil and down the other, so the two sides experience equal forces in opposite directions — a turning pair, and the coil rotates. There is one flaw in the design: half a turn later, the same pair of forces would turn it back the other way.

The fix is a small piece of genius called the split-ring commutator — a copper ring cut in two that reverses the current through the coil every half-turn, so the turning effect always acts the same way. That is a DC motor, the machine inside every drill, fan and electric vehicle, and a diagram you will come to know intimately. Run the film backwards — spin the coil with wind, water or steam — and a current is driven through the wire instead: that machine is a generator, and it is the whole subject of Area of Study 3.

The other great field machine is the particle accelerator, whose logic is a two-line summary of this Area of Study: a uniform electric field adds kinetic energy to a charge along a straight line, and a uniform magnetic field bends the charge's path without changing its speed. The Australian Synchrotron in Clayton chains the two together, holding electrons at more than 99.99 per cent of the speed of light.

Connects The motor — with the labelled split-ring commutator diagram examiners ask about almost every year — is built in Motors & accelerators. Area of Study 3 will run the identical hardware in reverse to light the state.

Where the field idea is heading

The last thread points past the horizon of this Area of Study. Among its dot points is a small act of sorting: classify every field as static or changing, and as uniform or non-uniform. It looks like housekeeping; it is the most consequential classification of the year.

Changing magnetic fields drive currents in nearby conductors — that single fact, Faraday's discovery of induction, is how almost every power station on Earth generates electricity, and it is Area of Study 3 in one sentence. Unit 4 then takes the idea somewhere astonishing: a changing electric field creates a changing magnetic field, which recreates the electric field, and the self-sustaining pair detaches from charges and wires altogether and travels through empty space. That travelling ripple of fields is light. The field, invented as a bookkeeping device for forces, turns out to be a thing in its own right.

A second loose thread is printed in the study design itself: the circular-path rule for charges is guaranteed only while the speed v is much less than c. Synchrotron electrons and GPS satellite clocks both live outside that fine print, and Unit 4's relativity is the reading of it. A third: the practical investigation of motor torque — varying the current, the field and the number of loops — is a dress rehearsal for the student-designed investigation that ends Unit 4.
