Pass 1 · Big picture — read at the start of the year

Action without contact

All of Unit 3 Area of Study 2 — and much of the remaining year — develops from one question: how an object can move another object without contact. This overview presents the whole picture. None of it is to be solved at this stage, only surveyed.

The oldest magic trick

A released phone falls. Nothing visible reaches up to take it — no string, no hand, no air current — yet the instant it is released, something accelerates it toward the floor at 9.8 m s⁻², in every case.

A compass needle, sealed inside its plastic case, swings to the north anywhere in Victoria. A balloon rubbed on a jumper attracts hair from a handsbreadth away. These three small observations share one puzzle: in each case, motion arises with no contact at all.

This puzzle has occupied prominent scientists. Newton wrote the law of gravitation that still guides spacecraft, yet he privately regarded the notion of bodies acting on one another across empty space as an absurdity, and declined to propose how it occurred. The whole of this Area of Study — its official title is How do things move without contact? — is the modern response.

The response is a single idea, the field, together with a year of consequences. This overview presents the topic end to end, so that when the detail arrives in Pass 2 it arrives in an already familiar landscape.

Connects A field strength has been in use since Year 11: the value 9.8 multiplied by masses carries the unit N kg⁻¹ — newtons of force for every kilogram placed at that point. Pass 2 chapter 4 makes that unit the centre of Unit 3.

The invention of the field

The answer arrived from an unlikely direction. Michael Faraday — a bookbinder's apprentice who taught himself science between jobs — spent the 1830s observing iron filings arrange themselves into arcs around magnets. Where others saw only a pattern, Faraday proposed something more: the magnet alters the space around it, and that altered condition of space — the region he termed a field — is real, present whether or not anything is there to be acted on.

The modern definition retains his idea and refines it: a field is a physical quantity that has a value at every point in space. Some fields hold a single number at each point — a sports analyst's heat map, recording how often a player occupied each patch of the MCG, is exactly this kind of field.

The fields in this Area of Study hold something richer at every point: an arrow. The arrow's direction indicates the direction of the force exerted on a suitable test object placed there, and its length indicates the strength of that force. A field diagram, with its lines and arrowheads, is a map of those arrows — direction along the line, strength shown by how closely the lines are spaced.

One drawing rule has real significance: field lines never cross. If two lines met, the field at that point would have two directions at once, and no force can act in two directions at once.

Connects A field diagram is the Unit 2 force diagram extended: instead of drawing the arrows acting on one object, the arrow is determined in advance for every point in space. The precise definition, test objects and field-line rules open the core at What is a field?

Three fields, one grammar

Unit 3 introduces three of these maps. The gravitational field is sourced by mass and acts on mass. The electric field is sourced by charge and acts on charge. The magnetic field is sourced by moving charge — a current in a wire, or the orbiting and spinning electrons inside a bar magnet — and acts on moving charge. Figure 1.1 draws the three side by side, and the family resemblance is significant: one grammar, three dialects.

The differences carry the physics. Mass occurs in one kind only — there is no negative mass — so gravity only ever attracts, and every gravitational field line points inward. In the terms of Figure 1.1, a mass is purely a sink: lines flow in, never out.

Electric charge occurs in two signs. Field lines flow out of positive charges (sources) and into negative charges (sinks), and either kind can exist on its own — an isolated charge is called a monopole. Two signs means electric forces can attract or repel.

Magnetism follows the most unusual rule of the three. No isolated north or south pole has ever been found: a bar magnet cut in half yields two complete magnets, each with both poles, down to subatomic particles. Magnetic sources are dipoles only — a source and a sink permanently paired — which is why the magnet's lines in Figure 1.1 leave the north face and loop around to the south.

This three-way contrast — gravity: monopoles of one sign; electricity: monopoles of both signs; magnetism: dipoles only — turns the three maps from three separate things to memorise into one idea with three settings. Simulation 1.1 allows all three to be tested.

Position is stored energy

This is the first of four threads carried through the year. Lifting a schoolbag from the floor to the desk does work against the gravitational field. That energy does not vanish: it is stored in the bag's raised position, and the moment the bag slides off the desk it is returned as kinetic energy, in full.

Every field in this Area of Study operates the same way. Separating a positive and a negative charge stores energy in their separation; releasing them returns that energy to motion. Position in a field is stored energy — potential energy — and the whole topic is double-entry bookkeeping: energy out of one account, into another, total unchanged.

The accounting unit is the detail to note. Gravitational stores are tallied per kilogram of mass; electrical stores are tallied per coulomb of charge — and "energy per coulomb" is a quantity used since Unit 1 under its everyday name, voltage. One formula, W = qV not on sheet, states exactly this: the energy transformed when a charge q moves through a potential difference V — the formula sheet carries the same statement folded into ½mv² = qV. Nothing is to be calculated yet; this is Unit 1's definition of voltage read in reverse.

When a field is not uniform, the store is read graphically, as the area under a force–distance graph — the same method Units 1&2 used for springs. One ledger, new deposit types.

Connects Gravitational storage is banked in Gravity and orbits and electrical storage in Electric fields. Area of Study 1 runs the identical ledger for projectiles and collisions, and in Unit 4 the photoelectric effect will use voltage as a measuring instrument for single electrons.

The circle that keeps returning

Thread two is a shape. Newton imagined a cannon on a mountaintop above the atmosphere, fired horizontally. A slow cannonball arcs to the ground nearby; a faster one lands further away; and at about 7.9 km s⁻¹ a notable result occurs — the ball falls toward Earth exactly as fast as Earth's surface curves away beneath it. It is falling continuously, and missing continuously: that is all an orbit is.

The Moon is Newton's cannonball, fired long ago. In every second it falls about 1.4 mm toward Earth — the distance its curved path drops below the straight line it would otherwise follow — and in that same second its sideways velocity carries it far enough that the drop never brings it closer. A satellite requires no engine to stay up; it requires sideways speed and a single inward force: gravity.

This corrects a common belief about astronauts. At the International Space Station's altitude the gravitational field strength is still about 8.7 N kg⁻¹ — close to 90 per cent of its surface value — so the statement that there is no gravity in space is incorrect. Astronauts float because the station and everyone inside it are falling together: no surface exerts a normal force on anyone, and the normal force is what is felt as weight. This state is termed apparent weightlessness, and the word "apparent" is significant.

The same mathematics appears in a different form. A charged particle moving across a magnetic field experiences a force, named by F = qvB on formula sheet, that points at right angles to the particle's velocity — always. A sideways force of constant size changes a path's direction but never its speed, so the particle is steered into a circle, exactly as the satellite is. Whether the inward role is played by gravity on a satellite or by a magnetic field on an electron in a synchrotron ring, it is one piece of circular-motion mathematics in two forms — constant speed, never constant velocity, because the direction never stops changing.

Connects Orbits are built properly in Satellites and circling charges in Circular paths. Area of Study 1 supplies the circular-motion rule both obey — identifying which force plays the inward, centripetal role reduces many of the year's hardest problems to one.

Fields put to work

Thread three: fields are not only for explaining nature; they can also be used to build. The starting point is the central fact of this Area of Study: a current-carrying wire placed across a magnetic field experiences a force, named by F = nIlB on formula sheet. Reversing the current reverses the force.

When the wire is bent into a rectangular coil on an axle between two magnet poles, current travels up one side of the coil and down the other, so the two sides experience equal forces in opposite directions — a turning pair, and the coil rotates. There is one flaw in this design: half a turn later, the same pair of forces would turn it back the other way.

The remedy is the split-ring commutator — a copper ring cut in two that reverses the current through the coil every half-turn, so the turning effect always acts in the same direction. This is a DC motor, the machine inside every drill, fan and electric vehicle, and a diagram studied in detail later. Run in reverse — the coil spun by wind, water or steam — a current is driven through the wire instead: that machine is a generator, the whole subject of Area of Study 3.

The other major field machine is the particle accelerator, whose operation is a two-line summary of this Area of Study: a uniform electric field adds kinetic energy to a charge along a straight line, and a uniform magnetic field bends the charge's path without changing its speed. The Australian Synchrotron in Clayton chains the two together, holding electrons at more than 99.99 per cent of the speed of light.

Connects The motor — with the labelled split-ring commutator diagram examiners ask about almost every year — is built in Motors & accelerators. Area of Study 3 runs the identical hardware in reverse to supply the state's electricity.

Where the field idea is heading

The last thread points beyond this Area of Study. Among its dot points is a small act of sorting: classify every field as static or changing, and as uniform or non-uniform. This appears to be a minor matter; it is the most consequential classification of the year.

Changing magnetic fields drive currents in nearby conductors — that single fact, Faraday's discovery of induction, is how almost every power station on Earth generates electricity, and it is Area of Study 3 in one sentence. Unit 4 then extends the idea further: a changing electric field creates a changing magnetic field, which recreates the electric field, and the self-sustaining pair detaches from charges and wires altogether and travels through empty space. That travelling ripple of fields is light. The field, introduced as a bookkeeping device for forces, proves to be a thing in its own right.

A second loose thread is printed in the study design itself: the circular-path rule for charges is guaranteed only while the speed v is much less than c. Synchrotron electrons and GPS satellite clocks both lie outside that condition, and Unit 4's relativity addresses it. A third: the practical investigation of motor torque — varying the current, the field and the number of loops — is a preliminary to the student-designed investigation that ends Unit 4.
