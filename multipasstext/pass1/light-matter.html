Pass 1 · Big picture — read at the start of the year

The light that would not behave

Unit 4 Area of Study 1 concerns three established models — light as a wave, matter as particles, and space and time as fixed — and the experiments that overturned each of them. It is intended to be read as a single overview. Nothing here requires solving.

The question nobody could close

The question that organises the whole of this page is the nature of light. Newton held that it was a stream of tiny particles, which he termed corpuscles. His contemporary Huygens held that it was a wave. The dispute continued for more than a century, because both models accounted for the everyday evidence of reflection, refraction and shadows.

The implications are substantial: almost all knowledge of the universe — the temperature of stars, the composition of galaxies, the structure of a virus — arrives as light. An incorrect account of light entails an incorrect account of everything that depends on it.

This Area of Study — its official question is How has understanding about the physical world changed? — proceeds in stages. Each stage opens with an established model, introduces one persistent experimental result, and follows the model to its failure. Figure 1.1 summarises the evidence: stripes that particles cannot produce, electrons ejected in a manner waves cannot explain, and particles of matter producing wave patterns. By the conclusion, space and time themselves are also revised.

Connects The Fields material concluded with a key result: a changing electric field and a changing magnetic field can regenerate each other, detach from every wire and charge, and travel through empty space — see Where the field idea is heading. This page develops that result, and then shows it overturned, twice.

The wave wins the first round

In 1801 Thomas Young appeared to settle the long-standing dispute. Light was passed through two narrow slits onto a screen. Were light composed of Newton's corpuscles, two bright stripes should appear, one behind each slit. Instead the screen showed many: a long series of evenly spaced bright and dark bands.

Only a wave produces this result. Where two ripples on a pond meet crest on crest, they reinforce; crest on trough, they cancel. A bright band marks a position where the waves from the two slits arrive in step — a path difference of a whole number of wavelengths, nλ — and a dark band marks a half-wavelength of mismatch, (n + ½)λ, in the same manner as overlapping sound and water waves. The pattern is sufficiently regular that one formula, Δx = λL/d on formula sheet, relates the fringe spacing to the wavelength. Young had thereby measured the wavelength of light with little more than a ruler.

The question of what was waving was answered in the 1860s from material already established. James Clerk Maxwell showed that a changing electric field creates a changing magnetic field, which recreates the electric field, and that this self-sustaining pair must travel — at a speed his equations fixed at 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹, the measured speed of light. Light can therefore be modelled as a transverse electromagnetic wave, produced wherever charges accelerate, and every electromagnetic wave — radio to gamma — travels at the same speed c in a vacuum.

A practical example of this production has already been encountered. Electrons forced around the Australian Synchrotron's storage ring are continuously accelerating — circular motion is acceleration — and accelerating charges radiate. That accounts for the machine's emission of light. By 1900, with Young's pattern and Maxwell's fields in agreement, the matter appeared resolved.

The crack in the wave

The experiment that reopened the matter appears unremarkable. When ultraviolet light is directed onto a clean metal surface, electrons are ejected from it — the photoelectric effect.

The wave model makes three predictions about it. Brighter light carries more energy, so the ejected electrons should leave faster. Any colour should be effective, given time for the metal to absorb energy. And in dim light there should be a delay while the energy accumulates.

Experiment contradicted each prediction. The maximum kinetic energy of the electrons depends only on the light's frequency; increasing the brightness ejects more electrons, not faster ones. Below a threshold frequency, no electrons are emitted at all, at any brightness. And emission is effectively instantaneous, even in the dimmest light.

In 1905 Einstein proposed an account the wave model could not accommodate: light delivers its energy in indivisible amounts. Each amount — each photon — carries an energy given by E = hf on formula sheet, where h is Planck's constant, equal to 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s. One photon transfers all its energy to one electron, which expends the metal's exit energy — the work function φ — and retains the remainder as kinetic energy: Ek max = hf − φ on formula sheet. Each observation follows from this: frequency sets the energy of each amount, brightness sets only the number of amounts, and an amount is transferred whole or not at all.

The measuring method derives from Unit 3. To determine the electrons' energy, a reverse voltage is applied and increased until even the fastest electron no longer crosses — the stopping voltage — which is W = qV not on sheet applied in reverse, as a brake rather than an accelerator. The electron-volt, the energy unit defined when charges are accelerated through potential differences, becomes the working unit of everything that follows.

Matter joins the conspiracy

In 1924 a French physicist, Louis de Broglie, posed the symmetric question. If light — taken to be a wave — behaves as a stream of particles, matter — taken to be particles — might behave as a wave. He proposed that every moving object carries a wavelength, given by λ = h/p on formula sheet: the larger the momentum, the shorter the wave.

Three years later the test was conducted: a beam of electrons was fired at a thin crystal. The beam emerged producing concentric rings on a screen — the same family of pattern that X-rays of equal wavelength produce through the same crystal, as panel (c) of Figure 1.1 shows. The electron, the particle accelerated with voltages and steered into circular paths with magnetic fields in Unit 3, diffracts.

A cricket ball does not diffract through a doorway because h is so small. A 150 g ball bowled at 20 m s⁻¹ has a de Broglie wavelength of about 2.2 × 10⁻³⁴ m — far smaller than any gap it could pass through, and diffraction becomes noticeable only when the wavelength is comparable to the opening. Electrons are sufficiently light that their wavelengths match the spacing between atoms, which is why crystals act as effective diffraction gratings for them.

The most fundamental version of the experiment is the one Simulation 1.1 demonstrates. Electrons — or photons — are sent through Young's double slits one at a time. Each arrives at the screen as a single localised dot: a particle. But as thousands of dots accumulate, the interference fringes form out of the statistics: a wave. No single particle produces a pattern, yet every particle conforms to one.

Atoms on a ladder

Two unresolved facts about atoms preceded these ideas. First, on the classical account atoms should not be stable: a classical electron circling a nucleus is accelerating, and accelerating charges radiate — Maxwell's own rule, the one that accounts for the synchrotron's emission — so the electron should radiate its energy away and spiral into the nucleus in a fraction of a second.

Second, glowing gases do not emit a continuous spectrum. Each element emits only certain sharp colours — a line spectrum, distinctive to that element. Sodium street lamps emit their two yellow lines; metal vapour lamps and neon signs each show their own line spectrum; sunlight carries thousands of dark lines where cooler gas has removed exactly those colours.

The explanation is quantisation. An electron bound in an atom can hold only certain energies — discrete levels, with none in between. A transition from a higher level to a lower one causes the atom to emit a single photon whose energy E = hf exactly matches the gap; absorption of a photon of exactly a gap's energy causes the electron to move to a higher level. Bright emission lines and dark absorption lines correspond to the same set of levels in opposite directions, and a spectrum thereby indicates the atom's energy levels.

De Broglie's wave also accounts for the existence of discrete levels. When the electron's wave is wrapped around the atom, it must close on itself smoothly — a standing wave, comparable to a string that sounds only its harmonics. Only certain wavelengths fit the loop, so only certain energies are permitted.

The description is a simplified model, refined by later physics, but it accounts for the one feature the classical atom could not: why an atom has a lowest level — a ground state — below which the electron cannot fall, and therefore why atoms are stable. Quantisation is not confined to light; it is fundamental to matter.

The speed limit rewrites space and time

One unresolved question remained from the wave model's first success. Every wave known to physics waves in a medium — so the question arose of what carries light across empty space. Physicists named the proposed medium the aether, and in 1887 Michelson and Morley built an instrument precise enough to detect it: two light beams travelled along perpendicular arms, recombined to interfere, and the whole apparatus was rotated and re-tested through the seasons as Earth moved around the Sun. If Earth moves through an aether, the beam times should differ; they never did — the most well-known null result in physics.

In 1905 — the same year as the photon — Einstein accepted the result as it stood and reconstructed mechanics from two postulates: the laws of physics are the same in every inertial frame of reference, and the speed of light in a vacuum is the same for every observer, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer. The second postulate appears unremarkable but is not. If every observer measures the same c, then time and distance themselves must differ between observers.

The consequences carry names used throughout the year. Moving clocks run slow — time dilation, given by t = γt0 on formula sheet. Moving lengths contract along the direction of travel — length contraction, given by L = L0/γ on formula sheet.

The factor γ, the Lorentz factor, is only marginally above 1 at everyday speeds — which is why the effect was not detected for three centuries — and grows without limit as speed approaches c. The qualification "v << c" attached to Unit 3's circular-path formula indicated this condition, stated a unit in advance.

The evidence is observed from cosmic rays. Muons — heavy, short-lived relatives of the electron, created kilometres overhead by cosmic rays — have an average lifetime of about 2.2 μs. Even at 0.995c this permits roughly 660 m of travel, yet muons reach the ground in large numbers. In the ground frame of reference their internal clocks run about ten times slow, so they survive long enough to complete the journey; in the muon's frame of reference the atmosphere is contracted about tenfold, so the journey is short enough to complete — two descriptions, one consistent outcome.

The satellites used to locate a phone carry clocks that run slow by a few microseconds each day because of their orbital speed; uncorrected, GPS positions would drift by kilometres each day. A phone applies special relativity each time it determines a location.

The exchange rate of the universe

Einstein's relativity papers of 1905 concluded with a brief result that proved more significant than the rest: mass is a concentrated form of energy. The rest energy contained in any object is given by E0 = mc² on formula sheet, and because the conversion factor is c², a teaspoon of water contains enough rest energy to supply twenty thousand homes with electricity for a year. When the object is set moving, its total energy becomes γmc² on formula sheet, with the kinetic share given by Ek = (γ − 1)mc² on formula sheet — at everyday speeds this reduces to the familiar formula, a further approximation whose qualification is now apparent.

The Sun operates on this conversion. In its core, hydrogen fuses into helium, and the products have slightly less mass than the reactants; the missing mass is emitted as light. The Sun converts about 4.2 × 10⁹ kg of itself into radiated energy each second — sunlight is the Sun's mass, converted at the universal rate.

The conversion operates in both directions. When an electron meets its antiparticle, the positron, both are annihilated and two gamma-ray photons travel apart in opposite directions — opposite, because momentum must still balance. Hospital PET scanners image the body by detecting exactly these photon pairs.

In particle accelerators the conversion proceeds in reverse: the kinetic energy of a collision is converted into new particles that did not exist before the impact. The synchrotron studied earlier as a circular-motion exercise is, more precisely, a machine whose every magnet is engineered around γ.
