Pass 1 · Big picture — read at the start of the year

The light that would not behave

Unit 4 Area of Study 1 is the story of three superb models — light as a wave, matter as particles, space and time as fixed — and the experiments that broke each of them. Read it in one sitting. Nothing here needs to be solved, only seen.

The question nobody could close

Hold one question in mind for the whole of this page: what, exactly, is light? Newton said it was a stream of tiny particles — he called them corpuscles. His contemporary Huygens said it was a wave. The argument ran for more than a century, because both models explained the everyday evidence of reflection, refraction and shadows.

The stakes are high: almost everything we know about the universe — the temperature of stars, the composition of galaxies, the structure of a virus — arrives as light. Get light wrong and you get everything wrong.

This Area of Study — its official question is How has understanding about the physical world changed? — is built like a detective story. Each act opens with a confident model, introduces one stubborn experiment, and watches the model break. Figure 1.1 is the evidence board: stripes that particles cannot make, electrons ejected in a way waves cannot explain, and particles of matter drawing wave patterns. By the end, even space and time will have given way.

The wave wins the first round

In 1801 Thomas Young settled the old argument — apparently. He let light pass through two narrow slits onto a screen. If light were Newton's corpuscles, two bright stripes should appear, one behind each slit. Instead the screen showed many: a long ladder of evenly spaced bright and dark bands.

Only a wave can do this. Where two ripples on a pond meet crest on crest, they reinforce; crest on trough, they cancel. A bright band marks a place where the waves from the two slits arrive in step — a path difference of a whole number of wavelengths, nλ — and a dark band marks a half-wavelength of mismatch, (n + ½)λ, exactly as overlapping sound and water waves behave. The pattern is so lawful that one formula, Δx = λL/d on formula sheet, connects the fringe spacing to the wavelength — Young had measured the wavelength of light with little more than a ruler.

But a wave of what? The answer came in the 1860s from territory you already own. James Clerk Maxwell showed that a changing electric field creates a changing magnetic field, which recreates the electric field, and that this self-sustaining pair must travel — at a speed his equations fixed at 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹, exactly the measured speed of light. Light, then, can be modelled as a transverse electromagnetic wave, produced wherever charges accelerate, and every electromagnetic wave — radio to gamma — travels at the same speed c in a vacuum.

You have already met the production line. Electrons forced around the Australian Synchrotron's storage ring are continuously accelerating — circular motion is acceleration — and accelerating charges radiate. That is why the machine glows. By 1900, with Young's stripes and Maxwell's fields in agreement, the case looked closed.

The crack in the wave

The experiment that reopened the case looks harmless. Shine ultraviolet light on a clean metal surface and electrons jump out of it — the photoelectric effect.

The wave model makes three confident predictions about it. Brighter light carries more energy, so the ejected electrons should leave faster. Any colour should work, given time for the metal to soak up energy. And in dim light there should be a delay while the energy accumulates.

Experiment said no, three times. The maximum kinetic energy of the electrons depends only on the light's frequency — turning up the brightness ejects more electrons, never faster ones. Below a threshold frequency, no electrons leave at all, at any brightness. And emission is effectively instantaneous, even in the dimmest light.

In 1905 Einstein proposed something the wave model could not survive: light delivers its energy in indivisible lumps. Each lump — each photon — carries an energy named by E = hf on formula sheet, where h is Planck's constant, an absurdly small 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s. One photon gives all its energy to one electron, which pays the metal's exit fee — the work function φ — and keeps the change as kinetic energy: Ek max = hf − φ on formula sheet. Every awkward observation follows at a stroke: frequency sets the energy of each lump, brightness only counts the lumps, and a lump arrives whole or not at all.

The measuring instrument is pure Unit 3. To find the electrons' energy, experimenters apply a reverse voltage and increase it until even the fastest electron no longer crosses — the stopping voltage — which is W = qV not on sheet run backwards, as a brake instead of an accelerator. And the electron-volt, the energy unit born when you accelerated charges through potential differences, becomes the working currency of everything that follows.

Matter joins the conspiracy

In 1924 a French physicist, Louis de Broglie, asked the symmetric question. If light — supposedly a wave — behaves as a stream of particles, might matter — supposedly particles — behave as a wave? He proposed that every moving object carries a wavelength, named by λ = h/p on formula sheet: the larger the momentum, the shorter the wave.

Three years later the test was run: fire a beam of electrons at a thin crystal. The beam emerged drawing concentric rings on a screen — the same family of pattern that X-rays of equal wavelength make through the same crystal, as panel (c) of Figure 1.1 shows. The electron, the particle you accelerated with voltages and steered into circles with magnetic fields in Unit 3, diffracts.

Why does a cricket ball not diffract through a doorway? Because h is so small. A 150 g ball bowled at 20 m s⁻¹ has a de Broglie wavelength of about 2.2 × 10⁻³⁴ m — unimaginably smaller than any gap it could ever pass through, and diffraction only becomes noticeable when the wavelength is comparable to the opening. Electrons are so light that their wavelengths match the spacing between atoms, which is why crystals make perfect diffraction gratings for them.

The deepest version of the experiment is the one Simulation 1.1 lets you run. Send electrons — or photons — through Young's double slits one at a time. Each arrives at the screen as a single localised dot: a particle. But as thousands of dots accumulate, the interference fringes assemble themselves out of the statistics: a wave — no single particle makes a pattern, yet every particle obeys one.

Atoms on a ladder

Two awkward facts about atoms were waiting for these ideas. First, atoms should not exist: a classical electron circling a nucleus is accelerating, and accelerating charges radiate — Maxwell's own rule, the one that lights the synchrotron — so the electron should broadcast its energy away and spiral into the nucleus in a fraction of a second.

Second, glowing gases do not emit a rainbow. Each element emits only certain sharp colours — a line spectrum, a barcode unique to that element. Sodium street lamps glow with their twin yellow lines; metal vapour lamps and neon signs each show their own barcode; sunlight carries thousands of dark lines where cooler gas has subtracted exactly those colours.

The explanation is quantisation. An electron bound in an atom can hold only certain energies — rungs on a ladder, with nothing in between. Drop from a higher rung to a lower one and the atom emits a single photon whose energy E = hf exactly matches the gap; absorb a photon of exactly a gap's energy and the electron climbs. Bright emission lines and dark absorption lines are the same ladder read in opposite directions, and reading a spectrum becomes reading the atom's energy levels.

But why rungs at all? De Broglie's wave answers this too. Wrap the electron's wave around the atom and it must close on itself smoothly — a standing wave, like a guitar string that can play only its harmonics. Only certain wavelengths fit the loop, so only certain energies are allowed.

The picture is a simplified model, refined by later physics, but it explains the one thing the classical atom never could: why an atom has a lowest rung — a ground state — below which the electron cannot fall, and therefore why atoms are stable. Quantisation is not a quirk of light; it is the architecture of matter.

The speed limit rewrites space and time

One loose thread remained from the wave's first victory. Every wave known to physics waves in something — so what carries light across empty space? Physicists named the proposed medium the aether, and in 1887 Michelson and Morley built an instrument exquisite enough to find it: two light beams raced along perpendicular arms, recombined to interfere, the whole apparatus turned and re-tried through the seasons as Earth swung around the Sun. If Earth moves through an aether, the beam times should differ — they never did, the most famous null result in physics.

In 1905 — the same year as the photon — Einstein took the result at face value and rebuilt mechanics from two postulates: the laws of physics are the same in every inertial frame of reference, and the speed of light in a vacuum is the same for every observer, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer. The second postulate sounds innocent. It is not. If everyone measures the same c, then time and distance themselves must differ between observers.

The consequences carry names you will use all year. Moving clocks run slow — time dilation, named by t = γt0 on formula sheet. Moving lengths shrink along the direction of travel — length contraction, named by L = L0/γ on formula sheet.

The factor γ, the Lorentz factor, is barely above 1 at everyday speeds — which is why nobody noticed for three centuries — and grows without limit as speed approaches c. The fine print "v << c" attached to Unit 3's circular-path formula was this warning, planted a unit early.

The evidence falls out of the sky. Muons — heavy, short-lived cousins of the electron, created kilometres overhead by cosmic rays — live about 2.2 μs on average. Even at 0.995c that allows roughly 660 m of travel, yet they arrive at the ground in abundance. In the ground frame of reference their internal clocks run about ten times slow, so they live long enough to make the trip; in the muon's frame of reference the atmosphere is contracted about tenfold, so the trip is short enough to survive — two descriptions, one agreed outcome.

And the satellites that locate your phone carry clocks that tick slow by a few microseconds each day because of their orbital speed — left uncorrected, GPS fixes would drift by kilometres every day. Your phone applies special relativity every time it finds you.

The exchange rate of the universe

Einstein's relativity papers of 1905 ended with a short afterthought that outgrew everything else: mass is a concentrated form of energy. The rest energy locked in any object is named by E0 = mc² on formula sheet, and because the exchange rate is c², a teaspoon of water holds enough rest energy to supply twenty thousand homes with electricity for a year. Set the object moving and its total energy becomes γmc² on formula sheet, with the kinetic share named by Ek = (γ − 1)mc² on formula sheet — at everyday speeds this collapses back to the familiar formula, one more approximation with its fine print now visible.

The Sun runs on this exchange. In its core, hydrogen fuses into helium, and the products weigh slightly less than the ingredients; the missing mass leaves as light. The Sun converts about 4.2 × 10⁹ kg of itself into radiated energy every second — sunshine is the Sun's mass, spent at the universal rate.

The exchange runs in both directions. When an electron meets its antiparticle, the positron, both vanish and two gamma-ray photons fly apart in opposite directions — opposite, because momentum must still balance. Hospital PET scanners image the body by catching exactly those photon pairs.

And in particle accelerators the trade reverses: the kinetic energy of a collision condenses into new particles that did not exist before the impact. The synchrotron you studied as a circular-motion exercise is, in the fine print, a machine whose every magnet is engineered around γ.
