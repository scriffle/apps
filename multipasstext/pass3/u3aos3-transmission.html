Transmission of electricity

Dot points KK 3.5–3.8: the vocabulary of sinusoidal AC, rms and the DC voltage it stands in for, the ideal transformer, and transmission losses. This cluster carries the supply-system archetype — generator, step-up transformer, lossy lines, step-down transformer, town — on which 85% of the state scored zero in 2025, and the worst-answered multiple-choice item of recent exams: the meaning of rms. Almost every formula here is printed on the formula sheet — f = 1/T is the main exception — so the marks are paid for keeping three voltages and two currents distinct, never for recall.

KK 3.5 compare sinusoidal AC voltages produced as a result of the uniform rotation of a loop in a constant magnetic field with reference to frequency, period, amplitude, peak-to-peak voltage (Vp–p) and peak-to-peak current (Ip–p)

What you must be able to do

  • Read the frequency, period, amplitude (peak value), peak-to-peak voltage Vp–p and peak-to-peak current Ip–p of a sinusoidal output from a gridded trace, converting between f and T with f = 1T not on sheet.
  • Predict how the trace changes when the rotation rate of the loop changes: frequency and amplitude always change together.
  • Identify the modifications that change the amplitude alone — the number of turns N, the field strength B, or the loop area A — and justify each through ε = −N ΔΦBΔt on formula sheet.

The amplitude of a trace is its peak value, measured from zero to a crest. The peak-to-peak voltage is measured from trough to crest, so Vp–p = 2Vpeak not on sheet — oscilloscope questions favour it because it can be read without locating the zero line. The period is the time for one complete cycle, read between consecutive matching points, and f = 1/T must be known: it is not on the formula sheet. Trace values are designed to land on gridlines; if a reading falls between lines, recheck the axis scale.

Rotating the loop at twice the rate halves the period and doubles the frequency — and, at the same time, the same flux change occurs in half the time, so the rate of change of flux and with it the peak emf both double. Applied in reverse, the same logic excludes a rotation-rate change: a trace whose amplitude has doubled at an unchanged frequency cannot be the result of faster rotation, so the change must be a doubling of N, B or A. A current trace through a resistive load is read the same way, locked to the voltage by I = VR on formula sheet, so Ip–p = Vp–p/R.

Trap A rotation-rate change never alters the amplitude alone — it moves frequency and amplitude together. If a question states or shows that the frequency is unchanged, rule out any change to the rotation rate before listing modifications, and match the question quantitatively: a doubled amplitude demands the answer "double N, B or A", not "increase" them.
Assessor's advice On the 2024 alternator-trace question the common errors were giving the peak voltage when peak-to-peak was asked, and the period when frequency was asked. On the follow-up modification question, 48% scored zero: "The most common error was to refer only to increasing the identified quantities rather than doubling them" — and a change of rotation rate is excluded by the unchanged frequency on the trace.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna connects a small hand-cranked alternator to an oscilloscope and turns the handle at a steady rate. The output voltage is shown in Figure 5.1.

a. State the peak-to-peak voltage and the frequency of the output. (2 marks)

b. Shanna doubles the rotation rate of the handle. State the new peak voltage and the new period of the output. (2 marks)

c. Explain why doubling the rotation rate changes both the amplitude and the period of the trace. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The peak voltage is 6.0 V, so Vp–p = 2 × 6.0 = 12 V (1 mark). One cycle takes T = 20 ms = 0.020 s, so f = 1/T = 1 ÷ 0.020 = 50 Hz (1 mark).

1 mark: Vp–p = 12 V · 1 mark: f = 50 Hz.

Do not accept (a): 6.0 V (the peak, not peak-to-peak); 0.020 s or 20 ms (the period, not the frequency). Answer the quantity that was asked.

b. The peak voltage doubles to 12 V (1 mark) and the period halves to 10 ms (1 mark).

1 mark: peak voltage 12 V · 1 mark: period 10 ms (frequency 100 Hz).

c. At twice the rotation rate the flux through the coil sweeps from maximum to zero in half the time, so the rate of change of flux doubles and, by ε = −NΔΦB/Δt, the peak emf — the amplitude — doubles (1 mark). Each revolution now takes half the time, so the period of the trace halves (1 mark).

1 mark: same ΔΦB in half the time → doubled rate of change of flux → doubled amplitude, via Faraday's law · 1 mark: half the time per revolution → halved period.

Assessor's advice The 2024 version of part a averaged 1.6 of 2 marks: students reported the peak instead of the peak-to-peak voltage, or the period instead of the frequency. Both numbers were on the trace — the marks were lost in reading the question, not the graph.
In the style of VCAA · MC

The coil of an alternator rotates at a constant rate in a uniform magnetic field, producing a sinusoidal output with an amplitude of 8.0 V and a period of 20 ms. The rotation rate of the coil is then halved. Which one of the following best describes the new output? (1 mark)

  1. amplitude 4.0 V and period 20 ms
  2. amplitude 8.0 V and period 40 ms
  3. amplitude 4.0 V and period 40 ms
  4. amplitude 4.0 V and period 10 ms
Worked solution

C. Halving the rotation rate halves the rate of change of flux, so by ε = −NΔΦB/Δt the amplitude halves to 4.0 V; each revolution takes twice as long, so the period doubles to 40 ms. A speed change always moves both quantities.

Why each distractor is wrong: A changes the amplitude only, as if the rotation rate did not set the period · B changes the period only, as if the rate of change of flux were unaffected · D halves the period instead of doubling it, confusing period with frequency.

KK 3.6 compare alternating voltage expressed as the root-mean-square (rms) to a constant DC voltage developing the same power in a resistive component

What you must be able to do

  • State what rms means: the rms value of an alternating voltage equals the constant DC voltage that develops the same average power in the same resistive component.
  • Convert with Vrms = 1√2 Vpeak on formula sheet and Irms = 1√2 Ipeak on formula sheet, and chain through to Vp–p when asked.
  • Calculate average power as P = VrmsIrms, using P = VI on formula sheet — never with peak values.

The straight average of a sinusoidal voltage is zero — every positive half-cycle is cancelled by a negative one — so the average voltage is useless as a rating. The rms value is defined through power instead: it is the dot point verbatim, and the definition itself is examined. Australian mains is 230 V rms at 50 Hz, so Vpeak = 230 × √2 = 325 V and Vp–p = 650 V, with T = 20 ms; the 230 V is the effective value, not the maximum.

Average power in a resistive component is VrmsIrms. The product of peak values is the instantaneous maximum power, which is exactly twice the average: VpeakIpeak = (√2 Vrms)(√2 Irms) = 2VrmsIrms. The sheet carries P = VI and I = V/R but not P = V²/R not on sheet, so for a resistive load find Irms = Vrms/R first and multiply. Two checks apply: an rms value is always smaller than its peak, so a computed rms that exceeds the peak signals an inverted √2; and unless a question explicitly asks for a peak or peak-to-peak value, the working remains in rms throughout and answers are stated in rms.

Trap Computing "the power" from peak values. VpeakIpeak overstates the average power by a factor of 2 — it is the power at the single instant the sinusoid crests. Average power calculations use rms values only; that equivalence is the entire reason rms exists.
Assessor's advice On the 2025 exam only 30% identified that a 6 V rms supply and a 6 V battery transfer the same average power to identical globes — the worst-answered AOS 3 multiple-choice item of recent years. The most popular wrong answer (33%) was equal average potential difference, which for a sinusoid is zero. The 2025 report states the definition: "RMS voltage is defined as the AC voltage that delivers the same average power as a DC voltage of the same value."
In the style of VCAA · MC

Two identical filament globes are connected to separate supplies. Globe 1 is connected to an AC supply of 12 V rms; globe 2 is connected to a 12 V DC battery. Which one of the following best describes the two circuits? (1 mark)

  1. The average current is the same in each globe.
  2. The average power transferred to each globe is the same.
  3. The average potential difference across each globe is the same.
  4. The maximum potential difference across each globe is the same.
Worked solution

B. Equal average power in the same resistive component is the definition of the rms value: a 12 V rms alternating supply develops the same average power as a constant 12 V DC supply.

Why each distractor is wrong: A — the average of the alternating current is zero, not the battery current · C — the average of a sinusoidal potential difference is zero, while the battery's is 12 V · D — the AC maximum is Vpeak = 12 × √2 = 17 V, larger than the constant 12 V.

In the style of VCAA · MC

The heating element of an electric blanket dissipates an average power of 23 W when the rms current through it is 0.10 A. Which one of the following is closest to the peak-to-peak voltage across the element? (1 mark)

  1. 230 V
  2. 325 V
  3. 460 V
  4. 650 V
Worked solution

D. Average power gives the rms voltage: Vrms = P/Irms = 23 ÷ 0.10 = 230 V. Then Vpeak = √2 × 230 = 325 V, and Vp–p = 2Vpeak = 650 V. The three-step chain rms → peak → peak-to-peak must be completed in full.

Why each distractor is wrong: A stops at the rms voltage · B stops at the peak voltage · C doubles the rms value instead of the peak, skipping the √2 step.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Lucia's portable heater contains a single heating element of constant resistance 23 Ω. It is connected to the Australian mains supply of 230 V rms at 50 Hz.

a. Calculate the rms current in the element. (1 mark)

b. Calculate the average power dissipated in the element. (1 mark)

c. Magnus multiplies the peak voltage by the peak current and obtains 4.6 kW. He claims this is the average power dissipated in the element. Explain why Magnus is incorrect, and state how his value is related to the average power. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Irms = Vrms/R = 230 ÷ 23 = 10 A (1 mark).

1 mark: 10 A.

b. P = VrmsIrms = 230 × 10 = 2300 W = 2.3 kW (1 mark).

1 mark: 2.3 kW (accept 2300 W).

c. Average power must be calculated from rms values, because the rms quantities are defined as the constant DC values that develop the same average power in a resistive component; the peak values occur only for an instant in each cycle (1 mark). Magnus has found the maximum instantaneous power, VpeakIpeak = 2VrmsIrms — exactly twice the average power of 2.3 kW (1 mark).

1 mark: average power requires rms values; peak values give the instantaneous maximum · 1 mark: his value is twice the average power.

Do not accept: "he used the wrong numbers" without identifying peak versus rms; the factor stated as √2 rather than 2 (each of V and I carries a √2).

KK 3.7 analyse transformer action with reference to electromagnetic induction for an ideal transformer: N1N2 = V1V2 = I2I1

What you must be able to do

  • Apply N1N2 = V1V2 = I2I1 on formula sheet, noting that the current ratio is printed inverted: voltage follows the turns, current runs the other way.
  • Explain transformer action as electromagnetic induction: alternating current in the primary → changing magnetic flux in the iron core → changing flux through the secondary → induced emf by ε = −NΔΦB/Δt.
  • State the ideal assumptions and use the consequence V1I1 = V2I2, and explain why a constant DC input produces zero output.

Subscript 1 is the primary — the winding on the supply side — and should be identified before any substitution; a transformer is named step-up or step-down by what it does to the voltage. N1 pairs with V1, but the current ratio inverts because an ideal transformer transfers all of the power: V1I1 = V2I2, so stepping the voltage up steps the current down by the same factor. The frequency of the AC passes through unchanged. "Ideal" packages three assumptions: all of the flux in the core links both windings, the windings have no resistance, and no energy is lost in the core — real transformers reach 98–99%, and exam stems settle the matter with "Assume the transformer is ideal."

The ratios work with rms, peak or peak-to-peak values, provided one convention is used throughout — there is no reason to convert, and inserting a √2 mid-calculation was the most common error on the 2024 transformer question. The AC requirement is mechanism, not vocabulary: a constant DC current in the primary makes a constant flux in the core, a constant flux through the secondary induces no emf, and the output is zero. The only exception is the instant of switching, when the flux briefly changes and a momentary pulse appears in the secondary.

Trap Bolting rms↔peak conversions onto a transformer calculation. The turns ratio relates voltages and currents of the same type; if the question supplies rms values, the answer emerges in rms with no √2 anywhere. Convert only if the question separately asks for a peak value.
Assessor's advice 2024 report, on why transformers require AC: answering "because they need an alternating current in the primary coil" is "a tautology since the alternating current has been identified in the stem" — 40% scored zero. The marks sit in the mechanism: changing flux in the core, induced emf in the secondary, and the constant-DC contrast stated with its reason.
Connects The ideal-transformer ratio is a statement of energy conservation: V1I1 = V2I2 holds because the volt is a joule per coulomb and the ampere a coulomb per second — the same relation that underlies W = qV for the electron gun in AOS 2.
In the style of VCAA · MC

An ideal transformer has 1000 turns on its primary winding and 50 turns on its secondary winding. The primary is connected to the 230 V rms mains, and the secondary delivers an rms current of 4.0 A to a load. Which one of the following is closest to the rms current in the primary winding? (1 mark)

  1. 0.20 A
  2. 0.28 A
  3. 4.0 A
  4. 80 A
Worked solution

A. The current ratio is the inverse of the turns ratio: I2/I1 = N1/N2, so I1 = I2 × N2/N1 = 4.0 × 50 ÷ 1000 = 0.20 A. Check with power: 230 × 0.20 = 46 W in, 11.5 × 4.0 = 46 W out.

Why each distractor is wrong: B inserts a spurious √2 conversion (0.20 × √2) — the given values are already consistent · C assumes the current passes through unchanged, as if the transformer only altered voltage · D applies the turns ratio the wrong way (4.0 × 1000/50), stepping the current up alongside a stepped-down voltage, which would multiply the power by 400.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Kenji builds a bench power supply for his electronics projects. It contains an ideal transformer that steps the 230 V rms mains down to 11.5 V rms; the primary winding has 600 turns.

a. Calculate the number of turns on the secondary winding. (1 mark)

b. The supply delivers an rms current of 2.0 A to a test circuit. Calculate the rms current drawn from the mains by the primary winding. (2 marks)

c. Explain, with reference to electromagnetic induction, why the transformer operates from an AC supply but produces no output when connected to a constant DC supply of the same voltage. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. N2 = N1 × V2/V1 = 600 × 11.5 ÷ 230 = 30 turns (1 mark).

1 mark: 30 turns.

b. I1 = I2 × V2/V1 (1 mark: ratio the right way up) = 2.0 × 11.5 ÷ 230 = 0.10 A (1 mark). Power check: 230 × 0.10 = 23 W = 11.5 × 2.0.

1 mark: current ratio inverted relative to the voltage ratio · 1 mark: 0.10 A.

Do not accept: 40 A (ratio inverted the wrong way); 0.14 A (a √2 conversion inserted — both given values are rms, so the answer is rms with no conversion).

c. The alternating current in the primary winding produces a continually changing magnetic flux in the iron core (1 mark). This changing flux passes through the secondary winding, so by ε = −NΔΦB/Δt a changing flux through its turns induces an emf in the secondary (1 mark). A constant DC current produces a constant flux in the core; a constant flux through the secondary induces no emf, so the output is zero (1 mark).

1 mark: AC in primary → changing flux in the core · 1 mark: changing flux through the secondary induces an emf (Faraday's law) · 1 mark: constant DC → constant flux → no induced emf, zero output.

Accept: a note that a momentary pulse appears at the instant of switching the DC on or off, since the flux changes briefly. Do not accept: "transformers need AC because they run on alternating current" (tautology); "DC does not work" with no reference to flux.

KK 3.8 analyse the supply of power by considering transmission losses across transmission lines

What you must be able to do

  • Model a supply system as a series circuit — generator, line resistance, load — and use Vdrop = IlineRline on formula sheet and Ploss = I²lineRline on formula sheet.
  • Keep three voltages distinct: the voltage at the supply end of the lines, the drop across the lines, and the voltage at the load end — connected by Vdrop = supply-end voltage − load-end voltage.
  • Argue the high-voltage case quantitatively: the same power at n times the voltage means one nth of the current and 1/n² of the line loss.

Because the system is a series circuit, the current in the lines equals the current delivered to the load, and that single current determines the remaining quantities: the drop along the lines is IlineRline and the heating of the lines is I²lineRline. There are two legitimate routes to Iline: divide the transmitted power by the voltage at the same point in the circuit, or divide the voltage drop across the lines by Rline. Dividing the full supply voltage by Rline is neither — it pretends the entire supply voltage is dropped across the lines, which would only be true if the load were a short circuit.

The line is not the load. Using P = V²/R not on sheet with the supply voltage across Rline is the classic zero-scorer: the lines drop only Vdrop, a small fraction of the supply voltage. Both printed loss formulas depend on Iline, so the line current is found first in every case. The step-down transformer at the consumer end exists to return the voltage to the level the load is designed for; it raises the consumer-side current above the line current, so "stepping down makes it safer by reducing the current" contradicts the circuit.

The high-voltage argument is a fixed chain: the power to be delivered is fixed, so transmitting at a higher voltage means a proportionally smaller line current (P = VI), and the loss I²lineRline falls with the square of that current — ten times the voltage, one hundredth of the loss. When a question says "support your answer with a calculation", the comparison must be numerical: compute the loss at both voltages and put the two numbers side by side.

Key idea Write the three voltages down before any algebra: supply end, drop across the lines, load end. Every transmission mark flows from Iline, and Iline comes from the drop — or from power over the voltage at the same point — never from the supply voltage over Rline.
Trap Two zero-scorers recur: treating the supply voltage as if it were dropped across the lines (I = Vsupply/Rline), and treating the line as the load (P = V²supply/Rline). Both produce currents or losses that are absurdly large — a sanity check on the size of the answer catches them.
Assessor's advice The 2025 supply-system question averaged 0.3 of 3 marks — more than 80% of the state scored zero. The report states that responses "did not use the voltages at each end of the lines to find the voltage drop", did not find the line current, and "a significant number of students attempted to use the 5.00 kW value from the stem with no justification". The remedy named in the report: practise "more complex, application type problems such as this".
In the style of VCAA · MC

A power station supplies a fixed amount of electrical power to a distant town through transmission lines of fixed resistance. Which one of the following best explains why the power is transmitted at high voltage? (1 mark)

  1. High voltage reduces the total resistance of the long transmission lines, so less power is dissipated in them.
  2. High voltage drives a larger current through the lines, so more power is delivered to the town for the same generation.
  3. For a fixed power, a higher voltage means a smaller line current, and the line loss is proportional to the square of that current.
  4. Increasing the voltage increases the power that the lines carry, since P = VI, so the town receives more power.
Worked solution

C. The power to be delivered is fixed, so P = VI forces the line current down as the transmission voltage goes up; the line loss Ploss = I²lineRline then falls with the square of the current.

Why each distractor is wrong: A — the resistance is set by the conductors (material, length, thickness) and is unchanged by the operating voltage · B — for fixed power a higher voltage means a smaller current, not a larger one · D — the power carried is fixed by what the station generates and the town demands; raising V lowers I, leaving P = VI the same.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Anika's bore pump is supplied from a generator at her farmhouse, 400 m away. The generator maintains 250 V rms at its end of the cable run, and the total resistance of the cable run is 8.0 Ω. A voltmeter at the pump reads 230 V rms. All values are rms.

a. Determine the rms current in the cables. (2 marks)

b. Calculate the power dissipated in the cables. (1 mark)

c. Calculate the power delivered to the pump. (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. The drop across the cables is the difference between the voltages at the two ends of the cable run: Vdrop = 250 − 230 = 20 V (1 mark). Then Iline = Vdrop/Rline = 20 ÷ 8.0 = 2.5 A (1 mark).

1 mark: Vdrop = 20 V from the two end voltages · 1 mark: 2.5 A.

Do not accept: I = 250 ÷ 8.0 = 31 A or 230 ÷ 8.0 = 29 A — the full supply or load voltage is not dropped across the cables. Either substitution earns zero for the question, since every later part depends on the current.

b. Ploss = I²lineRline = 2.5² × 8.0 = 50 W (1 mark).

1 mark: 50 W (consequential on the current from part a).

c. The same current flows through the pump, at the pump's voltage: P = VI = 230 × 2.5 = 575 W ≈ 5.8 × 10² W (1 mark).

1 mark: 5.8 × 10² W (accept 575 W; accept the route P = 250 × 2.5 − 50 = 575 W).

Assessor's advice This is the exact chain on which 85% of the 2025 cohort scored zero: end voltages → drop → line current → load power. Two given voltages in a transmission stem are to be subtracted to obtain the drop.

Mixed practice

The supply-system question — generator, step-up transformer, lossy lines, step-down transformer, town — appears on almost every paper, at up to seven marks, and is the worst-answered calculation in the AOS. Before the algebra, formulate a strategy: label the three voltages and the line current on the diagram, then work left to right. Question 10 is a complete instance; attempt it under exam conditions before reading the solution. In questions worth more than one mark, working must be shown — formula, substitution and answer as separate visible lines — and a wrong early value used correctly in later parts still earns those marks under consequential marking.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

A community hydroelectric station in the Victorian high country supplies a small town. The generator produces 200 kW of electrical power at an rms voltage of 4.0 kV, and the power is carried to the town through transmission lines of total resistance 40 Ω, as shown in Figure 5.2. An ideal step-up transformer T1, with turns ratio N1 : N2 = 1 : 5, connects the generator to the lines. An ideal step-down transformer T2, with turns ratio 80 : 1, connects the lines to the town. All voltages and currents are rms values.

a. Show that the voltage at the input to the transmission lines is 20 kV. (1 mark)

b. Calculate the current in the transmission lines. (2 marks)

c. Calculate the power loss in the transmission lines and the voltage drop across them. (2 marks)

d. Determine the voltage delivered to the town by T2 and the power delivered to the town. (3 marks)

e. A resident asks why the lines are not connected directly to the generator. Explain why the power is transmitted at 20 kV rather than at 4.0 kV, supporting your answer with a calculation of the power that would be lost in the lines at 4.0 kV. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. V2 = V1 × N2/N1 = 4.0 × 10³ × 5 ÷ 1 = 2.0 × 10⁴ V = 20 kV (1 mark: full substitution shown — the value is given, so the mark is entirely in the working).

1 mark: turns ratio applied with substitution reaching 20 kV.

b. T1 is ideal, so all 200 kW passes to the lines (1 mark: power conserved through the transformer). Iline = P/V = 2.00 × 10⁵ ÷ 2.0 × 10⁴ = 10 A (1 mark).

1 mark: 200 kW carried at 20 kV (or I1 = 50 A stepped down by factor 5) · 1 mark: 10 A.

Do not accept: I = 2.0 × 10⁴ ÷ 40 = 500 A (the supply voltage is not dropped across the lines); 50 A (the generator-side current — the lines are on the secondary side of T1).

c. Ploss = I²lineRline = 10² × 40 = 4.0 × 10³ W = 4.0 kW (1 mark). Vdrop = IlineRline = 10 × 40 = 4.0 × 10² V (1 mark).

1 mark: 4.0 kW · 1 mark: 4.0 × 10² V. Consequential on the current from part b.

Do not accept: P = (2.0 × 10⁴)² ÷ 40 = 10 MW — this treats the line as the load, with the full 20 kV across its resistance. The lines drop only 4.0 × 10² V of the 20 kV.

d. The voltage at the input to T2 is the line-input voltage minus the drop: 2.0 × 10⁴ − 4.0 × 10² = 1.96 × 10⁴ V (1 mark). T2 steps this down by 80: Vtown = 1.96 × 10⁴ ÷ 80 = 245 V (1 mark). The power delivered is the generated power minus the line loss: 200 − 4.0 = 196 kW (1 mark).

1 mark: Vdrop subtracted to give 19.6 kV at T2 · 1 mark: 245 V · 1 mark: 196 kW (or via Itown = 80 × 10 = 800 A and 245 × 800 = 196 kW).

Do not accept: 2.0 × 10⁴ ÷ 80 = 250 V (the drop ignored); 200 kW delivered (lines treated as lossless after computing a loss in part c).

e. The power to be transmitted is fixed, so at 4.0 kV the line current would be Iline = 2.00 × 10⁵ ÷ 4.0 × 10³ = 50 A — five times larger (1 mark). The loss would be Ploss = I²lineRline = 50² × 40 = 1.0 × 10⁵ W = 1.0 × 10² kW, half of the generated power, compared with 4.0 kW (2.0%) at 20 kV (1 mark: numerical comparison). Because the loss is proportional to the square of the line current, transmitting at five times the voltage carries the same power with one-fifth the current and one twenty-fifth the loss (1 mark: the I² causal link).

1 mark: 50 A at 4.0 kV · 1 mark: 1.0 × 10² kW against 4.0 kW, stated as a comparison · 1 mark: fixed power → higher voltage → lower current → loss ∝ I²line.

Do not accept: "high voltage reduces the resistance of the lines"; P = V²/R with 4.0 kV or 20 kV across the line resistance; a qualitative answer with no numerical comparison — the question demands a calculation, and the comparison is the answer.

Assessor's advice The examiners' standing complaint on this archetype is that students "are generally able to complete single-step, substitution questions" but collapse on the chain. The circuit is labelled first: three voltages (20 kV, 4.0 × 10² V drop, 19.6 kV) and one line current. Each labelled value corresponds to a mark, awarded in order.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Ishva tests a hand-cranked alternator by connecting it to a 12 Ω resistive load and an oscilloscope. At a steady cranking rate the output is sinusoidal with a peak voltage of 17 V and a period of 25 ms.

a. Calculate the frequency of the output. (1 mark)

b. Calculate the rms voltage across the load. (1 mark)

c. Calculate the average power delivered to the load. (2 marks)

d. Ishva halves the cranking rate. Determine the new average power delivered to the load. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. f = 1/T = 1 ÷ 0.025 = 40 Hz (1 mark, with the period converted to seconds).

1 mark: 40 Hz.

b. Vrms = (1/√2)Vpeak = 17 ÷ √2 = 12 V (1 mark).

1 mark: 12 V.

c. Irms = Vrms/R = 12.0 ÷ 12 = 1.0 A (1 mark). P = VrmsIrms = 12.0 × 1.0 = 12 W (1 mark).

1 mark: rms current 1.0 A · 1 mark: 12 W. Peak values used instead give 24 W — zero for the power mark.

d. Halving the cranking rate halves the rate of change of flux, so the peak voltage halves to 8.5 V; the frequency also halves, to 20 Hz, but average power does not depend on the frequency (1 mark: new amplitude identified). Then Vrms = 8.5 ÷ √2 = 6.0 V and Irms = 6.0 ÷ 12 = 0.50 A (1 mark). P = VrmsIrms = 6.0 × 0.50 = 3.0 W — one-quarter of the original, since both Vrms and Irms halve (1 mark).

Accept: the proportionality route — power scales with the square of the voltage in a fixed resistance, so halving the amplitude quarters the power: 12 ÷ 4 = 3.0 W. Do not accept: 6.0 W (power assumed to scale linearly with amplitude); any solution that halves the power "because the frequency halved".
