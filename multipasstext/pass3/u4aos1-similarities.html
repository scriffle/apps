Similarities between light and matter

Dot points KK 1.13–1.18: the momentum that photons and matter share at the same wavelength, the line spectra that quantised energy states produce, and the single-particle double slit that shows both natures in one experiment. Spectra and energy-level items have appeared on every recent paper, the same-wavelength photon–electron comparison is recycled almost yearly, and KK 1.13 carries the area of study's main discuss-level prose item — most of the marks in this cluster are awarded for mechanism sentences, not algebra.

KK 1.13 discuss the importance of the idea of quantisation in the development of knowledge about light and in explaining the nature of atoms

What you must be able to do

  • State the idea itself: energy is exchanged in discrete amounts — light is absorbed and emitted as photons of energy E = hf on formula sheet, and the electrons of an atom can occupy only certain discrete energy states.
  • Discuss its importance for light by naming a specific photoelectric finding that the wave model could not explain and the photon model could.
  • Discuss its importance for atoms by connecting discrete energy states to discrete line spectra.
  • Sort observations into quantisation evidence and non-evidence — multiple-choice items test exactly this discrimination.

Discuss is an evaluation-level command term: a bare definition earns little. A full-mark three-mark response has three distinct links — the idea (discrete photon energies and discrete atomic states), a specific consequence for light (threshold frequency, maximum kinetic energy set by frequency rather than intensity, or the absence of a time delay), and a specific consequence for atoms (spectral lines occur only at the discrete photon energies matching differences between states). Each link must name actual physics, not assert importance in general terms.

Know which observations carry the discreteness fingerprint. Line spectra — absorption spectra especially — and the photoelectric threshold frequency can only be explained with quantised energy. Diffraction and interference are evidence for wave behaviour, not for quantisation, and a photocurrent that grows with intensity is explicable under both models.

Key idea Quantised means restricted to discrete values: light energy arrives in indivisible packets of hf, and a bound electron's energy can sit only on the rungs of its atom's ladder — never between them.
Assessor's advice On the 2025 multiple-choice item asking which phenomenon can only be explained by quantisation, only 58% chose absorption spectra: "Absorption spectra show evidence of discrete energies for the electrons. Diffraction, interference and photocurrent do not show evidence of discrete behaviour." Nearly a quarter chose the photocurrent option.
In the style of VCAA · MC

Which one of the following observations can be explained only by a model in which energy is quantised? (1 mark)

  1. the spreading of light as it passes through a narrow slit
  2. the discrete dark lines in the absorption spectrum of hydrogen gas
  3. the bright and dark bands produced in Young's double slit experiment
  4. the increase in photocurrent when the intensity of light on a metal surface is increased
Worked solution

B. Dark absorption lines occur only at photon energies that exactly match differences between discrete electron energy states — they are direct evidence that the energy of an atom is quantised. No continuous-energy model produces sharp lines.

Why each distractor is wrong: A — diffraction is wave behaviour, explained without any quantisation · C — interference is likewise pure wave evidence · D — a wave model also predicts more emitted electrons at higher intensity, so a rising photocurrent does not discriminate between the models.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

In the early twentieth century, physicists adopted the idea that energy is quantised. Discuss the importance of the idea of quantisation in the development of knowledge about light and in explaining the nature of atoms. (3 marks)

Worked solution

Quantisation is the idea that energy comes in discrete amounts rather than a continuous range: light is absorbed and emitted as photons, each of energy E = hf, and the electrons of an atom can occupy only certain discrete energy states (1 mark: the idea, stated for light and for atoms).

For light, the photon model explained photoelectric observations that the wave model could not — there is a threshold frequency below which no electrons are emitted regardless of intensity, and the maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons depends on the frequency of the light, not its intensity (1 mark: a specific consequence for knowledge about light).

For atoms, quantised electron energy states explain why atoms emit and absorb light only at certain discrete wavelengths: each spectral line corresponds to a transition between two states, with the photon energy equal to the difference between them (1 mark: a specific consequence for the nature of atoms).

1 mark: idea of quantisation (discrete photon energy E = hf and/or discrete atomic energy states) · 1 mark: specific photoelectric finding explained by the photon model · 1 mark: discrete line spectra explained by transitions between discrete states.

Accept: the absence of a time delay at low intensity as the photoelectric finding; the stability of atoms as the atomic consequence, provided discrete energy states are explicitly named. Do not accept: general claims such as "quantisation changed physics" with no named phenomenon; an answer that treats only light or only atoms — the dot point demands both.

Assessor's advice On the 2024 wave-model question, assessors warned that many responses were "an amalgam of all three findings" lifted from the A3 sheet: "copying material directly from their A3 sheet without consideration of the specifics of the question is unlikely to result in marks being awarded." Pick one finding per link and tie it to the question asked.

KK 1.14 compare the momentum of photons and of matter of the same wavelength including calculations using: p = hλ

What you must be able to do

  • Apply p = h/λ on formula sheet to photons and to matter alike: the same wavelength means the same momentum, whatever the mass.
  • Contrast the energies: a photon of momentum p carries E = pc not on sheet, while a slow electron carries Ek = p²2m not on sheet.
  • Select the correct constant: h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s whenever momentum is involved; 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s only for photon energies in electron-volts.

p = h/λ is the bridge between the two halves of this area of study — the formula sheet prints it twice, as photon momentum p = h/λ and as the de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p, and the two are the same statement read in opposite directions. Equal wavelength therefore means equal momentum, which is why electrons tuned to an X-ray wavelength reproduce the X-ray diffraction rings through the same crystal. That same-pattern, same-wavelength, same-momentum chain has been examined in three consecutive papers.

Equal momentum never means equal energy. A photon converts momentum to energy at the rate E = pc; a slow electron at only Ek = p²/2m = ½pv, and since v << c the electron's energy is always far smaller at the same momentum. The 2024 exam planted exactly this trap: electrons of 600 eV shared their wavelength with X-ray photons of about 2.5 × 10⁴ eV.

Trap Applying photon formulas to electrons. The 2025 assessors reported many students "incorrectly using c when considering the speed of electrons" — an electron is not a photon, so E = hc/λ and E = pc never apply to it. For matter, go through momentum: p = h/λ, then Ek = p²/2m.
Assessor's advice On the 2024 de Broglie calculation, "the most common error … was to use the wrong Planck's constant". The eV s value belongs only with photon energies in eV; momentum calculations take the J s value, because kg m s⁻¹ is an SI unit.
Connects The electron beams of Unit 3 return with a new identity: the accelerating voltage that set the speed in the electron gun of U3 AOS 2 now sets the momentum, and through λ = h/p the wavelength, of a matter wave.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Ishva uses X-rays of wavelength 2.0 × 10⁻¹⁰ m and a beam of electrons to study the same thin crystal. The electron gun is adjusted so that the electrons have a de Broglie wavelength equal to the wavelength of the X-rays. Ignore relativistic effects.

a. Calculate the momentum of one X-ray photon. (2 marks)

b. State the momentum of one electron in the beam. Justify your answer. (2 marks)

c. Determine which has the greater energy — one X-ray photon or one electron in the beam — and by approximately what factor. Give both energies in electron-volts. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. p = h/λ = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ 2.0 × 10⁻¹⁰ (1 mark: formula and substitution with the J s value of h) = 3.3 × 10⁻²⁴ kg m s⁻¹ (1 mark: answer).

1 mark: p = h/λ with correct substitution · 1 mark: 3.3 × 10⁻²⁴ kg m s⁻¹.

b. The electron's momentum is also 3.3 × 10⁻²⁴ kg m s⁻¹ (1 mark: value). Momentum depends only on wavelength through p = h/λ, and the wavelengths are equal, so the momenta are equal — the electron's mass does not enter (1 mark: justification).

1 mark: same momentum, 3.3 × 10⁻²⁴ kg m s⁻¹ · 1 mark: because p = h/λ depends only on λ, which is the same.

c. Photon: E = hcλ = (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ 2.0 × 10⁻¹⁰ = 6.2 × 10³ eV (1 mark: photon energy). Electron: Ek = p²2m = (3.315 × 10⁻²⁴)² ÷ (2 × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹) = 6.03 × 10⁻¹⁸ J = 6.03 × 10⁻¹⁸ ÷ 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ ≈ 38 eV (1 mark: electron kinetic energy via p²/2m with conversion to eV). The photon has the greater energy, by a factor of about 6.2 × 10³ ÷ 38 ≈ 1.6 × 10² (1 mark: explicit comparison and factor).

1 mark: photon energy 6.2 × 10³ eV · 1 mark: electron kinetic energy 38 eV · 1 mark: photon greater by a factor of approximately 1.6 × 10².

Accept: photon energy via E = pc = 9.9 × 10⁻¹⁶ J converted to eV; factors in the range 1.6–1.7 × 10². Do not accept: "they have the same energy because they have the same momentum"; an electron energy computed with E = hc/λ (that is the photon formula); energies left in joules when the question specifies electron-volts.

KK 1.15 explain the production of atomic absorption and emission line spectra, including those from metal vapour lamps

What you must be able to do

  • Explain emission spectra as a two-step mechanism: atoms are excited (by collisions in an electric discharge or in a heated vapour), then electrons fall to lower energy states, each transition emitting one photon whose energy equals the difference between the states.
  • Explain absorption spectra: light with a continuous spectrum passes through a cooler gas; photons whose energy exactly matches a difference between states are absorbed, and the re-emitted light leaves in all directions, so dark lines appear in the transmitted beam.
  • Describe a metal vapour lamp: the discharge vaporises the metal and excites its atoms, so the lamp emits the metal's characteristic line spectrum — sodium's strong yellow line near 589 nm is the standard example.

The emission mechanism has two halves — excitation, then emission — and an Explain answer needs both, plus the reason the lines are discrete: only certain energy states exist, so only certain energy differences, and therefore only certain photon energies, are possible. Stopping at "electrons fall and emit photons" omits the link that makes the spectrum a line spectrum.

The dark-line subtlety is where absorption questions are won. An absorbed photon's energy is re-emitted, at the same wavelength, almost immediately — yet the line is dark. The resolution is directional: the re-emitted photons leave in random directions, so almost none continue along the original beam toward the observer, and that wavelength is depleted in the transmitted light. In a cool gas nearly all atoms sit in the ground state, which is why absorption lines correspond to transitions starting from the ground state.

Trap Writing that the absorbed light is "used up", "destroyed" or "blocked". The energy is re-emitted at the very same wavelength — the line is dark only because the re-emission is spread over all directions. An absorption explanation that omits the re-radiation step is incomplete.
Key idea Emission and absorption lines of an element fall at the same wavelengths because both are fixed by the same set of energy-state differences — one element, one ladder, one fingerprint.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A sodium vapour street lamp glows with a characteristic yellow colour. Inside the lamp, an electric discharge passes through sodium vapour at low pressure.

a. Explain how the discharge produces an emission spectrum that contains only certain wavelengths. (3 marks)

b. Anika shines light with a continuous spectrum through a cool sample of sodium vapour and examines the transmitted light with a spectrometer. Dark lines appear at some of the wavelengths that are bright in the lamp's emission spectrum. Explain why these lines appear dark, given that the absorbed energy is re-emitted by the atoms. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Collisions in the discharge transfer energy to sodium atoms, exciting electrons to higher energy states (1 mark: excitation mechanism). The excited electrons then fall to lower energy states, and each downward transition emits a photon whose energy equals the difference between the two states, ΔE = hf (1 mark: emission with photon energy equal to the state difference). Because the energy states of the sodium atom are discrete, only certain energy differences — and therefore only certain photon wavelengths — are possible, producing a line spectrum (1 mark: discreteness of states explains discreteness of lines).

1 mark: excitation by collisions in the discharge · 1 mark: photons emitted with energy equal to the difference between states · 1 mark: discrete states give only discrete wavelengths.

b. Photons whose energy exactly matches the difference between two sodium energy states are absorbed, promoting electrons to higher states (1 mark: selective absorption). The energy is re-emitted as photons travelling in random directions, so almost none of the re-emitted light continues toward the spectrometer — the intensity at those wavelengths in the transmitted beam is greatly reduced, and the lines appear dark (1 mark: re-radiation in all directions depletes the beam).

1 mark: absorption of photons matching a state difference · 1 mark: re-emission in all directions, so the original direction is depleted.

Do not accept (b): "the light is absorbed" with no account of the re-emitted energy — the stem explicitly grants the re-emission; "the energy is converted to heat".

KK 1.16 interpret spectra and calculate the energy of absorbed or emitted photons: E = hf

What you must be able to do

  • Convert between a spectral line's wavelength or frequency and its photon energy with E = hf = hcλ, in joules and in electron-volts.
  • Match the constant to the unit: 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s gives eV directly; 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s gives joules; convert between J and eV with 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ J per eV.
  • Place a line on the spectrum: visible light spans roughly 400 nm to 750 nm, so visible photons carry between about 1.7 eV and 3.1 eV.

Answer boxes set the unit — recent papers have demanded eV, nm and Hz, and the conversion is where the marks leak. Working in eV from the start with hc = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 3.0 × 10⁸ = 1.24 × 10⁻⁶ eV m avoids a J-to-eV conversion entirely. When a wavelength is required in nanometres, write the answer in metres first, then convert on a separate line: 6.54 × 10⁻⁷ m = 654 nm.

Assessor's advice On the 2025 photon-wavelength calculation, "the most common error was demonstrating a correct substitution but incorrectly converting the answer to nanometres." The physics mark and the conversion mark are separate — claim both.
Connects The electron-volt earned here is the same currency as the photoelectric effect's stopping voltage in KK 1.8: a photon's eV value and an electron's eV value can be compared digit for digit.

  1. 2.1 × 10⁻⁹ eV
  2. 5.1 × 10¹⁴ eV
  3. 2.1 eV

KK 1.17 analyse the emission or absorption of a photon by an atom in terms of a change in the electron energy state of the atom, with the difference in the states’ energies being equal to the photon energy: E = hf = hcλ

What you must be able to do

  • Equate the photon energy to the difference between two states: ΔE = Eupper − Elower = hf — never to the energy of a single level.
  • Read energy-level diagrams in both printed conventions: ground state at 0 eV with energies increasing up to the ionisation energy, or negative level energies with 0 eV at ionisation. The differences are identical either way.
  • Draw transitions as arrows: emission is a downward arrow, absorption an upward arrow, and an arrowhead is compulsory.
  • Find the pair of levels whose difference matches a given photon energy, and decide which transitions give visible photons (about 1.7 eV to 3.1 eV).

Every mark in this dot point hangs on the word difference. An atom dropping from a 12.1 eV state to a 10.2 eV state emits a 1.9 eV photon — not a 12.1 eV photon and not a 10.2 eV one. The same discipline applies in reverse: a 1.9 eV photon can be absorbed only by an atom sitting in the 10.2 eV state, because no other pair of levels is separated by exactly 1.9 eV. The lowest-energy line in a spectrum comes from the smallest gap between occupied levels, which is rarely a transition to the ground state.

The 2025 exam printed its hydrogen diagram with the ground state at 0 eV and the ionisation energy of 13.6 eV at the top; many textbooks instead label the ground state −13.6 eV with ionisation at 0 eV. Check the convention before any arithmetic — and note that with the ground state at 0 eV, a level's printed energy happens to equal its difference from the ground state, which can disguise the level-versus-difference error until a transition between two excited states exposes it.

Trap Quoting the energy of a level as the energy of the photon. Assessors repeatedly flag answers that take the initial level's energy (12.1 eV) instead of the difference between the initial and final states (12.1 − 10.2 = 1.9 eV). Before substituting into ΔE = hf, write the subtraction explicitly.
Assessor's advice On the 2025 transition-drawing question, "Common errors included drawing incorrect arrow direction (indicating absorption) or just a line with no direction." Emission arrows point downward, from the higher state to the lower one — a bare line earns nothing.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Figure 6.1 shows some of the energy levels of a hydrogen atom, with the ground state assigned an energy of 0 eV. Magnus studies a sample of hydrogen gas in which atoms have been excited to the n = 3 state.

a. State the minimum energy required to ionise a hydrogen atom that is in the ground state. (1 mark)

b. One atom returns from the n = 3 state to the ground state via the n = 2 state, emitting two photons. Calculate the energy of each photon. (2 marks)

c. Determine whether either of these photons lies in the visible range, taken as 400 nm to 750 nm. Show your working. (3 marks)

d. Lucia states that the photon emitted in the n = 3 to n = 2 transition has an energy of 12.1 eV, because the electron starts in the 12.1 eV level. Identify the error in Lucia's reasoning and state the correct photon energy. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. 13.6 eV — the difference between the ionisation level and the ground state (1 mark).

1 mark: 13.6 eV.

b. n = 3 → n = 2: ΔE = 12.1 − 10.2 = 1.9 eV (1 mark). n = 2 → n = 1: ΔE = 10.2 − 0 = 10.2 eV (1 mark).

1 mark: 1.9 eV · 1 mark: 10.2 eV.

c. For the 1.9 eV photon: λ = hcE = 1.242 × 10⁻⁶ ÷ 1.9 = 6.54 × 10⁻⁷ m = 654 nm, which lies between 400 nm and 750 nm, so it is visible (1 mark: wavelength; 1 mark: comparison with the range and conclusion). For the 10.2 eV photon: λ = 1.242 × 10⁻⁶ ÷ 10.2 = 1.2 × 10⁻⁷ m = 122 nm, which is below 400 nm — ultraviolet, not visible (1 mark: second wavelength with conclusion).

1 mark: 6.5 × 10⁻⁷ m (650–655 nm) for the 1.9 eV photon · 1 mark: explicit comparison with 400–750 nm and the conclusion that it is visible · 1 mark: 1.2 × 10⁻⁷ m for the 10.2 eV photon, identified as not visible.

Accept: the comparison run in energy instead of wavelength — visible photons span about 1.7 eV to 3.1 eV, so 1.9 eV is visible and 10.2 eV is not — provided the conversion of at least one boundary is shown. Do not accept: wavelengths left as 6.5 × 10⁻⁷ with no comparison against the stated range; "the 1.9 eV photon is red" asserted without working.

d. Lucia has used the energy of a single level instead of the difference between the two states (1 mark: error identified). The photon energy equals the difference between the initial and final states: 12.1 − 10.2 = 1.9 eV (1 mark: correct photon energy).

1 mark: photon energy is the difference between states, not a level energy · 1 mark: 1.9 eV.

KK 1.18 interpret the single photon and the electron double slit experiment as evidence for the dual nature of light and matter

What you must be able to do

  • Describe the experiment: single photons, or single electrons, pass through a double slit one at a time, and each is detected as one small localised dot on the screen.
  • Interpret the build-up: each localised dot is particle-like behaviour; the pattern of bands that the dots accumulate into is wave-like interference; the one experiment therefore shows both natures.
  • State the statistical reading: no single particle produces a pattern — the wave behaviour governs the probability of where each particle arrives, and the pattern emerges only over many arrivals.

The exam-ready argument has three sentences. Each photon or electron arrives whole, at a single point — detection as a localised dot is particle behaviour. The arrival positions are not uniformly spread: over many particles the dots build up bands with exactly the spacing Δx = λLd on formula sheet predicts for a wave of that wavelength — for electrons, the de Broglie wavelength. Because the particles pass one at a time, the pattern cannot come from particles interfering with one another; the wave behaviour belongs to each particle individually and fixes the probability of where it lands.

Trap Citing Young's original double slit experiment as evidence of dual nature. With bright light it demonstrates the wave nature only. The dual-nature evidence is the single-particle version, where the same experiment also records each arrival as a localised dot. Name the one-at-a-time condition explicitly.
Key idea One dot at a time, yet the dots land in interference bands: particle-like arrival, wave-like distribution — both natures in a single experiment, for light and for matter alike.
In the style of VCAA · MC

Electrons are fired one at a time at two narrow slits and are detected on a screen behind the slits. The experiment runs until a very large number of electrons has been detected. Which one of the following best describes the observations? (1 mark)

  1. Each electron is detected as a single dot, and over time the dots build up an interference pattern of many bands.
  2. Each electron is detected as a faint interference pattern spread across the whole screen.
  3. The dots build up two bright bands, one directly behind each slit.
  4. No pattern forms, because a pattern requires many electrons to pass through the slits at the same time.
Worked solution

A. Each electron arrives as one localised dot (particle-like detection), but the accumulated distribution of dots forms interference bands matching the electron's de Broglie wavelength (wave-like behaviour). The pattern emerges statistically, one arrival at a time.

Why each distractor is wrong: B — a single electron never paints a pattern; it is always detected at one point · C — two bands behind the slits is the pure-particle prediction, which is not what is observed · D — the pattern forms even though the electrons pass strictly one at a time, so it cannot depend on electrons interfering with one another.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

In a double slit experiment, the light source is made so faint that only one photon is in the apparatus at any time. Each photon arrives at the detector screen as a single small dot. After many millions of photons have been detected, the dots form the same pattern of bright and dark bands that intense light produces. Explain how this experiment provides evidence for the dual nature of light. (3 marks)

Worked solution

Each photon is detected at a single localised point on the screen, which is particle-like behaviour — the photon arrives whole, at one place (1 mark: particle-like detection).

The positions of the dots are not uniform: over many photons they accumulate into bands of constructive and destructive interference, and interference is wave behaviour (1 mark: the accumulated pattern is wave-like interference).

Because only one photon is in the apparatus at a time, the pattern cannot arise from photons interfering with one another — the wave behaviour determines the probability of each individual photon's arrival position, so light displays both particle-like and wave-like properties in the same experiment (1 mark: one-at-a-time condition linked to the dual-nature conclusion).

1 mark: localised dot = particle-like arrival · 1 mark: accumulated interference bands = wave-like behaviour · 1 mark: pattern forms one photon at a time, so each photon's arrival is governed by wave interference — both natures in one experiment.

Do not accept: "photons interfere with each other" — the stem rules this out; "light is sometimes a wave and sometimes a particle" with no reference to the two observations; an answer describing Young's bright-light experiment, which evidences the wave nature only.

Mixed practice

Spectra and energy-level arithmetic have appeared on every recent paper, and the same-wavelength photon–electron comparison is a standing archetype. The two questions below interleave the cluster's dot points the way the exam does. In questions worth more than one mark, appropriate working must be shown: formula, substitution and answer as separate visible lines. Answer in the unit the question specifies, and remember that later parts are marked independently — restate reasoning rather than relying on an earlier part.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Kenji studies the blue line in the emission spectrum of a mercury vapour street lamp. The wavelength of this line is 436 nm.

a. Calculate the energy, in eV, of one photon of this light. (2 marks)

b. Calculate the momentum of one photon of this light. (2 marks)

c. Calculate the speed of an electron that has the same momentum as one of these photons. Ignore relativistic effects. (2 marks)

d. The kinetic energy of this electron is much smaller than the energy of the photon, even though their momenta are equal. Explain why, with reference to the appropriate energy–momentum relationships. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. E = hcλ = (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ 4.36 × 10⁻⁷ (1 mark: substitution with the eV s value of h and nm converted to m) = 2.8 eV (1 mark: answer).

1 mark: correct substitution with 4.36 × 10⁻⁷ m · 1 mark: 2.8 eV.

b. p = hλ = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ 4.36 × 10⁻⁷ (1 mark: substitution, now with the J s value of h) = 1.5 × 10⁻²⁷ kg m s⁻¹ (1 mark: answer).

1 mark: p = h/λ with h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s · 1 mark: 1.5 × 10⁻²⁷ kg m s⁻¹.

Do not accept (b): a momentum computed with h = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s — momentum in kg m s⁻¹ is an SI quantity and requires the J s value.

c. p = mv, so v = pm = 1.521 × 10⁻²⁷ ÷ 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ (1 mark: substitution with the electron mass) = 1.7 × 10³ m s⁻¹ (1 mark: answer).

1 mark: v = p/m with the unrounded momentum · 1 mark: 1.7 × 10³ m s⁻¹.

d. A photon's energy is related to its momentum by E = pc, while a slow electron's kinetic energy is Ek = p²/2m = ½pv (1 mark: the two energy–momentum relationships). At equal momentum the ratio of photon energy to electron energy is 2c/v, and since the electron's speed of 1.7 × 10³ m s⁻¹ is far smaller than c, the photon's energy is larger by a factor of about 3.6 × 10⁵ (1 mark: applying the relationships — because v << c, the electron's energy is far smaller).

1 mark: E = pc for the photon and Ek = p²/2m (or ½pv) for the electron · 1 mark: same p but v << c, so the electron's energy is much smaller (ratio or equivalent reasoning).

Accept (d): a numerical comparison — photon 2.8 eV against electron Ek ≈ 7.9 × 10⁻⁶ eV — with both formulas named. Do not accept: "the photon moves faster so it has more energy" without the energy–momentum relationships; applying E = pc to the electron.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna models a gas atom using the three lowest energy levels shown in Figure 6.2. The ground state is at 0 eV, the first excited state at 2.1 eV and the second excited state at 3.5 eV. The ionisation energy of the atom is 5.0 eV.

a. White light passes through a cool sample of the gas, in which nearly all atoms are in the ground state. State the energies of the photons that can be absorbed by transitions between the levels shown. (2 marks)

b. In a discharge tube, atoms of the same gas are excited to the second excited state. State all the photon energies that can appear in the emission spectrum as the atoms return to the ground state. (2 marks)

c. Calculate the wavelength of the lowest-energy emission line and determine whether it lies in the visible range of 400 nm to 750 nm. (3 marks)

d. Explain why the absorption spectrum of the cool gas contains fewer lines than the emission spectrum of the excited gas. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. From the ground state, the possible upward transitions are to the 2.1 eV state and to the 3.5 eV state, so photons of 2.1 eV (1 mark) and 3.5 eV (1 mark) can be absorbed. A photon must match a level difference exactly, and 1.4 eV photons are not absorbed because no atoms occupy the 2.1 eV state in the cool gas.

1 mark: 2.1 eV · 1 mark: 3.5 eV (with no extra incorrect energies listed).

b. The downward transitions are 3.5 → 2.1 eV, 3.5 → 0 eV and 2.1 → 0 eV, giving photon energies of 1.4 eV, 3.5 eV and 2.1 eV (2 marks: all three correct; 1 mark: any two correct).
