Application of field concepts

The last seven dot points are where Section B spends most of its fields marks: satellites in circular orbit, the force on a current-carrying conductor, the DC motor and the particle accelerator. Each chevron below is one study-design dot point — the required skills, the subtleties, the assessor’s advice and exam-style practice with mark-annotated solutions.

KK 2.10 apply the concepts of force due to gravity and normal force including in relation to satellites in orbit where the orbits are assumed to be uniform and circular

What you must be able to do

  • Draw and defend the free-body diagram of an orbiting satellite: a single force, the gravitational force exerted on the satellite by the central body, directed toward its centre.
  • Apply Fg = G m1m2r² on formula sheet with r measured centre to centre.
  • Use the normal force to explain apparent weight on the ground — and its absence in orbit.

A satellite in a stable circular orbit experiences a single force. Nothing balances the gravitational force: it is the net force, and it supplies the centripetal acceleration. “Centripetal force” is not an additional force to add to the diagram; it is the role the gravitational force plays. A free-body diagram for an orbiting satellite therefore shows a single arrow, toward the centre, as shown in Figure 3.1.

The normal force is the force a person experiences. For a person standing on the ground, the floor exerts an upward normal force FN on the person, equal in magnitude to Fg; that normal force is the person’s apparent weight. In orbit, the station and the astronaut are both in free fall, accelerating toward Earth together, so the floor never presses on the astronaut’s feet: FN = 0 while Fg remains large. At an altitude of 400 km the field strength is g = GM/r² = 8.7 N kg⁻¹ — about 89% of the surface value.

Key idea Apparent weightlessness in orbit means the normal force is zero. It never means the gravitational force is zero.
Trap “There is no gravity in space.” The field at typical orbital altitudes is most of its surface value, and without it there would be no orbit at all — the satellite would move in a straight line at constant speed. Answers that explain floating astronauts by a vanishing gravitational force score zero.
Assessor’s advice Free-body-diagram multiple-choice items on this dot point plant two recurring wrong diagrams: a forward “thrust” arrow along the velocity, and a separate “centripetal force” arrow alongside the gravitational one. The correct diagram has a single centre-directed force. Assessors also flag a spurious Fnet arrow on force diagrams; it must not be drawn.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A satellite of mass 320 kg moves in a stable circular orbit around Earth, as shown in Figure 3.1. Which one of the following best describes the forces acting on the satellite? (1 mark)

  1. The gravitational force exerted on the satellite by Earth is balanced by an equal outward force, so the net force on the satellite is zero.
  2. The only force acting on the satellite is the gravitational force exerted on it by Earth, and this force is unbalanced.
  3. The gravitational force is negligible at the satellite’s altitude, so the satellite continues in its orbit with no force acting on it.
  4. The gravitational force exerted on the satellite by Earth acts together with a forward force that maintains the satellite’s motion along the orbit.
Worked solution

B. A satellite in a stable circular orbit has exactly one force acting on it: the gravitational force exerted on it by Earth, directed toward Earth’s centre. This force is unbalanced — it is the net force — and it produces the satellite’s centripetal acceleration. The satellite’s mass is irrelevant to the count of forces.

Why each distractor is wrong: A — if the forces were balanced the net force would be zero and the satellite would move in a straight line at constant speed (Newton’s first law); nothing supplies an outward force. C — the field at typical orbital altitudes is most of its surface value (8.7 N kg⁻¹ at 400 km), and with no force the path could not curve. D — no forward force is needed to maintain speed; uniform circular motion requires only the centre-directed force.

KK 2.11 model satellite motion (artificial, Moon, planet) as uniform circular orbital motion: a = v²/r = 4π²r/T²

What you must be able to do

  • Equate the gravitational force to the centripetal requirement and rearrange for any one of v, T, r or M.
  • Convert altitude to orbital radius as a ritual first line: r = RE + h, with the altitude converted to metres.
  • Explain the qualitative facts: constant speed but not constant velocity; acceleration toward the centre by Newton’s second law; orbit independent of satellite mass; zero work done by the gravitational force over the orbit.
G Mmr² = mv²r = 4π²rmT² not on sheet assembled from Fg = Gm1m2/r², Fnet = mv²/r and v = 2πr/T, which are all on the formula sheet · M central mass (kg) · m satellite mass (kg) — cancels from every term · r orbital radius (m), centre to centre · T period (s)

Every satellite calculation in this area of study starts from this chain. Cancelling m and rearranging gives v = √(GM/r) and T = √(4π²r³/GM): for a given central mass, choosing the radius fixes the speed and the period. The satellite’s own mass appears nowhere — a 320 kg satellite and a 3.2 × 10⁵ kg station at the same radius orbit identically. Lower orbits are faster: as r decreases, v = √(GM/r) increases.

“Show that” items give the period so that later parts survive an early error. The marks are entirely for the working: formula, complete substitution with every value shown, evaluation to one more significant figure than the given answer. Writing the given answer with incomplete algebra earns nothing, and working backwards from the answer earns nothing.

Why is the satellite accelerating? By Newton’s second law: the gravitational force exerted on the satellite by Earth is unbalanced, so the satellite accelerates in the direction of that force, toward Earth’s centre, with a = F/m. The acceleration continuously changes the direction of the velocity while the speed remains constant. Newton’s first law describes what happens with no net force, and Newton’s third law describes the partner force acting on Earth — neither explains the satellite’s acceleration.

Over one complete circular orbit the gravitational force does no work, because the force is perpendicular to the velocity at every instant — there is never a component of force along the motion. The reasoning is assessed as well as the result: “zero, because the net force is zero” is incorrect, since the net force is the gravitational force itself. As a check, an orbit at Earth’s surface would take approximately 84 min, so any computed period shorter than this places the satellite inside the planet.

Trap Altitude is not orbital radius. The radius in every formula is measured from the centre of the central body: r = RE + h = 6.37 × 10⁶ m plus the altitude in metres. Exams almost always quote the altitude, usually in km.
Assessor’s advice On the 2025 satellite question, “around one-third” of students “went on to use the altitude in km, rather than convert it to m and add it to the Earth’s radius” — and the same error repeated in the next part. On the explanation part, “the most common error was referring to either Newton’s first or third laws.” For “show that” items: “a complete substitution … must be provided for marks to be awarded.”

a. Orbital radius first: r = RE + h = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 8.20 × 10⁵ = 7.19 × 10⁶ m. (1 mark: altitude converted to metres and added to Earth’s radius)

b. v = 2πr/T = (2π × 7.19 × 10⁶) ÷ (6.07 × 10³) (1 mark: formula and substitution using the orbital radius)

v = 7.4 × 10³ m s⁻¹. (1 mark: answer with unit; consequential on the radius from part a)

c. By Newton’s second law, the gravitational force exerted on the satellite by Earth is unbalanced, so the satellite accelerates toward Earth’s centre with a = F/m. (1 mark) This acceleration continuously changes the direction of the satellite’s velocity while leaving its speed unchanged — acceleration is a change in velocity, and velocity includes direction. (1 mark)

Accept for b: v = √(GM/r) = 7.4 × 10³ m s⁻¹. Do not accept for a: working that begins from the given 6.1 × 10³ s, or substitution using r = 8.20 × 10⁵ m or r = 7.19 × 10³ km. Do not accept for c: Newton’s first or third law as the operative principle, or “the gravitational force is balanced by an outward force” (0 marks). In the style of VCAA · MC

A satellite in a uniform circular orbit completes one full revolution around Earth. Which one of the following best describes the work done on the satellite by the gravitational force during one complete orbit? (1 mark)

  1. Zero, because the gravitational force is perpendicular to the satellite’s velocity at every point on the orbit.
  2. Zero, because the net force acting on the satellite is zero.
  3. Greater than zero, because the gravitational force acts on the satellite at every point along the orbital path.
  4. Less than zero, because the gravitational force opposes the satellite’s motion along its path.
Worked solution

A. Work requires a component of force along the displacement. In a circular orbit the gravitational force points toward the centre while the velocity is tangent to the circle, so the angle between them is 90° at every instant: no work is done, and the kinetic energy and speed are constant.

Why each distractor is wrong: B — the right value for the wrong reason, which is exactly what this item examines; the net force is not zero, it is the gravitational force itself, supplying the centripetal acceleration. C — a force acting along a path does work only through its component parallel to the motion, which is zero here. D — the gravitational force is never antiparallel to the velocity in a circular orbit; it is perpendicular throughout.

KK 2.12 describe the interaction of two fields, allowing that electric charges, magnetic poles and current carrying conductors can either attract or repel, whereas masses only attract each other

What you must be able to do

  • State which interactions can go both ways — electric charges, magnetic poles and current-carrying conductors can attract or repel — and which cannot: masses only attract.
  • Reason about the combined field of two sources: where the two fields reinforce and where they oppose.
  • Predict the force between parallel currents: same direction attract, opposite directions repel.

The asymmetry comes from the sources. Electric charge has two signs and magnetic poles come in north–south pairs, so two electric or magnetic fields can meet either head-on or head-to-tail. Mass has one sign only — there is no negative mass — so the gravitational field of every object points inward and two masses can only ever pull each other together. The full comparison of the three fields is KK 2.2; this dot point asks what happens when two fields share a region.

Superposition does the work. Midway between two identical like poles (or two equal like charges), the two fields have equal magnitudes and opposite directions, so the combined field is close to zero; between unlike poles the fields reinforce and the gap field is large and nearly uniform. Two parallel conductors interact through their fields: each wire sits in the field of the other, and applying the right-hand grip rule (for the field of one wire) then the right-hand slap rule (for the force on the other) shows that currents in the same direction attract while opposite currents repel. That rule is not on the formula sheet; it is reconstructed from the two steps each time.

Trap At the midpoint between two facing like poles, students who add field strengths answer “double”. Fields add as vectors: equal magnitudes in opposite directions sum to approximately zero.
In the style of VCAA · MC

Two identical bar magnets are placed in line with their north poles facing each other, separated by a small gap. Point P is at the midpoint of the gap. Which one of the following best describes the magnetic field at P? (1 mark)

  1. It is twice the strength of the field that one magnet alone would produce at P, because the two fields combine.
  2. It is directed at right angles to the line joining the two magnets, because the opposing fields deflect each other.
  3. It is equal in strength to the field of one magnet alone, because each magnet’s field stops at the gap.
  4. It is close to zero, because the two fields at P have equal magnitudes and opposite directions.
Worked solution

D. Each north pole produces a field directed away from itself along the line of the magnets, so at the midpoint the two fields point toward each other with equal magnitudes. Their vector sum is approximately zero.

Why each distractor is wrong: A — adds magnitudes and ignores direction; fields are vectors. B — by symmetry there is nothing to pick a perpendicular direction on the axis midway between identical magnets; the two axial fields cancel. C — fields do not stop at a boundary; both magnets’ fields exist throughout the gap and superpose.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna claims that any two objects that exert non-contact forces on each other can be made to either attract or repel, depending on how the objects are arranged. Evaluate Shanna’s claim with reference to gravitational, electric and magnetic interactions. No calculations are required. (3 marks)

Worked solution

Shanna’s claim is not correct in general. (1 mark: verdict)

It holds for electric and magnetic interactions: like charges and like poles repel while unlike charges and unlike poles attract, and reversing the direction of one of two parallel currents changes attraction between the conductors into repulsion. (1 mark: charges, poles or conductors can attract or repel, with at least one arrangement described)

It fails for gravitation: mass has only one sign, every gravitational field points toward its source, and so two masses attract each other in every arrangement — gravitational repulsion is not possible. (1 mark: masses only attract, with the reason)

1 mark: claim evaluated (false in general) · 1 mark: attract-or-repel cases identified for charges, poles or conductors · 1 mark: masses only attract, justified. Do not accept a bare “the claim is wrong” without support — a verdict without reasoning is treated as a guess.

KK 2.13 investigate and analyse theoretically and practically the force on a current carrying conductor due to an external magnetic field, F = nIlB, where the directions of I and B are either perpendicular or parallel to each other

What you must be able to do

  • Calculate F = nIlB when the current is perpendicular to the field, converting mT to T and cm to m as deliberate working lines.
  • State that the force is zero when the current is parallel (or antiparallel) to the field — and justify it by the angle, every time.
  • Give the force direction with the right-hand slap rule, stated as an explicit physical direction.
F = nIlB on formula sheet F force exerted on the conductor (N) · n number of current-carrying conductors or turns · I current (A) · l length of conductor in the field (m) · B magnetic field strength (T) · valid when I is perpendicular to B; F = 0 when I is parallel to B

n counts conductors: a coil of n loops presents n parallel wires to the field, and a ribbon of n wires carrying the same current does the same. The two unit conversions — mT to T and cm to m — are required; a missed conversion corresponds exactly to a multiple-choice distractor. Direction comes from the right-hand slap rule (thumb along the current, fingers along the field, palm facing the direction of the force) and must be reported physically: “vertically upward”, “toward the wire”, “out of the page”.

The parallel case is the most frequently lost mark. The justification that earns the mark names the angle: the current in that conductor is parallel to the magnetic field, so the field exerts no force on it. Restating the orientation, or appealing to currents “flowing in opposite directions”, earns nothing.

Assessor’s advice The zero-force parallel case was examined in both 2024 and 2025 and failed both times (averages 0.9/2 and 0.7/2, with 61% scoring zero in 2025). The reports single out the standard wrong reason — “because the current flows in opposite directions in those wires” — and responses that “referred to motors, generators or commutators” instead of the angle between I and B.

KK 2.14 investigate and analyse theoretically and practically the operation of simple DC motors consisting of one coil, containing a number of loops of wire, which is free to rotate about an axis in a uniform magnetic field and including the use of a split ring commutator

What you must be able to do

  • Narrate one full revolution: maximum torque with the coil plane parallel to the field, zero torque a quarter-turn later, commutation, then torque restored in the same rotational sense.
  • Distinguish the zero-force condition (a side whose current is parallel to B) from the zero-torque condition (forces acting along the coil plane when the plane is perpendicular to B).
  • Explain the function of the split-ring commutator — and what a pair of slip rings would do instead.

Figure 3.2 shows the standard examinable diagram: coil JKLM, with sides JK and LM parallel to the axle and sides KL and MJ joining them. JK and LM are always perpendicular to the field, so each experiences a constant force of magnitude nIlB — one up, one down — whatever the coil’s orientation. Sides KL and MJ carry current parallel to the field when the coil plane is parallel to B, so in the position shown the force on them is zero.

The rotation narrative has three positions. Position 1, plane parallel to B (as shown): the up–down force pair acts across the full width of the coil and the torque is maximum. Position 2, a quarter-turn later, plane perpendicular to B: the forces are unchanged in magnitude but now act along the plane of the coil, directly toward and away from the axle, so the torque is zero — and at the same instant the commutator gaps pass under the brushes, interrupting the current; a rotating coil is carried through this zero-torque position by its own motion. Position 3, immediately past the zero-torque position: each brush has crossed to the other half-ring, the coil current is reversed, the forces on JK and LM reverse directions, and the torque acts in the same rotational sense as before.

That reversal is the split-ring commutator’s entire function: it reverses the current through the coil every half revolution, as the coil passes through the zero-torque position, so the torque always turns the coil the same way. Slip rings provide the contrast case: each end of the coil retains its own continuous ring, the current never reverses, and beyond the perpendicular position the torque acts in the opposite sense, so the coil oscillates about the plane-perpendicular position instead of rotating.

Key idea Zero force and zero torque are different conditions. A side parallel to B feels zero force (plane parallel to B); the whole coil feels zero torque when the plane is perpendicular to B, even though the forces on JK and LM are at full strength.
Trap A motor is not a generator. Motor questions are answered with forces on current-carrying conductors (F = nIlB) — never with induction, flux or EMF. Assessors report students “seemingly confusing motors and generators” on exactly this dot point.
Assessor’s advice “Questions regarding the role of slip rings and split ring commutators appear on almost every exam.” And justifications must not be tautologies: “When the loop is horizontal, because if the loop is horizontal, FG is parallel to the magnetic field” restates the orientation twice — name the mechanism (current parallel to B ⇒ no force; forces through the axle ⇒ no torque) instead.

Magnus builds a simple DC motor identical to the one shown in Figure 3.2. He then replaces the split-ring commutator with a pair of slip rings, leaving everything else unchanged, and switches the motor on from the position shown. Which one of the following best describes the subsequent motion of the coil? (1 mark)

  1. The coil rotates continuously in one direction, because slip rings also reverse the coil current every half revolution.
  2. The coil does not move at all, because slip rings cannot carry current into a rotating coil.

KK 2.15 investigate, qualitatively, the effect of current, external magnetic field and the number of loops of wire on the torque of a simple motor

What you must be able to do

  • State, qualitatively, that increasing the current, the external field strength or the number of loops increases the torque — and trace each effect through F = nIlB.
  • Read the constraints in the stem: “the same coil and the same battery” silently rules out more turns and a higher voltage.
  • Keep force and torque separate: the force on a force-carrying side has constant magnitude at every orientation, while the torque varies with orientation.

All three levers act through the force on the coil sides. A larger current (a higher supply voltage, or a lower coil resistance) increases F = nIlB directly; stronger magnets increase B; each additional loop adds one more conductor on which the field exerts a force. Conversely, adding resistance lowers the current and therefore the torque, which is frequently examined in negative-stem items. No torque calculation is ever required: the dot point is explicitly qualitative.

Reading the constraints is where marks are commonly lost. A part asking for a torque increase “using exactly the same single coil and the same battery” excludes the two most common answers (more turns, more voltage); the acceptable answer is to increase the field strength with stronger magnets. State the change and the mechanism: stronger field ⇒ larger force on each side ⇒ larger torque.

Trap Asked to sketch the force on one coil side over a full revolution, most students draw a sine curve. The magnitude nIlB does not depend on orientation: the graph is a square wave — constant magnitude, sign reversing at each commutation. It is the torque, not the force, that rises and falls.

Lucia rewinds the coil of her simple DC motor so that it has twice as many loops of the same wire, keeping the same battery and the same magnets. The maximum torque is unchanged. (1 mark: claim)

KK 2.16 model the acceleration of particles in a particle accelerator (including synchrotrons) as uniform circular motion (limited to linear acceleration by a uniform electric field and direction change by a uniform magnetic field)

What you must be able to do

  • Assign each field its single job: a uniform electric field increases the particle’s speed in straight sections; a uniform magnetic field changes its direction in the bends.
  • Justify why the magnetic field does no work: the force is perpendicular to the velocity at every instant, so speed and kinetic energy are unchanged.
  • Explain the synchrotron’s defining feature: the bending field strength is increased in step with the particles’ momentum so that the orbit radius stays constant.

The VCE accelerator model has exactly two ingredients. In the straight sections a charge q crossing a potential difference V gains kinetic energy W = qV, written on the formula sheet in its from-rest form ½mv² = qV on formula sheet. In the bends, a particle moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field follows a circular arc of radius

r = mvqB on formula sheet r radius of the circular path (m) · m mass (kg) · v speed (m s⁻¹) · q charge (C) · B magnetic field strength (T) · valid for v ≪ c

The bending is uniform circular motion because the magnetic force has constant magnitude qvB and acts perpendicular to the velocity at every instant. Perpendicular force means no work is done, so the speed never changes in a bend — only the direction does. Electric fields change speed; magnetic fields change direction. Exam stems flag the model’s limit with “Ignore relativistic effects”: r = mv/qB as used here is valid for v ≪ c, and Unit 4 examines what happens when accelerated particles approach the speed of light.

The synchrotron — Australia’s is in Clayton — adds one examinable mechanism. As the particles’ momentum mv grows, r = mv/qB would force the radius to grow with it, and the beam would leave the ring. The machine therefore increases the bending field strength B in step with the momentum, holding r constant so the beam stays inside the one ring of magnets. The synchronisation of B with the particles’ momentum is what the name means.

Key idea E accelerates, B steers. The electric field does work and changes speed; the magnetic field does no work and changes only direction — and in a synchrotron B grows with the particles’ momentum to hold the radius constant.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

At the Australian Synchrotron, electrons are accelerated in a linear accelerator and a booster ring before being stored in a large storage ring, where bending magnets steer them around the circuit.

a. State the role of the uniform electric fields in the linear accelerator. (1 mark)

b. Explain why the force exerted on an electron by a bending magnet changes the electron’s direction but not its speed. (2 marks)

c. In the booster ring the electrons’ momentum increases while they continue to travel on the same circular path. Explain why the strength of the bending field must be increased during this process. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The uniform electric fields accelerate the electrons in a straight line, increasing their speed and kinetic energy (W = qV for each stage). (1 mark)

b. The magnetic force exerted on the electron is perpendicular to its velocity at every instant. (1 mark) A force perpendicular to the motion does no work on the electron, so its kinetic energy — and therefore its speed — is unchanged; only the direction of the velocity changes. (1 mark)

c. The radius of the circular path is r = mv/qB. (1 mark) As the momentum mv increases, B must increase in the same proportion to keep r constant; with B fixed, the radius would grow and the electrons would no longer follow the ring. (1 mark)

Do not accept for b: “the magnetic field does not affect speed” asserted without the perpendicular-force/no-work chain. Do not accept for c: answers that increase B “to make the electrons go faster” — the magnetic field never changes the speed.

  3. increasing the resistance of the single-turn coil
  4. replacing the single-turn coil with a multiple-turn coil
Worked solution

C. Increasing the resistance of the single-turn coil would *decrease* the current (I = V/R) and so *decrease* the torque (τ = BIA). This is the modification that would not increase the torque — it reduces it.
