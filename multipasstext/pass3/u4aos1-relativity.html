Every relativity formula is on the formula sheet, so the marks are awarded for identifying whose clock and whose ruler is proper, for clean γ algebra, and for explanations that name a frame of reference and stay in it. KK 1.19 describe the limitation of classical mechanics when considering motion approaching the speed of light

What you must be able to do

  • Describe what classical (Newtonian) mechanics predicts at high speed — unlimited speeds under a constant force, kinetic energy ½mv² — and state that experiment contradicts both as v approaches c.
  • State when classical mechanics is an acceptable approximation (v << c, where γ ≈ 1) and when relativistic equations are required.
  • Reason qualitatively near c: added energy raises γ and the energy of a particle by a large factor while changing its speed only slightly.

Classical mechanics places no ceiling on speed: a constant force should produce constant acceleration, and a particle pushed for long enough should pass c. Particle accelerators contradict this directly — each additional joule produces a smaller and smaller increase in speed, and no particle with mass has ever been measured at or beyond c. What grows without limit is not the speed but the Lorentz factor γ, and with it the particle's energy.

The boundary is quantitative. At v = 0.100c, γ = 1.005 and classical mechanics is in error by under one per cent; at 0.995c, γ = 10.0 and the classical kinetic energy is wrong by a factor of about 18. Exam stems flag the classical regime with the instruction "Ignore relativistic effects" — take it at face value: use the classical formulas when it appears, and expect relativistic working when it does not.

Key idea Near c, energy and speed decouple: increasing a particle's energy by a factor of 10 raises γ by a factor of 10 but changes the speed only in the fourth or fifth decimal place of v/c.
Connects The condition v << c attached to r = mv/qB in Unit 3 KK 2.7 is this dot point planted early: synchrotron designers cannot ignore γ, so students were quarantined from it there.
In the style of VCAA · MC

In the booster ring of a synchrotron, electrons are accelerated so that their Lorentz factor increases from γ = 50 to γ = 500. Which one of the following best describes the changes in the speed and the total energy of the electrons? (1 mark)

  1. The speed and the total energy both increase by a factor of about 10.
  2. The speed increases only slightly and the total energy increases by a factor of about 10.
  3. The speed increases by a factor of about 10 and the total energy increases only slightly.
  4. The speed and the total energy both increase only slightly.
Worked solution

B. Total energy is directly proportional to the Lorentz factor (Etot = γmc²), so it increases by a factor of 10. The speed barely changes: at γ = 50, v = 0.99980c; at γ = 500, v = 0.999998c.

Why each distractor is wrong: A treats energy and speed as proportional — the classic misconception, and the strongest distractor on the matching 2024 question (31% chose it) · C inverts the relationship; speed cannot increase tenfold from 0.99980c without passing c · D assumes that because the speed saturates, the energy must too — energy has no ceiling.

KK 1.20 describe Einstein's two postulates for his special theory of relativity that: the laws of physics are the same in all inertial (non-accelerated) frames of reference; the speed of light has a constant value for all observers regardless of their motion or the motion of the source

What you must be able to do

  • State both postulates exactly, including the word inertial in the first and the full clause "for all observers regardless of their motion or the motion of the source" in the second.
  • Define an inertial frame of reference as a non-accelerated frame — one moving at constant velocity — and decide whether a given frame qualifies.
  • Recognise that a postulate is a starting assumption, not a derived result: Einstein assumed both statements and worked out their consequences, which experiments then tested.

Postulate 1: the laws of physics are the same in all inertial (non-accelerated) frames of reference. An inertial frame moves at constant velocity — constant speed and constant direction. A train cornering at constant speed is accelerating (its velocity direction changes), so it is not an inertial frame; a train braking, or one pulling away from a station, also fails. No experiment performed entirely inside an inertial frame can reveal whether that frame is "moving" — there is no preferred frame at rest.

Postulate 2: the speed of light has a constant value for all observers regardless of their motion or the motion of the source. Every clause is load-bearing. An observer racing toward a light beam, or away from it, measures the same speed c; a source moving toward the observer does not add its own speed to the light it emits. This single assumption is what forces measured times and lengths to differ between frames.

Trap Truncating either postulate. "The laws of physics are the same in all frames" (dropping inertial) is wrong — accelerated frames are excluded. "The speed of light is constant" (dropping the observers clause) was explicitly marked inadequate on the 2025 exam: light travels more slowly in glass, so the bare statement is not even true. Learn both postulates word for word.
Assessor's advice 2025 report: many responses "stated that Einstein's postulate is simply that 'the speed of light is constant'. In formulating his special theory of relativity, the postulate is that 'the speed of light is constant for all observers regardless of their relative motion'." The same report's multiple-choice data: only 60% recognised that travelling around a bend at constant speed is not inertial motion.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

State Einstein's two postulates of the special theory of relativity. (2 marks)

Worked solution

Postulate 1: the laws of physics are the same in all inertial (non-accelerated) frames of reference (1 mark).

Postulate 2: the speed of light has a constant value for all observers regardless of their motion or the motion of the source (1 mark).

1 mark: first postulate including "inertial" or "non-accelerated" · 1 mark: second postulate including "for all observers regardless of their motion or the motion of the source" (or equivalent wording covering both the observer's motion and the source's motion).

Accept: "non-accelerating frames" for inertial; "the same for all observers regardless of their relative motion" for the second clause. Do not accept: "the laws of physics are the same in all frames" with inertial omitted; "the speed of light is constant" or "the speed of light is the same everywhere" with the observers clause omitted; "nothing can travel faster than light" (a consequence, not a postulate).

KK 1.21 interpret the null result of the Michelson-Morley experiment as evidence in support of Einstein's special theory of relativity

What you must be able to do

  • Describe what the experiment compared: the travel time of light along two perpendicular paths of equal length, as shown in Figure 7.1.
  • State what was expected, what was observed (the null result), and what the result means for the ether and for the second postulate.
  • Use the word supports — the null result is evidence in support of special relativity, never proof of it.

Nineteenth-century physics held that light waves travel through a medium, the ether, filling all space. Earth's orbital motion through this ether should create an "ether wind", making light slower along the wind than across it. Michelson and Morley split a single beam at a half-silvered mirror, sent the two halves along perpendicular arms of equal length, and recombined them: any difference in travel time would shift the interference pattern as the apparatus was rotated.

No shift was ever observed — not at any orientation, time of day or season. This is the null result: the measured speed of light was the same along both perpendicular paths, independent of Earth's motion. The result gives no evidence for an ether, and it is exactly what Einstein's second postulate requires — the speed of light is the same for all observers regardless of their motion. The null result therefore supports special relativity; a single experiment cannot prove a theory.

Trap Writing that the experiment "proves" special relativity, or that it was performed to test Einstein's theory. It predates the theory by 18 years: it was designed to measure Earth's motion through the ether, failed to detect any, and was later interpreted as supporting the second postulate. The required verb chain is expected difference → null result → no ether / c invariant → supports the second postulate.
Assessor's advice 2025 report, where only 28% earned full marks: many students "did not identify that it was between two perpendicular paths, which was a key part of the experimental design". A rehearsed four-sentence answer — purpose, design, null result, link to the postulate — covers every recent variant of this question.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Describe what the Michelson–Morley experiment was designed to detect, and explain how its result provides support for Einstein's special theory of relativity. (3 marks)

Worked solution

The experiment compared the travel times of light along two perpendicular paths of equal length, expecting Earth's motion through the proposed ether to make light slower along one path than the other (1 mark: design and expectation, with the perpendicular paths named). No difference in the speed of light along the two paths was detected, regardless of the orientation of the apparatus or the time of year — the null result (1 mark). This gives no evidence for an ether and shows that the measured speed of light does not depend on Earth's motion, which supports Einstein's second postulate: the speed of light has a constant value for all observers regardless of their motion or the motion of the source (1 mark: link to the full postulate).

1 mark: two perpendicular paths and the expected time difference due to motion through the ether · 1 mark: null result — no difference at any orientation · 1 mark: conclusion linked to the constancy of c for all observers (second postulate).

Accept: answers phrased in terms of frames of reference, provided they are correct. Do not accept: "the experiment proved special relativity"; "the speed of light is constant" as the stated postulate, with the observers clause omitted; descriptions that never mention the two perpendicular paths.

KK 1.22 compare Einstein's special theory of relativity with the principles of classical physics

What you must be able to do

  • Compare means similarities and differences: state what the two frameworks share as well as where they part.
  • Contrast the classical and relativistic treatment of time, length, velocity addition, kinetic energy and the maximum possible speed.
  • State that special relativity contains classical mechanics as its low-speed limit: as v/c → 0, γ → 1 and every relativistic formula collapses to its classical counterpart.

The shared ground is the first postulate. Classical physics already held (since Galileo) that the laws of mechanics are the same in all inertial frames — a ball dropped on a smoothly moving ship falls exactly as it does on shore. Einstein kept this principle and extended it to all the laws of physics, including the behaviour of light.

The differences flow from the second postulate. Classical physics treats time intervals and lengths as absolute — the same for every observer — and adds velocities linearly, so light from a torch on a spacecraft moving at 0.60c should travel at 1.60c. Special relativity makes measured time intervals and lengths depend on the observer's frame, caps every speed at c, and replaces Ek = ½mv² with Ek = (γ − 1)mc². At everyday speeds the two frameworks agree to many decimal places, which is why classical mechanics survived unchallenged for two centuries.

Key idea Special relativity does not overthrow classical mechanics; it contains it. Every relativistic formula reduces to the classical one when v << c, because γ ≈ 1 there.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A spacecraft moves directly toward a space station at 0.60c and shines a beam of light forward, toward the station. Which one of the following best describes the speed of the beam as measured by an observer on the station, according to classical physics and according to special relativity? (1 mark)

  1. classical physics: c · special relativity: c
  2. classical physics: 1.60c · special relativity: 1.60c
  3. classical physics: 1.60c · special relativity: c
  4. classical physics: c · special relativity: 1.60c
Worked solution

C. Classical physics adds velocities linearly: 0.60c + c = 1.60c. Special relativity's second postulate fixes the measured speed of light at c for all observers, regardless of the motion of the source.

Why each distractor is wrong: A denies the classical prediction — linear velocity addition is precisely the classical principle being compared · B applies velocity addition to light within relativity, contradicting the second postulate · D swaps the two frameworks.

KK 1.23 describe proper time (t0) as the time interval between two events in a reference frame where the two events occur at the same point in space

What you must be able to do

  • State the definition exactly: proper time is the time interval between two events measured in a reference frame where the two events occur at the same point in space.
  • Identify, in any scenario, which observer measures t0 — before touching the formula.
  • Use the sanity check: proper time is the shortest time any frame measures; every other frame measures the dilated time t = γt0 ≥ t0.

The operational test is a single clock: if one clock can be present at both events, the frame carrying that clock measures proper time. A muon's creation and decay happen at the same point in the muon's frame, so the rest lifetime 2.20 μs is proper time. Two ticks of a spacecraft's on-board clock happen at the same point in the spacecraft, so the pilot measures t0; two ticks of a GPS satellite's atomic clock belong to the satellite. In each case every other observer needs two separated, synchronised clocks to time the pair of events — and records a longer interval.

Proper time belongs to the events, not to whichever observer feels stationary. In the muon problem the "stationary" physicist on the ground measures the dilated lifetime, not the proper one. Begin every relativity response by naming the two events and asking in whose frame they occur at the same point.

Trap Assigning t0 to the observer "at rest" by default. There is no absolute rest. The ground observer owns the proper time only when both events happen at one point on the ground — for a moving particle's lifetime they do not. Assessors report this inversion as the single most common relativity error.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A GPS satellite travels at 3.9 × 10³ m s⁻¹ relative to the ground; the Lorentz factor for this speed is γ = 1 + 8.5 × 10⁻¹¹. The satellite's atomic clock ticks twice, 1.000 s apart as measured on the satellite. Consider only the effect of the satellite's orbital speed and treat both frames as inertial. Which one of the following best describes the time between the two ticks as measured by an observer on the ground? (1 mark)

  1. slightly longer than 1.000 s, because the ground observer measures a dilated time interval
  2. slightly shorter than 1.000 s, because moving clocks are measured to run fast
  3. exactly 1.000 s, because the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames
  4. exactly 1.000 s, because relativistic effects occur only at speeds approaching c
Worked solution

A. The two ticks occur at the same point in the satellite's frame, so the satellite clock measures the proper time t0 = 1.000 s. The ground observer measures the dilated interval t = γt0 = (1 + 8.5 × 10⁻¹¹) × 1.000 s — longer by about 85 ps.

Why each distractor is wrong: B inverts the effect; moving clocks are measured to run slow, never fast · C misapplies the first postulate — the laws are the same in both frames, but measured intervals between frames still differ · D treats relativity as switching on at some threshold speed; the effect exists at every speed and is merely small here, which is exactly why GPS must correct for it.

KK 1.24 describe proper length (L0) as the length that is measured in the frame of reference in which objects are at rest

What you must be able to do

  • State the definition exactly: proper length is the length measured in the frame of reference in which the object is at rest.
  • Identify the owner of L0 in each standard scenario: the laboratory owns a beamline's proper length, a pilot owns the spacecraft's, and Earth owns the atmosphere-to-ground distance in the muon problem.
  • Apply the two sanity rules: proper length is the longest measurement (L = L0/γ ≤ L0), and contraction occurs only along the direction of relative motion.

A length is a distance between two points at rest in some frame; that frame measures L0. An accelerator's straight section is bolted to the laboratory, so the laboratory measures its proper length and the particles flying through measure it contracted. A spacecraft is at rest relative to its pilot, so the pilot measures its proper length and the space-station observer measures it as contracted. The 6.00 km from the upper atmosphere to a mountain detector is at rest relative to Earth, so Earth's frame owns that L0 — and the muon measures it contracted.

Here is the inversion to guard against: in a single problem, the proper time and the proper length usually belong to different frames. The muon owns the proper lifetime (its creation and decay are co-located in its frame) while Earth owns the proper distance (the atmosphere and the ground are at rest in Earth's frame). Pairing both proper quantities with one observer breaks the physics. Finally, only the dimension parallel to the relative motion contracts: a circular window on a passing rocket is measured as an ellipse — narrowed along the motion, unchanged across it.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Magnus pilots a spacecraft that travels in a straight line at 0.800c relative to a space station. Ishva is on the station. A beacon fixed to the station flashes twice, with both flashes occurring at the same point on the station.

a. Identify the observer who measures the proper time interval between the two flashes. (1 mark)

b. Identify the observer who measures the proper length of the spacecraft. (1 mark)

c. State whether the length of the spacecraft as measured by Ishva is greater than, less than or equal to its proper length. Justify your answer. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Ishva. The two flashes occur at the same point in space in the station's frame, so the station's frame measures the proper time between them (1 mark).

1 mark: Ishva (Magnus earns nothing — proper time belongs to the frame in which the two events are co-located, not to the "moving" or "stationary" observer by default).

b. Magnus. The spacecraft is at rest in his frame, and proper length is measured in the frame in which the object is at rest (1 mark).

1 mark: Magnus.

c. Less than its proper length (1 mark: claim). In Ishva's frame the spacecraft moves at 0.800c, and a moving object is measured as contracted along its direction of motion: L = L0/γ with γ = 1.67 > 1, so she measures 0.600L0 (1 mark: reasoning via L = L0/γ).

1 mark: less than L0 · 1 mark: justified by contraction of moving objects, L = L0/γ with γ > 1 (the numerical value 0.600L0 is not required).

Do not accept (c): "the spacecraft shrinks" with no frame stated — the measurement is frame-dependent, the spacecraft is unchanged in its own frame; greater than L0 (dividing and multiplying by γ confused).

↑ Pass 1 · The speed limit rewrites space and time ● Pass 2 · Proper time and proper length
KK 1.25 model mathematically time dilation and length contraction at speeds approaching c using the equations: t = γt0 and L = L0/γ where γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²)

What you must be able to do

  • Compute γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) on formula sheet cleanly, then apply t = γt0 on formula sheet and L = L0/γ on formula sheet.
  • Invert γ to find the speed: v = c√(1 − 1/γ²) not on sheet — the rearrangement must be performed, not recalled.
  • Answer in the unit the question specifies — relativity answer boxes are routinely printed in multiples of c.

Compute γ in four written steps: square the speed ratio (v²/c²), subtract from 1, take the square root, then take the reciprocal. The classic slip is mangling the square root — taking the reciprocal before the root, or skipping the root entirely — which shows up downstream as γ < 1 or as the square root of a negative number. When v is given as a multiple of c, the c values cancel inside the root: at 0.800c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.640) = 1/√0.360 = 1.67.

Three self-checks catch nearly every error. First, γ ≥ 1 always — a value below 1 means the fraction inside the root was inverted. Second, a dilated time is always longer than the proper time, and a contracted length always shorter than the proper length; an answer on the wrong side means γ was applied upside down. Third, units pass straight through: microseconds in, microseconds out; kilometres in, kilometres out.

Keep γ at three to four significant figures and round only the final answer — never quote γ to one significant figure, where the entire effect disappears.

Trap γ < 1. The 2025 assessors: "Some responses calculated gamma incorrectly as 0.990. Students should be aware that gamma can never be less than 1.0." The same slip later produces √(negative) errors when inverting for v. Treat any γ below 1 as an instruction to recompute, not to continue.
Assessor's advice On the 2025 speed-from-γ question (51% full marks), students "were generally able to calculate gamma correctly but did not make further progress". Practise the inversion as written algebra: from γ, form 1/γ², subtract from 1, square-root, multiply by c.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna travels in a spacecraft moving at constant velocity relative to Earth. A light on her control panel flashes regularly. Shanna measures the time between flashes as 6.00 s. Jo, observing from Earth, measures the time between the same pair of flashes as 10.0 s. Determine the speed of Shanna's spacecraft relative to Earth. Give your answer as a multiple of c. (3 marks)

Worked solution

The flashes occur at the same point in the spacecraft's frame, so Shanna measures the proper time: t0 = 6.00 s and t = 10.0 s. From t = γt0, γ = 10.0 ÷ 6.00 = 1.667 (1 mark: proper time identified and γ calculated).

Rearranging γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²): v²/c² = 1 − 1/γ² = 1 − 1/1.667² = 1 − 0.360 = 0.640 (1 mark: correct inversion).

v = √0.640 × c = 0.80c (1 mark: answer as a multiple of c).

1 mark: γ = 1.67 with t0 = 6.00 s · 1 mark: v²/c² = 1 − 1/γ² evaluated · 1 mark: 0.80c (accept 2.4 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ only if also expressed as a multiple of c, as the question specifies).

Do not accept: γ = 0.600 (ratio inverted — gamma can never be less than 1) and any speed derived from it; t0 = 10.0 s (proper time assigned to the Earth observer).

KK 1.26 explain and analyse examples of special relativity including that: muons can reach Earth even though their half-lives would suggest that they should decay in the upper atmosphere; particle accelerator lengths must be designed to take the effects of special relativity into account; time signals from GPS satellites must be corrected for the effects of special relativity due to their orbital velocity

What you must be able to do

  • Explain each of the three named examples from one explicitly named frame of reference, staying in that frame for the whole argument.
  • For muons: argue either that the lifetime is dilated (Earth frame) or that the distance is contracted (muon frame) — both reach the same conclusion.
  • For accelerators: identify the beamline's proper length as the laboratory's, and contract it in the particle frame.
  • For GPS: state both that satellite clocks are measured from the ground to run at a different rate because of their orbital velocity, and that the broadcast time signals must therefore be corrected.

Muons. Created several kilometres up by cosmic rays and travelling near c, muons live about 2.2 μs at rest — classically far too short to reach the ground. In the Earth frame, the muon's lifetime is dilated by γ, giving it time to arrive. In the muon's frame, the atmosphere's thickness is contracted by γ, so the trip is short enough to survive. One frame, one mechanism, argued to an explicit numerical comparison: that is the full-mark structure (worked completely in Question 11 below).

Stems may quote a "half-life" or a "mean lifetime"; either way the quoted value is measured in the muon's rest frame and plays the role of t0.

Particle accelerators. A beamline is at rest in the laboratory, so the laboratory measures its proper length L0. In the frame of a particle with Lorentz factor γ, that length is contracted to L0/γ — to electrons at γ = 1.0 × 10⁴, a 3.2 km linear accelerator is contracted to 0.32 m. Designers must account for this: the machine's length, and the timing of the accelerating fields the particles meet along it, are engineered for the contracted geometry the beam actually experiences.

GPS. A receiver fixes its position from the arrival times of satellite signals travelling at c, so timing errors convert directly to position errors: each nanosecond of clock error displaces the computed position by about 0.30 m. Because of its orbital velocity, a satellite's atomic clock is measured from the ground to run slow (its ticks are proper time; ground observers measure the dilated interval), and the offset accumulates continuously. The time signals are therefore corrected for special relativity before being used. Gravitational effects on the clocks also exist, but they belong to general relativity and are outside this study design; exam stems confine the question to orbital speed.

Assessor's advice From the 2024 muon question (33% full marks on the explanation): "Students frequently stated that '… the muon experiences time dilation …', which is not the case" — the muon's lifetime is dilated as measured in the physicist's frame. Students who "jumped between using a time dilation argument and a length contraction argument … generally led to a contradiction or greater confusion." Pick one frame, name it, stay in it.
Connects The synchrotron of Unit 3's accelerator work runs its electrons at γ in the thousands — the machine that needed v << c quarantined there is the standard context for relativistic design here.
In the style of VCAA · MC

Protons travel through a straight accelerator section that is 150 m long as measured in the laboratory. The Lorentz factor of the protons is γ = 2.50. Which one of the following is closest to the length of the section as measured in the frame of reference of the protons? (1 mark)

  1. 150 m
  2. 375 m
  3. 24.0 m
  4. 60.0 m
Worked solution

D. The section is at rest in the laboratory, so the laboratory measures the proper length L0 = 150 m. In the protons' frame the section moves, so it is contracted: L = L0/γ = 150 ÷ 2.50 = 60.0 m.

Why each distractor is wrong: A assumes length is absolute — the classical principle this dot point replaces · B multiplies by γ, treating the protons' measurement as the proper length · C divides by γ², applying the contraction twice.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

A GPS receiver determines its position from the arrival times of radio signals broadcast by satellites, using the speed of light. Each satellite carries an atomic clock and orbits Earth at about 3.9 × 10³ m s⁻¹. Explain why the time signals from GPS satellites must be corrected for the effects of special relativity. Consider only the effect of the satellites' orbital velocity. (3 marks)

Worked solution

Two ticks of a satellite's clock occur at the same point in the satellite's frame, so the satellite clock measures proper time; because of the satellite's orbital velocity, an observer on the ground measures the dilated interval t = γt0, so the satellite clock is measured from the ground to run slow (1 mark: time dilation applied with the frames identified). Although γ − 1 is tiny, the discrepancy accumulates with every second of operation, so the satellite's broadcast time drifts steadily away from ground time (1 mark: accumulation). The receiver converts signal travel times to distances by multiplying by c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹, so even microsecond timing errors become position errors of hundreds of metres; the time signals must therefore be corrected for special relativity for GPS positions to remain accurate (1 mark: link from timing error to position error via c).

1 mark: satellite clock runs slow as measured from the ground, with proper time correctly assigned to the satellite · 1 mark: the offset accumulates continuously · 1 mark: position is computed from signal travel time × c, so uncorrected drift produces large position errors.

Do not accept: "the satellite experiences time dilation" with no measuring frame stated; answers attributing the correction to the satellite's altitude or gravity alone (a general-relativity effect, outside this study design and excluded by the stem); "clocks in space are less accurate".

KK 1.27 interpret Einstein's prediction by showing that the total 'mass-energy' of an object is given by: Etot = Ek + E0 = γmc² where E0 = mc², and where kinetic energy can be calculated by: Ek = (γ − 1)mc²

What you must be able to do

  • Use the three sheet relations together: rest energy E0 = mc² on formula sheet, total energy Etot = Ek + E0 = γmc² on formula sheet and kinetic energy Ek = (γ − 1)mc² on formula sheet.
  • Select the correct energy: rest, kinetic or total — the three differ by exactly one term, and the wrong choice is always a planted distractor.
  • Decide when ½mv² is acceptable (v < 0.10c, error under 1%) and when the relativistic form is required.
  • Explain why no object with mass can reach c: as v → c, γ → ∞, so the kinetic energy required grows without limit.

Mass itself carries energy. An object at rest has rest energy E0 = mc² — for an electron, 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × (3.00 × 10⁸)² = 8.20 × 10⁻¹⁴ J, or 0.512 MeV. Set it moving and its total energy is γmc²; the kinetic energy is whatever exceeds the rest energy, Ek = (γ − 1)mc². The bookkeeping Etot = Ek + E0 is itself examinable: given any two of the three energies, the third follows by addition or subtraction before any γ appears.

The classical formula is the low-speed limit, and the error grows fast. At 0.10c the relativistic kinetic energy exceeds ½mv² by under 1%; at 0.968c (γ = 4.00) the classical value is more than six times too small. Near c almost all added energy raises γ rather than speed — which closes the loop with KK 1.19: the classical formula fails because it assumes added energy always produces a proportional gain in speed.

Trap Answering with the wrong member of the energy family. γmc² when kinetic energy is asked, (γ − 1)mc² when total is asked, and ½mv² at relativistic speed all appear as multiple-choice distractors — the sample exam's relativistic-energy item planted the classical value in the stem as a decoy. Read which energy is requested, then write its formula before substituting.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Electrons in a linear accelerator reach a Lorentz factor of γ = 4.00.

a. Calculate the kinetic energy of one of these electrons. (2 marks)

b. Show that the speed of these electrons is approximately 0.97c. (2 marks)

c. Magnus calculates the kinetic energy of one of these electrons using Ek = ½mv² with v = 0.968c and obtains 3.8 × 10⁻¹⁴ J. Explain why Magnus's value is incorrect, with reference to the speed of the electrons. (2 marks)

Worked solution

KK 1.28 apply the energy-mass relationship to mass conversion in the Sun, to positron-electron annihilation and to nuclear transformations in particle accelerators (details of the particular nuclear processes are not required)

What you must be able to do

  • Convert between a mass change and an energy change with E0 = mc² in all three named contexts.
  • For the Sun: calculate the mass converted per second from the radiated power, and the reverse.
  • For positron–electron annihilation: use the masses of both particles, halve the total for each photon's energy, and explain the opposite photon directions by conservation of momentum.
  • Respect the stated boundary: details of the particular nuclear processes are not required — no reaction equations or nuclide bookkeeping will be asked, only the energy–mass conversion itself.

The Sun. Fusion in the core converts a small fraction of the reacting mass to energy, which the Sun radiates at 3.8 × 10²⁶ J s⁻¹. The mass converted each second follows directly: m = E/c² = 3.8 × 10²⁶ ÷ (3.00 × 10⁸)² = 4.2 × 10⁹ kg. Four million tonnes of mass leave as light every second — and quoting "energy released" as a positive quantity is the convention.

Annihilation. A positron has the same mass as an electron, 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg, and opposite charge. When the pair annihilates at negligible speed, the entire mass of both particles converts: E = 2mec² = 1.64 × 10⁻¹³ J, shared equally between two photons of 8.20 × 10⁻¹⁴ J (0.512 MeV) each. The photons travel in opposite directions because momentum is conserved: the initial total momentum is approximately zero, so the two photon momenta must cancel. This is the operating principle of PET scanners, which locate annihilations along the line joining two simultaneous gamma detections.

Accelerators. Run the conversion in reverse: the kinetic energy of collisions in a particle accelerator can become the rest energy of new, heavier particles — nuclear transformations in which mass is created from energy. A question supplies the masses or energies; the only physics required is E0 = mc² applied to the difference.

Trap Two errors dominate annihilation marking. Using the mass of a single electron — the most common error on the 2024 PET question, where 60% scored zero — halves every energy. And explaining the opposite photon directions by the particles' opposite charges: charge is irrelevant; the answer is conservation of momentum. Only 15% earned that mark in 2024.
Assessor's advice 2024 report on the annihilation directions: "Most students stated that it was because the electron and positron are oppositely charged. Some referred to conservation of energy." Neither earns the mark — write "to conserve momentum, since the initial momentum is approximately zero".
Key idea Sun-style mass conversion has not yet appeared on an exam under this study design while annihilation has — treat the Sun calculation as statistically due, and the arithmetic is one division by c².

Worked solution

a. t = dv = 6.00 × 10³ ÷ (0.995 × 3.00 × 10⁸) = 6.00 × 10³ ÷ 2.985 × 10⁸ = 2.01 × 10⁻⁵ s ≈ 20.1 μs (1 mark: complete substitution shown — the answer is given, so a bare restatement earns nothing).

1 mark: t = d/v with full substitution reaching 20.1 μs.

b. No — a muon is very unlikely to reach the detector (1 mark: claim). The classical travel time of 20.1 μs is about nine mean lifetimes (20.1 ÷ 2.20 = 9.14), so almost every muon would decay long before arriving (1 mark: quantitative comparison with the lifetime).

1 mark: claim that the muon is unlikely to arrive · 1 mark: comparison of 20.1 μs with 2.20 μs (about nine lifetimes).

Do not accept (b): a claim with no comparison to the mean lifetime — the justification is the mark.

c. γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) = 1/√(1 − 0.995²) (1 mark: formula and substitution) = 1/√(1 − 0.990025) = 1/√0.009975 = 1/0.09987 = 10.01 ≈ 10.0 (1 mark: evaluation).

Earth frame: as measured in the physicists' frame of reference, the muons' mean lifetime is dilated from 2.20 μs to 22.0 μs (1 mark: dilated quantity with its frame named). The journey requires 20.1 μs in this frame, which is less than one dilated mean lifetime, so a substantial fraction of muons survives the trip (1 mark for the explicit comparison; see markrow).

VCAA 2024 · Section A · Q14

A rocket passes a space station at a horizontal speed of 0.85c parallel to the side of the space station. There is a circular window on the space station, as shown in the diagram below. A person inside the passing rocket observes and measures the window of the space station.

Which one of the following statements best describes the measurements taken of the window of the space station?

  1. Both the horizontal and vertical measurements will be less than the actual window.
  2. Both the horizontal and vertical measurements will be the same as the actual window.
  3. The horizontal measurement will be the same as the actual window but the vertical measurement will be less than the actual window.
  4. The horizontal measurement will be less than the actual window but the vertical measurement will be the same as the actual window.
Worked solution

D. Length contraction only happens along the direction of relative motion. The rocket moves horizontally (parallel to the side of the station), so the person in the rocket measures the window's horizontal dimension as shorter than its actual size, while the vertical dimension (perpendicular to the motion) is unchanged. Statement D.

Why the other options are wrong: lengths perpendicular to the motion are not contracted, so 'both less' (A) and 'vertical less' (C) are wrong; and there *is* contraction along the motion, ruling out 'both the same' (B).

VCAA 2024 · Section A · Q15

At the Australian Synchrotron, electrons are accelerated to close to the speed of light, c, by the linear accelerator.

Consider electrons that enter the Australian Synchrotron booster ring with a Lorentz factor γ = 200, and as a result of further acceleration in that ring reach a much higher value, with a Lorentz factor γ = 2000.

Which one of the following best describes the effect of the booster ring on each electron's speed and energy?

  1. The electron's speed increases by a large amount and its energy increases by a large amount.
  2. The electron's speed increases by a small amount and its energy increases by a large amount.
  3. The electron's speed increases by a large amount and its energy increases by a small amount.
  4. The electron's speed increases by a small amount and its energy increases by a small amount.
Worked solution

B. At γ = 200 the electron is already travelling at about 0.9999875c — extremely close to c. Raising γ to 2000 brings the speed only a tiny bit closer to c (a small change in speed), but the total energy E = γmc² rises ten-fold (a large change in energy). Statement B.

Why the other options are wrong: near the speed of light, large energy increases produce only tiny speed increases — so the speed change is small (ruling out A, C) and the energy change is large (ruling out C, D).

These muons are unstable and have been observed to rapidly decay. They decay with a mean lifetime of 2.20 μs.

As a result of their short half-life, physicists have used the muons produced in Earth's upper atmosphere to test Einstein's special theory of relativity.

The muons produced in the upper atmosphere, at a height of 9700 m above Earth's surface, travel downwards at a speed of 0.985c.

This is shown in Figure 17.

a. Using classical mechanics, show that a muon would reach the muon detector at the top of the mountain in 10.8 μs. (1 mark)

b. According to classical mechanics, is a muon likely to reach the muon detector at the top of the mountain? Justify your answer. (2 marks)

c. Use the following information to answer parts c, d and e.

Experiments conducted by physicists show that a significant number of muons, created at a height of 9700 m above Earth's surface and travelling at a speed of 0.985c, consistently arrive at the muon detector at the top of the mountain.

Calculate the Lorentz factor (γ) for a muon travelling at 0.985c. (2 marks)

d. What is the mean half-life of the muon in the physicists' frame of reference? (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. The muons travel 9700 − 6500 = 3200 m at 0.985c. Classically, t = d/v = 3200 ÷ (0.985 × 3.0×10⁸) = 1.08×10⁻⁵ s = 10.8 µs.

1 mark: distance = 3200 m, t = d/v = 10.8 µs.

b. A travel time of 10.8 µs is about 10.8/2.2 ≈ 4.9 mean lifetimes. The surviving fraction is roughly e⁻⁴·⁹ ≈ 0.7%, so classically almost all the muons would have decayed — they are not likely to reach the detector.

1 mark: 10.8 µs is ~5 mean lifetimes, so the surviving fraction is tiny.

1 mark: concludes (classically) the muons are unlikely to reach the detector.

c. γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) = 1/√(1 − 0.985²) = 1/√0.0298 = 5.8.

1 mark: γ = 1/√(1 − 0.985²) = 5.8.

d. In the physicists' (Earth) frame the muon's lifetime is dilated: t = γ × t₀ = 5.8 × 2.2 = 12.8 µs (about 13 µs).

1 mark: t = γt₀ = 5.8 × 2.2 ≈ 13 µs.

(Equivalently, in the muon's own frame the 3200 m distance is length-contracted to 3200/γ ≈ 550 m, which it can cross within its short proper lifetime.) 1 mark: time dilation lengthens the muon lifetime in the Earth frame (≈13 µs > 10.8 µs).

1 mark: equivalently, length contraction shortens the distance in the muon frame.

1 mark: clear conclusion that relativity explains why many muons survive to the detector.

VCAA 2025 · Section A · Q14

Ignoring the motion of Earth, which one of the following travellers is not in an inertial frame of reference?

A person

  1. seated on a train while the train is stationary at a platform.
  2. seated on a train while the train is travelling at constant velocity.
  3. walking at constant velocity along the corridor of a train that is stationary at a platform.
  4. standing in the corridor of a train that is travelling around a bend at constant speed.
Worked solution

D. An inertial frame is one that is not accelerating. A person standing in a train going around a bend at constant speed is accelerating — the direction of the velocity is changing (centripetal acceleration) — so this is *not* an inertial frame. Statement D.
