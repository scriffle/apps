Effects of fields

Dot points KK 2.6–2.9: electric fields accelerating charges, magnetic fields bending their paths, gravitational fields accelerating masses, and energy read from field graphs. This cluster carries the highest-value Section B archetype — the 6–7 mark accelerated-charge chain — and three of the lowest-scoring questions of recent exams; almost every formula in it is on the formula sheet, so the marks are awarded for selecting, rearranging and justifying, not for recall.

KK 2.6 analyse the use of an electric field to accelerate a charge, including: electric field and electric force concepts: E = kQr² and F = kq1q2r²; potential energy changes in a uniform electric field: W = qV, E = Vd; the magnitude of the force on a charged particle due to a uniform electric field: F = qE

What you must be able to do

  • Calculate the field of a single point charge with E = kQ/r² on formula sheet and the force between two charges with F = kq1q2/r² on formula sheet — and select the correct one of the two.
  • Analyse a charge in a uniform field with E = V/d on formula sheet and F = qE on formula sheet, and the energy it gains with W = qV not on sheet (the sheet carries it only as ½mv² = qV).
  • Add the fields of two point charges as vectors and state the resultant as a magnitude and a compass direction.

The two inverse-square formulas are adjacent on the formula sheet and differ by exactly one symbol: one charge makes a field, two charges make a force. E = kQ/r² takes the source charge only and answers in N C⁻¹; F = kq1q2/r² takes both charges and answers in N. A question that asks for the field at an empty point gives no second charge to substitute — if a calculation needs both charges, the quantity being found is a force.

Direction conventions are marked separately from magnitudes. The field of a positive charge points radially away from it, the field of a negative charge radially toward it, and the force exerted on a negative charge is opposite to the field direction. Where two like charges face each other, their fields oppose between them and cancel at a null point; for unequal charges the null point lies nearer the smaller charge, with the distance ratio equal to the square root of the charge ratio. Resultant-field questions supply a compass rose, and the direction mark requires a compass answer such as "south-west".

In a uniform field between plates, E = V/d sets the field, F = qE sets the constant force, and W = qV sets the energy transferred to a charge crossing the full gap — note that d appears in the field but not in the work. A charge e accelerated through 10 kV gains 1.0 × 10⁴ eV by definition of the electron-volt, with no calculation; in joules the same energy is W = qV = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 1.0 × 10⁴ = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁵ J. Watch the planted prefixes: μC, mm and kV conversions each land exactly on a multiple-choice distractor.

Trap Field–force confusion: using F = kq1q2/r² when the question asks for the electric field. On the 2025 exam's net-field question, assessors reported that many students computed a Coulomb force instead of a field; the average was 1.2 of 3 marks and 45% scored zero. Check the requested quantity and its unit before choosing the formula: field → N C⁻¹, one charge; force → N, two charges.
Trap "Wider plate separation means less kinetic energy gained." Wrong — and this was one of the lowest-scoring questions of the 2025 exam (average 0.6 of 2 marks; 68% scored zero). At fixed voltage, doubling d halves E = V/d and therefore halves F = qE, but the charge travels twice as far, so W = Fd = qV is unchanged. The kinetic energy gained crossing the gap depends only on q and V, never on d. An answer that notes the smaller force or longer transit time but does not conclude "so the kinetic energy is unchanged" earns nothing.
Assessor's advice 2025 report: "It is recommended that students should be familiar with electrical fields as they are with gravitational and magnetic fields." Electric-field questions are consistently the weakest of the three field types — averages of 1.2/2 on a routine E = V/d calculation and 0.6/2 on the plate-separation energy question.
Connects W = qV returns in Unit 4, where the stopping voltage in the photoelectric effect converts a measured voltage into the maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A point charge Q = +8.0 μC is fixed in place. A small charge q = +2.0 μC is placed 0.50 m from Q. Which one of the following is closest to the magnitude of the electric field at the location of q due to Q? (1 mark)

  1. 2.9 × 10⁶ N C⁻¹
  2. 2.9 × 10⁵ N C⁻¹
  3. 1.4 × 10⁵ N C⁻¹
  4. 5.8 × 10⁻¹ N C⁻¹
Worked solution

B. The field is due to the source charge alone: E = kQ/r² = (8.99 × 10⁹ × 8.0 × 10⁻⁶) ÷ (0.50)² = 7.19 × 10⁴ ÷ 0.25 = 2.9 × 10⁵ N C⁻¹. The charge q placed at the point does not appear — it would be needed only to find the force exerted on it.

Why each distractor is wrong: A squares the distance incorrectly (0.50² taken as 0.025) · C divides by r instead of r² · D includes the test charge, computing the force kq1q2/r² = 0.58 N and mislabelling it as a field.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Anika sets up two horizontal parallel plates separated by 40 mm, with a potential difference of 320 V between them. An electron is released from rest at the negative plate. Assume the field between the plates is uniform.

a. Calculate the magnitude of the force exerted on the electron by the electric field. (2 marks)

b. The plate separation is doubled to 80 mm while the potential difference remains 320 V. Kenji predicts that the electron will now arrive at the positive plate with less kinetic energy, because the force exerted on it is smaller. Evaluate Kenji's prediction. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. E = V/d = 320 ÷ 0.040 = 8.0 × 10³ V m⁻¹ (1 mark: field strength, with d converted to metres). F = qE = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 8.0 × 10³ = 1.3 × 10⁻¹⁵ N (1 mark: force).

1 mark: E = V/d evaluated correctly · 1 mark: F = qE evaluated with the electron charge.

b. Kenji is wrong: the kinetic energy gained is unchanged (1 mark: claim). The energy transferred to the electron crossing the full gap is W = qV, which depends only on the charge and the potential difference; doubling d halves the field and the force, but the electron travels twice the distance, so the work Fd — and therefore the final kinetic energy — is the same (1 mark: reasoning via W = qV independence of d).

1 mark: states the kinetic energy is unchanged · 1 mark: justifies via W = qV (or halved force over doubled distance giving equal work).

Accept: reasoning through W = Fd = (qV/2d)(2d) = qV. Do not accept: "the force is smaller" or "the electron takes longer" without the explicit conclusion that the final kinetic energy is unchanged; agreement with Kenji on any reasoning.

Assessor's advice On the 2025 version of part b, many students correctly identified the smaller force, smaller acceleration or longer transit time, "however, they did not explain that these effects would not change the final kinetic energy" — and scored zero. The reasoning must return to the final kinetic energy.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Two charges, each of +1.50 μC, are fixed at points A and B. Point A is 3.00 cm due north of point P, and point B is 3.00 cm due east of point P, as shown in Figure 2.1.

a. Show that the magnitude of the electric field at P due to the charge at A alone is 1.50 × 10⁷ N C⁻¹. (1 mark)

b. Determine the magnitude and direction of the resultant electric field at P due to both charges. State the direction using the compass directions shown in Figure 2.1. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. E = kQ/r² = (8.99 × 10⁹ × 1.50 × 10⁻⁶) ÷ (0.0300)² = 1.349 × 10⁴ ÷ 9.00 × 10⁻⁴ = 1.50 × 10⁷ N C⁻¹ (1 mark: complete substitution shown — a "show that" answer with no visible substitution earns nothing).

1 mark: formula with full substitution, cm converted to m, reaching the given value.

b. Each field at P points away from its positive source charge: the field due to A points due south, and the field due to B points due west (1 mark: both component directions). By symmetry both magnitudes are 1.50 × 10⁷ N C⁻¹ and they are perpendicular, so the resultant is E = √((1.50 × 10⁷)² + (1.50 × 10⁷)²) = √2 × 1.50 × 10⁷ = 2.12 × 10⁷ N C⁻¹ (1 mark: vector addition and magnitude). The resultant points south-west (1 mark: compass direction).

1 mark: fields directed away from each charge (south and west) · 1 mark: Pythagorean magnitude 2.1–2.12 × 10⁷ N C⁻¹ · 1 mark: direction stated as south-west.

Do not accept: a force calculated with F = kq1q2/r² (no second charge sits at P); adding the two magnitudes arithmetically to 3.00 × 10⁷ N C⁻¹; a bare arrow with no compass label when the question demands a compass direction.

Assessor's advice The matching 2025 question averaged 1.2 of 3 marks, with 45% scoring zero — "many students tried to use" the Coulomb force formula. The direction is a separate, precise-communication mark: the compass bearing the figure supplies is to be stated.

KK 2.7 analyse the use of a magnetic field to change the path of a charged particle, including: the magnitude and direction of the force applied to an electron beam by a magnetic field: F = qvB, in cases where the directions of v and B are perpendicular or parallel; the radius of the path followed by an electron in a magnetic field: qvB = mv²r, where v << c

What you must be able to do

  • Determine the magnitude (F = qvB on formula sheet) and direction (right-hand slap rule, reversed for negative charges) of the force exerted on a moving charge, in both the perpendicular and the parallel case.
  • Explain why perpendicular entry produces uniform circular motion, and use r = mv/qB on formula sheet — the sheet carries the rearranged form, not qvB = mv²/r.
  • Rearrange r = mv/qB for any variable and predict the change in r when m, v, q or B changes.

The magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it does no work: the speed and kinetic energy are constant, and only the direction of motion changes. That is why the path is a circle, and why "the field increases the electron's speed" is never a correct statement. When v is parallel (or antiparallel) to B, the force is zero and the particle continues in a straight line at constant velocity — the parallel case is named in the dot point and examined.

Equating qvB with the net force mv²/r required for circular motion gives r = mv/qB: radius grows with momentum mv and shrinks with charge and field. The condition v << c is part of the dot point; exam stems with speeds approaching 10⁸ m s⁻¹ add "Ignore relativistic effects", and that instruction is to be applied directly. Field strengths are routinely planted in millitesla — convert mT to T as a separate written line before substituting.

Trap Applying the right-hand slap rule to an electron without reversing the result. On the 2025 exam this direction question averaged 0.6 of 1 mark; the standard wrong answer applied the rule correctly "but did not account for the negative charge on the electron". Either the final direction is reversed, or the thumb is pointed opposite to the electron's motion.
Assessor's advice On particle-swap questions (electron → proton), students "either only addressed one change in the path or stated the changes without justification" (2024, average 1.4 of 3). Every stated change requires its cause: opposite curvature because the charge sign reverses the force; larger radius because r ∝ m at the same speed.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna studies the beam from an electron gun. The electrons travel horizontally due north at 4.0 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹ when they enter a region of uniform magnetic field of magnitude 1.5 mT directed vertically upward. Ignore relativistic effects.

a. State the direction of the force exerted on an electron by the magnetic field as it enters the region. (1 mark)

b. Calculate the radius of the path followed by an electron inside the region. (2 marks)

c. The gun is rotated so that the electrons enter the region travelling vertically upward, parallel to the field. Describe the path of the electrons inside the region. (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. Due west. The slap rule for a positive charge moving north in an upward field gives a force toward the east; the electron's negative charge reverses this to west (1 mark — direction only, but the reversal is where the mark is lost).

1 mark: west (east earns nothing — the negative charge was not accounted for).

b. r = mvqB = (9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 4.0 × 10⁷) ÷ (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 1.5 × 10⁻³) (1 mark: formula and substitution with B converted from mT to T) = 3.64 × 10⁻²³ ÷ 2.40 × 10⁻²² = 0.15 m (1 mark: answer).

1 mark: correct substitution including 1.5 × 10⁻³ T · 1 mark: 0.15 m.

Do not accept: 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ m or 150 m (mT conversion missed or inverted); stopping at F = qvB without continuing to a radius.

c. A straight line at constant velocity: the velocity is parallel to the field, so the magnetic force F = qvB is zero (1 mark: straight-line path with the parallel-field reason).

1 mark: straight line/undeflected because v is parallel to B so the force is zero.

In the style of VCAA · MC

A charged particle moves in a circular path of radius r in a uniform magnetic field, with its velocity perpendicular to the field. Which one of the following best describes a change that, made on its own, would double the radius of the path? (1 mark)

  1. doubling the speed of the particle
  2. doubling the magnitude of the magnetic field
  3. doubling the charge of the particle
  4. halving the mass of the particle
Worked solution

A. From r = mv/qB, the radius is proportional to v: doubling the speed doubles the momentum and therefore doubles the radius.

Why each distractor is wrong: B — B is in the denominator, so doubling it halves the radius · C — q is also in the denominator; a doubled charge experiences twice the force and turns more tightly · D — halving m halves the momentum and the radius. All three distractors invert a dependence in r = mv/qB.

KK 2.8 analyse the use of gravitational fields to accelerate mass, including: gravitational field and gravitational force concepts: g = GMr² and Fg = Gm1m2r²; potential energy changes in a uniform gravitational field: Eg = mgΔh

What you must be able to do

  • Calculate g = GM/r² on formula sheet and Fg = Gm1m2/r² on formula sheet with centre-to-centre distances, converting any altitude with r = RE + h.
  • Use Eg = mgΔh on formula sheet near a surface, where the field is approximately uniform.
  • Identify the assumption hidden in mgΔh and state the direction of the error when it is applied over a large altitude change.

This dot point is the gravitational mirror of KK 2.6: one mass makes a field (g = GM/r², N kg⁻¹), two masses make a force (Fg = Gm1m2/r², N), and the same field–force discipline applies. The one structural difference is that masses only attract, so every gravitational field points toward its source — yet between two bodies the two inward fields still oppose each other, and there is a null point nearer the smaller mass, exactly as for two like charges.

Eg = mgΔh assumes a uniform field: it treats g as the same at the bottom and the top of the climb. Near the surface this approximation is accurate — over a 400 m climb, g changes by about 0.013% — but over a 400 km climb g falls from 9.81 N kg⁻¹ to about 8.7 N kg⁻¹, and the formula is no longer exact. For large altitude changes the correct method is the area under a force–distance or field–distance graph (KK 2.9, next section).

Trap Using mgΔh with surface g over a large altitude change overestimates the energy required. The real gravitational force falls with the inverse square of distance during the climb, so the assumed constant force is too large for the whole journey above the start. Two separately earnable marks recur here: name the assumption (g constant at its surface value) and give the direction of the error (overestimate). On the 2025 exam this pairing averaged 1.3 of 2 marks — the direction of the error was the mark most often dropped.
Assessor's advice 2025 report, on the mgΔh assumption question: errors "tended to be statements that did not logically connect to the concepts being assessed", and a small number argued the actual work would be greater. The assumption and then the consequence are to be stated in two linked sentences.
In the style of VCAA · MC

The mass of the Moon is approximately 1/81 of the mass of Earth. The distance between their centres is d. Point X lies on the line joining the two centres, where the resultant gravitational field due to Earth and the Moon is zero. Which one of the following is closest to the distance of X from the centre of Earth? (1 mark)

  1. 0.10d
  2. 0.50d
  3. 0.90d
  4. 0.99d
Worked solution

C. At X the two field magnitudes are equal: GME/x² = GMM/(d − x)². Taking the square root, x/(d − x) = √(ME/MM) = √81 = 9, so x = 9(d − x), giving x = 0.90d. The null point sits nearer the smaller mass, and the distance ratio is the square root of the mass ratio (9 : 1), not the mass ratio itself.

Why each distractor is wrong: A puts the null point nearer the larger mass (the ratio inverted) · B assumes the fields cancel midway, ignoring the unequal masses · D uses the linear ratio 81 : 1 instead of its square root.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

A 350 kg payload is to be carried from Earth's surface to an altitude of 400 km. Lucia estimates the energy this requires using Eg = mgΔh, taking g = 9.81 N kg⁻¹.

a. Calculate Lucia's estimate of the energy required. (1 mark)

b. Identify the assumption in Lucia's method, and state and explain whether her estimate is greater than or less than the actual energy required to raise the payload. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Eg = mgΔh = 350 × 9.81 × 4.00 × 10⁵ = 1.37 × 10⁹ J (1 mark: substitution with the altitude converted to metres).

1 mark: 1.37 × 10⁹ J, with km converted to m.

b. Lucia has assumed the gravitational field strength remains constant at 9.81 N kg⁻¹ for the whole climb (1 mark: assumption). In fact g = GM/r² decreases with altitude — to about 8.7 N kg⁻¹ at 400 km — so the force assumed is too large above the surface, and her estimate is greater than the actual energy required: an overestimate (1 mark: direction of error with reason).

1 mark: assumption of constant (uniform) g · 1 mark: overestimate, because g decreases with altitude.

Do not accept: "g changes" without identifying that it decreases and that the estimate is therefore too large; "underestimate"; an assumption about air resistance (irrelevant correct physics earns nothing).

KK 2.9 analyse the change in gravitational potential energy from area under a force vs distance graph and area under a field vs distance graph multiplied by mass

What you must be able to do

  • Determine ΔEg directly as the area under a force–distance graph.
  • Determine ΔEg as the area under a field–distance graph multiplied by the mass.
  • Estimate a curved area by counting squares (or by trapezium), quoting one square's energy first and accepting a tolerance band on the answer.

Neither method appears on the formula sheet — the graphs are the method, and the unit of each grid square identifies which method applies. Under a force–distance graph each square is N × m = J, energy directly. Under a field–distance graph each square is N kg⁻¹ × m = J kg⁻¹, energy per kilogram: the field graph depends on the source mass alone, not on any particular object, which is why one extra step — multiplying by the mass — converts it to the energy of a specific probe. The axis scale factors are to be read first: distances are routinely plotted in km or in units of 10⁶ m, and a missed scale factor shifts the answer by whole powers of ten.

To count squares: compute the energy of one grid square, count every square that is at least half-covered by the region under the curve, then multiply. Assessors mark counting-squares answers within a stated tolerance band, so a careful count a square or two either way still earns full marks — but the working must show the per-square energy and the count, not a final number alone.

Trap Forgetting the × mass step on a field–distance graph. The two graph types look almost identical; the vertical axis label is the only warning. Force axis (N) → area is the energy. Field axis (N kg⁻¹) → area must be multiplied by the mass. Checking that the final unit reduces to joules detects this error.
Key idea A field–distance graph is mass-independent — the same curve serves a 1 kg instrument and a 500 kg probe. The area therefore requires the mass to be reinstated before it becomes an energy.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Figure 2.2 shows the magnitude of the gravitational force exerted on a 500 kg space probe by Earth, as a function of the distance r of the probe from Earth's centre, for r = 1.0 × 10⁷ m to r = 3.0 × 10⁷ m. Each grid square represents 250 N on the force axis and 0.25 × 10⁷ m on the distance axis.

a. Use the graph to estimate the increase in the gravitational potential energy of the probe as it moves from r = 1.0 × 10⁷ m to r = 3.0 × 10⁷ m. Show your method. (3 marks)

b. Ishva is given the same information as a graph of gravitational field strength versus distance. The area under that graph between the same two distances is 2.7 × 10⁷ J kg⁻¹. Explain how Ishva should use this area to find the change in gravitational potential energy of the probe, and show that the result is consistent with part a. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The increase in gravitational potential energy equals the area under the force–distance graph between the two distances (1 mark: method). One grid square represents 250 × 0.25 × 10⁷ = 6.25 × 10⁸ J (1 mark: per-square energy). Counting squares at least half-covered gives approximately 21 squares: ΔEg ≈ 21 × 6.25 × 10⁸ = 1.3 × 10¹⁰ J (1 mark: answer within tolerance).

1 mark: area under the curve identified as ΔEg · 1 mark: energy per square 6.25 × 10⁸ J · 1 mark: any answer in the range 1.2 × 10¹⁰ J to 1.5 × 10¹⁰ J with a visible count.

Accept: 19 to 23 squares; a trapezium estimate inside the same range. Do not accept: a bare final number with no per-square energy or count shown; an answer using mgΔh with surface g (the field is non-uniform across this range).

b. The area under a field–distance graph is the energy change per kilogram, so Ishva must multiply it by the probe's mass (1 mark): ΔEg = 2.7 × 10⁷ × 500 = 1.35 × 10¹⁰ ≈ 1.4 × 10¹⁰ J, consistent with the estimate in part a (1 mark).

1 mark: multiply the area by the mass, with the J kg⁻¹ reasoning · 1 mark: 1.4 × 10¹⁰ J and the consistency statement.

Mixed practice

The accelerated-charge chain — E = V/d, then qV = ½mv², then r = mv/qB, then a qualitative particle swap or redesign — has appeared on every recent exam, worth six to seven marks in Section B. Question 9 is a complete instance; it should be worked under exam conditions before the solution is read. In questions worth more than one mark, appropriate working must be shown, and assessors mark a scanned image: write in ink, with formula, substitution and answer as separate visible lines.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

The Australian Synchrotron in Clayton begins by accelerating electrons in an electron gun. Jo models the first stage of a similar machine as two parallel plates separated by 25 mm with a potential difference of 1.80 kV between them, as shown in Figure 2.3. Electrons are released from rest at the negative plate and leave the gun through a small hole in the positive plate. They then enter a region of uniform magnetic field of magnitude 2.0 mT directed into the page, travelling at right angles to the field. Ignore relativistic effects.

a. Calculate the magnitude of the electric field between the plates. (1 mark)

b. Show that the speed of an electron as it leaves the gun is approximately 2.5 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹. (2 marks)

c. Calculate the radius of the circular path followed by the electron in the magnetic field. Give your answer to two significant figures. (2 marks)

d. The electron source is replaced by a source of protons, which are accelerated from rest through the same potential difference and enter the same magnetic field. Describe two ways in which the path of a proton in the magnetic field differs from the path of an electron, justifying each difference. No calculations are required. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. E = V/d = 1.80 × 10³ ÷ 0.025 = 7.2 × 10⁴ V m⁻¹ (1 mark: substitution with both kV and mm converted).

1 mark: 7.2 × 10⁴ V m⁻¹.

b. The work done by the field becomes kinetic energy: qV = ½mv², so v = √(2qV/m) (1 mark: correct relation rearranged for v). Substituting, v = √(2 × 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 1.80 × 10³ ÷ 9.11 × 10⁻³¹) = √(6.32 × 10¹⁴) = 2.51 × 10⁷ ≈ 2.5 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹ (1 mark: complete substitution and evaluation). A "show that" answer must display the full substitution — the target value is given, so the marks are entirely for the visible working.

1 mark: qV = ½mv² rearranged · 1 mark: complete substitution reaching 2.5 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹.

c. r = mvqB = (9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 2.514 × 10⁷) ÷ (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 2.0 × 10⁻³) (1 mark: substitution, using the unrounded speed and B in tesla) = 2.290 × 10⁻²³ ÷ 3.20 × 10⁻²² = 7.2 × 10⁻² m (1 mark: answer to two significant figures).

1 mark: substitution with 2.0 × 10⁻³ T · 1 mark: 7.1–7.2 × 10⁻² m to two significant figures.

Accept (c): 7.1 × 10⁻² m (from the rounded speed 2.5 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹) — consequential marking applies. Do not accept: 7.2 × 10⁻⁵ m or 72 m (mT conversion missed); answers not given to two significant figures.

d. First difference: the proton curves in the opposite sense to the electron, because its charge is positive, so the magnetic force on it is opposite in direction for the same velocity and field (1 mark: change plus cause). Second difference: the proton's path has a much larger radius. Accelerated through the same potential difference it gains the same kinetic energy, so the much more massive proton moves more slowly with v ∝ 1/√m; its momentum mv is nevertheless larger, growing as √m, and r = mv/qB grows with it (1 mark: change plus cause).

1 mark: opposite curvature, justified by the positive charge · 1 mark: larger radius, justified through the same qV giving larger momentum (or r ∝ √m at fixed V).

Do not accept (d): "larger radius because the proton is faster" — it is slower; "same speed as the electron" — the same energy, not the same speed, is shared; either change stated without its justification.

Assessor's advice On the 2024 particle-swap part, students who missed marks "either only addressed one change in the path or stated the changes without justification" (average 1.4 of 3). The mark grammar is one change plus its cause per mark — exactly two such sentences are required.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Magnus sprays fine oil droplets into the gap between two horizontal parallel plates separated by 12 mm. One droplet, of mass 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁵ kg and carrying a charge of magnitude 4.8 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, remains stationary when the potential difference between the plates is adjusted so that the upper plate is positive. Use g = 9.8 m s⁻². Assume the field between the plates is uniform.

a. State the sign of the charge on the droplet. Justify your answer. (2 marks)

b. Determine the potential difference between the plates. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. The charge is negative (1 mark: claim). The field between the plates points from the positive upper plate toward the negative lower plate — vertically downward — and the electric force on the droplet must act vertically upward to balance the downward gravitational force; a force opposite to the field direction requires a negative charge (1 mark: reasoning from field direction to force direction).

b. The droplet is stationary, so the electric force balances the gravitational force: qE = mg (1 mark: equating the two forces).
