Generation of electricity

Dot points KK 3.1–3.4: magnetic flux, Faraday's law and the direction of induced emf, the two generator architectures, and photovoltaic cells. AOS 3 has been worth exactly 22 of 120 marks on every recent exam, and the generation half rests on three archetypes: the slip-ring/commutator question that assessors report appears on almost every exam, the flux–emf graph pair that more candidates shape-matched than answered correctly, and the average-emf-over-a-quarter-turn calculation. Both formulas are on the sheet, so the marks are awarded for graph gradients, scenario-specific Lenz arguments and named causal chains, never for recall.

KK 3.1 calculate magnetic flux when the magnetic field is perpendicular to the area, and describe the qualitative effect of differing angles between the area and the field: ΦB = B⊥A

What you must be able to do

  • Calculate ΦB = B⊥A on formula sheet when the field is perpendicular to the area, with the area converted to m² and the answer in webers (Wb).
  • Describe the qualitative effect of the angle: flux is a maximum when the field is perpendicular to the plane of the coil, decreases as the coil tilts, and is zero when the field is parallel to the plane.
  • State why the flux through a rotating coil changes: the angle between the plane of the coil and the magnetic field changes as it rotates.

The flux calculation is only ever examined with the field perpendicular to the area — the formula-sheet entry is written with the perpendicular subscript, and no cos θ appears anywhere on the sheet or in any recent paper. Angle effects are strictly qualitative: in the maximum-flux orientation every field line passes through the face of the loop; in the parallel orientation none do, and the flux is zero. A numerical answer at an intermediate angle is outside the study design and is never required.

Because 1 Wb = 1 T m², the area must be in square metres before substitution. Flux through a loop changes in three examinable ways — change the field strength, change the area inside the field (move the loop in or out), or rotate the loop. Rotation is the mechanism used in generators, and it directs flux through the loop in alternating directions, which is why a rotating coil's flux–time graph crosses zero.

Trap Converting cm² to m² by dividing by 100. Area is a squared quantity: divide by 100² = 10⁴, so 25 cm² = 2.5 × 10⁻³ m². Both wrong conversions — ÷100 and no conversion at all — are planted as multiple-choice distractors, and the area-conversion slip is among the most common calculation errors in this AOS.
Assessor's advice The 2025 exam asked, for one mark, why the flux through a rotating coil changes — about one-third of students scored zero. The creditable answer names the geometry: the flux is determined by the angle between the plane of the coil and the magnetic field, and that angle changes as the coil rotates. Answers that restated the stem or talked about the field changing "did not address the question".
In the style of VCAA · MC

A circular coil of a single turn encloses an area of 25 cm². It is placed with its plane perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field of magnitude 0.40 T. Which one of the following is closest to the magnitude of the magnetic flux through the coil? (1 mark)

  1. zero
  2. 1.0 × 10⁻¹ Wb
  3. 1.0 × 10⁻³ Wb
  4. 10 Wb
Worked solution

C. With the plane of the coil perpendicular to the field, the field passes squarely through the face of the coil and the flux is a maximum: ΦB = B⊥A = 0.40 × 25 × 10⁻⁴ = 1.0 × 10⁻³ Wb. The area conversion is 25 cm² = 25 × 10⁻⁴ m².

Why each distractor is wrong: A misreads the geometry — a plane perpendicular to the field means the field threads the loop face, giving maximum flux, not zero · B converts cm² to m² by dividing by 100 instead of 10⁴ · D performs no area conversion at all.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Jo winds a single-turn square coil of side length 9.0 cm and places it between the poles of two large magnets, where the magnetic field is uniform with a magnitude of 0.20 T. Initially the plane of the coil is perpendicular to the magnetic field.

a. Calculate the magnitude of the magnetic flux through the coil. Show your working. (2 marks)

b. Jo now rotates the coil slowly until its plane is parallel to the magnetic field. Describe qualitatively how the magnetic flux through the coil changes during this rotation. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. A = 0.090 × 0.090 = 8.1 × 10⁻³ m² (1 mark: area converted to m²). ΦB = B⊥A = 0.20 × 8.1 × 10⁻³ = 1.6 × 10⁻³ Wb (1 mark: answer with unit).

1 mark: area 8.1 × 10⁻³ m² with cm converted to m · 1 mark: 1.6 × 10⁻³ Wb.

Do not accept: 1.6 × 10⁻² Wb or 1.6 Wb (side length converted incorrectly or not at all); an answer without a unit.

b. The flux decreases throughout the rotation (1 mark): it starts at its maximum value, because the field is perpendicular to the plane of the coil, and falls to zero when the plane is parallel to the field, where no field lines pass through the coil (1 mark: both endpoints identified).

1 mark: flux decreases · 1 mark: from a maximum (plane perpendicular to the field) to zero (plane parallel to the field).

Do not accept: any calculated value at an intermediate angle — the study design requires only the qualitative description; "the flux increases".

KK 3.2 investigate and analyse theoretically and practically the generation of electromotive force (emf) including AC voltage and calculations using induced emf: ε = −NΔΦBΔt, with reference to: rate of change of magnetic flux; number of loops through which the flux passes; direction of induced emf in a coil

What you must be able to do

  • Calculate the average emf with ε = −NΔΦB/Δt on formula sheet, identifying the change in flux through one loop and the time over which it occurs — in a rotating coil a quarter turn takes Δt = T/4, with T = 1/f not on sheet.
  • Translate between a Φ–t graph and an ε–t graph in both directions: the emf is the negative gradient of the flux graph.
  • Determine the direction of the induced emf or current in a coil using Lenz's law and the right-hand coil rule, and justify it with the specific objects in the scenario.
  • Identify the factors determining emf size — the rate of change of flux and the number of loops N — and recognise when no emf is induced.

For magnitude calculations the minus sign is dropped: identify ΔΦB, divide by Δt, multiply by N. The sign stands for Lenz's law, and a direction is never computed — it is argued, from what the flux through the coil is doing. The formula gives an average emf over the interval Δt; the instantaneous emf at one moment is read from the gradient of the Φ–t graph at that moment. Over a full rotation the net ΔΦB is zero even though the coil generated an emf throughout, which is why exam questions specify the quarter turn from maximum flux to zero.

An emf requires changing flux, not merely the presence of flux or of motion. A magnet held stationary inside a coil induces nothing; a loop translating entirely within a uniform field induces nothing, because the flux through it never changes. The emf also exists whether or not a circuit is complete — closing the circuit is what converts the emf into a current.

On paired graphs, the ε–t curve is the negative gradient of the Φ–t curve: the emf is zero wherever the flux is at a maximum or minimum, and at an extreme wherever the flux crosses zero, changing fastest. A sinusoidal flux therefore produces a sinusoidal emf of the same period, shifted by a quarter period — never an emf in phase with the flux. Either overall polarity is accepted in sketches provided it is consistent, since the sign convention is not fixed by the question.

Trap Matching the shape of a flux graph to the shape of a current or emf graph. A constant induced current needs a constantly changing flux — a straight ramp — and a larger current needs a steeper ramp. On the 2024 exam's graph-matching question only 34% answered correctly, while 35% chose the flux graph shaped like the current graph. Match gradients, not shapes.
Trap Using the full period as Δt for a quarter turn. The flux falls from maximum to zero in T/4, not T. On the 2025 average-emf calculation the most common error was substituting Δt = 0.02 s — the whole period — instead of 5.0 × 10⁻³ s, which shrinks the answer by a factor of four. Write Δt = T/4 as its own working line.
Assessor's advice From the 2025 report, on a magnet falling into a solenoid: "many simply gave a definition of Lenz's law, which did not address the question", and "there were several responses that contained only generic statements and did not score any marks. All questions require a specific response, in this case addressing the falling magnet and the solenoid." Every direction answer needs three scenario-specific sentences: what the flux through the named coil is doing, what the induced current must oppose, and the resulting direction by the right-hand coil rule.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A flat coil of 200 turns is connected to a data logger. The magnitude of the magnetic flux through each turn increases from zero to 6.0 × 10⁻³ Wb at a constant rate over 2.0 s, remains constant for the next 2.0 s, then decreases to zero at a constant rate over the final 1.0 s. Which one of the following best describes the emf induced in the coil during the three stages? (1 mark)

  1. a constant 0.60 V, then zero, then a constant 1.2 V of opposite polarity
  2. a constant 0.60 V, then zero, then a constant 1.2 V of the same polarity
  3. an emf that rises and falls with the same shape as the flux, largest while the flux is constant
  4. a constant 3.0 × 10⁻³ V, then zero, then a constant 6.0 × 10⁻³ V of opposite polarity
Worked solution

A. The emf follows the gradient of the flux. Stage 1: ε = NΔΦB/Δt = 200 × 6.0 × 10⁻³ ÷ 2.0 = 0.60 V. Stage 2: the flux is constant, so the emf is zero. Stage 3: the gradient is twice as steep and reversed in sign — 200 × 6.0 × 10⁻³ ÷ 1.0 = 1.2 V of opposite polarity.

Why each distractor is wrong: B keeps the same polarity when the flux change reverses — the emf must reverse with the gradient · C is the shape-matching misconception: emf tracks the rate of change of flux, and is zero (not largest) while the flux is constant · D forgets the 200 turns, dividing the per-loop values straight through.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

The coil of a small alternator rotates at a constant frequency in a uniform magnetic field. Figure 4.1 shows the magnetic flux through the coil as a function of time for two complete cycles: a sinusoid of amplitude 2.4 × 10⁻³ Wb and period 40 ms, starting at its maximum value at t = 0.

a. On the blank axes provided in Figure 4.1, sketch the emf induced in the coil as a function of time for the two cycles shown. No values are required on the emf axis. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. The emf at each instant is the negative gradient of the flux graph, since ε = −NΔΦB/Δt. At t = 0 the flux is at a maximum, so its gradient is zero and the emf is zero. The flux changes fastest where it crosses zero — at 10, 30, 50 and 70 ms — so the emf is at an extreme at those times. The sketch is a sinusoid of the same 40 ms period, starting at zero and reaching its first extreme at 10 ms: the flux curve shifted by a quarter period.

Marking guide — 1 mark: sinusoidal curve with the same 40 ms period maintained across both cycles · 1 mark: emf zero at t = 0, 20, 40, 60 and 80 ms, where the flux is at a maximum or minimum · 1 mark: emf at its extremes at t = 10, 30, 50 and 70 ms, where the flux crosses zero (the quarter-period phase shift).

Accept: either polarity for the first extreme, provided the curve is consistent throughout. Do not accept: an emf in phase with the flux (maxima aligned with flux maxima); one-polarity rectified humps (no commutator is present); a curve whose amplitude or period drifts between cycles.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Anika investigates electromagnetic induction with a flat circular coil lying horizontally on a bench, connected to a sensitive ammeter. She holds a bar magnet vertically above the centre of the coil with its north pole facing downward, and moves the magnet downward toward the coil at a constant speed, as shown in Figure 4.2.

a. State whether the induced current in the coil flows clockwise or anticlockwise when viewed from above, and justify your answer. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. The current flows anticlockwise when viewed from above (1 mark: direction). As the magnet's north pole approaches the coil, the downward magnetic flux through the coil increases (1 mark: the change in flux through this coil, named and directed). By Lenz's law the induced current opposes this increase by creating flux directed upward inside the coil; by the right-hand coil rule, a current producing upward flux flows anticlockwise viewed from above (1 mark: opposition to the increase, with the rule applied).

1 mark: anticlockwise viewed from above · 1 mark: downward flux through the coil is increasing as the magnet approaches · 1 mark: induced current opposes the increase (upward induced flux) and the right-hand coil rule gives the direction.

Do not accept: a statement of Lenz's law alone ("the induced current opposes the change") with no reference to this magnet and this coil — assessors award such answers zero; "the current opposes the field" — it opposes the increase in flux, not the field itself; the correct direction with no justification (the direction mark is contingent on reasoning when the question says justify).

Assessor's advice On the 2025 magnet-and-solenoid question, more than one-quarter of students scored zero, often for "poor wording, such as 'the magnet has a changing field'", or for explaining induction "without referencing either the magnet or the solenoid". Write the three-sentence chain with the scenario's own nouns every time.

KK 3.3 explain the production of DC voltage in DC generators and AC voltage in alternators, including the use of split ring commutators and slip rings respectively

What you must be able to do

  • Explain how an alternator's slip rings deliver AC, and how a DC generator's split-ring commutator delivers DC — each explanation specific to the generator, not the motor.
  • Identify each device on a diagram: slip rings are two separate complete rings, each fixed to one end of the coil; a split-ring commutator is a single ring cut into segments.
  • Predict the output trace after a stated modification — rotation speed, N, B, area, or a device swap — and run the logic in reverse from a modified trace to the change that caused it.

An alternator and a DC generator are the same machine with different terminals. Slip rings maintain continuous electrical contact between the rotating coil and the stationary brushes, so the coil's sinusoidal emf reaches the external circuit unchanged: AC, with no twisting of the connecting leads. A split-ring commutator swaps the coil's connections to the brushes every half turn — at the instant the coil's emf reverses — so the output polarity never reverses: DC.

Commutator DC is not smooth DC. The trace is a rectified sine: one-polarity humps rising from zero to a peak twice per rotation. It is still a DC voltage, because the current always flows in the same direction; "varying" does not mean AC. Real machines smooth the output with several coils at different angles and a many-segment commutator, but the single-coil bumpy output is what is examined.

Rotation speed affects two quantities at once: doubling the frequency doubles the rate of change of flux, so the peak emf doubles and the period halves. The contrapositive is examined frequently — a trace whose amplitude has doubled at an unchanged period cannot result from a speed change, and must come from doubling N, B or the coil area. Expect the question in either direction.

Key idea Slip rings preserve the sine wave (AC); a split-ring commutator rectifies it (DC of varying magnitude). On a diagram: two separate rings means slip rings, one segmented ring means a commutator.
Trap Answering for the wrong machine, or for either machine. "It connects the coil to the circuit" is true of both devices and earns nothing. In a motor the commutator reverses the coil current every half turn so the torque stays one-way; in a generator it reverses the connections so the output emf stays one-way. Both recent exams penalised exactly this: slip rings explained as if in a DC motor (2024), and the commutator's motor role given in a generator question (2025).
Assessor's advice 2024 report, verbatim: "Given that questions regarding the role of slip rings and split ring commutators appear on almost every exam, it is important that students ensure they understand these devices." The same report's model answer for slip rings has two parts — maintain continuous contact with the spinning loop, and transfer the generated current to the external circuit — and answers giving only the connection scored one of the two marks.
Connects The split-ring commutator first appeared in the DC motor of AOS 2 — same hardware, mirrored job: in the motor it reverses the current to keep the torque one-way, in the generator it reverses the connections to keep the output one-way. Confusing the two explanations is a named examiner complaint.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A single-coil generator fitted with a split-ring commutator is rotated at a constant rate in a uniform magnetic field. Its output is displayed on an oscilloscope. Which one of the following best describes the displayed output? (1 mark)

  1. a DC voltage of constant magnitude
  2. a DC voltage whose magnitude varies periodically
  3. a sinusoidal AC voltage
  4. an AC voltage whose magnitude peaks twice per rotation
Worked solution

B. The commutator reverses the coil's connections every half turn, producing a rectified sine wave: the voltage always drives current in the same direction — so it is DC — but its magnitude still rises and falls from zero to a peak twice per rotation.

Why each distractor is wrong: A assumes the commutator smooths the output as well as rectifying it — on the matching 2024 question 32% made this choice · C is the slip-ring output; the commutator has been mistaken for slip rings · D correctly counts two peaks per rotation but mislabels a one-polarity voltage as AC — "varying" does not mean alternating.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Magnus builds a model alternator: a coil rotated at a constant rate in a uniform magnetic field, connected through slip rings to an oscilloscope. The oscilloscope displays a sinusoidal voltage with a peak value of 3.0 V and a period of 50 ms.

a. Explain the purpose of the slip rings in this alternator. (2 marks)

b. Magnus modifies the apparatus. The new trace is sinusoidal with a peak value of 6.0 V and a period still of 50 ms. Identify two possible modifications Magnus could have made, and justify each with reference to ε = −NΔΦB/Δt. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. The slip rings maintain continuous electrical contact between the rotating coil and the stationary brushes (1 mark), so that the alternating emf generated in the coil is transferred to the oscilloscope without the connecting leads twisting and without the connections ever being reversed (1 mark).

1 mark: continuous contact between rotating coil and stationary circuit · 1 mark: the alternating output is transferred unchanged (no twisting, no reversal of connections).

Do not accept: "they connect the coil to the oscilloscope" alone — a split-ring commutator also does that, so the statement does not distinguish the device; any account of reversing connections every half turn (that is the commutator).

b. The peak voltage has doubled while the period — and so the rotation frequency — is unchanged. Doubling the number of turns N doubles the emf directly, since ε ∝ N (1 mark). Doubling the magnetic field strength B (or doubling the coil's area) doubles the flux ΦB = B⊥A and therefore doubles ΔΦB in each quarter turn, doubling the emf (1 mark). In each case the rotation rate is untouched, so the period stays 50 ms; increasing the rotation speed is ruled out because it would also halve the period (1 mark: justification tied to the unchanged period).

1 mark: first modification stated as doubling a correct quantity · 1 mark: second modification stated as doubling another correct quantity (any two of N, B, A) · 1 mark: justification through ε = −NΔΦB/Δt including why a speed change is excluded.

Do not accept: "increase" without "double" — the trace fixes the factor; "rotate the coil faster" — the period would change; a third modification offered in place of justification.

Assessor's advice On the 2024 version of part b, 48% of students scored zero and the average was 0.9 of 3. "The most common error was to refer only to increasing the identified quantities rather than doubling them." A quantitative trace demands a quantitative answer.

KK 3.4 describe the production of electricity using photovoltaic cells and the need for an inverter to convert power from DC to AC for use in the home (not including details of semiconductors action or inverter circuitry)

What you must be able to do

  • Describe the production of electricity by photovoltaic cells at dot-point depth: light falling on the cell is converted directly into electrical energy, and the output is DC.
  • State the function of the inverter: it converts the DC output of the panels into AC for use in the home and the grid.
  • Analyse series strings and parallel combinations of identical panels — voltages add in series at a common current; currents add in parallel at a common voltage — with panel power given by P = VI on formula sheet.

The dot point's parenthesis — "not including details of semiconductors action or inverter circuitry" — is a genuine scope boundary, and no recent exam has crossed it. What is examined is the system view: photovoltaic cells produce DC; Australian homes and the grid run on AC at 230 V rms and 50 Hz; the inverter converts the array's DC to AC. The inverter-function mark requires little — "converts DC power into AC power" earns it — yet 28% of students missed it in 2024.

The marks most commonly lost in photovoltaic questions are Units 1&2 circuit marks. Panels connected in series form a string: the voltages add and the current equals that of a single panel. Identical strings connected in parallel: the voltage equals that of a single string and the currents add. The total power is the same either way — the wiring choice determines the voltage–current combination delivered, not the total power.

Trap Multiplying both voltage and current by the number of parallel strings. Only 41% of students answered the 2024 parallel-array question correctly; most scaled the voltage as well as the current. Across parallel strings the voltage does not multiply — it is the same single-string voltage, while the currents add.
Trap Ignoring the unit printed beside the answer box. The most common error on the 2024 array-power question was writing 30 000 kW in a box already labelled kW. The printed unit is an instruction: convert before writing.
Assessor's advice 2024 report, after the parallel-array question: "Students attempting Units 3 and 4 must master the relevant underpinning concepts in Units 1 and 2." Series and parallel circuit behaviour is assumed knowledge, and AOS 3 is where the assumption is tested.
Connects Why light can knock electrons loose at all is Unit 4 territory: light arriving as discrete packets of energy is the heart of the photoelectric effect, and the photovoltaic cell is its everyday application.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A rooftop photovoltaic array delivers a steady DC voltage to an inverter, which supplies a household's switchboard. Which one of the following best describes the output of the inverter? (1 mark)

  1. a steady DC voltage of greater magnitude than the input
  2. a series of voltage pulses that are all of the same polarity
  3. a steady DC voltage of smaller magnitude than the input
  4. a sinusoidal voltage that alternates in polarity
Worked solution

D. The inverter converts DC to AC: its output alternates in polarity, matching the 230 V rms, 50 Hz mains supply the household equipment is designed for.

Why each distractor is wrong: A and C describe a device that changes the size of a DC voltage — the inverter changes the kind of voltage, not merely its size (and a transformer, which does change size, cannot operate on steady DC at all) · B is a rectified, one-polarity output — the commutator's signature, exactly backwards: the inverter turns DC into AC, not AC into bumpy DC.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Lucia designs a rooftop solar array for a community workshop using 24 identical photovoltaic panels. In full sunlight each panel delivers a maximum power of 200 W at a voltage of 40 V. The panels are wired as three identical parallel strings, with eight panels connected in series in each string. The array's output feeds an inverter.

a. Calculate the maximum power output of the array, in kW. (1 mark)

b. Show that the maximum current delivered by one panel is 5.0 A. (1 mark)

c. Determine the maximum voltage and maximum current delivered by one string of eight panels. (2 marks)

d. Determine the maximum voltage and maximum current delivered by the whole array to the inverter. (2 marks)

e. State the function of the inverter in this system. (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. P = 24 × 200 = 4800 W = 4.8 kW (1 mark: value in the requested unit).

1 mark: 4.8 kW — 4800 written in a kW answer box earns nothing.

b. P = VI, so I = P/V = 200 ÷ 40 = 5.0 A (1 mark: substitution shown — the value is given, so the mark is for the visible working).

1 mark: formula and substitution reaching 5.0 A.

c. In series the voltages add: V = 8 × 40 = 320 V (1 mark). The current in a series string equals that of one panel: 5.0 A (1 mark).

1 mark: 320 V · 1 mark: 5.0 A.

d. Identical strings in parallel share the same voltage: 320 V (1 mark). The string currents add: I = 3 × 5.0 = 15 A (1 mark). Check: 320 × 15 = 4800 W, consistent with part a.

1 mark: 320 V (unchanged across parallel strings) · 1 mark: 15 A.

Do not accept (d): 960 V — multiplying the voltage by the number of parallel strings; this was the dominant error on the matching 2024 question (41% correct).

e. The inverter converts the array's DC output into AC so that the workshop's standard 230 V rms, 50 Hz mains equipment can use the power (1 mark).

1 mark: converts DC to AC (for use by mains equipment/the grid).

Mixed practice

Two multi-part archetypes carry most of the Section B marks in this cluster: the rotating coil read end to end (flux → quarter-turn average emf → device and speed modifications) and the magnet falling through a coil (Faraday's chain, then the polarity and size of the pulses). Assessors recommend "more complex, application type problems such as this" as the highest-value preparation. Work both under exam conditions; in questions worth more than one mark, working must be shown — formula, substitution and answer as separate visible lines.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna builds a hand-driven generator for a science fair. A rectangular coil of 40 turns, measuring 8.0 cm × 4.0 cm, rotates in a uniform magnetic field of magnitude 0.25 T and is connected to an oscilloscope through slip rings, as shown in Figure 4.3. Shanna cranks the coil at a constant rotation frequency of 25 Hz.

a. State why the magnetic flux through the coil changes as the coil rotates. (1 mark)

b. Show that the magnitude of the magnetic flux through the coil at the instant its plane is perpendicular to the magnetic field is 8.0 × 10⁻⁴ Wb. (1 mark)

c. Calculate the magnitude of the average emf induced in the coil while it rotates through a quarter turn, starting from the position in part b. Show your working. (3 marks)

d. Shanna now cranks the coil at 50 Hz. State two changes to the displayed trace. (2 marks)

e. Suggest one change to the apparatus that would make the output voltage DC, and explain how the change achieves this. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. As the coil rotates, the angle between the plane of the coil and the magnetic field changes, so the area presented perpendicular to the field — and with it ΦB = B⊥A — changes (1 mark).

1 mark: the changing angle between the plane of the coil (its area) and the field.

b. ΦB = B⊥A = 0.25 × (0.080 × 0.040) = 0.25 × 3.2 × 10⁻³ = 8.0 × 10⁻⁴ Wb (1 mark: complete substitution shown, with both dimensions converted to metres — the value is given, so the working earns the mark).

1 mark: full substitution reaching 8.0 × 10⁻⁴ Wb.

c. Over the quarter turn the flux falls from its maximum to zero: ΔΦB = 8.0 × 10⁻⁴ Wb (1 mark). The quarter turn takes Δt = T/4 = (1 ÷ 25) ÷ 4 = 0.010 s (1 mark). ε = NΔΦBΔt = 40 × 8.0 × 10⁻⁴ ÷ 0.010 = 3.2 V (1 mark).

1 mark: ΔΦB = 8.0 × 10⁻⁴ Wb (maximum to zero) · 1 mark: Δt = T/4 = 0.010 s · 1 mark: 3.2 V with N included.

Do not accept: 0.80 V (Δt taken as the full period 0.040 s — the most common error on the matching 2025 question); 0.080 V (N omitted as well); zero (ΔΦB taken over a full rotation).

d. The peak voltage doubles, to about 6.4 V (1 mark), and the period halves to 20 ms — equivalently, the frequency of the trace doubles to 50 Hz (1 mark). Doubling the rotation rate doubles the rate of change of flux and halves the rotation time: both features change together.

1 mark: amplitude/peak doubles · 1 mark: period halves (frequency doubles).

Do not accept: either change alone for both marks; "the voltage increases" without the factor of two.

e. Replace the slip rings with a split-ring commutator (1 mark: device correctly named). The commutator reverses the connections between the coil and the brushes every half turn, at the instants the coil's emf reverses, so the output voltage never changes polarity — a DC voltage of varying magnitude (1 mark: function in the generator).

1 mark: split-ring commutator named · 1 mark: reverses the connections every half turn so the output keeps one polarity.

Do not accept: "commutator" or "split rings" as the device name without "split-ring commutator"; the motor's version of the explanation (reversing the current to maintain torque); "it converts AC to DC" with no mechanism. On the matching 2025 question, 29% scored zero for exactly these errors.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Kenji mounts a cardboard tube vertically and winds a coil around its middle, connecting the coil to an oscilloscope. He releases a bar magnet, north pole downward, from just above the tube; the magnet falls along the axis of the tube, through the coil, and out the bottom. The oscilloscope records the voltage trace shown in Figure 4.4: a smaller, wider pulse A of one polarity as the magnet enters the coil, followed by a larger, narrower pulse B of the opposite polarity as the magnet leaves.

a. Explain why an emf is recorded as the magnet falls through the coil. (2 marks)

b. Explain why pulse B is opposite in polarity to pulse A, and why it is larger in magnitude. (3 marks)

c. Kenji repeats the experiment, releasing the magnet from a greater height above the tube. State and explain one change to pulse A. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. As the magnet falls toward and through the coil, the magnetic flux through the coil changes (1 mark: changing flux through the named coil, caused by the moving magnet). By Faraday's law, ε = −NΔΦB/Δt, this changing flux induces an emf in the coil, which the oscilloscope records (1 mark: Faraday's law connecting the change to the emf).

1 mark: the falling magnet changes the flux through the coil · 1 mark: the changing flux induces an emf (Faraday's law).

Do not accept: "the magnet has a changing field" — the magnet's field is constant; it is the flux through the coil that changes; a recitation of Lenz's law — it does not answer why an emf appears; any answer that names neither the magnet nor the coil.

b. While the magnet approaches and enters, the downward flux through the coil is increasing; while it leaves, the downward flux is decreasing (1 mark: increasing then decreasing flux — not a field that "changes direction"). The induced emf depends on the sign of the change in flux, so the change from increasing to decreasing flux reverses the polarity of the pulse (1 mark). Because the magnet accelerates as it falls, it leaves the coil faster than it entered, so a similar ΔΦB occurs in a shorter Δt; by ε = −NΔΦB/Δt the emf at exit is larger, which is why pulse B is taller and narrower (1 mark).

1 mark: flux increasing on entry, decreasing on exit · 1 mark: the reversed change in flux reverses the polarity · 1 mark: higher exit speed gives a shorter Δt for a similar ΔΦB, so the magnitude is larger.

Do not accept: "the magnetic field changes direction" — it never does; the flux change does; "the poles swap as the magnet passes" — narrating the apparatus instead of the flux; the amplitude argument without the polarity argument for full marks.

Assessor's advice On the 2025 original of this question, 44% of students scored zero. "While many responses related the different amplitudes to acceleration of the magnet …, very few explained the opposing polarity. Very few students articulated the increasing and decreasing flux, with many stating that the magnetic field changed direction." The polarity mark lives in the words increasing and decreasing.

c. Pulse A becomes taller and narrower (1 mark: change). Released from higher, the magnet is moving faster when it reaches the coil, so the same change in flux occurs over a shorter time; by ε = −NΔΦB/Δt, the induced emf is larger and the pulse is briefer (1 mark: reason through the rate of change of flux).

1 mark: larger (and narrower) pulse · 1 mark: greater speed → same ΔΦB in shorter Δt → larger emf.

Do not accept: "the pulse is bigger because the magnet has more energy" — the link must run through the rate of change of flux.

1 mark: peak emf doubles (εpeak ∝ f)

1 mark: output frequency also doubles (period halves)

A bar magnet is dropped vertically through a stationary solenoid connected to a sensitive datalogger. The recorded emf–time trace shows two pulses of opposite sign, labelled A (as the magnet enters) and B (as it leaves), as shown.

a. Explain why an emf is induced in the solenoid as the magnet falls through it. (2)

b. Explain why pulse B is taller and narrower than pulse A. (2)

c. State how the areas under the two pulses compare, and explain why. (2)

d. Explain, using Lenz's law, why the two pulses have opposite signs. (2)

Worked solution

a. As the magnet moves, the magnetic flux threading the coil changes. By Faraday's law a changing flux induces an emf, and the faster the flux changes the larger the emf.

1 mark: the magnet's motion changes the flux through the coil

1 mark: a changing flux induces an emf (Faraday's law)

b. The magnet accelerates as it falls, so it is moving faster when it leaves the bottom than when it entered the top. The greater speed gives a greater rate of change of flux (a taller pulse) over a shorter time (a narrower pulse).

1 mark: the magnet is faster on the way out (it has accelerated)

1 mark: faster motion → greater rate of flux change → taller, narrower pulse

c. The two areas are equal in magnitude. The area under an emf–time graph equals the total change in flux linkage, and the magnet produces the same magnitude of flux change entering as leaving — so the taller, narrower pulse encloses the same area as the shorter, wider one.

1 mark: the areas are equal in magnitude

1 mark: area = total flux change, which is the same for entry and exit

d. The induced current always opposes the change producing it (Lenz's law). As the magnet approaches, the coil opposes the increasing flux (repelling the magnet); as it recedes, the coil opposes the decreasing flux (attracting it). The induced current therefore flows in opposite directions for entry and exit, giving pulses of opposite sign. This opposition is required by conservation of energy.

1 mark: induced effect opposes the change (Lenz's law)

1 mark: opposite current directions for approaching vs receding magnet

A bank of solar photovoltaic panels delivers 12.0 kW in full sunlight through an inverter to a dairy, as shown. The panels are wired in a combination of series and parallel groups.

a. State the function of the inverter in this system. (1)

b. Explain how connecting panels in series rather than in parallel affects the output voltage and current. (2)

c. The bank delivers 12.0 kW at 240 V. Calculate the current supplied. (2)

d. Explain one reason the panels are wired in a series–parallel combination rather than all in series or all in parallel. (1)

Worked solution

a. The inverter converts the DC output of the solar panels into AC, as required by the mains appliances and the grid.

1 mark: converts DC to AC

b. Connecting panels in series adds their voltages (a higher total voltage) while the current stays the same. Connecting them in parallel keeps the voltage the same but adds their currents.

1 mark: series adds the voltages (current unchanged)

1 mark: parallel adds the currents (voltage unchanged)

c. I = P/V = 12 000 / 240 = 50 A.

1 mark: I = P/V

1 mark: 50 A

d. The combination lets the array reach both a suitable working voltage (from the series groups) and a suitable current/power (from the parallel groups), matching the inverter's input requirements.

1 mark: combination sets both the voltage and the current/power to suit the inverter
