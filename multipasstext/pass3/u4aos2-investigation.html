The scientific investigation

Unit 4 Area of Study 2 is a student-designed practical investigation that generates primary data on fields, motion or light, with one continuous independent variable, and is reported as a scientific poster of no more than 600 words. The poster is school-assessed, not examined. The key science skills it develops — uncertainty and its bars, the distinction between error and uncertainty, accuracy versus precision, significant figures, lines of best fit, linearisation and the physical meaning of a gradient — are examined throughout the written examination, in both the multiple-choice section and the extended-response questions of every Area of Study. This page sets out those skills in the wording against which student responses are marked.

Design identify the independent, dependent and controlled variables; explain the chosen methodology and method; and evaluate the validity of an investigation

What you must be able to do

  • Identify the independent variable (the quantity deliberately changed), the dependent variable (the quantity measured in response) and the controlled variables (the quantities held constant) for a given investigation.
  • Recognise that the scope requires one continuous independent variable, and explain why qualitative data may also be recorded alongside the quantitative measurements.
  • State the definition of validity and identify the two ways validity is threatened: controlled variables allowed to change, and observer bias.
  • Distinguish the methodology (the overall type of investigation, such as a controlled experiment) from the method (the specific procedure used to generate and analyse the data).

The independent variable is the single quantity the investigator chooses to vary; in this Area of Study it must be continuous, meaning it can take any value over a range rather than belonging to discrete categories. The dependent variable is the quantity measured at each value of the independent variable. Every other quantity that could affect the dependent variable is a controlled variable and is held constant so that any change in the dependent variable can be attributed to the independent variable alone. Primary data are data generated by the investigator; they may be quantitative (numerical measurements) or qualitative (descriptive observations, such as the colour of an interference fringe or whether a surface was smooth).

Key idea Validity: a valid experiment investigates what it sets out or claims to investigate. Validity is threatened if controlled variables are allowed to change, or by observer bias. An experiment can be precise and still be invalid: a quantity measured repeatably and reproducibly is worthless if an uncontrolled variable means the experiment is not measuring what it claims to measure.
Trap Confusing validity with accuracy or precision. Validity is a property of the experimental design — whether the procedure tests the stated hypothesis. Accuracy is closeness to the true value and precision is the agreement of repeated readings; neither guarantees validity. A response that "improves validity" by repeating measurements is wrong: repetition improves precision, not validity. Validity is improved by controlling additional variables or by removing a source of bias.
Assessor's advice Reports note that students frequently propose improvements that do not match the deficiency named in the question. An improvement to validity must address a controlled variable that was not held constant, or a source of observer bias; an improvement to precision must address a source of random error. State which one the named problem affects before proposing the fix.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A student investigates how the period of a simple pendulum depends on its length. The student measures the period for a range of lengths, keeping the mass of the bob and the angle of release constant, and using the same timing method throughout.

a. Identify the independent and the dependent variables in this investigation. (2 marks)

b. State one controlled variable and explain how failing to control it would threaten the validity of the investigation. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Independent variable: the length of the pendulum (1 mark). Dependent variable: the period (1 mark).

1 mark: length identified as independent · 1 mark: period identified as dependent.

b. A controlled variable is the angle of release (accept the mass of the bob). If the angle of release were allowed to change between trials, the period would vary for a reason other than the change in length, so any relationship found between length and period would be unreliable; the experiment would no longer be investigating only the effect of length, and so would not be valid (2 marks).

1 mark: a correct controlled variable named · 1 mark: explanation that an uncontrolled variable means the experiment is not testing only the stated relationship (validity).

Do not accept (b): "repeat the measurement to reduce error" — repetition addresses precision, not validity.

Measure apply the concepts of accuracy, precision, repeatability, reproducibility and resolution to the evaluation of measurements

What you must be able to do

  • State, in the study-design wording, the meaning of accuracy, precision, repeatability, reproducibility and resolution, and use each term correctly.
  • Recognise that accuracy is qualitative — described as "more accurate" or "less accurate", never with a number — while precision describes the spread of repeated readings.
  • Distinguish repeatability (same conditions) from reproducibility (changed conditions), and explain what each one checks.
  • Distinguish resolution from uncertainty: resolution is the smallest perceptible change in the reading; uncertainty is half the smallest scale increment.

These five terms are marked against fixed definitions, and the examiner reports repeatedly identify accuracy and precision being treated as synonyms. They are not synonyms: a set of readings can be precise (closely agreeing) yet inaccurate (all far from the true value), for example when a zero error shifts every reading by the same amount.

Key idea The definitions, verbatim:
  • Accuracy is qualitative: a measurement is accurate if it is close to the true value. Use "more accurate" or "less accurate"; never assign accuracy a number.
  • Precision is how close repeated measurements are to each other (the spread); it is a measure of repeatability and reproducibility. Precision gives no indication of closeness to the true value — it is separate from accuracy.
  • Repeatability is the closeness of agreement between successive measurements under the same conditions (same observer, instrument, method, location, short time). It evaluates precision.
  • Reproducibility is the closeness of agreement under changed conditions (different observer, instrument, method or location). It checks claimed precision and can uncover systematic error, and so links to accuracy.
  • Resolution is the smallest change in the measured quantity that produces a perceptible change in the reading (a millimetre ruler resolves 1 mm; a stopwatch reading to 0.01 s resolves 0.01 s). Resolution is not the same as uncertainty.
Trap Three recurring errors. (1) Calling a precise result accurate, or the reverse — precision is agreement among repeats, accuracy is closeness to the true value. (2) Quoting accuracy as a number — accuracy is qualitative, so only precision and uncertainty carry numbers. (3) Confusing resolution with uncertainty: for a millimetre ruler the resolution is 1 mm but the uncertainty is half the smallest increment, ±0.5 mm.
Assessor's advice When a question asks whether reproducibility or repeatability has been demonstrated, check what was changed. If only successive readings under identical conditions are described, the evidence is for repeatability. Reproducibility requires that a condition — observer, instrument, method or location — was changed; because changing the instrument can reveal a consistent offset, reproducibility, unlike repeatability, can expose systematic error.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Two students each measure the same length five times. Student X obtains 24.7, 24.8, 24.7, 24.8, 24.7 cm. Student Y obtains 23.1, 25.9, 24.0, 26.2, 22.8 cm. The true value of the length is 24.8 cm.

a. Compare the precision of the two sets of measurements. (1 mark)

b. Comment on the accuracy of Student X's measurements. (1 mark)

c. Explain why a set of measurements can be precise but not accurate. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Student X's measurements are more precise than Student Y's, because they agree more closely with each other (a smaller spread) (1 mark).

1 mark: X more precise, justified by the smaller spread of repeated readings.

b. Student X's measurements are accurate, because their mean (24.74 cm) is close to the true value of 24.8 cm (1 mark).

1 mark: accurate, justified by closeness to the true value. (Qualitative comparison only — no numerical "accuracy".)

c. Precision describes only how closely repeated measurements agree with each other; it gives no indication of closeness to the true value (1 mark). A systematic error, such as a zero error, shifts every reading by the same amount, so the readings can agree closely (precise) while all lie far from the true value (inaccurate) (1 mark).

1 mark: precision is agreement among repeats, separate from the true value · 1 mark: a systematic error displaces all readings equally, giving precision without accuracy.

Error distinguish random from systematic error, identify their causes, and state the appropriate remedy for each

What you must be able to do

  • Define random error and systematic error, and state which one affects precision and which affects accuracy.
  • Identify the likely source of a stated error (for example parallax, a zero error, reaction time) and classify it as random or systematic.
  • State the correct remedy: random error is reduced by repeating and averaging; systematic error is not reduced by averaging and must be removed at its source.
  • Distinguish error from uncertainty: error is the difference between a measurement result and the true value; uncertainty is the interval within which the true value is expected to lie.

The distinction between error and uncertainty is examined directly and is frequently confused. They are not the same quantity.

Key idea Error and uncertainty, verbatim:
  • Measurement error is the difference between a measurement result and the true value.
  • Uncertainty: the true value is expected to lie in the interval mean ± uncertainty. For example, 20 ± 1 mm means the interval 19–21 mm; plotted on a graph this interval is an uncertainty bar. By VCE convention the uncertainty of a measuring instrument is half its smallest scale increment: a millimetre ruler gives ±0.5 mm; a stopwatch reading to 0.01 s gives ±0.005 s.
  • Random error produces unpredictable variations that cause a spread of readings; it affects precision, and is reduced by repeating and averaging.
  • Systematic error causes readings to differ from the true value in a consistent direction by the same amount each time; it affects accuracy, arises from calibration, the instrument or the method (for example parallax or a zero error), and is not reduced by averaging.
Trap "Averaging reduces systematic error." This is wrong, and the examiner reports flag it every year. Averaging reduces random error only, because random variations scatter above and below the true value and partly cancel. A systematic error displaces every reading by the same amount and in the same direction, so the mean is displaced by that same amount; averaging cannot remove it. Systematic error is removed only by correcting the source — recalibrating, eliminating a zero error, or changing the method.
Assessor's advice When asked to reduce a stated error, first classify it. Parallax and a zero offset are systematic, so the remedy is to view the scale square-on or to correct the zero, not to repeat. A spread of stopwatch times caused by reaction time is largely random, so the remedy is to repeat and average (or to use light gates to remove the human element). Pairing a remedy with the wrong class of error is the most common reason these marks are lost.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A student measures the diameter of a wire with a micrometer. The micrometer reads 0.04 mm when its jaws are fully closed, and this is not noticed. The student then records the diameter several times and finds the readings agree closely. Which one of the following best describes the error in the recorded diameters? (1 mark)

  1. A random error, which would be reduced by repeating and averaging.
  2. A random error, which would be reduced by using a finer instrument.
  3. A systematic error, which would be reduced by repeating and averaging.
  4. A systematic error, which would not be reduced by repeating and averaging.
Worked solution

D. An unnoticed zero error of 0.04 mm adds the same amount to every reading in the same direction, so it is a systematic error. Because the displacement is identical for every reading, the mean is displaced by the same 0.04 mm; averaging does not reduce it. The error would be removed only by reading and subtracting the zero offset.

Why each distractor is wrong: A and B classify a consistent zero offset as random — but a fixed offset in one direction is systematic; closely agreeing readings confirm good precision, not the absence of systematic error · C correctly names the error systematic but then applies the random-error remedy — averaging does not reduce systematic error.

Data apply the conventions of significant figures and uncertainty when recording and reporting primary data

What you must be able to do

  • Apply the significant-figure rules for addition and subtraction (keep the fewest decimal places) and for multiplication and division (keep the fewest significant figures), rounding only at the final step.
  • State a measurement as mean ± uncertainty and interpret the interval it defines.
  • Assign the uncertainty of an instrument as half its smallest scale increment.
  • Record data and observations in a logbook to authenticate primary data, noting that a contemporaneous, dated logbook is the evidence that the data are the investigator's own.

Reported results must carry the correct number of significant figures and, where required, an uncertainty. The number of significant figures signals the precision claimed for the result; over-stating it claims a precision the measurement does not support.

Key idea Significant figures in calculations:
  • Addition and subtraction: the answer keeps the fewest decimal places of any value used. For example, 12.1 + 7.45 = 19.6 (one decimal place, from 12.1).
  • Multiplication and division: the answer keeps the fewest significant figures of any value used. For example, 4.2 × 1.732 = 7.3 (two significant figures, from 4.2).
  • Round only at the final step. Carry extra figures through intermediate working and round once, at the end, to avoid accumulating rounding error.
Key idea The logbook authenticates primary data. A dated, contemporaneous logbook in which raw readings and observations are recorded at the time they are taken is the evidence that the data are the investigator's own primary data; it records the method actually carried out (including departures from the plan) and is the basis on which the poster's results are validated.
Trap Applying the multiplication rule to a sum, or the reverse. When adding or subtracting, count decimal places, not significant figures; when multiplying or dividing, count significant figures, not decimal places. The other recurring error is rounding at every line: round only once, at the end, or the final figure may be wrong.
Assessor's advice Reports note marks lost for answers quoted to too many significant figures, implying a precision the data cannot support, and for answers stated without a unit. Match the significant figures of a multiplied or divided result to the least precise datum, and always attach the correct unit. A final answer that copies the calculator display in full is penalised.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A trolley travels a distance of 1.62 m, measured with a millimetre ruler, in a time of 0.84 s, measured with a stopwatch reading to 0.01 s.

a. State the uncertainty in the distance measurement, and explain how it was determined. (2 marks)

b. Calculate the average speed of the trolley, expressed to the correct number of significant figures. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The uncertainty is ±0.5 mm (±0.0005 m). By convention the uncertainty of a measuring instrument is half its smallest scale increment; the smallest increment of a millimetre ruler is 1 mm, so the uncertainty is half of 1 mm, which is 0.5 mm (2 marks).

1 mark: ±0.5 mm (or ±0.0005 m) · 1 mark: stated as half the smallest scale increment of 1 mm.

b. v = dt = 1.620.84 = 1.928… = 1.9 m s⁻¹ (2 marks). The division keeps the fewest significant figures of the inputs; 0.84 s has two significant figures, so the answer is quoted to two significant figures.

1 mark: correct substitution into v = d/t · 1 mark: 1.9 m s⁻¹, two significant figures with the unit.

Do not accept (b): 1.928571 m s⁻¹ or 1.93 m s⁻¹ (too many significant figures); any answer without the unit m s⁻¹.

Graphs draw a line of best fit, linearise data, and extract a physical quantity from the gradient, with correct units, scale factors and uncertainty bars

What you must be able to do

  • Draw a line of best fit that follows the trend of all the data, without forcing it through the origin or any single point, and without extending it beyond the data.
  • Linearise a relationship by transforming the correct variable and relabelling its axis with the transformed units, so that the gradient equals a physical quantity.
  • Calculate a gradient using two widely separated points that lie on the line of best fit, account for any ×10n axis scale factor, and state the gradient with its correct units.
  • Draw and interpret uncertainty bars, and recognise that a line of best fit that does not pass through the origin where it is expected to indicates a systematic error.

The gradient of linearised data is the central quantitative skill of this Area of Study and is examined in every paper. The procedure has three stages: choose the variable to transform so that the relationship becomes a straight line; relabel the transformed axis with its new units; then read the gradient from two points on the line and identify it with a physical quantity.

Key idea Gradient with units and scale factors. The gradient equals the rise divided by the run, taken from two points that lie on the line of best fit and are chosen far apart — never from raw table points that may not sit on the line. The units of the gradient are the y-axis units divided by the x-axis units. If an axis is labelled with a ×10n scale factor (for example "distance (×10⁻² m)"), every value read from that axis must be multiplied by the factor before the gradient is calculated.
Key idea Linearisation. Transform the variable and its axis units together. To linearise s = ½at², plot s against t² and label the horizontal axis "time² (s²)"; the line is then straight, and its gradient is ½a, from which the acceleration is found. Transforming the value while leaving the axis units unchanged, or transforming the wrong variable, loses the marks.
Trap Line-of-best-fit and gradient errors. Do not draw the line through only the first and last points; do not force it through a chosen point or through the origin; do not extend it beyond the range of the data. When finding the gradient, do not use table points that are not on the line, do not read from points close together, do not ignore an axis ×10n factor, and do not state the gradient without units. Never adjust ("fudge") a gradient to match a known constant.
Trap Uncertainty-bar errors. A vertical uncertainty bar shows the uncertainty in the dependent variable; a horizontal bar shows the uncertainty in the independent variable. The true value is expected to lie within the bars, so the line of best fit should pass within the bars of the points. A line expected to pass through the origin that instead misses it — its intercept lies outside the uncertainty bar at the origin — indicates a systematic error.
Assessor's advice Reports consistently identify two faults: reading gradient values from the data table rather than from points on the line, and ignoring axis scale factors. Show the rise and run explicitly on the graph, from two clearly marked points on the line that are far apart, and convert any ×10n-scaled readings before dividing. Always quote the gradient with units equal to the y-axis units divided by the x-axis units.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A student releases a trolley from rest down a ramp and measures the distance s travelled in time t. The motion is expected to obey s = ½at². To obtain a straight line, the student plots s against t². On the resulting graph the vertical axis is labelled "distance s (×10⁻² m)" and the horizontal axis is labelled "time² (s²)". The line of best fit passes through the two points (0.10, 5.0) and (0.90, 45.0) read from the axes.

a. Calculate the gradient of the line, giving its value and units. (2 marks)

b. Determine the acceleration of the trolley. (2 marks)

c. The line of best fit passes slightly below the origin rather than through it, with the origin lying outside the uncertainty bar of the first point. State what this indicates about the measurements. (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. First convert the vertical readings using the ×10⁻² m scale factor: 5.0 → 5.0 × 10⁻² m and 45.0 → 45.0 × 10⁻² m. Gradient = riserun = (45.0 − 5.0) × 10⁻²0.90 − 0.10 = 40.0 × 10⁻²0.80 = 0.50 m s⁻² (2 marks). The units are the vertical-axis units (m) divided by the horizontal-axis units (s²), giving m s⁻².

1 mark: scale factor ×10⁻² applied and rise/run taken from the two points on the line · 1 mark: 0.50 m s⁻² with correct units.

Do not accept (a): 50 (scale factor ignored); a value without units of m s⁻².

b. The gradient of an s-versus-t² graph equals ½a, so a = 2 × gradient = 2 × 0.50 = 1.0 m s⁻² (2 marks).

1 mark: gradient identified as ½a · 1 mark: a = 1.0 m s⁻².

c. It indicates a systematic error: a relationship expected to pass through the origin that instead has a non-zero intercept outside the uncertainty bar shows that every reading is displaced consistently in one direction — for example a zero error or a consistent timing offset (1 mark).

1 mark: systematic error identified from the unexpected non-zero intercept.

Evidence evaluate evidence, state assumptions and limitations, draw a conclusion in the correct language, and communicate findings on a scientific poster

What you must be able to do

  • Discuss the nature of evidence that supports or refutes a hypothesis, model or theory, and state the assumptions and limitations of the data and the method.
  • Draw a conclusion using the language "supports", "partially supports" or "does not support" the hypothesis — never "proves" or "disproves".
  • Identify a limitation of an investigation and propose an improvement that addresses it specifically.
  • Apply the conventions of a scientific poster: the prescribed sections, succinct communication within 600 words, a central one-sentence Communication statement that answers the question, and the acknowledgement of references.

A conclusion states the extent to which the evidence supports the hypothesis and answers the investigation question; it introduces no new information. Scientific evidence can support or fail to support a claim, but a finite set of measurements can never prove a universal statement, so the conclusion language is restricted.

Key idea Conclusion language. State that the evidence "supports", "partially supports" or "does not support" the hypothesis. Do not write "proves" or "disproves": a hypothesis can be supported or refuted by evidence, but no finite experiment proves it absolutely. A conclusion answers the question, acknowledges the limitations of the evidence, may note implications or further work, and contains no information not already presented.
Key idea The poster. The investigation is reported as a scientific poster of no more than 600 words, with the prescribed sections: Title (the investigation question) · Student name · Introduction (aim, hypothesis or prediction, background physics) · Methodology and methods · Results (data shown to reveal trends, plus the central one-sentence Communication statement) · Discussion (interpret and evaluate the data, limitations and improvements, relate to the physics, link to the hypothesis) · Conclusion (answers the question, its limitations, implications or further work, no new information) · References and acknowledgements. A single-sentence Communication statement sits at the centre of the poster, occupies 20–25 per cent of the area, and answers the investigation question directly. References must be acknowledged.
Trap Writing "the experiment proves the hypothesis" or "disproves the relationship". The correct verbs are supports, partially supports and does not support. A second recurring error is confusing a limitation with a mistake: a limitation is an inherent constraint of the method or data (for example, friction was not measured, or only one trial was possible at each value), not a procedural slip; the proposed improvement must address the stated limitation directly rather than offering a generic "repeat the experiment".
Assessor's advice A strong evaluation names a specific assumption (for example, that air resistance was negligible) or limitation, explains how it affected the result, and proposes a targeted improvement. Generic statements such as "use more accurate equipment" or "be more careful" earn no marks. On the poster, the Communication statement must be a single sentence that answers the question; a statement that restates the aim, or that hedges without answering, does not meet the requirement.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A student hypothesised that the period of a pendulum is directly proportional to the square root of its length. After linearising and plotting the data, the line of best fit was straight and passed within the uncertainty bars of all points, but had a small positive vertical intercept rather than passing through the origin.

a. State an appropriate conclusion regarding the hypothesis, using correct scientific language. (2 marks)

b. Identify one limitation of the investigation suggested by the non-zero intercept, and propose an improvement that addresses it. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The straight line passing within all the uncertainty bars supports a linear relationship between the period and the square root of the length; however, the non-zero intercept means the data do not pass through the origin as a directly proportional relationship requires, so the evidence partially supports the hypothesis (2 marks).

1 mark: the linear trend is identified as evidence for the relationship · 1 mark: "partially supports" used, justified by the unexpected non-zero intercept. (Award zero for "proves" or "disproves".)

b. The non-zero intercept suggests a systematic error — a likely limitation is a consistent timing offset, for example a reaction-time delay in starting and stopping the stopwatch that adds the same amount to every period (1 mark). An improvement that addresses it is to time the period electronically using a photogate, removing the human reaction time and so the consistent offset (1 mark).

1 mark: a specific limitation consistent with a systematic offset · 1 mark: a targeted improvement that removes that offset (not a generic "repeat" or "be careful").
