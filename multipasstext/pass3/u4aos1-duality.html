Wave–particle duality

Each phenomenon is evidence: the two models each predict, and the observation decides. Light is both a wave and a particle; so is matter; the single-particle double slit shows both natures in one experiment. The table below sets every exam-relevant contention side by side, cross-referenced to the study-design key knowledge (KK 1.1–1.18). This page is designed to print to a single A4 landscape sheet.

Wave model of light

Transverse electromagnetic wave produced by accelerating charges: a changing electric field and its associated changing magnetic field regenerate each other, perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel; no medium; speed c. Described by λ, f (c = fλ), superposition.

Explains diffraction · interference · standing waves   Fails photoelectric effect · discrete spectra

Particle (photon) model · matter

Light is a stream of photons — indivisible quanta of energy E = hf = hc/λ and momentum p = h/λ; one photon is absorbed by one electron. Matter is classically particles (mass, p = mv) yet carries a de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p.

Explains photoelectric effect · line spectra · quantisation   Fails alone interference · diffraction

A · Light behaves as a WAVE — wave model succeeds, particle model fails
DiffractionKK 1.5 Spreads through a gap or around an obstacle; spread set by λ/w, growing as λ/w → 1. Straight lines, sharp shadow; does not bend into the geometric shadow. Light spreads into the shadow; more spread at larger λ/w (radio bends round a hill, light barely). WAVE
Young's double slitKK 1.6 Bright and dark fringes; constructive nλ, destructive (n+½)λ; Δx = λL/d. Two bright bands only (one per slit); light + light can never make darkness. Many evenly spaced bright and dark bands. WAVEDark bands = destructive superposition — particles cannot cancel.
Standing waves & superpositionKK 1.3–1.4 Wave + reflection superpose → fixed nodes (cancel) & antinodes (reinforce); λn = 2L/n. No node and antinode structure. Fixed node–antinode pattern (microwave hot spots, laser cavity, string harmonics). WAVE
B · Light behaves as a PARTICLE (photon) — photon model succeeds, wave model fails
Photoelectric: threshold frequencyKK 1.8–1.9 Any frequency works if bright enough or left on long enough (energy accumulates). Only if hf ≥ φ (f ≥ f0); below f0, nothing at any intensity. A threshold f0 exists; below it no emission, however bright or prolonged. PHOTON
Photoelectric: no time delayKK 1.9 Dim light → delay while spread-out energy accumulates to φ. One photon absorbed by one electron → emission is instant. Emission within nanoseconds even for very dim light. PHOTON
Photoelectric: Ek max set by f, not intensityKK 1.8–1.9 Brighter light → faster electrons; frequency irrelevant to the energy. Ek max = hf − φ: rises with f (gradient h), independent of intensity. Ek max ∝ f (line of gradient h, intercept −φ); intensity has no effect. PHOTON
Photoelectric: intensity sets count, not energyKK 1.8 More intensity → more energy per electron (Ek rises). At fixed f, intensity = photon rate → number of photoelectrons (current); Ek unchanged. Saturation current ∝ intensity; stopping voltage & Ek max unchanged. PHOTONConstant power, higher f → fewer photons → lower current.
Emission line spectraKK 1.13/1.15/1.17 Atoms radiate over a continuous range of wavelengths. Discrete states; each downward drop emits one photon E = ΔE = hf → only discrete energies. Discrete bright lines at wavelengths unique to each element. QUANTISED
Absorption line spectraKK 1.15/1.17 Uniform absorption across the continuous spectrum. Only photons matching a gap ΔE are absorbed, then re-emitted in all directions → that wavelength depleted. Discrete dark lines at the element's emission wavelengths. QUANTISEDOnly spectra require quantisation.
C · Matter behaves as a WAVE — here the “wave” column is the de Broglie matter-wave; the “particle” column is the classical particle
Electron diffractionKK 1.10–1.11 Electrons diffract as waves of λ = h/p when λ ≈ atomic spacing → ring pattern. Beam through a foil → single central patch, spread only by collisions. Concentric rings matching λ = h/p; rings shrink as accelerating voltage (so p) rises. MATTER WAVE
de Broglie wavelengthKK 1.12 Every particle has λ = h/p: cricket ball ≈ 10⁻³⁴ m (never diffracts); electron at 100s V ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ m (diffracts). A particle has no wavelength and never diffracts. Electrons diffract; macroscopic objects never do — as λ = h/p predicts. MATTER WAVEWhy duality is invisible day-to-day.
Photon vs electron at the same λKK 1.14 p = h/λ holds for both → equal λ ⇒ equal momentum ⇒ identical diffraction through the same crystal; but not equal energy (photon E = pc; electron Ek = p²/2m). Electron tuned to an X-ray's λ gives identical rings, yet the photon carries far more energy (≈ 600 eV vs ≈ 25 keV). SHARED pSame λ ⇒ same p, not same energy.
D · DUALITY — both natures in one experiment, for light and for matter — KK 1.18 · neither pure model alone is correct
Single-photon double slitKK 1.18 Smooth continuous distribution; no localised arrivals. Two bands behind the slits; no interference. Each photon arrives as a single dot (particle); over millions of one-at-a-time arrivals the dots build the interference pattern (wave). DUAL — LIGHT
Single-electron double slitKK 1.18 Smooth continuous distribution; no localised arrivals. Two bands behind the slits; no interference. Each electron arrives as a single dot; the dots build interference bands at the de Broglie λ, even with only one electron in the apparatus at a time. DUAL — MATTER
