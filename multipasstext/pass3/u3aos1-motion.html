Motion in two dimensions

Unit 3 Area of Study 1 is examined through extended calculation and short justification. The marks concentrate in a small number of well-rehearsed errors: treating the normal force on an incline as mg; inventing an outward force in circular motion; mishandling the sign of weight at the top and bottom of a vertical circle; leaving a satellite's altitude in kilometres or omitting the radius of the Earth; and assuming kinetic energy is conserved in every collision. Each section below states the skill, sets out the fine detail, and rehearses the corrections in the examiner's own framing.

Newton 2D apply Newton's three laws of motion to two or more coplanar forces acting along a line and in two dimensions, including on an inclined plane

What you must be able to do

  • Draw a labelled free-body diagram showing every real force acting on the object and nothing else.
  • Resolve forces into perpendicular components and apply Fnet = ma independently along each axis.
  • On an inclined plane, resolve parallel and perpendicular to the slope, not horizontally and vertically.
  • State which of Newton's three laws justifies a given conclusion.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • For a block of mass m on a frictionless incline of angle θ, the weight mg resolves into a component mg sinθ down the slope and a component mg cosθ into the slope. The normal force balances only the perpendicular component, so N = mg cosθ, and the acceleration along the slope is a = g sinθ.
  • The normal force is a response force. It takes whatever value is required to give zero acceleration perpendicular to the surface; it equals mg only when the surface is horizontal and no other vertical force acts.
  • When friction acts, the friction force lies along the surface, opposing relative motion or its tendency, with magnitude up to μN. Because N = mg cosθ on an incline, the maximum friction depends on cosθ, not on mg alone.
  • Newton's third-law pairs act on different objects and are never combined in a single free-body diagram. The normal force on the block and the block's push on the surface are a third-law pair; the normal force and the weight are not.
Key idea On an inclined plane choose axes parallel and perpendicular to the slope. The normal force is N = mg cosθ; the net force along the slope is mg sinθ minus any up-slope friction.
Trap Writing N = mg on an incline. This is correct only for a horizontal surface. The perpendicular component of the weight is mg cosθ, and the normal force matches that component, not the full weight.
Assessor's advice Examination reports repeatedly note that students "did not resolve the weight force into components" and instead used the full weight where a component was required. A clearly labelled free-body diagram with the chosen axes drawn on the slope earns method marks even when the arithmetic slips, and prevents the cosθ/sinθ confusion that costs most of the marks here.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A 4.0 kg block is released from rest on a frictionless ramp inclined at 30° to the horizontal. Take g = 9.8 N kg⁻¹.

a. Calculate the magnitude of the normal force acting on the block. (2 marks)

b. Calculate the acceleration of the block down the ramp. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The normal force balances the perpendicular component of the weight: N = mg cosθ = 4.0 × 9.8 × cos30° = 33.9, so N ≈ 34 N. (1 mark for N = mg cosθ; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: N = mg cosθ, not mg.

1 mark: N ≈ 34 N.

b. Along the slope, ma = mg sinθ, so a = g sinθ = 9.8 × sin30° = 4.9 m s⁻² directed down the ramp. (1 mark for a = g sinθ; 1 mark for the value with direction.)

1 mark: resolves along the slope, a = g sinθ.

1 mark: a = 4.9 m s⁻² down the ramp.

Projectile investigate and analyse projectile motion near Earth's surface, treating horizontal and vertical motion independently, with a qualitative description of the effect of air resistance

What you must be able to do

  • Separate a projectile's velocity into horizontal and vertical components and treat each motion independently.
  • Apply the constant-acceleration equations vertically with a = g downward, and constant velocity horizontally.
  • Determine launch speed, time of flight, maximum height, range and impact velocity.
  • Describe qualitatively how air resistance changes the trajectory, the maximum height and the range.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • After launch, gravity is the only force on a projectile (air resistance neglected). The acceleration is therefore constant: g downward, throughout the flight, including at the highest point where the vertical velocity is momentarily zero.
  • A ball moving upward still has a downward force on it. The force does not reverse at the top; only the vertical velocity changes sign.
  • The horizontal component of velocity is constant because no horizontal force acts. The two motions share only one quantity: the time of flight.
  • Choose a positive direction (up = + or up = −) and apply it consistently to displacement, velocity and acceleration. Mixing conventions is the commonest source of sign errors.
  • At the highest point the vertical velocity is zero but the speed is not zero: the horizontal component remains. The acceleration there is still g downward.
  • Air resistance opposes the motion at every instant. It reduces the maximum height and the range, makes the descent steeper than the ascent, and means the impact speed is less than the launch speed. The trajectory is no longer a symmetric parabola.
Key idea Resolve at launch, solve the vertical motion for the time of flight, then use that time in the horizontal motion. Acceleration is g downward at every point, including the top.
Trap Claiming the acceleration, or the net force, is zero at the highest point. The vertical velocity is zero there for an instant; the acceleration remains 9.8 m s⁻² downward because gravity still acts.
Assessor's advice Reports note that many students believe a projectile experiences "no force" or "zero acceleration" at the top of its path, and that air-resistance answers are too often left as "it slows the ball" without stating the effect on height and range. State explicitly that air resistance acts opposite to the velocity and reduces both the maximum height and the horizontal range.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A ball is launched from ground level at 25 m s⁻¹ at 37° above the horizontal. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻² and ignore air resistance. (sin37° = 0.60, cos37° = 0.80.)

a. Calculate the horizontal and vertical components of the launch velocity. (2 marks)

b. Calculate the maximum height reached. (2 marks)

c. State the magnitude and direction of the ball's acceleration at the highest point, and justify your answer. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. ux = 25 cos37° = 25 × 0.80 = 20 m s⁻¹; uy = 25 sin37° = 25 × 0.60 = 15 m s⁻¹. (1 mark each.)

1 mark: ux = 20 m s⁻¹. 1 mark: uy = 15 m s⁻¹.

b. Take up as positive. At maximum height vy = 0. Using vy² = uy² − 2gh: 0 = 15² − 2 × 9.8 × h, so h = 225 ⁄ 19.6 = 11.5, giving h ≈ 11 m. (1 mark for method; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: uses vy = 0 at the top with the vertical equation. 1 mark: h ≈ 11 m.

c. The acceleration is 9.8 m s⁻² directed vertically downward. At the highest point gravity is still the only force acting, so the acceleration is unchanged; only the vertical velocity is momentarily zero. (1 mark for the value and direction; 1 mark for the justification.)

1 mark: 9.8 m s⁻² downward. 1 mark: gravity is the only force throughout, so a is constant.

Circular analyse uniform circular motion in a horizontal plane, using Fnet = mv²⁄r, for a vehicle on a circular road and an object on a string

What you must be able to do

  • State that the net force in uniform circular motion is directed toward the centre and has magnitude Fnet = mv²⁄r.
  • Identify which real force provides the centripetal force in a given situation.
  • Calculate the required net force, the speed or the radius for horizontal circular motion.
  • State that the velocity is tangential and the acceleration is radial.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • "Centripetal" names a direction, not a new kind of force. The centripetal force is always supplied by a real force already in the free-body diagram. For a car on a flat road it is friction between the tyres and the road; for an object whirled on a horizontal string it is the horizontal component of the tension.
  • There is no outward "centrifugal" force in an inertial frame. The sensation of being thrown outward is the body's inertia; the only horizontal force on the object points inward, toward the centre.
  • The velocity is tangential to the circle and the acceleration is radial — perpendicular to the velocity, directed at the centre. The two are at 90° at every instant.
  • Because the speed is constant, the centripetal force changes only the direction of the velocity, not its magnitude. A force perpendicular to the displacement does no work (see the Energy section), so no work is done in uniform circular motion.
  • For an object on a string swung in a horizontal circle (a conical pendulum), the string is not horizontal: the vertical component of the tension balances the weight (T cosθ = mg) and the horizontal component supplies the centripetal force (T sinθ = mv²⁄r).
Key idea Fnet = mv²⁄r is not an extra force to add. It is the value the real inward force must take. Always name the real force that provides it.
Trap Adding an outward "centrifugal force" to the free-body diagram, or drawing the net force tangentially. The net force in uniform circular motion points to the centre; the velocity points along the tangent.
Assessor's advice Reports consistently warn that "students should not refer to a centrifugal force" and that marks are lost when free-body diagrams include an outward arrow. When a question asks for the force providing the centripetal force, name the specific real force (friction, tension component, normal-force component) rather than writing "centripetal force".
In the style of VCAA · MC

A car travels at constant speed around a flat circular bend. Which row correctly describes the direction of the car's velocity and the direction of the net force on it? (1 mark)

  1. Velocity toward the centre; net force tangential.
  2. Velocity tangential; net force directed outward, away from the centre.
  3. Velocity tangential; net force directed toward the centre, provided by friction.
  4. Velocity tangential; net force zero, because the speed is constant.
Worked solution

C. In uniform circular motion the velocity is tangential and the net force points to the centre, supplied here by the friction of the tyres on the road. (1 mark.)

Why each distractor is wrong: A swaps the two directions. B invents an outward centrifugal force — no such real force acts. D confuses constant speed with zero acceleration; the direction of the velocity is changing, so a non-zero net force is required.

Banked analyse uniform circular motion of a vehicle on a banked track, where the horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force at the design speed

What you must be able to do

  • Draw a free-body diagram for a vehicle on a banked track showing the normal force and the weight.
  • Resolve the normal force into horizontal and vertical components.
  • Apply N sinθ = mv²⁄r horizontally and N cosθ = mg vertically at the design speed.
  • Derive and use v = √(rg tanθ) for the design speed at which no friction is required.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • At the design speed no friction acts. The horizontal component of the normal force alone provides the centripetal force, and the vertical component balances the weight.
  • Dividing the horizontal equation by the vertical equation: N sinθN cosθ = mv²⁄rmg, so tanθ = v²⁄(rg). The centripetal acceleration at the design speed is therefore ac = g tanθ and the design speed is v = √(rg tanθ).
  • The mass cancels: the design speed depends only on the radius, the bank angle and g, not on the vehicle's mass.
  • The normal force on a banked track is larger than mg, because N = mg⁄cosθ. This is the opposite of the incline case (N = mg cosθ), and the two must not be confused.
  • Above the design speed the vehicle tends to slide up and out, so friction acts down the slope; below it the vehicle tends to slide down and in, so friction acts up the slope. The design speed is the single speed at which friction is not needed at all.
Key idea At the design speed: N sinθ = mv²⁄r and N cosθ = mg, giving v = √(rg tanθ). No friction is required.
Trap Resolving the weight instead of the normal force, or using N = mg cosθ as on an incline. On a banked track the normal force is resolved, the weight stays vertical, and N cosθ = mg gives N = mg⁄cosθ > mg.
Assessor's advice Reports note that better responses "resolved the normal reaction force into horizontal and vertical components" and that weaker ones resolved the weight or assumed friction. State explicitly that at the design speed friction is zero, then write the two component equations before substituting. The phrase "the horizontal component of the normal force provides the centripetal force" is worth stating in any justification.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A circular test track of radius 80 m is banked at 25° to the horizontal. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻² and tan25° = 0.466.

a. Show that, at the design speed, the centripetal acceleration is ac = g tanθ. (2 marks)

b. Calculate the design speed of the track. (2 marks)

c. Explain why the design speed does not depend on the mass of the vehicle. (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. At the design speed friction is zero, so vertically N cosθ = mg and horizontally N sinθ = mv²⁄r = mac. Dividing the second equation by the first: tanθ = ac⁄g, hence ac = g tanθ. (1 mark for the two component equations; 1 mark for the division.)

1 mark: N cosθ = mg and N sinθ = mv²⁄r. 1 mark: divides to eliminate N.

b. v = √(rg tanθ) = √(80 × 9.8 × 0.466) = √365.3 = 19.1, so v ≈ 19 m s⁻¹. (1 mark for the formula; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: v = √(rg tanθ). 1 mark: v ≈ 19 m s⁻¹.

c. The mass m appears in both the centripetal-force term mv²⁄r and the weight term mg, so it cancels when the equations are divided. The design speed depends only on r, θ and g. (1 mark.)

1 mark: m cancels, leaving v = √(rg tanθ).

Vertical apply Newton's second law to circular motion in a vertical plane, considering the forces at the highest and lowest points only

What you must be able to do

  • Identify the forces acting at the highest and lowest points of a vertical circle.
  • Write Fnet = mv²⁄r taking toward the centre as positive at each point.
  • Determine the tension or normal force at the top and bottom for a string, a track or a vehicle.
  • Find the minimum speed at the top, where the tension or normal force falls to zero.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • At the highest point both the weight and the inward tension or normal force point toward the centre (downward), so Fnet = T + mg = mv²⁄r.
  • At the lowest point the tension or normal force points toward the centre (upward) while the weight points away (downward), so Fnet = T − mg = mv²⁄r, giving the largest tension of the cycle.
  • The minimum speed at the top is the speed at which the tension just reaches zero. Then gravity alone supplies the centripetal force: mg = mv²⁄r, so vmin = √(gr). Below this speed the object cannot maintain contact or string tension and leaves the circular path.
  • For a vehicle over a crest, the centre is below, so mg − N = mv²⁄r and N = m(g − v²⁄r); the apparent weight is reduced, reaching zero when v = √(gr). In a dip the centre is above, so N − mg = mv²⁄r and N = m(g + v²⁄r); the apparent weight is increased.
  • The sign of the weight term is the whole game. It is added at the top and subtracted at the bottom because "toward the centre" is downward at the top and upward at the bottom.
Key idea Top: T + mg = mv²⁄r. Bottom: T − mg = mv²⁄r. Minimum speed at the top: vmin = √(gr), where the tension is zero.
Trap Using the same sign for the weight at the top and the bottom. At the top the weight helps provide the centripetal force (added); at the bottom it opposes it (subtracted). Decide which way "toward the centre" points before writing the equation.
Assessor's advice Reports note that the minimum-speed-at-the-top question is frequently misread: the condition is "tension equals zero", not "speed equals zero". Set the contact or string force to zero, equate gravity to mv²⁄r, and obtain v = √(gr). Defining "positive toward the centre" at the start of the working prevents the sign errors that lose method marks.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A 0.50 kg ball is attached to a string of length 1.2 m and swung in a vertical circle. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻².

a. Calculate the minimum speed the ball must have at the highest point to keep the string taut. (2 marks)

b. At the lowest point the ball moves at 6.0 m s⁻¹. Calculate the tension in the string at that point. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. At the minimum speed the tension is zero and gravity alone provides the centripetal force: mg = mv²⁄r, so v = √(gr) = √(9.8 × 1.2) = √11.76 = 3.4 m s⁻¹. (1 mark for the zero-tension condition; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: tension zero, so mg = mv²⁄r. 1 mark: vmin = 3.4 m s⁻¹.

b. Taking up (toward the centre at the bottom) as positive: T − mg = mv²⁄r, so T = m(g + v²⁄r) = 0.50 × (9.8 + 6.0²⁄1.2) = 0.50 × (9.8 + 30) = 0.50 × 39.8 = 19.9, giving T ≈ 20 N. (1 mark for the correct sign of the weight; 1 mark for substitution; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: T − mg = mv²⁄r (weight subtracted at the bottom). 1 mark: correct substitution. 1 mark: T ≈ 20 N.

Satellite model natural and artificial satellite motion as uniform circular motion, using a kinematic treatment only

What you must be able to do

  • Model a satellite's orbit as uniform circular motion, with the net force directed toward the centre of the orbit.
  • Use the orbital radius r = REarth + altitude, in metres.
  • Apply Fnet = mv²⁄r to relate the satellite's speed, period and orbital radius.
  • Explain, using Newton's second law, why a satellite in a circular orbit is accelerating.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • In this Area of Study the treatment is kinematic only: the satellite is modelled as an object in uniform circular motion. The gravitational source of the centripetal force, and the orbital relationships derived from F = GMm⁄r², belong to Unit 3 Area of Study 2 (Fields). Cross-link to the gravitation treatment of orbits for the field-based derivation.
  • The orbital radius is measured from the centre of the Earth, not from its surface: r = REarth + altitude. Both terms must be in metres before substitution.
  • The orbit is independent of the satellite's mass: the mass cancels from the circular-motion equation just as it does on the banked track. Two satellites at the same altitude orbit with the same speed and period regardless of their masses.
  • A satellite in a circular orbit moves at constant speed but is accelerating, because the direction of its velocity is continually changing. The acceleration is centripetal, directed toward the centre of the Earth.
  • The correct justification for the acceleration is Newton's second law: an unbalanced force (gravity) acts on the satellite and produces an acceleration toward the centre. It is not Newton's first law (which describes the absence of a net force) nor the third law (which concerns the equal and opposite reaction on the Earth).
Key idea Treat a satellite as an object in uniform circular motion with r = REarth + altitude in metres. The orbit is independent of the satellite's mass. The acceleration is explained by Newton's second law.
Trap Leaving the altitude in kilometres, or using the altitude as the orbital radius without adding REarth. The orbital radius is measured from the centre of the Earth and must be in metres.
Assessor's advice Reports record that the "single most common error" in satellite questions is failing to add the Earth's radius or to convert the altitude to metres. The conceptual companion error is attributing a satellite's acceleration to the wrong law: a satellite accelerates because of an unbalanced force, which is Newton's second law, not the first. Both are reliable mark-losers and both are easily avoided.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A satellite moves at constant speed in a circular orbit around the Earth. A student is asked which of Newton's laws best explains why the satellite is accelerating. The correct answer is: (1 mark)

  1. Newton's first law, because the satellite continues at constant speed.
  2. Newton's second law, because an unbalanced force (gravity) produces an acceleration directed toward the centre of the orbit.
  3. Newton's third law, because the satellite pulls on the Earth as the Earth pulls on the satellite.
  4. None — an object moving at constant speed has zero acceleration.
Worked solution

B. The velocity changes direction continually, so the satellite is accelerating toward the centre. By Newton's second law this acceleration is caused by the unbalanced gravitational force. (1 mark.)

Why each distractor is wrong: A describes constant speed but a satellite is not in equilibrium, so the first law does not apply. C states a true third-law pair but the reaction force acts on the Earth, not on the satellite, so it does not explain the satellite's acceleration. D confuses constant speed with constant velocity; the direction is changing, so the acceleration is non-zero.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

A satellite orbits the Earth at an altitude of 800 km. The radius of the Earth is 6.37 × 10⁶ m and the satellite's orbital speed is 7.45 × 10³ m s⁻¹.

a. State the orbital radius of the satellite, in metres. (1 mark)

b. Calculate the magnitude of the satellite's centripetal acceleration. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. r = REarth + altitude = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 8.00 × 10⁵ = 7.17 × 10⁶ m. (1 mark — altitude converted to metres and added to the Earth's radius.)

1 mark: r = 7.17 × 10⁶ m.

b. a = v²⁄r = (7.45 × 10³)²⁄(7.17 × 10⁶) = 5.55 × 10⁷ ⁄ 7.17 × 10⁶ = 7.74, so a ≈ 7.7 m s⁻² directed toward the centre of the Earth. (1 mark for a = v²⁄r; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: a = v²⁄r. 1 mark: a ≈ 7.7 m s⁻² toward the Earth's centre.

Momentum apply conservation of momentum and the impulse relationship FΔt = mΔv to one-dimensional collisions

What you must be able to do

  • Apply conservation of momentum to an isolated system in one dimension, assigning + and − to opposite directions consistently.
  • Use the impulse relationship FΔt = mΔv = Δp for a straight-line collision.
  • Determine impulse as the area under a force–time graph.
  • Relate the average force, the contact time and the change in momentum.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • Momentum is a vector. In one dimension this is handled by choosing a positive direction and giving velocities in the opposite direction a negative sign. A common error is to add momenta as if they were always positive.
  • Impulse equals the change in momentum: FΔt = mΔv = mv − mu. Because Δv is a vector change, a ball that rebounds undergoes a larger change in momentum than one that stops, even at the same speed.
  • The impulse delivered by a varying force is the area under the force–time graph. For a graph made of straight segments, compute the area geometrically (rectangles and triangles).
  • For a fixed change in momentum, a longer contact time gives a smaller average force (F = Δp⁄Δt). This is the principle behind crumple zones and follow-through.
  • Momentum is conserved only for an isolated system — one with no net external force. Internal forces between the colliding objects are equal and opposite (Newton's third law) and cancel in the total.
Key idea FΔt = mΔv = Δp. Impulse is the change in momentum and equals the area under a force–time graph. Treat momentum as a vector: assign signs to directions.
Trap Ignoring the vector nature of momentum. For a ball that rebounds, Δv = v − u uses opposite signs for v and u, so the change in momentum is the sum of the speeds times the mass, not the difference.
Assessor's advice Reports note that rebound impulse questions are routinely under-answered because students take Δv as the difference of the speeds rather than the difference of the signed velocities. Always write a positive-direction convention beside the diagram, then substitute signed values. For graph questions, state that impulse is the area under the force–time graph before computing it.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

During a collision, the force on a 0.16 kg ball varies with time as shown in a force–time graph. The force rises linearly from 0 to 400 N over 5.0 ms, then falls linearly back to 0 over a further 5.0 ms. The ball is initially moving at 15 m s⁻¹ toward a wall.

a. Determine the impulse delivered to the ball by the wall. (2 marks)

b. Hence calculate the speed of the ball immediately after the collision, and state its direction. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The impulse is the area under the force–time graph, a triangle of base 10 ms = 0.010 s and height 400 N: J = ½ × 0.010 × 400 = 2.0 N s, directed away from the wall. (1 mark for "area under the graph"; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: impulse = area = ½ × base × height. 1 mark: J = 2.0 N s.

b. Take the initial direction (toward the wall) as positive, so u = +15 m s⁻¹ and the impulse is negative: J = mΔv, so −2.0 = 0.16(v − 15), giving v − 15 = −12.5, v = +2.5 m s⁻¹. The ball moves at 2.5 m s⁻¹ still toward the wall — its speed is reduced but not reversed. (1 mark for signed substitution; 1 mark for the value and direction.)

1 mark: J = mΔv with consistent signs. 1 mark: v = 2.5 m s⁻¹ toward the wall.

↑ Pass 1 · Momentum and impulse ● Pass 2 · Impulse and force–time graphs
Energy analyse energy transformations using Ek = ½mv², Es = ½kx² and Eg = mgΔh, distinguishing elastic from inelastic collisions and accounting for dissipated energy

What you must be able to do

  • Apply conservation of energy to isolated systems, transforming among kinetic, elastic potential and gravitational potential energy.
  • Classify a collision as elastic or inelastic by comparing total kinetic energy before and after.
  • Calculate work as W = Fs and as the area under a force–distance graph.
  • Account for energy dissipated to the environment as heat, sound and deformation.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • Momentum is conserved in every collision in an isolated system. Kinetic energy is conserved only in an elastic collision. If Ek after is less than Ek before, the collision is inelastic and the missing energy is dissipated as heat, sound and permanent deformation.
  • The energy stored in an ideal spring is Es = ½kx², which is quadratic in the extension. Doubling the extension quadruples the stored energy. This is also the area under the force–extension graph (a triangle, since F = kx).
  • Gravitational potential energy Eg = mgΔh applies in a uniform field, that is, near the Earth's surface. Over large altitude changes the field is not uniform and this expression is not valid; the field-based treatment is in the Fields material.
  • Work is the area under a force–distance graph. For a constant force this is W = Fs; for a variable force, such as a spring, compute the area geometrically.
  • A force perpendicular to the displacement does zero work. The centripetal force in uniform circular motion is perpendicular to the velocity at every instant, so it does no work and the speed stays constant. This links directly to the Circular and Satellite sections.
Key idea Momentum is conserved in all collisions; kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic ones. Es = ½kx² is quadratic in x. A force perpendicular to the displacement does no work.
Trap Assuming kinetic energy is conserved in a collision. Conservation of momentum holds in every collision; conservation of kinetic energy holds only when the collision is elastic. Always compare Ek before and after to classify it.
Assessor's advice Reports note two recurring errors: treating Es = ½kx² as linear in x (so doubling the extension is wrongly said to double the energy), and stating that a collision conserves kinetic energy without checking. For dissipation questions, name the destinations of the lost energy — heat, sound and deformation — rather than writing only "energy is lost".
In the style of VCAA · Section B

A 2.0 kg trolley moving at 3.0 m s⁻¹ collides head-on with a stationary 1.0 kg trolley. After the collision the two trolleys move together as one.

a. Calculate the common velocity of the trolleys after the collision. (2 marks)

b. By comparing the total kinetic energy before and after, classify the collision and state what happens to any missing energy. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. Conservation of momentum: m1u1 = (m1 + m2)v, so 2.0 × 3.0 = 3.0 × v, giving v = 2.0 m s⁻¹ in the original direction. (1 mark for conservation of momentum; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: pbefore = pafter. 1 mark: v = 2.0 m s⁻¹.

b. Before: Ek = ½ × 2.0 × 3.0² = 9.0 J. After: Ek = ½ × 3.0 × 2.0² = 6.0 J. Since Ek after (6.0 J) is less than Ek before (9.0 J), the collision is inelastic. The 3.0 J of missing kinetic energy is dissipated as heat, sound and deformation. (1 mark for each Ek value; 1 mark for the classification with the energy destination.)

1 mark: Ek,before = 9.0 J, Ek,after = 6.0 J. 1 mark: inelastic. 1 mark: 3.0 J to heat, sound and deformation.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

An ideal spring obeys Hooke's law with spring constant k = 200 N m⁻¹.

a. Calculate the elastic potential energy stored when the spring is extended by 0.10 m. (2 marks)

b. The extension is doubled to 0.20 m. State the new stored energy and explain why it is not simply double the answer to part a. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Es = ½kx² = ½ × 200 × 0.10² = ½ × 200 × 0.010 = 1.0 J. (1 mark for the formula; 1 mark for the value.)

1 mark: Es = ½kx². 1 mark: Es = 1.0 J.

b. Es = ½ × 200 × 0.20² = ½ × 200 × 0.040 = 4.0 J. The stored energy is quadratic in the extension, so doubling x multiplies the energy by 2² = 4, not by 2. (1 mark for the value; 1 mark for the quadratic justification.)

1 mark: Es = 4.0 J. 1 mark: energy scales as x², so it quadruples.

VCAA 2024 · Section A · Q1

Jo is standing inside a lift that is travelling vertically upwards at a constant speed of 2.0 m s⁻¹ as shown. Jo has a mass of 75 kg.

Which one of the following is closest to the force exerted on Jo by the lift floor?

  1. 0 N
  2. 590 N
  3. 740 N
  4. 890 N
Worked solution

C. At constant velocity the acceleration is zero, so the net force on Jo is zero. The floor's normal force must therefore balance her weight exactly: N = mg = 75 × 9.8 = 735 N, closest to 740 N.

Why the other options are wrong: A (0 N) treats Jo as weightless, which only happens in free fall. B (590 N) understates the weight. D (890 N) adds an extra upward ma term — but at constant speed there is no acceleration, so no such term exists.

The diagram below shows a small ball travelling at a constant speed on a horizontal circular path while suspended from the ceiling by a string.

Which one of the following best represents the force(s) acting on the ball as seen from a front-on view?

The four options A–D are force diagrams acting on the ball (see the options figure above).

Worked solution

A. Only two real forces act on the ball: its weight, straight down, and the tension along the string, directed up toward the suspension point. Their vector sum is horizontal and points to the centre of the circle, supplying the centripetal force. Diagram A shows exactly these two forces.

VCAA 2024 · Section A · Q4

The diagram below shows a 50 g ball and a spring inside a horizontal launching tube. Assume that the launching tube is frictionless and the mass of the spring is negligible.

A force vs compression graph for the spring is shown below. Consider the spring to be ideal.

The spring is initially compressed by a distance of 10.0 cm, with the ball in contact with the compressed spring. A short time later the spring is released.

Which one of the following is closest to the speed of the ball as it leaves the tube?

  1. 2.8 m s⁻¹
  2. 4.9 m s⁻¹
  3. 8.9 m s⁻¹
  4. 12.6 m s⁻¹
Worked solution

C. The energy stored in the spring is the area under the force–compression graph, a triangle: ½ × 0.100 m × 40 N = 2.0 J. On the frictionless track this all becomes kinetic energy: ½ × 0.050 × v² = 2.0, so v = √80 = 8.9 m s⁻¹.

Why the other options are wrong: D (12.6) uses the full rectangle 40 × 0.100 = 4.0 J instead of the triangular area. A and B follow from an incorrect energy or a wrong mass.

VCAA 2024 · Section B · Q1

Tug boats often have to tow two larger boats around the Port of Melbourne. Boat 1, a tug boat, with a mass of 2.5 × 10⁵ kg, is towing two larger boats, as shown in Figure 1.

All three boats are moving in a straight line and Boats 2 and 3 do not have their engines on. Boat 2 has a mass of 5.0 × 10⁵ kg and Boat 3 has a mass of 6.0 × 10⁵ kg.

A resistance force of 1.2 × 10⁴ N acts on Boat 2 and a resistance force of 1.4 × 10⁴ N acts on Boat 3.

The tension in Rope 1 is 9.2 × 10⁴ N.

In Figure 1, Rope 1 connects Boat 1 to Boat 2, and Rope 2 (tension T) connects Boat 2 to Boat 3.

a. Show that the acceleration of the two boats being towed, Boat 2 and Boat 3, is 6.0 × 10⁻² m s⁻². (1 mark)

b. Calculate the tension, T, in Rope 2. Show your working. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Treat Boats 2 and 3 as one system. The forward pull is the Rope 1 tension; the two resistances oppose it: Fnet = 9.2×10⁴ − 1.2×10⁴ − 1.4×10⁴ = 6.6×10⁴ N. The towed mass is 5.0×10⁵ + 6.0×10⁵ = 1.1×10⁶ kg, so a = 6.6×10⁴ ÷ 1.1×10⁶ = 6.0×10⁻² m s⁻².

1 mark: correct net force and total mass giving a = 6.0×10⁻² m s⁻².

b. Apply Newton's second law to Boat 3 alone. The only forward force is the Rope 2 tension T; its resistance opposes it: T − 1.4×10⁴ = 6.0×10⁵ × 6.0×10⁻², so T = 3.6×10⁴ + 1.4×10⁴ = 5.0×10⁴ N.

1 mark: applies Fnet = ma to Boat 3 with its own resistance.

1 mark: T = 5.0×10⁴ N.

VCAA 2024 · Section B · Q2

Engineers are testing a new high-speed electric vehicle (EV) on a high-speed test track. In one test, the EV travels around a circular banked track with a radius of 1.2 km. The angle of bank is 33°. This is shown in Figure 2.

a. Calculate the maximum speed the car can travel around this banked track if there is no sideways friction between the wheels and the track. Show your working. (3 marks)

b. Explain how the normal force from the banked track provides the centripetal force on the car. (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. With no sideways friction the horizontal component of the normal force supplies the centripetal force: N sinθ = mv²⁄r and N cosθ = mg. Dividing eliminates N and m: tanθ = v²⁄(rg), so v = √(rg tanθ). With r = 1200 m and θ = 33°: v = √(1200 × 9.8 × tan33°) = √7640 ≈ 87 m s⁻¹.

1 mark: resolves the normal force (N sinθ horizontal, N cosθ vertical).

1 mark: v = √(rg tanθ).

1 mark: v ≈ 87 m s⁻¹.

b. The track surface is tilted, so the normal force (always perpendicular to the surface) tilts inward. Its horizontal component points toward the centre of the circle and, at the design speed, is exactly the centripetal force required; its vertical component balances the weight.

1 mark: identifies the horizontal component of the tilted normal force as the centripetal force.

VCAA 2024 · Section B · Q3

A golf ball near the edge of a cliff is hit at a speed of 48 m s⁻¹ and at an angle of 35° to the horizontal, as shown in Figure 3. The golf ball lands on the ground 6.2 s after it is hit. Ignore air resistance.

a. Determine the height, h, of the cliff. (3 marks)

b. Calculate the horizontal distance, x, that the golf ball travels before it reaches the ground below. (2 marks)

c. Describe how, in real life, air resistance would affect the golf ball's flight path. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Resolve the launch velocity: uy = 48 sin35° = 27.5 m s⁻¹ upward. Taking up as positive over t = 6.2 s: Δy = uy t − ½gt² = 27.5 × 6.2 − ½ × 9.8 × 6.2² = 170.7 − 188.4 = −17.7 m. The ball finishes 17.7 m below the launch point, so the cliff is about 18 m high.

1 mark: uy = 27.5 m s⁻¹.

1 mark: uses Δy = uy t − ½gt² with t = 6.2 s.

1 mark: h ≈ 18 m.

b. Horizontal motion is at constant velocity: ux = 48 cos35° = 39.3 m s⁻¹. Then x = ux t = 39.3 × 6.2 ≈ 2.4×10² m (about 240 m).

1 mark: ux = 39.3 m s⁻¹.

1 mark: x ≈ 240 m.

c. Air resistance acts opposite to the velocity throughout the flight, reducing both the horizontal and vertical speeds. The ball does not travel as far, reaches a lower maximum height, and the path becomes asymmetric — steeper on the way down than on the way up — rather than a symmetric parabola.

1 mark: air resistance opposes the motion, reducing the range (and height).

1 mark: the trajectory becomes asymmetric (steeper descent), not a symmetric parabola.

VCAA 2024 · Section B · Q5

A basketball of mass 0.63 kg, travelling vertically down, hits the floor at a speed of 12 m s⁻¹ and rebounds at a speed of 10 m s⁻¹, as shown in Figure 7.

a. Calculate the magnitude and direction of the change in momentum of the basketball. (2 marks)

b. Is the collision elastic or inelastic? Justify your answer. No calculations are required. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Take up as positive, so the ball arrives at −12 m s⁻¹ and leaves at +10 m s⁻¹. Δp = m(vf − vi) = 0.63 × (10 − (−12)) = 0.63 × 22 = 14 kg m s⁻¹, directed upward (away from the floor).

1 mark: Δp = m(vf − vi), treating the rebound velocity as opposite in sign.

1 mark: 14 kg m s⁻¹ upward.

b. Inelastic. The ball leaves the floor more slowly than it arrived (10 m s⁻¹ versus 12 m s⁻¹), so its kinetic energy after the bounce is less than before. Kinetic energy is not conserved, which is the signature of an inelastic collision.

1 mark: identifies the collision as inelastic.

1 mark: justifies it by the loss of kinetic energy (speed drops from 12 to 10 m s⁻¹).
