Light as a particle · matter as particles or waves

Dot points KK 1.7–1.12: photon energy, the photoelectric effect and its two graphs, the wave model's failure, electron diffraction and the de Broglie wavelength. Photoelectric graph reading carries the most marks in this area of study — it anchored the 20-mark experimental question in 2024 and returned as a compact three-mark version in 2025 — and the de Broglie chain has appeared on every recent exam, entered at a different link each year. Nearly every formula here is on the sheet, so the marks reward unit discipline (joule versus electron-volt) and explanations that name the mechanism.

KK 1.7 apply the quantised energy of photons: E = hf = hcλ

What you must be able to do

  • Calculate the energy of a photon from its frequency or wavelength with E = hf = hc/λ on formula sheet, in joules or in electron-volts.
  • Convert between joule and electron-volt in both directions: multiply by 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ for eV → J, divide for J → eV.
  • Determine how many photons a source of known power emits each second, by dividing the power by the energy of one photon not on sheet.

The formula sheet prints Planck's constant twice — h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s and h = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s — and the two values are not interchangeable. The choice is dictated by the unit of the answer: J s delivers joules, eV s delivers electron-volts, in either form of the formula. Answer boxes name the required unit, and recent boxes have demanded eV, eV s and nm; an answer in the wrong unit does not earn the mark.

A photon is an indivisible packet of energy. The power of a source equals the number of photons emitted per second multiplied by hf, so at fixed power a higher frequency means fewer photons per second — fewer, more energetic packets. This relation determines the photocurrent questions of KK 1.8.

Trap The wrong Planck's constant. Examiners reported on consecutive photon and de Broglie calculations that "the most common error … was to use the wrong Planck's constant". Before substituting, write down the unit the answer must carry, then pick the matching h — and convert nm to m on its own working line, not in passing.
Assessor's advice On the 2025 photon-wavelength question, "the most common error was demonstrating a correct substitution but incorrectly converting the answer to nanometres." The physics mark and the conversion mark are separate; treat the final unit change as a step that can be checked, not an afterthought.
Connects Every line in an atomic spectrum is priced by this same formula: in Atoms and spectra the photon energy E = hf must match a gap between energy levels — the arithmetic learned here is reused unchanged on the next page, Light–matter similarities.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A laser pointer used in a lecture theatre emits red light of wavelength 650 nm at a power of 5.0 mW. Which one of the following is closest to the number of photons the laser emits each second? (1 mark)

  1. 3.1 × 10⁻¹⁹
  2. 2.6 × 10⁻³
  3. 1.6 × 10¹⁶
  4. 1.6 × 10¹⁹
Worked solution

C. One photon carries E = hc/λ = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ 6.50 × 10⁻⁷ = 3.06 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. The emission rate is the power divided by the energy per photon: n = 5.0 × 10⁻³ ÷ 3.06 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 1.6 × 10¹⁶ photons per second.

Why each distractor is wrong: A is the energy of one photon in joules, not a count — the calculation stopped one step early · B divides the power in watts by the photon energy in electron-volts (1.9 eV), mixing the two energy units · D misses the mW → W conversion, inflating the count a thousandfold.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Anika tests a green laser pointer labelled with a wavelength of 532 nm. She models the beam as a stream of identical photons.

a. Show that the energy of one photon of the laser light is approximately 2.3 eV. (2 marks)

b. Calculate the energy of one photon in joules. (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. E = hc/λ with h in eV s (1 mark: formula with the eV-matched constant): E = (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ 5.32 × 10⁻⁷ = 1.242 × 10⁻⁶ ÷ 5.32 × 10⁻⁷ = 2.33 ≈ 2.3 eV (1 mark: complete substitution and evaluation). The target value is given, so the marks sit entirely in the visible working.

1 mark: E = hc/λ with the eV-matched constant and λ converted to metres · 1 mark: full substitution reaching 2.3 eV.

b. E = 2.33 × 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 3.7 × 10⁻¹⁹ J (1 mark).

1 mark: 3.7 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.

Accept (b): the direct route E = hc/λ = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ 5.32 × 10⁻⁷ = 3.7 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. Do not accept (a): the value 2.3 eV asserted without a visible substitution; a substitution using h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s whose result is labelled eV without the ÷ 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ conversion shown.

KK 1.8 analyse the photoelectric effect with reference to: evidence for the particle-like nature of light; experimental data in the form of graphs of photocurrent versus electrode potential, and of kinetic energy of electrons versus frequency; kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons: Ek max = hf − φ, using energy units of joule and electron-volt; effects of intensity of incident irradiation on the emission of photoelectrons

What you must be able to do

  • Read a photocurrent–potential graph: the stopping voltage V0 where the curve meets the negative axis, and the saturation plateau at positive potential.
  • Read and transform an Ek max–frequency graph: gradient = h, x-intercept = threshold frequency f0, extrapolated y-intercept = −φ.
  • Calculate Ek max = hf − φ on formula sheet with both terms in the same energy unit, and use φ = hf0 not on sheet.
  • Translate between stopping voltage and energy: Ek max in eV equals the magnitude of V0 in volts.
  • Predict the effect of intensity changes at fixed frequency, and of frequency changes, on both graphs.

In the photocell, light frees electrons from the metal surface; making the collector electrode increasingly negative turns back ever-faster electrons, until at the stopping voltage even the most energetic are brought to rest short of the collector and the photocurrent reads zero. The bookkeeping is qV0 = Ek max — the Unit 3 relation W = qV run in reverse. A stopping voltage of magnitude 0.80 V therefore means a maximum kinetic energy of 0.80 eV, with no calculation at all.

The two graphs divide the experiment between them, as shown in Figure 5.1 and Figure 5.2. The Ek max–frequency line has gradient h for every metal — different metals give parallel lines — while the intercepts are metal-specific: the x-intercept is the threshold frequency f0, and extending the line below the axis gives a y-intercept of −φ. The photocurrent–potential curve carries the other two readings: the stopping voltage on the negative axis and the saturation current, which measures how many photoelectrons are emitted per second.

Ek max = hf − φ gives a meaningless result when the two terms are in different units: hf computed with h in J s cannot have a work function in eV subtracted from it. When φ is given in eV — as it usually is — work entirely in eV with h = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s, or convert φ to joules first. Intensity, at fixed frequency, sets only the photon arrival rate: the photocurrent is proportional to intensity, while Ek max and the stopping voltage do not move, because each photoelectron still absorbs one photon of the same energy hf.

Trap Unit mismatch inside Ek max = hf − φ. Computing hf = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 7.0 × 10¹⁴ = 4.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J and then subtracting a work function of 2.1 eV gives a meaningless negative number — examiners flag the J s/eV s confusion as the recurring calculation error of this topic. Convert first, subtract second.
Trap The rote line "intensity changes the current, frequency does not" fails when the power of the source is held constant and the frequency is raised: each photon then carries more energy, so fewer photons arrive per second, and the saturation current falls. On the 2024 exam this distinction was worth 2 marks and 93% of students scored zero, most of them reciting an answer from a previous exam. Check what the stem holds constant — photon rate or power — before answering.
Assessor's advice 2024 report, on what stopping-voltage readings reveal: students "frequently referred to the photon rather than the photoelectron" and "were also unclear that it is the maximum kinetic energy not simply 'the energy'" — 72% scored zero. The stopping voltage measures the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons. Both italicised words are load-bearing.
Connects The stopping voltage is W = qV from Unit 3 run in reverse: there a voltage gave a charge energy, here a measured voltage prices the energy a photoelectron arrived with. The electron-volt defined there is the working currency of this dot point.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Jo studies the photoelectric effect using an evacuated photocell with a caesium cathode. The work function of caesium is 2.1 eV. Jo illuminates the cathode with light of frequency 7.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz.

a. Calculate the maximum kinetic energy, in eV, of the emitted photoelectrons. (2 marks)

b. State the magnitude of the stopping voltage. (1 mark)

c. Jo doubles the intensity of the light, keeping the frequency unchanged. State the effect of this change on:

i. the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons (1 mark)

ii. the photocurrent (1 mark)

Worked solution

a. Working in eV throughout: hf = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 7.0 × 10¹⁴ = 2.90 eV (1 mark: photon energy with the eV-matched constant). Ek max = hf − φ = 2.90 − 2.1 = 0.80 eV (1 mark: answer).

1 mark: hf = 2.9 eV (or 4.64 × 10⁻¹⁹ J with φ converted to 3.36 × 10⁻¹⁹ J) · 1 mark: 0.80 eV.

Do not accept (a): 4.64 × 10⁻¹⁹ − 2.1 or any subtraction mixing joules and electron-volts; a negative final energy.

b. 0.80 V (1 mark) — the maximum kinetic energy in eV converts directly to the stopping voltage in volts, since qV0 = Ek max.

1 mark: 0.80 V (consequential on part a).

c. i. Unchanged (1 mark) — each photoelectron still absorbs a single photon of the same energy hf, so the energy available to any one electron does not change.

ii. The photocurrent doubles (1 mark) — doubling the intensity at fixed frequency doubles the number of photons arriving per second, so twice as many photoelectrons are emitted per second.

1 mark: Ek max unchanged · 1 mark: photocurrent doubled.

Do not accept (c): "the electrons are more energetic" in any form — intensity changes the count, never the energy per electron, at fixed frequency.

In the style of VCAA · MC

A photocell is illuminated with monochromatic light of frequency above the threshold frequency of its cathode, and the photocurrent is recorded as the electrode potential is varied. The intensity of the light is then halved, with the frequency unchanged. Which one of the following best describes the changes to the photocurrent–potential curve? (1 mark)

  1. The saturation current is unchanged and the stopping voltage is halved.
  2. The saturation current is halved and the stopping voltage is halved.
  3. The saturation current is unchanged and the stopping voltage is unchanged.
  4. The saturation current is halved and the stopping voltage is unchanged.
Worked solution

D. Halving the intensity halves the number of photons arriving per second, so half as many photoelectrons are emitted per second and the saturation current halves. Each emitted electron still absorbs one photon of unchanged energy hf, so Ek max — and with it the stopping voltage — does not move.

Why each distractor is wrong: A and B both let intensity change the energy of individual photoelectrons — intensity sets the photon arrival rate, not the photon energy · C predicts no effect at all, but fewer photons per second must mean fewer photoelectrons per second and a lower plateau.

KK 1.9 describe the limitation of the wave model of light in explaining experimental results related to the photoelectric effect

What you must be able to do

  • State, for each of the three photoelectric findings, what the wave model predicts and what is observed: the threshold frequency, the absence of a time delay, and the dependence of Ek max on frequency rather than intensity.
  • Assemble the full three-part answer: wave-model prediction → observed result → why only the photon model accounts for it.

Every wave-model prediction descends from one premise: wave energy arrives continuously, spread across the whole wavefront, at a rate set by intensity — frequency plays no energetic role. From that premise the wave model predicts that light of any frequency should eject electrons given enough intensity or time; that dim light should show a delay while energy accumulates; and that brighter light should produce faster electrons. The experiment contradicts all three: no emission below f0 at any intensity or duration, emission within nanoseconds however dim the light, and a maximum kinetic energy set by frequency alone. The photon model explains each at a stroke, because every photoelectron absorbs exactly one photon of energy hf.

Key idea The full-mark answer has a fixed shape: "The wave model predicts X. The experiment shows Y. The photon model explains Y because each electron absorbs a single photon of energy hf." One finding, all three sentences.
Assessor's advice On the 2024 wave-limitation question, fewer than 30% of students supplied both the wave-model prediction and the particle-model explanation — most "could not articulate the wave model prediction". Worse, many wrote "an amalgam of all three findings" copied from an A3 sheet, which "is unlikely to result in marks being awarded". Choose ONE finding and complete the chain for it.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna directs an intense beam of red light of frequency 4.3 × 10¹⁴ Hz onto a clean zinc plate in an evacuated photocell. The threshold frequency of zinc is 1.04 × 10¹⁵ Hz. No photoelectrons are emitted, no matter how bright the beam is made or how long it is left on.

Explain why the wave model of light cannot account for this observation, and how the photon model does account for it. In your response, state the wave model's prediction, the experimental observation, and the photon model's explanation. (3 marks)

Worked solution

Wave-model prediction: in the wave model, energy arrives continuously across the plate and accumulates with intensity and exposure time, so a sufficiently intense or sufficiently prolonged beam of any frequency should eventually deliver enough energy to eject electrons from the zinc (1 mark).

Observation: no photoelectrons are emitted at 4.3 × 10¹⁴ Hz at any intensity or duration — the existence of a threshold frequency contradicts the wave-model prediction (1 mark).

Photon-model explanation: each electron can absorb only a single photon, of energy E = hf = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 4.3 × 10¹⁴ = 1.8 eV. This is less than the work function of zinc (φ = hf0 = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 1.04 × 10¹⁵ = 4.3 eV), and electrons cannot bank energy from multiple photons, so no electron ever receives enough energy to escape — however many photons arrive per second (1 mark).

1 mark: wave prediction (any frequency works, given intensity/time) · 1 mark: observation tied to the contradiction (no emission below f0 regardless of intensity) · 1 mark: one photon per electron with hf < φ, so intensity is irrelevant.

Do not accept: an amalgam of all three photoelectric findings; "there is a threshold frequency" restated without the wave-model prediction; "the photons are too weak" without comparing hf with φ.

↑ Pass 1 · The crack in the wave ● Pass 2 · Where the wave model fails
KK 1.10 interpret electron diffraction patterns as evidence for the wave-like nature of matter

What you must be able to do

  • Interpret the concentric-ring pattern formed when an electron beam passes through a thin polycrystalline foil as a diffraction pattern — behaviour only waves produce.
  • Explain why a crystal is the right "slit": interatomic spacings (about 10⁻¹⁰ m) are comparable to the de Broglie wavelength of accelerated electrons, so the λ/w ratio permits observable diffraction.
  • Predict how the pattern responds when the accelerating voltage changes.

If electrons were nothing but particles, a beam fired through a thin foil would land in a single central patch, spread only by collisions. What appears instead is a set of bright and dark concentric rings — alternating maxima and minima, the unmistakable signature of interference — and the ring spacing matches the diffraction of a wave with wavelength λ = h/p. The rings are circles, rather than the spot rows of a double slit, because the foil's many tiny crystals sit at every orientation, so each diffraction direction is swept around a full circle.

Raising the accelerating voltage raises the electrons' momentum, shrinking λ = h/p; since the spread of any diffraction pattern scales with λ, the rings contract toward the centre. The λ/w criterion is a ratio, not a cut-off: diffraction does not switch off the moment λ drops below the spacing.

Trap Treating electrons as photons. The 2025 report singles out students "incorrectly using c when considering the speed of electrons": E = hf, E = hc/λ and p = E/c belong to photons only. For electrons, energy and momentum connect through the mass: Ek = ½mv² and p = mv.
Assessor's advice 2024 report: "Diffraction will begin to be observable when the λ/w ratio is ~10 and will continue to be observable as the ratio falls below 1." The examiners were troubled by "the number of students who stated that diffraction would not occur if the wavelength was less than the spacing" — a wavelength somewhat smaller than the spacing still diffracts usefully.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Ishva fires electrons from an electron gun through a thin polycrystalline graphite foil inside an evacuated tube. A pattern of bright concentric rings appears on the fluorescent screen behind the foil.

a. Explain how this pattern provides evidence for the wave-like nature of matter. (2 marks)

b. Ishva increases the accelerating voltage of the electron gun. State and explain the effect of this change on the diameters of the rings. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The rings are alternating maxima and minima — a diffraction (interference) pattern — and interference is behaviour that only waves exhibit; particles passing through the foil would produce a single central patch, not discrete rings (1 mark: the pattern identified as interference, wave-only behaviour). The electrons must therefore propagate through the foil as waves, with a wavelength given by λ = h/p that is comparable to the interatomic spacing of the graphite (1 mark: conclusion that matter exhibits wave behaviour, tied to the de Broglie wavelength).

1 mark: rings = interference/diffraction, which particles cannot produce · 1 mark: electrons behave as waves of wavelength λ = h/p.

b. The ring diameters decrease (1 mark: effect). A larger accelerating voltage gives each electron more kinetic energy and therefore more momentum, so λ = h/p decreases; the angular spread of a diffraction pattern scales with the λ/w ratio, so a smaller wavelength pulls every ring inward (1 mark: cause, through momentum and wavelength).

1 mark: diameters decrease · 1 mark: larger p → smaller λ = h/p → smaller spread.

Do not accept: "the electrons travel in a wavy path" — wave nature means diffraction and interference behaviour, not a wiggling trajectory; reasoning for part b through photon relations such as E = hc/λ.

KK 1.11 distinguish between the diffraction patterns produced by photons and electrons

What you must be able to do

  • Compare two diffraction patterns through the one variable that sets their scale: the wavelength.
  • Conclude from identical patterns (same target) that the wavelengths — and therefore the momenta, p = h/λ on formula sheet — are identical; from different scales, identify which wavelength is larger.
  • Distinguish same wavelength from same energy: a photon of wavelength λ carries E = hc/λ, an electron of the same wavelength carries Ek = p²/2m — very different numbers.

The mechanism sentence carries the mark: the spread of a diffraction pattern is determined by the wavelength, so two patterns of the same dimensions, produced by the same target, imply equal wavelengths. On the 2024 exam most students reached "same wavelength" but "very few linked the spacing of the pattern to wavelength" — only 23% earned full marks. Write the link, then the conclusion.

Equal wavelengths mean equal momenta, because p = h/λ applies to photons and matter alike — but not equal energies. At λ = 5.0 × 10⁻¹¹ m, an X-ray photon carries about 2.5 × 10⁴ eV while an electron carries only about 6.0 × 10² eV: the same momentum, roughly forty times less energy. Figure 5.3 shows the canonical comparison image that VCAA has reused across three papers.

Key idea Same pattern ⇒ same wavelength ⇒ same momentum — not same energy. Energy follows different rules for the two: E = pc for the photon, Ek = p²/2m for the electron.

In a materials laboratory, a beam of electrons and a beam of X-rays are passed in turn through the same thin polycrystalline metal foil. (1 mark)

  1. They have the same energy.
  2. They have the same wavelength.
  3. They travel at the same speed.
  4. They have the same mass.

KK 1.12 calculate the de Broglie wavelength of matter: λ = hp

What you must be able to do

  • Calculate λ = h/p on formula sheet with p = mv, always using h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s.
  • Run the accelerating-voltage chain in both directions: qV = ½mv² on formula sheet, then p = mv (or p = √(2mEk) not on sheet), then λ = h/p.
  • Reason proportionally: doubling the speed doubles the momentum and halves the wavelength; λ ∝ 1/√V for electrons accelerated from rest.

The chain λ ↔ p ↔ Ek ↔ V is one reversible pipeline, and recent exams have entered it at a different link each year: wavelength to momentum in 2024, wavelength back to accelerating voltage in 2025, energy to wavelength on the sample exam. The connecting relation p = √(2mEk) is not printed on the sheet — derive it from p = mv and Ek = ½mv², or go through the speed explicitly. Stems add "Ignore relativistic effects"; take the instruction at face value.

The same formula explains why nothing in daily life diffracts: a 150 g cricket ball bowled at 20 m s⁻¹ has p = 3.0 kg m s⁻¹ and λ = 2.2 × 10⁻³⁴ m, immeasurably smaller than any aperture in existence. An electron accelerated through a few hundred volts, by contrast, lands near 10⁻¹⁰ m — the atomic-spacing scale — which is the design logic of every diffraction tube and electron microscope.

Key idea One reversible chain links four quantities: λ = h/p · p = mv = √(2mEk) · Ek = qV. Whatever the question supplies, the chain links it to the quantity required.
Trap The eV s value of Planck's constant never enters λ = h/p: momentum is in kg m s⁻¹, an SI unit, so only h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s gives a wavelength in metres. If the kinetic energy is supplied in eV, convert it to joules before computing p = √(2mEk).
Assessor's advice On the 2025 chain question, "nearly half the cohort either did not attempt the question or did not make a meaningful start", and a significant number found the speed but stalled. The report's advice: this "required an understanding of physics to solve a complex problem rather than an application of simple mathematics … practise more detailed problems similar to this question." Map the chain before substituting anything.
Connects The first half of the chain is the Unit 3 electron gun: qV = ½mv² set the speed of charges in accelerators. The same electrons now carry a wavelength, and the accelerating voltage sets λ.
In the style of VCAA · MC

An electron moving well below the speed of light has its speed doubled. Which one of the following best describes the effect on the de Broglie wavelength of the electron? (1 mark)

  1. It is halved.
  2. It is doubled.
  3. It is quartered.
  4. It is unchanged.
Worked solution

A. λ = h/p = h/mv: doubling the speed doubles the momentum, so the wavelength halves.

Why each distractor is wrong: B inverts the relation, treating λ as proportional to v · C squares the speed dependence, confusing momentum with kinetic energy (λ depends on p, not on v²) · D treats the wavelength as a fixed property of the electron, but λ depends on its motion.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Magnus operates a small electron microscope in which electrons are accelerated from rest through a potential difference of 2.0 kV. He models each electron as a matter wave in order to predict the instrument's resolving power. Ignore relativistic effects.

Calculate the de Broglie wavelength of the electrons as they leave the electron gun. Show your working. (3 marks)

Worked solution

Step 1 — energy: Ek = qV = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 2.0 × 10³ = 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁶ J (1 mark: kinetic energy from the accelerating voltage, in joules).

1 mark: Ek = 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁶ J · 1 mark: p = 2.4 × 10⁻²³ kg m s⁻¹ · 1 mark: λ = 2.7 × 10⁻¹¹ m.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Kenji investigates the photoelectric effect using an evacuated photocell with a sodium cathode and a set of narrow-band filters. For each of five light frequencies he records the stopping voltage — the smallest potential difference that reduces the photocurrent to zero — and converts each reading to a maximum kinetic energy in electron-volts. His results, with uncertainty bars and the line of best fit, are shown in Figure 5.4.

a. Explain what the stopping-voltage readings tell Kenji about the emitted photoelectrons. (2 marks)

b. Using two widely spaced points on the line of best fit in Figure 5.4, determine an experimental value for Planck's constant. Give your answer in eV s. (2 marks)

c. Determine the threshold frequency of sodium and the work function of the sodium cathode. (2 marks)

d. Keeping the frequency fixed at 8.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz, Kenji halves the intensity of the light.

i. State and explain the effect of this change on the photocurrent. (2 marks)

ii. State the effect of this change on the stopping voltage. (1 mark)

e. The sodium cathode is replaced with a metal of larger work function and the experiment is repeated. Describe how the new line on the graph of maximum kinetic energy versus frequency compares with the line in Figure 5.4. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The magnitude of each stopping voltage gives the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons: a stopping voltage of magnitude V0 means Ek max = qV0, which in electron-volts is numerically equal to V0 in volts (1 mark: maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons identified). At the stopping voltage even the most energetic photoelectrons are brought to rest before they reach the collector, which is why the photocurrent falls to zero there (1 mark: mechanism).

1 mark: V0 measures the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons · 1 mark: at V0 even the fastest photoelectrons are turned back, so the current is zero.

Do not accept (a): "the energy of the photons"; "the energy of the electrons" without maximum.

b. Planck's constant is the gradient of the line. Using the two widely spaced points (5.5 × 10¹⁴ Hz, 0 eV) and (10.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz, 1.9 eV), both on the line: gradient = (1.9 − 0) ÷ (10.0 × 10¹⁴ − 5.5 × 10¹⁴) (1 mark: rise over run from points on the line) = 1.9 ÷ 4.5 × 10¹⁴ = 4.2 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s (1 mark: value with unit).

1 mark: gradient demonstrated as rise over run using points that lie on the line · 1 mark: 4.0 × 10⁻¹⁵ to 4.4 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s.

Do not accept (b): gradients computed from data points not on the line; a bare fraction that cannot be identified as a gradient; a value nudged toward 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s — the gradient is read from the line as drawn.

A beam of electrons is fired through a thin polycrystalline foil and produces the ring pattern shown on the left. The pattern on the right is produced when a beam of X-rays is passed through the same foil.

a. Explain what the production of a ring pattern by the electron beam reveals about the nature of electrons. (2)

b. An electron in the beam has a momentum of 5.0 × 10⁻²⁴ kg m s⁻¹. Calculate its de Broglie wavelength. (2)

c. The voltage that accelerates the electrons is increased. State and explain how the diameter of the rings changes. (2)

d. Compare the ring pattern produced by these electrons with that produced by the X-rays, given that the two beams have the same wavelength. (2)

Worked solution

a. Diffraction — the bending and reinforcing of waves around the regularly spaced atoms of the foil — is a wave phenomenon, so the rings show that electrons exhibit wave-like behaviour. Each electron has an associated de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p.

1 mark: the rings are a diffraction (interference) pattern, a wave behaviour

1 mark: electrons therefore have a wave nature, with λ = h/p

b. λ = h/p = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ 5.0 × 10⁻²⁴ = 1.3 × 10⁻¹⁰ m.

1 mark: λ = h/p with correct substitution

1 mark: 1.3 × 10⁻¹⁰ m

c. The rings shrink (smaller diameter). A higher accelerating voltage gives the electrons more kinetic energy and hence greater momentum; a greater momentum means a shorter de Broglie wavelength (λ = h/p), which diffracts through a smaller angle, drawing the rings inward.

1 mark: the rings become smaller

1 mark: higher voltage → larger p → shorter λ → smaller diffraction angle

d. The two patterns are effectively identical. Diffraction depends only on the wavelength relative to the spacing of the atoms, so beams of the same wavelength passing through the same foil are diffracted through the same angles and produce rings of the same size. (Equal wavelength also means equal momentum, p = h/λ.)

1 mark: the patterns are the same / rings of the same size

1 mark: same wavelength ⇒ same diffraction angles through the same foil
