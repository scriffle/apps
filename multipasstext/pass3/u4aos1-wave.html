Light as a wave

Dot points KK 1.1–1.6: the electromagnetic wave, its single speed, standing waves, diffraction and Young's double slit. The fringe-spacing relation Δx = λL/d recurs on recent examinations, in both calculation and qualitative manipulation; the standing-wave wavelengths λn = 2L/n are not on the formula sheet; and assessors report "ongoing confusion between diffraction and interference". The two phenomena, and the symbols w and d, must be kept separate.

KK 1.1 describe light as a transverse electromagnetic wave which is produced by the acceleration of charges, which in turn produces changing electric fields and associated changing magnetic fields

What you must be able to do

  • Reproduce the production chain in the dot point's own order: an accelerating charge produces a changing electric field, which produces an associated changing magnetic field, and the disturbance propagates as a transverse wave.
  • State the geometry: the electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to each other and both perpendicular to the direction of travel, rising and falling in phase.
  • Identify accelerating charges in context: electrons driven up and down an antenna, and electrons steered around the Australian Synchrotron — circular motion at constant speed is acceleration.

This is a describe-level dot point, and the wording of the dot point is itself the model answer. A full-mark description names the cause (acceleration of charges), the mechanism (a changing electric field with its associated changing magnetic field, each regenerating the other) and the structure (transverse, E ⊥ B ⊥ direction of travel). Answers that mention only one field, or that describe the fields as static, are incomplete.

Two refinements distinguish a complete answer. First, the wave requires no medium: it is an oscillation of fields, not of matter, which is why light crosses the vacuum between the Sun and Earth while sound cannot; the statement that light "vibrates the medium" is incorrect. Second, the two fields oscillate in phase: they peak together and pass through zero together, in perpendicular planes. The preferred form is "light can be modelled as an electromagnetic wave" rather than "light is a wave", because this area of study rests on the limits of each model.

Trap "The synchrotron emits light because its electrons travel at nearly the speed of light." This is incorrect: the emission occurs because the electrons are accelerating, since on the curved path their direction changes continuously and an accelerating charge radiates. A charge moving in a straight line at constant velocity, however fast, emits nothing. The speed is incidental; the acceleration is the cause.
Key idea Light is a transverse electromagnetic wave produced by the acceleration of charges: the changing electric field and its associated changing magnetic field regenerate each other, perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel, with no medium required.
Connects The synchrotron from 5 · Motors & accelerators closes the loop with Unit 3: the same magnetic fields that bend the electron beam force the continuous acceleration that makes the machine a light source.
In the style of VCAA · MC

At the Australian Synchrotron in Clayton, electrons travel around a storage ring at constant speed and emit intense electromagnetic radiation. Which one of the following best describes why these electrons emit electromagnetic radiation? (1 mark)

  1. The electrons are travelling at a speed close to the speed of light.
  2. The electrons collide with gas molecules in the storage ring and release energy as light.
  3. The magnetic fields that steer the electrons transfer their own energy directly into the light.
  4. The electrons are continuously accelerating, because their direction of motion is continuously changing.
Worked solution

D. Circular motion at constant speed is acceleration — the velocity changes direction at every instant. An accelerating charge produces a changing electric field and an associated changing magnetic field, which propagate away as an electromagnetic wave.

Why each distractor is wrong: A — high speed alone produces no radiation; a charge at constant velocity, however fast, does not emit · B — the storage ring is evacuated precisely so that collisions do not occur · C — the magnetic force is perpendicular to the velocity and does no work on the electrons (Unit 3), so the field cannot be the energy source; the energy is supplied by the accelerating structures.

KK 1.2 identify that all electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed, c, in a vacuum

What you must be able to do

  • Identify that every electromagnetic wave — radio, microwave, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma — travels at c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ in a vacuum, regardless of frequency.
  • Apply v = fλ on formula sheet with v = c, in both directions.
  • Convert the planted prefixes — nm, MHz, GHz — to base units as a separate written line.

An identify-level dot point, but it powers the arithmetic of the entire area of study: c sits on the data page of the formula sheet, and c = fλ converts between every frequency and wavelength this page and the next three use. Because the product fλ is pinned at c, frequency and wavelength are inversely proportional — doubling one halves the other.

The frequency of a wave belongs to its source and does not change in transit. When a question changes "the frequency" of a light source, the frequency must be translated to wavelength before any geometric reasoning: a lower frequency means a longer wavelength, hence more diffraction (KK 1.5) and wider fringes (KK 1.6). Reversing this direction inverts every later conclusion.

Trap A higher frequency never means a higher speed: in a vacuum a gamma ray and a radio wave travel at exactly the same speed. The other standing hazard is prefix conversion — 550 nm is 5.50 × 10⁻⁷ m, 101.1 MHz is 1.011 × 10⁸ Hz, 2.45 GHz is 2.45 × 10⁹ Hz — and each slip lands on a printed multiple-choice distractor.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A Melbourne FM station broadcasts radio waves at a frequency of 101.1 MHz. Take the speed of the waves to be c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹. Which one of the following is closest to the wavelength of this signal? (1 mark)

  1. 3.0 × 10⁻³ m
  2. 0.34 m
  3. 3.0 m
  4. 3.0 × 10³ m
Worked solution

C. λ = cf = (3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ (1.011 × 10⁸) = 3.0 m.

Why each distractor is wrong: A treats MHz as GHz (frequency 10³ times too large) · B inverts the fraction, computing f/c = 0.34 and mislabelling it as a wavelength · D treats MHz as kHz (frequency 10³ times too small).

KK 1.3 explain the formation of a standing wave resulting from the superposition of a travelling wave and its reflection

What you must be able to do

  • Explain the formation as a causal chain: reflection at a fixed end (inverted), two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions, superposition, permanent cancellation at nodes and reinforcement at antinodes.
  • Use the word superposition explicitly — it is in the dot point, and the explanation mark attaches to it.
  • State that a fixed end reflects the wave inverted and must itself be a node.

The mark grammar of this explanation is fixed: one mark for the reflection producing a second wave of the same frequency and amplitude travelling the opposite way, one for the superposition — displacements adding at every point at every instant — and one for the consequence: at the nodes the two waves always arrive with opposite displacements and cancel permanently, while at the antinodes they reinforce to twice the amplitude of either wave. The pattern travels neither left nor right, which is what "standing" means.

A travelling wave transmits energy; a standing wave stores it. The two component waves carry equal energy in opposite directions, so the net flow along the string is zero; the energy oscillates back and forth within each loop, between kinetic and potential, and never moves past a node. Twice in every period the whole string is momentarily flat: the wave has not vanished, because at that instant all the energy is kinetic and both component waves are still travelling.

Trap "The standing wave carries energy from the oscillator to the wall." It does not — that is the defining difference from a travelling wave. The incident and reflected waves transport equal energy in opposite directions, so the standing wave transmits no net energy; it stores energy in the oscillation of each loop. An explanation that treats the standing wave as a wave "bouncing back and forth" without superposition earns nothing.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna ties one end of a long elastic cord to a wall bracket and shakes the other end vertically at a steady frequency. After a short time the cord settles into a stable pattern in which some points never move at all, while points midway between them oscillate with large amplitude.

a. Explain how this pattern forms. In your answer, refer to superposition. (3 marks)

Worked solution

a. The travelling wave Shanna sends along the cord reflects from the fixed end at the wall and returns inverted, so the cord carries two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions (1 mark: reflection producing the second wave). The two waves superpose: at every point on the cord their displacements add at every instant (1 mark: superposition stated). At the points that never move — the nodes — the two waves always arrive with opposite displacements and cancel permanently; midway between them, at the antinodes, they arrive in phase and reinforce, producing the large-amplitude oscillation. The resulting pattern does not travel along the cord: it is a standing wave (1 mark: nodes and antinodes explained as permanent cancellation and reinforcement).

1 mark: reflection from the fixed end gives two waves of equal frequency and amplitude in opposite directions · 1 mark: superposition — displacements add · 1 mark: permanent cancellation at nodes, reinforcement at antinodes, pattern stationary.

Accept: "destructive interference at the nodes and constructive interference at the antinodes" in place of cancellation/reinforcement wording. Do not accept: "the wave bounces back and forth" with no superposition; naming the pattern a standing wave without explaining why the nodes are permanently still; "the wall absorbs the wave" (it reflects it).

KK 1.4 analyse the formation of standing waves (only those with nodes at both ends is required)

What you must be able to do

  • Use λn = 2Ln not on sheet and fn = nv2L = nf1 not on sheet — both must be recalled or rebuilt from "a whole number of half-wavelengths fits in L".
  • Test whether a given wavelength can form a standing wave: 2L/λ must be a whole number.
  • Read a diagram: the harmonic number n equals the number of antinodes, the number of nodes is n + 1 counting both ends, and adjacent nodes are λ/2 apart.
  • Track the pattern in time from a snapshot drawn at maximum displacement: the string is fully inverted at t = T/2 and momentarily flat at T/4 and 3T/4.

Nothing in this dot point is on the formula sheet — the sample exam asked directly which wavelength could not form on a string, and the only route was λn = 2L/n from memory. The harmonics are numbered as VCAA does: n = 1 is the fundamental and the first harmonic, with the longest wavelength 2L (longer than the string itself — a wavelength greater than L is not an error), and the nth harmonic has frequency nf1. The same analysis serves electromagnetic standing waves — microwaves between the reflecting walls of an oven, light between the mirrors of a laser cavity — because the formation argument of KK 1.3 never mentions a string.

A standing-wave diagram represents an envelope, not a single instant: the antinodes oscillate between the two drawn extremes, passing through zero between them. Questions that show a snapshot at t = 0 and ask for the string's appearance later require period bookkeeping: determine T = 1/f, then count quarters.

Trap Reading the wavelength off a diagram as the node-to-node distance. Adjacent nodes are λ/2 apart — one loop is half a wavelength, and a full wavelength spans two loops. Every harmonics answer should be checked against this: a 1.5 m string carrying the third harmonic has nodes 0.50 m apart and a wavelength of 1.0 m, not 0.50 m.
Assessor's advice On the 2024 snapshot question (59% correct), the assessors' reasoning was: "50 Hz gives a period of 0.02 seconds. Therefore, in half a period (0.010 s) the wave will have moved half a wavelength" — the string appears fully inverted at t = T/2. The flat string is the trap to avoid: it occurs at T/4 and 3T/4, not at T/2.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A string of length 4.8 m is stretched between two fixed ends. Which one of the following wavelengths cannot form a standing wave on this string? (1 mark)

  1. 9.6 m
  2. 4.0 m
  3. 4.8 m
  4. 2.4 m
Worked solution

B. With nodes at both ends, the allowed wavelengths are λn = 2L/n = 9.6 m, 4.8 m, 3.2 m, 2.4 m, … For 4.0 m, n = 2L/λ = 9.6 ÷ 4.0 = 2.4, which is not a whole number, so no standing wave forms.

Why each distractor is wrong (each one fits): A is the fundamental, n = 1 — a wavelength longer than the string is allowed · C is the second harmonic, n = 2 · D is the fourth harmonic, n = 4. The question is a recall test of λn = 2L/n, which is not on the formula sheet.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Anika stretches a string of length 1.5 m between two rigid clamps, as shown in Figure 4.1, and drives it with a small oscillator. Waves travel along the string at 3.0 × 10² m s⁻¹.

a. On a copy of Figure 4.1, draw the standing-wave pattern of the third harmonic of the string. Label each node N and each antinode A. (2 marks)

b. Calculate the wavelength and the frequency of the third harmonic. (2 marks)

c. Kenji claims that the wavelength of the third harmonic can be read directly from the drawing as the distance between adjacent nodes. Evaluate his claim. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The third harmonic has three equal loops: nodes at both clamped ends and at 0.50 m and 1.0 m from the left clamp (four nodes, evenly spaced), with three antinodes at the centres of the loops. The drawing shows a smooth, symmetric envelope — ideally both extremes of the motion as mirror-image curves — returning to zero displacement at every node.

Drawn-pattern marking guide — 1 mark: nodes at both fixed ends, with exactly four nodes (including the ends) and three antinodes, evenly spaced and labelled N and A · 1 mark: a smooth, symmetric envelope of three equal loops spanning the full 1.5 m.

Accept: a single extreme drawn as one smooth curve (the mirror-image second extreme is conventional but not required), provided the loops are equal in length and amplitude. Do not accept: antinodes at the clamped ends; two antinodes and three nodes (the second harmonic); a travelling-wave snapshot that does not reach zero at both ends; loops of visibly unequal length; missing N and A labels.

b. λ3 = 2L3 = 2 × 1.5 ÷ 3 = 1.0 m (1 mark: wavelength). f3 = vλ3 = 3.0 × 10² ÷ 1.0 = 3.0 × 10² Hz (1 mark: frequency).

1 mark: λ3 = 1.0 m from 2L/n · 1 mark: f3 = 3.0 × 10² Hz via v = fλ (consequential on the wavelength).

c. Kenji is incorrect (claim). Adjacent nodes are half a wavelength apart, not a full wavelength: the node spacing in the drawing is 0.50 m, so the wavelength is 2 × 0.50 = 1.0 m, consistent with part b (1 mark: claim with the λ/2 relationship; 1 mark: applying it to obtain 1.0 m).

1 mark: claim rejected, node-to-node spacing identified as λ/2 · 1 mark: wavelength recovered as twice the 0.50 m spacing.

Do not accept (c): agreement with Kenji; "the wavelength is the distance between nodes"; rejection of the claim with no corrected relationship.

KK 1.5 investigate and explain theoretically and practically diffraction as the directional spread of various frequencies with reference to different gap width or obstacle size, including the qualitative effect of changing the λ/w ratio, and apply this to limitations of imaging using electromagnetic waves

What you must be able to do

  • Explain diffraction as the directional spread of a wave passing a gap or an obstacle, governed qualitatively by the ratio λ/w not on sheet.
  • Predict the effect of changing the wavelength (or the frequency — convert first) or the gap width on the spread.
  • Apply the ratio to imaging: an electromagnetic wave cannot resolve detail much smaller than its own wavelength.

The ratio λ/w governs diffraction continuously rather than as an all-or-nothing threshold. Diffraction is significant when λ/w ≈ 1 or greater, where the gap behaves as a point source and the wave spreads into the whole region beyond, but it does not cease when λ drops below w: the spread decreases smoothly as the ratio falls, and remains observable well below 1. The assessors have penalised the hard-cutoff version of this idea directly. Diffraction changes direction only: the wavelength, frequency and speed of the wave are all unchanged by passing the gap.

The imaging limitation is the ratio applied in reverse. A wave diffracts almost undisturbed around any object much smaller than its own wavelength, so the object leaves no shadow and no usable image: visible light, at 4.0 × 10⁻⁷ m to 7.0 × 10⁻⁷ m, cannot resolve a virus a tenth that size, no matter how good the optics. Imaging finer detail requires a shorter wavelength, which is the basis for electron microscopy in the next cluster.

Trap "Higher frequency means more diffraction." This is inverted: diffraction grows with wavelength, and a higher frequency means a shorter wavelength, hence less spread. Questions deliberately specify the source by frequency so that students must perform the conversion before comparing; the reasoning must proceed through λ in every case.
Assessor's advice From the 2024 report, on a question where 60% scored zero: "Of concern was the number of students who stated that diffraction would not occur if the wavelength was less than the spacing. Diffraction will begin to be observable when the λ/w ratio is ~10 and will continue to be observable as the ratio falls below 1. Students should familiarise themselves with the process of diffraction."
Connects The same ratio with a smaller λ is the case for electron microscopes in 12 · Matter waves: electrons can carry wavelengths thousands of times shorter than visible light, so they image detail no light microscope can reach.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Lucia and Magnus direct a red laser beam of wavelength 650 nm through a single slit of width 1.3 μm. Lucia predicts that no observable diffraction will occur, because the wavelength is smaller than the slit width. Magnus predicts that the beam will spread noticeably after passing through the slit.

a. State who is correct and justify your answer. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Magnus is correct: λ/w = (6.5 × 10⁻⁷) ÷ (1.3 × 10⁻⁶) = 0.50, so the wavelength and the slit width are of the same order of magnitude and the diffraction is significant (1 mark: claim supported by the evaluated ratio). The spread of a wave through a gap is governed by the ratio λ/w and increases smoothly with it; there is no threshold at λ = w below which diffraction stops (1 mark: ratio reasoning rather than a cutoff).

1 mark: Magnus, with λ/w evaluated (≈ 0.5, same order of magnitude) · 1 mark: diffraction governed by the ratio, observable for ratios below 1 — no cutoff at λ = w.

Accept: "the wavelength is comparable to the slit width" without the numerical ratio, provided both values are converted and compared. Do not accept: a name with no justification (assessors treat it as a guess); "diffraction occurs only when λ ≥ w"; reasoning from the frequency without converting to wavelength.

Assessor's advice On the equivalent 2024 question the model answer turned on the same phrase: the two lengths "are of the same order of magnitude, there will be a useful diffraction pattern produced". The mark is for the comparison, not the conclusion alone.
In the style of VCAA · MC

An AM radio transmitter broadcasts at 1.0 MHz and a mobile-phone tower transmits at 2.0 GHz. Both signals reach a hill approximately 3.0 × 10² m across, with a receiver on the far side. The receiver picks up the AM signal clearly but has no mobile reception. Which one of the following best describes the reason for this observation, in terms of diffraction? (1 mark)

  1. The AM signal's wavelength is comparable to the size of the hill, so the AM signal diffracts strongly into the region behind the hill.
  2. Radio waves of lower frequency travel faster through air, so the AM signal can reach the receiver in the region behind the hill.
  3. The mobile signal's shorter wavelength diffracts more strongly, so its energy is spread too thinly behind the hill to be detected.
  4. The hill absorbs the higher-frequency mobile signal completely but allows the lower-frequency AM signal to pass through the rock.
Worked solution

A. The AM wavelength is λ = c/f = (3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ (1.0 × 10⁶) = 3.0 × 10² m, so λ/w ≈ 1 for the hill and the wave spreads strongly into the region behind it. The mobile signal's wavelength is 0.15 m, giving λ/w = 5.0 × 10⁻⁴ — negligible spread, so a dead spot forms behind the hill.

Why each distractor is wrong: B — all electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed; speed differences explain nothing here · C — inverts the dependence: a shorter wavelength gives a smaller λ/w and less diffraction · D — attributes the difference to absorption and transmission through rock, not to the wave behaviour the data point to; the stem asks for the explanation in terms of diffraction.

KK 1.6 explain the results of Young's double slit experiment with reference to: evidence for the wave-like nature of light; constructive and destructive interference of coherent waves in terms of path differences: nλ and (n + ½)λ respectively, where n = 0, 1, 2, …; effect of wavelength, distance of screen and slit separation on interference patterns: Δx = λLd when L >> d

What you must be able to do

  • Explain why the pattern is evidence for the wave-like nature of light: superposition — reinforcement and complete cancellation — is behaviour only waves show.
  • Define coherent waves (same frequency, constant phase difference), explain why the two slits behave as coherent sources, and why a laser suits the experiment.
  • Run the path-difference bookkeeping: bright bands at nλ on formula sheet, dark bands at (n + ½)λ on formula sheet, with n counting from zero.
  • Use Δx = λLd on formula sheet in every direction, and predict the effect of changing each variable.

Coherence is the essential condition of the experiment. The two slits are lit by the same source, so the waves leaving them maintain a constant phase difference; two independent sources drift in phase randomly, and the pattern is lost before it can be observed. A laser is the ideal source because its light is intrinsically coherent. The bookkeeping then runs on n counting from zero: the central maximum is bright with path difference 0λ (n = 0), the first dark band sits at ½λ (n = 0), and counting outward from the central maximum the mth bright band has path difference mλ and the mth dark band (m − ½)λ.

The fringe spacing Δx — between adjacent bright bands or adjacent dark bands, the value is the same — is proportional to λ and L and inversely proportional to d. The formula sheet prints its condition of validity, "when L >> d", and that condition should be quoted in any derivation. Lengths arrive in three different prefixes (λ in nm, d in mm, L in m): convert each to metres as a separate written line, because the conversion is the most common error the assessors report on this calculation. In multi-part questions, marking is independent part by part; the wavelength reasoning must be restated in a later part even if it repeats part a.

Trap Band counting. Both path-difference formulas count from n = 0, so the third dark band has path difference 2.5λ — via (n + ½)λ with n = 2 — not 3.5λ. Write the ladder out from the centre (0λ bright, ½λ dark, 1λ bright, 1½λ dark, …) rather than substituting the band's ordinal straight into n.
Trap Diffraction and interference are not the same phenomenon, and w is not d. Diffraction is the spread at each slit, governed by the slit width w; interference is the overlap of the waves from the two slits, with band spacing governed by the slit separation d. The 2025 report flags "ongoing confusion between diffraction and interference" — students substituted the slit separation as "w" and reasoned about the wrong phenomenon entirely.
Assessor's advice 2025 report, on the frequency-change part: full marks required the chain "Lowering the frequency will increase the wavelength… if the wavelength increases, the spacing will increase." Students who wrote only that the spacing increases, without relating it to the wavelength, earned one mark of two. The report adds that a response to an earlier part cannot be considered when marking a later part — restate the reasoning, "even if that means restating part of their response to part a".
Connects The pattern survives even when light arrives one photon at a time — the single-particle version in 12 · Matter waves is the dual-nature evidence of KK 1.18, and it begins with this experiment.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Jo directs monochromatic laser light at a pair of narrow slits and observes the interference pattern on a distant screen, part of which is shown in Figure 4.2. Point C marks the centre of the central bright band; point P marks the centre of the third dark band to the right of C.

a. State the path difference, in terms of the wavelength λ, for light travelling from the two slits to P, and explain why the band at P is dark. (2 marks)

b. The path difference at P is 1.38 × 10⁻⁶ m. Calculate the wavelength of the laser light. (2 marks)

c. Explain how the presence of dark bands in the pattern provides evidence for the wave-like nature of light. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. P is the third dark band, so the path difference is (n + ½)λ with n = 2: path difference = 2.5λ (1 mark). The waves from the two slits therefore arrive at P in antiphase — crest meeting trough — and interfere destructively, so the intensity at P is a minimum and the band is dark (1 mark).

1 mark: path difference 2.5λ · 1 mark: destructive interference — waves arrive in antiphase and cancel.

Do not accept (a): 3.5λ (substituting the ordinal 3 into n — the count starts at n = 0); "the waves cancel" with no path-difference connection.

b. 2.5λ = 1.38 × 10⁻⁶ m (1 mark: using the part-a relation — consequential marking applies), so λ = (1.38 × 10⁻⁶) ÷ 2.5 = 5.52 × 10⁻⁷ m (1 mark: answer).

1 mark: path difference equated to 2.5λ · 1 mark: λ = 5.52 × 10⁻⁷ m (552 nm).

c. The dark bands occur where light from the two slits arrives together yet produces no light: the two waves superpose destructively and cancel completely (1 mark: cancellation by superposition). Cancellation is behaviour that only waves show — two streams of particles arriving at the same point could only ever add, never produce darkness — so the dark bands cannot be explained by a particle model of light (1 mark: contrast with particles).

1 mark: dark bands as complete destructive superposition of light with light · 1 mark: particles cannot cancel, so the result demands a wave model.

Do not accept (c): "the pattern has bands, which waves make" — the mark needs the cancellation logic, not the pattern's existence; an answer about diffraction at a single slit (this is interference between the two slits).

Mixed practice

The Δx = λL/d archetype has appeared on every recent paper, alternating between direct calculation, rearrangement for d or λ, and multi-variable scaling — Question 9 rehearses the full manipulation. Question 10 runs standing waves and the wave equation through a single apparatus. In questions worth more than one mark, appropriate working must be shown: formula, substitution and answer as separate visible lines, with every length converted to metres before substituting.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Shanna directs a red laser of wavelength 620 nm at a pair of narrow slits separated by 0.50 mm. She observes the interference pattern on a screen 2.0 m from the slits.

a. Show that the spacing between adjacent bright bands on the screen is approximately 2.5 mm. (2 marks)

b. Shanna then replaces the red laser with a violet laser of wavelength 410 nm, halves the slit separation to 0.25 mm and moves the screen to 4.0 m from the slits. Determine the new spacing between adjacent bright bands. (3 marks)

c. State the condition under which the formula Δx = λL/d may be used. (1 mark)

d. Explain why the two slits must be illuminated by a single laser, rather than each slit by its own separate, identical laser. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Δx = λLd = (6.20 × 10⁻⁷ × 2.0) ÷ (5.0 × 10⁻⁴) (1 mark: complete substitution with all three lengths in metres) = 1.24 × 10⁻⁶ ÷ 5.0 × 10⁻⁴ = 2.48 × 10⁻³ m ≈ 2.5 mm (1 mark: evaluation). The target value is given, so the marks are entirely for the visible working.

1 mark: formula with full substitution, nm and mm converted · 1 mark: 2.48 × 10⁻³ m shown to round to the stated 2.5 mm.

b. All three variables change at once: Δx ∝ λ, Δx ∝ L and Δx ∝ 1/d (1 mark: dependences identified or fresh substitution set up). Δx′ = (4.10 × 10⁻⁷ × 4.0) ÷ (2.5 × 10⁻⁴) = 1.64 × 10⁻⁶ ÷ 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ = 6.56 × 10⁻³ m (1 mark: evaluation) ≈ 6.6 × 10⁻³ m = 6.6 mm (1 mark: answer to two significant figures with unit).

1 mark: new substitution (or scaling factors λ ×(410/620), L ×2, d ×½ combined as ×2.6) · 1 mark: 6.56 × 10⁻³ m · 1 mark: final answer 6.6 mm.

Accept (b): the ratio method 2.48 mm × (410/620) × 2 × 2 = 6.6 mm. Do not accept: applying only one or two of the three changes; inverting the d dependence (halving d must widen the bands).

c. The formula is valid when L >> d — the screen distance must be much greater than the slit separation (1 mark). Here 4.0 m is more than ten thousand times 0.25 mm, so the condition holds.

1 mark: L >> d stated (the condition is printed with the formula on the formula sheet).

d. The pattern requires the waves leaving the two slits to be coherent — the same frequency with a constant phase difference (1 mark: coherence defined). Lit by a single laser, both slits sample the same wavefronts, so their phase difference never changes; two separate lasers emit with randomly drifting relative phase, so the bright and dark positions would shift too rapidly for any stable pattern to be observed (1 mark: single source gives constant phase; independent sources do not).

1 mark: coherent — same frequency, constant phase difference · 1 mark: one source keeps the phase difference fixed; two independent sources drift and wash the pattern out.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Kenji investigates the microwaves inside a microwave oven, which operates at a frequency of 2.45 GHz. He removes the turntable, covers a flat heatproof tray with a thin, even layer of grated cheese and runs the oven briefly. The cheese melts first along a row of evenly spaced strips; the centres of adjacent melted strips are 61 mm apart.

a. Explain why a standing wave forms inside the oven. (2 marks)

b. Explain why the cheese melts quickly at some positions and barely melts at others. (2 marks)

c. Determine the wavelength of the microwaves. (2 marks)

d. Hence determine the speed of the microwaves, and explain whether your value is consistent with microwaves being electromagnetic waves. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The microwaves reflect from the metal walls of the oven, so the cavity carries waves of the same frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions (1 mark: reflection). These superpose to form a standing wave, with nodes at fixed positions where the waves always cancel and antinodes where they reinforce (1 mark: superposition giving fixed nodes and antinodes).

1 mark: reflection from the walls produces oppositely travelling waves of the same frequency · 1 mark: superposition forms a standing wave with fixed nodes and antinodes.

b. The melted strips lie at the antinodes, where the incident and reflected waves reinforce and the oscillating field — and therefore the energy delivered to the cheese — is a maximum (1 mark). The barely melted positions are the nodes, where the two waves always arrive in antiphase and cancel, so almost no energy is delivered there (1 mark). This is why the turntable exists: it moves the food through the fixed pattern.

1 mark: melted strips at antinodes — maximum oscillation and energy transfer · 1 mark: unmelted positions at nodes — permanent cancellation, minimal energy transfer.

c. Adjacent antinodes, like adjacent nodes, are λ/2 apart (1 mark: relation), so λ = 2 × 0.061 m = 0.122 m ≈ 0.12 m (1 mark: answer).

1 mark: strip spacing identified as λ/2 · 1 mark: λ = 0.12 m.

Do not accept (c): λ = 61 mm — the spacing between adjacent antinodes is half a wavelength, not a full wavelength.

d. v = fλ = 2.45 × 10⁹ × 0.122 = 2.99 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ ≈ 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ (1 mark: evaluation, using the unrounded wavelength). This equals c, the speed at which all electromagnetic waves travel in a vacuum — the measured speed is consistent with microwaves being electromagnetic waves (1 mark: explicit comparison with c).

1 mark: v = fλ evaluated to 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ (consequential on part c) · 1 mark: value identified as c, so consistent with the electromagnetic wave model.

Do not accept (d): "yes, it is consistent" without the computed speed compared with the value of c.
