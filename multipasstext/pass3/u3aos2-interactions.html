Fields and interactions

Key knowledge 2.1–2.5 is examined almost entirely without calculation: drawing and reading field diagrams, classifying fields, and using the study-design vocabulary exactly. These marks are commonly available and are lost to imprecise language and missing arrowheads more often than to incorrect physics.

KK 2.1 describe gravitation, magnetism and electricity using a field model

What you must be able to do

  • State the field model: a source object creates a field — a quantity with a magnitude and a direction at every point in the surrounding space — and the field exerts a force on any second object placed in it.
  • Name what each field acts on: gravitational fields act on mass, electric fields act on charge, magnetic fields act on magnetic poles and on moving charges.
  • Describe how a test object (a small mass, a small positive charge, a compass needle) reveals the field at a point.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • The field exists whether or not a test object is present. The test object reveals the field; it does not create it. An answer claiming the field "appears" when a second object arrives is wrong.
  • The field is not the force. Field strength is force per unit of the relevant property: g in N kg⁻¹, E in N C⁻¹, B in T. The force appears only when an object is placed in the field, e.g. Fg = mg.
  • Direction conventions: the gravitational field points in the direction of the force exerted on a test mass (always toward the source mass); the electric field points in the direction of the force exerted on a positive test charge; the magnetic field points in the direction the north end of a compass needle points.
  • A "describe … using a field model" answer must mention the field as the intermediary: "Earth creates a gravitational field; the field exerts a force on the satellite." A sentence that only says "Earth pulls the satellite" has not used the field model.
Trap Writing "the field is a force". A field strength of 9.8 N kg⁻¹ is not a force of 9.8 N — it states that 9.8 N of force is exerted on each kilogram placed at that point. Confusing field with force is the source of the most common errors in this area of study.
In the style of VCAA · MC

Which one of the following best describes the field model of gravitation? (1 mark)

  1. A gravitational field is the gravitational force exerted on an object by a distant mass, expressed in newtons.
  2. A gravitational field exists at a point only while a second object is present at that point to experience a force.
  3. A mass creates a gravitational field at every point around it, and the field exerts a force on any other mass placed in it.
  4. A gravitational field is the region of space within which the gravitational force exerted on an object remains constant.
Worked solution

C. The field model splits the interaction into two steps: the source mass creates a field at every point in space around it, and the field exerts a force on any other mass placed in it. The field has a value at every point whether or not a second object is there.

Why each distractor is wrong: A confuses the field with the force — field strength is force per kilogram (N kg⁻¹), not a force. B encodes the misconception that the test object creates the field; it only reveals it. D confuses a field with a uniform-field region; a real gravitational field extends everywhere and is generally non-uniform.

KK 2.2 investigate and compare theoretically and practically gravitational, magnetic and electric fields, including directions and shapes of fields, attractive and repulsive effects, and the existence of dipoles and monopoles

What you must be able to do

  • Compare the three fields: what creates each one, its direction convention, whether the interaction can attract and/or repel, and which of monopoles and dipoles exist.
  • Sketch and interpret the standard field shapes: a point mass, single and paired point charges, and a bar magnet.
  • Reason about the combined field of two sources, including the near-zero field midway between two like poles or like charges.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • Masses only attract. Gravitational sources are therefore monopoles only — every field line ends on a mass (a "sink") — and a gravitational dipole cannot exist because there is no repulsive partner to pair with.
  • Electric charge comes in two kinds, so electricity has both: an isolated positive or negative charge is a monopole; an equal positive–negative pair is a dipole.
  • Magnetism has dipoles only. Cutting a bar magnet produces two smaller dipoles, and an isolated magnetic pole has never been observed.
  • The field lines of two like charges, or two like poles, never meet. Between the sources the two fields point in opposite directions; midway between equal sources the net field is zero, and the lines curve away from the midline.
  • Field lines never cross. A crossing would mean two field directions at one point.
Assessor's advice 2024 examination report, on two magnets with like poles facing: "The net field between two like poles is effectively zero, as the field lines repel each other." Adding the two field magnitudes instead of the two field vectors gives a doubled field — the classic error this configuration is designed to catch.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Compare gravitational, electric and magnetic fields with reference to the existence of monopoles and dipoles. (3 marks)

Worked solution

Gravitational fields have monopoles only. Masses only attract, so every mass acts as an isolated sink of field lines and there is no repulsive counterpart with which to form a dipole. (1 mark)

Electric fields have both monopoles and dipoles. An isolated positive or negative charge is a monopole; an equal positive and negative charge placed together form a dipole. (1 mark)

Magnetic fields have dipoles only. North and south poles always occur together — cutting a magnet produces two smaller dipoles — and an isolated magnetic pole has never been observed. (1 mark)

1 mark per field: classification plus a supporting reason. Accept: "source/sink" language; "no magnetic monopole has ever been detected". Do not accept: a bare classification with no reason, or any claim that magnetic monopoles or gravitational dipoles exist.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Magnus places two identical bar magnets on a bench in a straight line with their north poles facing each other across a 4.0 cm gap, as shown in FIG 1.1. Point P lies midway between the two north poles.

Explain why the magnitude of the magnetic field at point P is close to zero. (2 marks)

Worked solution

The field of each magnet at P points directly away from that magnet's north pole, so the field due to the left-hand magnet points right and the field due to the right-hand magnet points left — the two fields are in opposite directions. (1 mark)

The magnets are identical and P is equidistant from both, so the two fields have equal magnitudes. The vector sum of two equal and opposite fields is approximately zero. (1 mark)

1 mark: the two fields at P point in opposite directions, each directed away from the facing north pole · 1 mark: equal magnitudes (identical magnets, midpoint), so the vector sum is approximately zero. Do not accept: "the fields cancel" with no direction reasoning, or adding the magnitudes to claim a stronger field.

KK 2.3 investigate and compare theoretically and practically gravitational fields and electrical fields about a point mass or charge (positive or negative) with reference to: the direction of the field; the shape of the field; the use of the inverse square law to determine the magnitude of the field; potential energy changes (qualitative) associated with a point mass or charge moving in the field

What you must be able to do

  • State and draw the direction and shape of the field about a point mass (radial, inward) and about a point charge (radial; outward for positive, inward for negative).
  • Use the inverse square law to scale field magnitudes with distance, measured from the centre of the source.
  • Describe, without calculation, how potential energy changes when a mass or charge moves in a radial field.
g = G Mr² on formula sheet g gravitational field strength (N kg⁻¹) · G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻² · M source mass (kg) · r distance from the centre of the source (m)
E = k Qr² on formula sheet E electric field strength (N C⁻¹) · k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N m² C⁻² · Q source charge (C) · r distance from the centre of the charge (m)

Fine detail and subtleties

  • The gravitational field of a point mass and the electric field of a negative point charge are drawn identically: radial lines pointing inward. The electric diagram flips outward when the source charge is positive; the gravitational diagram never flips.
  • Inverse square scaling: doubling r quarters the field; tripling r divides it by nine. The ratio form g2 = g1(r1/r2)² not on sheet answers scaling questions with no source mass needed.
  • r is measured from the centre of the source, not its surface. "At a distance 3R from the centre" and "at an altitude of 3R" differ by one Earth radius.
  • Between two positive charges there is a point of zero field. It lies closer to the smaller charge, and the two distances are in the ratio of the square roots of the charges.
  • Potential energy, qualitatively: moving against the field force increases potential energy; moving with it decreases potential energy. For attraction (mass–mass, or opposite charges), increasing separation increases PE. For repulsion (like charges), increasing separation decreases PE. Gravity has no repulsive case.
  • Field versus force: E = kQ/r² involves one charge (the source); F = kq1q2/r² involves two. If a question gives one charge and asks for the field, Coulomb's law is the wrong formula.
Assessor's advice On the 2025 paper's net-field question, "Many students tried to use" Coulomb's law where the field of a single charge was required, and the report recommends "that students should be familiar with electrical fields as they are with gravitational and magnetic fields." Electric fields are assessed as the weakest of the three and require at least equal practice.
Trap Linear thinking on an inverse square law. Doubling the distance does not halve the field — it quarters it. MC distractors are computed from the linear error, so a wrong method lands exactly on an offered option.
In the style of VCAA · MC

The gravitational field strength at the surface of a planet of radius R is 6.3 N kg⁻¹. Which one of the following is closest to the gravitational field strength at a distance of 3R from the centre of the planet? (1 mark)

  1. 0.39 N kg⁻¹
  2. 0.70 N kg⁻¹
  3. 2.1 N kg⁻¹
  4. 57 N kg⁻¹
Worked solution

B. The field obeys the inverse square law, with distance measured from the centre. The surface is at r = R, so at r = 3R: g = 6.3 × (R/3R)² = 6.3 ÷ 9 = 0.70 N kg⁻¹.

Why each distractor is wrong: A (0.39 N kg⁻¹) treats 3R as an altitude, using a total distance of 4R. C (2.1 N kg⁻¹) divides by 3 — the inverse-linear error. D (57 N kg⁻¹) multiplies by 9 instead of dividing — an inverted ratio.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Ishva considers two situations. In the first, a space probe coasts directly away from the Moon, moving from a distance r to a distance 2r from the Moon's centre. In the second, a small positively charged sphere moves directly away from a fixed positively charged sphere, its separation increasing from r to 2r. No calculations are required.

a. State whether the gravitational potential energy of the probe–Moon system increases or decreases. Explain your answer. (2 marks)

b. State whether the electric potential energy of the two charged spheres increases or decreases. Explain your answer. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. The gravitational potential energy increases. The gravitational force exerted on the probe by the Moon is attractive, directed toward the Moon's centre; the probe moves opposite to this force, so work is done against the field and the potential energy of the system increases.

1 mark: increases · 1 mark: the probe's displacement is opposite to the attractive force, so work is done against the field.

b. The electric potential energy decreases. The force exerted on the moving sphere by the fixed sphere is repulsive, directed away from the fixed sphere; the sphere's displacement is in the same direction as this force, so the field does positive work on the sphere and the potential energy decreases.

1 mark: decreases · 1 mark: the displacement is in the direction of the repulsive force, so the field does positive work. Do not accept: "potential energy always increases with separation" — that holds only when the force is attractive. Common error: transferring the gravitational result to the electric case without checking the force direction; award the claim mark only if the reasoning matches the claim.

KK 2.4 investigate and apply theoretically and practically a field model to magnetic phenomena, including shapes and directions of fields produced by bar magnets, and by current-carrying wires, loops and solenoids

What you must be able to do

  • Draw and interpret the four standard magnetic field shapes: bar magnet, straight current-carrying wire, single loop, and solenoid.
  • Apply the right-hand grip rule both ways: thumb along the current of a straight wire (fingers give the circular field), and fingers along the current of a coil (thumb gives the field inside, pointing to the N end).
  • Use the into-page (×) and out-of-page (•) notation, with a key, the first time it appears in an answer.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • Outside a magnet, field lines run from N to S; inside, they continue from S back to N so that every line is a closed loop. VCE diagrams show the external lines only. The closed-loop form follows from the absence of magnetic monopoles.
  • Inside a solenoid carrying a constant current the field is strong and uniform: parallel, equally spaced lines along the axis. Outside, the field matches a bar magnet's, and the N end is the end where the lines exit.
  • Around a straight wire the field lines are concentric circles whose spacing grows with distance — the field weakens, but never points toward or away from the wire.
  • Earth's field is modelled as a bar magnet's. A compass needle's north end points toward geographic north, so the pole near geographic north is a magnetic south pole — Earth's field lines enter the Earth at geographic north.
  • Drawing standards that earn marks: at least five lines when asked, an arrowhead on every line, sensible symmetric spacing, lines denser where the field is stronger, and no lines crossing or touching.
Trap Arrow direction is physics content and is assessed. A field-line diagram with missing arrowheads, crossing lines, or lines running S to N outside the magnet is a wrong answer even when the overall shape looks right.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

FIG 1.2 shows a bar magnet lying on a bench. Using arrows, draw at least five field lines to represent the magnetic field surrounding the magnet. (2 marks)

Worked solution

A correct response shows at least five continuous lines leaving the north pole, curving around the magnet and entering the south pole, with an arrowhead on every line pointing from N toward S outside the magnet. The lines are most closely spaced at the poles, approximately symmetric above and below the magnet, and no lines cross or touch.

1 mark: direction — every line carries an arrowhead, and the lines run from the north pole around to the south pole · 1 mark: shape and spacing — at least five smooth curved lines, closest together near the poles, roughly symmetric, with no crossings. Accept: a straight line along the magnet's axis; lines meeting the pole faces. Do not accept: lines without arrowheads; lines from S to N outside the magnet; straight radial lines as if for a point charge. Fewer than five lines: maximum 1 mark. Diagrams may be drawn in pencil; written responses should be in ink, because papers are marked from scans.

Assessor's advice On the 2024 paper's field-line sketching question (an electric field), "The most common error was to draw the lines pointing downwards" — students reproduced a remembered picture instead of reasoning from the configuration printed on the page. The same discipline applies to magnetic sketches: read the source arrangement first, then decide the direction; only then draw.
In the style of VCAA · Section B

Lucia winds a solenoid on a cardboard tube and connects it to a battery. Viewed from the left-hand end, the conventional current circulates clockwise, as shown in FIG 1.3.

State which end of the solenoid behaves as a north pole. Justify your answer. (2 marks)

Worked solution

The right-hand end behaves as a north pole. (1 mark)

By the right-hand grip rule for a coil — fingers curled in the direction of the conventional current, thumb along the field inside — the field inside the solenoid points from the left-hand end toward the right-hand end. The field lines therefore exit at the right-hand end, which makes that end the north pole. (1 mark)

1 mark: right-hand end identified · 1 mark: justification via the grip rule, linking the current direction to the internal field direction and to where the lines exit. Accept: "clockwise current viewed from an end makes that end a south pole" applied correctly. Do not accept: the correct end with no justification — the 2024 report states that where a choice must be explained, "there must be an attempted explanation in order for any marks to be awarded"; a bare name is treated as a guess.

KK 2.5 identify fields as static or changing, and as uniform or non-uniform

What you must be able to do

  • Define the two classifications: static means the field's magnitude and direction at each point are constant over time; uniform means the field has the same magnitude and direction at every point in a region of space.
  • Classify standard examples on both axes at once.

Fine detail and subtleties

  • The two axes are independent — static does not imply uniform. A stationary bar magnet's field is static and non-uniform; the field inside a solenoid carrying a constant current is static and uniform; the field inside a solenoid driven by alternating current is changing yet uniform across the interior at each instant.
  • Other stock classifications: a point charge or point mass gives a static, non-uniform field; the field between parallel charged plates at constant voltage, and the gravitational field near Earth's surface, are modelled as static and uniform.
  • A steady current produces a static field. The motion of the charges does not make the field "changing" — changing refers to variation in time at a point, not to anything moving inside the source.
  • Both uniform-field models are idealisations: plate fields fringe at the edges, and near-surface gravitational field lines converge very slightly. State the assumption ("Assume the field between the plates is uniform.") when a question relies on it.
  • Changing fields are required later in the course: Unit 3 AOS 3 treats electricity generation using fields that change with time. This vocabulary is the basis for that distinction.
Assessor's advice The 2025 paper assessed this dot point almost word for word: "Both the strength and direction of a static field are constant over time. A non-uniform field varies across the region." 81% of students answered correctly. These are recall marks, available to a student who has learned the two definitions verbatim.
In the style of VCAA · MC

A solenoid is connected to a battery that supplies a constant current. Which one of the following best describes the magnetic field inside the solenoid? (1 mark)

  1. changing and uniform
  2. changing and non-uniform
  3. static and non-uniform
  4. static and uniform
Worked solution

D. The current is constant, so the field's magnitude and direction at each point do not change over time: static. Inside a solenoid the field lines are parallel and equally spaced, so the field has the same magnitude and direction at every interior point: uniform.

Why each distractor is wrong: A and B call the field "changing" because charges are moving — a steady current produces a constant field; changing refers to variation in time, not to motion within the source. C describes the field of a bar magnet or the region outside the solenoid, which is non-uniform.

Mixed practice

Two multi-part questions spanning KK 2.1–2.5. Information given at the head of a question applies to every part that follows.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Kenji compares two field diagrams: the gravitational field of the Moon and the electric field of an isolated electron. No calculations are required.

a. State the direction of the gravitational field of the Moon at a point above its surface. (1 mark)

b. Compare the shapes and directions of the two fields. (2 marks)

c. Kenji claims that, because the two diagrams look identical, every feature of electric fields must have a gravitational counterpart. Explain why the existence of electric dipoles shows that this claim is incorrect. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Radially inward — at every point the field is directed toward the centre of the Moon.

1 mark: toward the Moon's centre (accept "radially inward"). Do not accept: "downward" without reference to the Moon's centre.

b. Similarity: both fields are radial and point inward toward the source, with straight field lines whose spacing increases with distance as the field weakens by the inverse square law. Difference: the electron's field points inward only because its charge is negative — the field of a positive charge points outward — whereas a gravitational field points inward for every mass, with no outward case.

1 mark: one valid similarity (radial shape; inward direction; inverse square weakening) · 1 mark: the difference — electric field direction depends on the sign of the source, gravitational fields always point inward. A Compare answer needs both a similarity and a difference.

c. An electric dipole exists because charge comes in two kinds: an equal positive and negative charge can be paired, giving a field with both a source and a sink. Mass comes in one kind only and masses only attract, so there is no repulsive "negative mass" to pair with a mass — a gravitational dipole cannot exist, and the analogy between the two fields breaks down.

1 mark: a dipole requires two opposite kinds of source, which charge provides · 1 mark: masses only attract (one kind of source only), so no gravitational dipole is possible. Do not accept: generic statements about fields being "different" that do not connect the dipole to the two signs of charge.

In the style of VCAA · Section B

Lucia winds a solenoid on a cardboard tube and connects it to a battery that supplies a constant current of 1.5 A.

a. Identify the magnetic field inside the solenoid as static or changing, and as uniform or non-uniform. (1 mark)

b. Lucia replaces the battery with a supply that delivers an alternating current. Explain how each of the two classifications in part a is affected. (2 marks)

c. Lucia then holds a compass far from the solenoid and observes that the needle's north end points toward geographic north. Explain what this observation indicates about the type of magnetic pole located near Earth's geographic north. (2 marks)

Worked solution

a. Static and uniform.

1 mark: both classifications correct. Consequential marking applies in part b to a wrong part-a answer used consistently.

b. The field becomes changing: the alternating current varies with time, so the field's magnitude varies and its direction reverses every half cycle. The field remains uniform: at any instant the turns still produce parallel, equally spaced lines across the interior — uniformity describes variation across space, and that is unaffected by variation in time.

1 mark: static becomes changing, with the reason tied to the time-varying current · 1 mark: uniform is unchanged, with the reason that uniformity concerns variation in space at an instant. Do not accept: "the field is changing because charges are moving" — a steady current also has moving charges yet gives a static field.

c. A compass needle's north end aligns with the local field and is attracted toward a magnetic south pole. Since the needle's north end points toward geographic north, the pole located near geographic north is a magnetic south pole — Earth's field lines therefore point into the Earth near geographic north.

1 mark: the needle's north end is attracted to a magnetic south pole (or: points along field lines, which end on south poles) · 1 mark: conclusion — a magnetic south pole lies near geographic north. Do not accept: "geographic north is a north pole because the compass points there" — that reverses the attraction rule.
