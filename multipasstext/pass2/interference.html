13 · Diffraction and interference

Waves spread around corners and add where they overlap. The first behaviour, diffraction, sets a hard limit on what any wave can image; the second, staged in Young’s double-slit experiment, produced the striped pattern that established light as a wave.

Diffraction the λ/w ratio

Diffraction is the spreading of a wave into the region behind a gap or an obstacle — the directional spread of the wavefronts away from straight-line travel. It is a change of direction only: the wavelength, frequency and speed of the wave are unchanged. Every wave diffracts — sound, water waves and light all spread when they pass an edge.

How much a wave spreads is governed by a single comparison: the wavelength λ against the width w of the gap (or the size of the obstacle).

diffraction ∝ λw not on sheet λ wavelength (m) · w gap width or obstacle size (m) · diffraction is significant when λ is comparable to w or larger (λ/w of order 1) and negligible when λ/w ≪ 1

When λ/w ≪ 1 the wave passes almost straight through, with only slight spreading at the edges. As the gap narrows (or λ grows) the spread increases; once λ ≥ w, the gap behaves as a point source and the emerging wavefronts spread into near-semicircles, filling the whole region beyond, as shown in Figure 10.1. This is why conversation carries through a doorway: for sound of wavelength 0.75 m through a 0.90 m doorway, λ/w = 0.83, so the sound bends far into the room — while for green light, λ/w = 5.5 × 10⁻⁷ ÷ 0.90 = 6.1 × 10⁻⁷, and the light travels in straight lines with sharp shadows.

An obstacle works the same way in reverse: the wave bends around it and fills in behind, so an obstacle casts a clean shadow only if it is much larger than λ. An AM radio signal of wavelength 3.0 × 10² m diffracts around a hill and is received behind it; a mobile-phone signal of wavelength 0.15 m leaves a dead spot behind the same hill.

Diffraction sets the limit on imaging. A wave can only carry information about an object if the object disturbs it — but a wave diffracts almost unchanged around anything much smaller than its own wavelength, leaving no shadow and no echo of the detail. Visible light, with wavelengths from 4.0 × 10⁻⁷ m to 7.0 × 10⁻⁷ m, therefore cannot resolve structures much smaller than this, however good the microscope: many viruses, at less than 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ m across, are invisible to it. Imaging finer detail requires a smaller wavelength.

Key idea A wave cannot be used to resolve detail much smaller than its own wavelength — diffraction removes information about that detail. Smaller detail requires smaller λ.
Connects This limit is why electron microscopes exist. In 15 · Matter waves electrons turn out to have wavelengths thousands of times shorter than visible light, so an electron beam can image individual atoms that no light microscope can ever see.

Young’s double-slit experiment path difference

In 1803 Thomas Young passed light from a single source through two narrow slits and observed the result on a screen. The particle model of the day predicted two bright strips, one behind each slit. What appeared instead was a long row of evenly spaced bright and dark fringes — including dark fringes at places that light from either slit alone could reach.

The arrangement matters. The two slits are lit by the same source, so the waves leaving them are coherent: they have the same frequency and a constant phase difference. Two independent lamps cannot do this — their phase difference changes rapidly and randomly, so any pattern shifts too fast to observe. Each slit is also made narrow, comparable to λ, so that the light diffracts strongly at each one: the two slits then behave as two coherent point sources whose waves overlap across the whole screen.

Where the overlapping waves arrive, the outcome depends on the path difference — the difference between the distances travelled from each slit to that point, as shown in Figure 10.2. If the path difference is a whole number of wavelengths, the waves arrive in phase, add constructively, and the point is bright. If it is an odd number of half wavelengths, crest meets trough, the waves cancel, and the point is dark.

path difference = nλ on formula sheet constructive interference — bright fringe · n = 0, 1, 2, … · λ wavelength (m)
path difference = (n + ½)λ on formula sheet destructive interference — dark fringe · n = 0, 1, 2, …

At the centre of the screen, equidistant from both slits, the path difference is zero — a whole number of wavelengths with n = 0 — so the pattern always has a bright central maximum. Moving out from the centre, the path difference grows steadily: the first dark fringe appears where it reaches ½λ, the next bright fringe where it reaches 1λ, and so on.

Trap In both formulas n counts from zero. The central maximum is the n = 0 bright fringe (path difference 0λ), and the first dark fringe is n = 0 (path difference ½λ). Counting outward from the central maximum, the m-th bright fringe has path difference mλ and the m-th dark fringe has (m − ½)λ — a question about the third dark fringe expects 2.5λ; writing 3.5λ is the classic counting error.

This pattern is the decisive evidence for the wave nature of light. Superposition — two disturbances adding to give reinforcement in some places and complete cancellation in others — is behaviour that only waves show: sound from two loudspeakers and water waves from two dippers produce exactly the same fringe geometry. Particles cannot cancel; two streams of particles arriving at the same point could never produce darkness there.

Connects A model is accepted when it predicts a result that was not anticipated. The wave model accounts for this pattern; in 14 · The photon the same model meets an experiment it cannot explain.

Fringe spacing Δx = λL/d

Near the centre of the pattern the fringes are evenly spaced. The fringe spacing Δx is the distance between the centres of adjacent bright fringes — or equally between adjacent dark fringes; the two spacings are the same, as shown in Figure 10.3.

Δx = λLd on formula sheet Δx fringe spacing (m) · λ wavelength (m) · L slit-to-screen distance (m) · d slit separation (m) — valid when L ≫ d

Each variable acts independently. A longer wavelength spreads the pattern: red fringes sit wider than blue, so increasing λ increases Δx. Moving the screen further away magnifies the whole pattern: increasing L increases Δx. Pushing the slits apart squeezes the fringes together: increasing d decreases Δx, because d sits in the denominator.

Rearranged as λ = Δx d/L, the same geometry becomes a measuring instrument: from three lengths measurable with a ruler, Young determined the wavelength of visible light — a few ten-millionths of a metre — a century before anything was known about photons.

Worked example — fringe spacing of a green laser

Ishva directs a green laser pointer of wavelength 532 nm through a pair of slits separated by 0.40 mm. She models the slits as two coherent point sources and observes the pattern on a screen 1.8 m away, so L ≫ d and the fringe-spacing formula applies.

Formula: Δx = λLd with λ = 5.32 × 10⁻⁷ m and d = 4.0 × 10⁻⁴ m.

Substitution: Δx = (5.32 × 10⁻⁷ × 1.8) ÷ (4.0 × 10⁻⁴)

Answer: Δx = 2.4 × 10⁻³ m = 2.4 mm. Replacing the laser with a red one of longer wavelength, or halving the slit separation, would widen this spacing.

Trap Δx = λL/d mixes units of very different sizes: λ arrives in nm, d in mm, L in m. Convert every length to metres before substituting — leaving d as 0.40 instead of 4.0 × 10⁻⁴ makes the answer 1000 times too small. The result should be checked for plausibility: fringe spacings on a laboratory bench are millimetres, not metres.

Quick checks

Check 10.1 Magnus stands inside a room behind a doorway 0.90 m wide. He can hear a conversation from the corridor (sound of wavelength 0.75 m) but cannot see the speakers. Explain, using the ratio λ/w.
For the sound, λ/w = 0.75 ÷ 0.90 = 0.83 ≈ 1, so diffraction is significant and the sound spreads around the doorway into the room. For visible light, λ/w ≈ 5.5 × 10⁻⁷ ÷ 0.90 = 6.1 × 10⁻⁷ ≪ 1, so the light passes straight through with negligible spreading and cannot bend around the corner to Magnus.
Check 10.2 In a double-slit pattern, light from the two slits reaches point P with a path difference of 2.5λ. State whether P is bright or dark, and which fringe from the centre it is.
Dark — 2.5λ is an odd number of half wavelengths, matching path difference = (n + ½)λ with n = 2. Since the first dark fringe is n = 0, n = 2 makes P the third dark fringe from the centre.
Check 10.3 A red laser of wavelength 650 nm illuminates two slits separated by 0.25 mm, with a screen 1.5 m away. Calculate the fringe spacing.
Δx = λL/d = (6.50 × 10⁻⁷ × 1.5) ÷ (2.5 × 10⁻⁴) = 3.9 × 10⁻³ m = 3.9 mm — all three lengths converted to metres before substituting.
Check 10.4 In a double-slit experiment the screen distance is doubled and the slit separation is halved, with the same laser. Determine the factor by which the fringe spacing changes.
Δx ∝ L/d, so doubling L doubles Δx and halving d doubles it again: the fringe spacing increases by a factor of 4. Applying only one of the two changes — or inverting the effect of d — gives the classic wrong answers of 2 or ¼.
Check 10.5 Two identical lamps placed side by side produce no interference pattern on a screen, yet one lamp behind a pair of narrow slits does. Explain the difference.
Independent lamps are not coherent — their phase difference changes rapidly and randomly, so the bright and dark positions shift far too quickly to observe. The two slits sample the same wavefront from a single source, so the waves leaving them keep a constant phase difference and the pattern is stable.
