12 · Light as a wave

Light is a travelling pair of changing electric and magnetic fields. This page states what oscillates in a light wave, why every electromagnetic wave moves at the same speed in a vacuum, and how a wave superposed with its own reflection produces a standing wave.

The electromagnetic wave E ⊥ B ⊥ travel

In Unit 3, every field belonged to a source. A charge carried its electric field with it; a current sustained the magnetic field around its wire. Light arises when a charge accelerates: the field around it must rearrange, and the rearrangement travels outward as a disturbance in the field itself.

The disturbance is self-sustaining. A changing electric field generates a changing magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field generates a changing electric field, so each field continually regenerates the other. Once launched, the travelling pair requires no charge, no current and no medium — it propagates through empty space at the speed of light.

Light is therefore a transverse electromagnetic wave produced by the acceleration of charges. The electric field oscillates in one plane, the magnetic field oscillates in the perpendicular plane, and both are perpendicular to the direction of travel, as shown in Figure 6.1. Sound, by contrast, is a longitudinal vibration of a medium and cannot cross a vacuum; an electromagnetic wave is an oscillation of fields, not of matter, which is why sunlight reaches Earth across empty space.

Any accelerating charge radiates. Electrons driven up and down a broadcast antenna produce radio waves; electrons oscillating within hot atoms produce visible light. Circular motion is acceleration too — a charge moving in a circle at constant speed is continuously changing direction, and it radiates continuously.

Key idea Light is a transverse electromagnetic wave produced by accelerating charges: a changing electric field and a changing magnetic field regenerate each other, perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel, with no medium required.
Connects The Australian Synchrotron from 8 · Motors & accelerators is a light source by design: electrons forced around the storage ring are continuously accelerating, and accelerating charges radiate. Unit 3 AOS 3 supplies the partner idea — changing magnetic fields driving currents — when electricity generation is studied.

One speed, one spectrum c = fλ

Radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays and gamma rays are all the same kind of wave: changing electric and magnetic fields regenerating each other. They differ only in frequency and wavelength. In a vacuum, all electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed, c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹.

c = fλ on formula sheet c speed of light in a vacuum = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ · f frequency (Hz) · λ wavelength (m) — printed on the sheet in the general form v = fλ

The frequency of a wave is set by its source — by how rapidly the charges that produced it oscillated — and does not change as the wave travels. Because the product fλ is fixed at c in a vacuum, frequency and wavelength are inversely proportional: doubling the frequency halves the wavelength.

Ordered from lowest frequency to highest, the electromagnetic spectrum runs radio, microwave, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma. The visible band is a narrow window within it, from about 750 nm at the red end to about 380 nm at the violet end. The boundaries between neighbouring bands are conventions of use, not changes of physics: an X-ray and a radio wave obey exactly the same wave equation.

Trap Higher frequency does not mean higher speed. In a vacuum a gamma ray and a radio wave travel at exactly the same speed, c; the greater frequency of the gamma ray is matched by a proportionally shorter wavelength, never by a greater speed.

Worked example — frequency of laser light

Kenji directs a green laser pointer across the laboratory and reads its label: wavelength 532 nm. He models the beam as an electromagnetic wave travelling at c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹.

Formula: c = fλ, so f = cλ with λ = 532 nm = 5.32 × 10⁻⁷ m.

Substitution: f = (3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ (5.32 × 10⁻⁷)

Answer: f = 5.6 × 10¹⁴ Hz. Every visible wavelength gives a frequency on this scale — hundreds of terahertz, far above the hundreds of kilohertz of AM radio.

Standing waves λn = 2L/n

When a travelling wave reaches a fixed end, it reflects and returns inverted. The string then carries two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions, and their displacements add at every point and every instant — superposition.

At certain points the two waves always arrive with opposite displacements and cancel. These points of permanent zero displacement are nodes. Midway between the nodes are antinodes, where the string oscillates with twice the amplitude of either travelling wave; adjacent nodes are λ/2 apart, and adjacent antinodes likewise. The pattern travels neither left nor right: it is a standing wave, formed from the superposition of a travelling wave and its reflection.

A string fixed at both ends must have a node at each end — the study design treats only standing waves with nodes at both ends. Between two fixed ends a distance L apart, a standing wave can form only if a whole number of half-wavelengths fits exactly: L = nλ/2. Rearranged, the allowed wavelengths are:

λn = 2Ln not on sheet λn wavelength of the nth harmonic (m) · L distance between the fixed ends (m) · n = 1, 2, 3, … the harmonic number
fn = nv2L = nf1 not on sheet fn frequency of the nth harmonic (Hz) · v speed of the travelling waves (m s⁻¹) · f1 fundamental frequency (Hz) — follows from v = fλ and λn = 2L/n

Here n is the harmonic number: n = 1 is the fundamental (the first harmonic), the lowest frequency the string can sustain; n = 2 is the second harmonic, and so on. The value of n equals the number of antinodes, and the number of nodes is n + 1, counting both ends. Because fn = nf1, the allowed frequencies are whole-number multiples of the fundamental, as shown in Figure 6.2.

Key idea A standing wave is the superposition of a travelling wave and its inverted reflection. Nodes never move; with nodes at both ends only the wavelengths λn = 2L/n fit, so the string sustains only the frequencies fn = nf1.

Worked example — harmonics on a stretched string

Ishva stretches a light string of length 1.20 m between two rigid clamps and drives it with a small oscillator. Waves travel along this string at 240 m s⁻¹, and she increases the driving frequency slowly from zero.

Formula: standing waves require λn = 2Ln, so the fundamental (n = 1) has λ1 = 2 × 1.20 = 2.40 m.

Substitution: f1 = vλ1 = 240 ÷ 2.40

Evaluation: f1 = 100 Hz. The third harmonic has λ3 = 2 × 1.20 ÷ 3 = 0.800 m, so f3 = 3f1 = 300 Hz (check: 240 ÷ 0.800 = 300 Hz).

Answer: the string first forms a standing wave at 100 Hz, with a single antinode between the clamps, and again at 200 Hz, 300 Hz and every whole-number multiple of 100 Hz. At the third harmonic, 300 Hz, the string shows three antinodes and four nodes — two at the clamped ends and two between them.

Trap A standing-wave diagram is an envelope, not a frozen shape. The antinodes oscillate between the solid and dashed extremes, and twice in every period the whole string passes through the flat position. A question asking for the shape of the string half a period later expects the mirror image of the starting shape, not the same shape.

Quick checks

Check 9.1 An electromagnetic wave travels horizontally toward the north. Describe the quantities that oscillate in the wave and their directions relative to the direction of travel.
A changing electric field and a changing magnetic field oscillate, perpendicular to each other and both perpendicular to the direction of travel (the wave is transverse) — for example, E vertical and B horizontal east–west, with both regenerating each other as the wave moves north.
Check 9.2 ABC Radio Melbourne broadcasts at a frequency of 774 kHz. Calculate the wavelength of these radio waves, taking their speed as c.
λ = c/f = (3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ (7.74 × 10⁵) = 3.9 × 10² m, a wavelength of nearly four hundred metres.
Check 9.3 A gamma ray has a far higher frequency than a radio wave. Compare the speeds of the two waves in a vacuum.
The speeds are identical — every electromagnetic wave travels at c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ in a vacuum. The higher frequency of the gamma ray corresponds to a proportionally shorter wavelength, since fλ = c, not to a greater speed.
Check 9.4 A string of length 4.0 m is fixed at both ends. Determine whether a standing wave of wavelength 3.0 m can form on this string.
A standing wave requires n = 2L/λ to be a whole number: 2 × 4.0 ÷ 3.0 = 2.7, which is not a whole number, so no standing wave forms. The allowed wavelengths near this value are λ2 = 4.0 m and λ3 = 2.7 m.
Check 9.5 Adjacent nodes on a standing wave are 0.40 m apart, and the string oscillates at 250 Hz. Calculate the speed of the travelling waves on the string.
Adjacent nodes are λ/2 apart, so λ = 2 × 0.40 = 0.80 m; then v = fλ = 250 × 0.80 = 2.0 × 10² m s⁻¹ — the standing wave is built from travelling waves, so v = fλ still applies.
