14 · The photon

A century of interference evidence supported the wave model of light. One experiment was inconsistent with it: light delivers its energy in indivisible packets called photons, and the photoelectric effect measures them.

Photon energy E = hf = hc/λ

Light transfers energy in indivisible packets called photons. A photon of frequency f carries one fixed quantity of energy, proportional to that frequency — it cannot deliver half of its energy, and a surface cannot accumulate it gradually. Planck’s constant h is the constant of proportionality:

E = hf = hcλ on formula sheet E photon energy (J or eV) · h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s · f frequency (Hz) · c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ · λ wavelength (m)

Photon energies in joules are inconveniently small, so this area of study works mostly in the electron-volt: the energy gained by one electron accelerated through a potential difference of 1 V. Applying W = qV with q = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C gives the conversion:

1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J not on sheet eV → J: multiply by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ · J → eV: divide by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ · the electron charge 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C is in the data on the sheet

The formula sheet prints Planck’s constant in both unit systems. Use h = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s when the energy is wanted in electron-volts and h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s when it is wanted in joules — choosing the right value replaces a conversion step.

Worked example — the energy of one green photon

Anika points a green laser pointer of wavelength 532 nm and output power 1.0 mW at a screen. She calculates the energy of each photon, in joules and in electron-volts, and the number of photons leaving the laser each second.

Formula: E = hcλ

Substitution (joules): E = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ (5.32 × 10⁻⁷) = 3.7 × 10⁻¹⁹ J

Substitution (electron-volts): E = (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ (5.32 × 10⁻⁷) = 2.3 eV — the same energy, since 3.74 × 10⁻¹⁹ ÷ 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 2.3.

Answer: each photon carries 2.3 eV. The laser delivers 1.0 × 10⁻³ J of energy each second, so the number of photons emitted per second is n = (1.0 × 10⁻³) ÷ (3.74 × 10⁻¹⁹) = 2.7 × 10¹⁵.

Key idea The energy of each photon is fixed by frequency alone: E = hf. Intensity sets how many photons arrive per second — never how much energy each one carries.

The photoelectric effect Ek max = hf − φ

The photoelectric effect is the ejection of electrons from a metal surface illuminated by light of sufficiently high frequency. The apparatus shown in Figure 8.1 measures it: monochromatic light enters an evacuated tube and strikes a metal plate, ejected photoelectrons cross the gap to a collector electrode, and the resulting photocurrent is read on an ammeter. A variable DC supply sets the potential difference between the electrodes, and its polarity can be reversed.

With the collector positive, every ejected electron is drawn across the gap and the photocurrent saturates: making the collector still more positive collects no extra current, because every photoelectron is already being collected. With the collector negative, each crossing electron must give up kinetic energy W = qV to the opposing field, so as the reverse voltage grows, the slower electrons are turned back first and the current falls.

At the stopping voltage V0 even the fastest photoelectrons are brought to rest before reaching the collector and the photocurrent reaches zero. The stopping voltage therefore measures the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons:

Ek max = eV0 not on sheet Ek max maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons (J) · e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C · V0 stopping voltage (V) — not printed in this form: it is W = qV from the Fields section of the sheet

Worked in electron-volts the conversion disappears: a stopping voltage of 1.40 V means Ek max = 1.40 eV = 2.24 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. Figure 8.2 shows the full photocurrent–voltage curve for the same light at two intensities.

Einstein explained these measurements with the photon model. Each photon is absorbed whole by a single electron — no electron absorbs two — and the most loosely bound electrons need a minimum energy φ, the work function of that metal, to escape the surface. A surface electron keeps whatever is left over:

Ek max = hf − φ on formula sheet Ek max maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons (J or eV) · h Planck’s constant · f frequency of the light (Hz) · φ work function of the metal (J or eV)

Electrons ejected from deeper in the metal lose additional energy on the way out, so they emerge with less than Ek max. Setting Ek max = 0 gives the threshold frequency f0, the minimum frequency that can eject any electron from that metal:

f0 = φh not on sheet f0 threshold frequency (Hz) · φ work function — obtained from Ek max = hf − φ with Ek max = 0

Plotting Ek max against f turns Einstein’s equation into a straight-line graph, shown in Figure 8.3. Comparing Ek max = hf − φ with y = mx + c reads the graph directly: the gradient is h — the same for every metal — the horizontal intercept is f0, and the line extrapolated backwards meets the vertical axis at −φ. Different metals give parallel lines: changing the metal moves both intercepts but can never change the gradient.

In the photon model, the intensity of monochromatic light is set by the number of photons arriving per second, each carrying hf. At a fixed frequency above f0, doubling the intensity doubles the number of photoelectrons ejected per second and therefore doubles the saturation current — but each electron is still ejected by a single photon of the same energy, so Ek max and V0 are unchanged, exactly as Figure 8.2 shows. If instead the frequency is raised while the light power is held constant, each photon carries more energy, so fewer photons arrive per second and the saturation current falls.

Trap Brighter never means faster. Changing the intensity changes the photocurrent only; Ek max and the stopping voltage are set by the frequency and the work function. Equally, light below the threshold frequency ejects nothing, however intense it is and however long it shines.

Worked example — violet light on caesium

Kenji shines violet light of frequency 7.5 × 10¹⁴ Hz onto the caesium surface of a photocell. The work function of caesium is φ = 2.1 eV.

Formula: Ek max = hf − φ, with h = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s because the data are in electron-volts.

Substitution: Ek max = (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 7.5 × 10¹⁴) − 2.1 = 3.105 − 2.1 = 1.0 eV

Answer: Ek max = 1.0 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J, so the stopping voltage is V0 = 1.0 V. The threshold frequency of caesium is f0 = φ/h = 2.1 ÷ (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵) = 5.1 × 10¹⁴ Hz, so the violet light is above the threshold frequency.

Where the wave model fails three predictions

By 1905 the wave model of light had a century of interference evidence behind it. Applied to the photoelectric effect, it makes three clear predictions — and every one of them fails.

  1. More intense light should eject faster electrons. A wave spreads its energy continuously across the wavefront, so a brighter wave should deliver more energy to each electron. Observed: Ek max, measured by the stopping voltage, is independent of intensity — only the photocurrent changes.
  2. Light of any frequency should eject electrons, given enough time. A continuous wave should let an electron accumulate energy until it escapes, whatever the frequency. Observed: below the threshold frequency f0 no electrons are ejected at any intensity, however long the light shines.
  3. Dim light should produce a measurable time delay. At low intensity, a wave would need minutes or longer to feed one electron enough energy to escape. Observed: photoelectrons appear at every intensity with no measurable delay.

The photon model explains all three observations at once. Energy arrives in indivisible packets of hf, one photon ejects one electron, and the transfer is immediate: if hf < φ the electron cannot escape and there is nothing to accumulate, while if hf > φ the very first photons absorbed eject electrons without delay. Frequency controls the energy of each packet; intensity controls only how many packets arrive per second.

Key idea The photoelectric effect is the evidence that light is quantised. The wave model fails on intensity, threshold frequency and time delay; the photon model explains all three with E = hf.
Connects Here a model meets evidence it cannot account for; the reverse case appears on the next page, where electron diffraction contradicts the particle model of matter.

Quick checks

Check 11.1 Calculate the energy of a photon of red light of wavelength 620 nm, in electron-volts and in joules.
Using h in eV s: E = hc/λ = (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ (6.2 × 10⁻⁷) = 2.0 eV; multiplying by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ gives 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.
Check 11.2 An X-ray photon carries 8.0 × 10⁻¹⁵ J of energy. Convert this energy to electron-volts.
Divide by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J per electron-volt: 8.0 × 10⁻¹⁵ ÷ 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 5.0 × 10⁴ eV.
Check 11.3 In a photoelectric experiment the photocurrent falls to zero at a reverse voltage of 0.80 V. State the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons in electron-volts and in joules.
The stopping voltage measures Ek max = eV0, so Ek max = 0.80 eV = 0.80 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 1.3 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.
Check 11.4 Light of frequency 5.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz shines on a metal of work function 2.9 eV. Determine whether photoelectrons are emitted.
Each photon carries hf = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 5.0 × 10¹⁴ = 2.1 eV, which is less than φ = 2.9 eV, so no electrons are emitted: the threshold frequency is f0 = φ/h = 7.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz and this light is below it. Increasing the intensity cannot change this.
Check 11.5 The intensity of the light in Figure 8.2 is doubled while the frequency is unchanged. State what happens to the saturation current and to the stopping voltage.
Twice as many photons arrive per second, so twice as many photoelectrons are ejected and the saturation current doubles; each photon still carries hf, so Ek max and the stopping voltage V0 are unchanged.
