5 · Gravity and orbits

The gravitational field is the first field treated quantitatively: how strong it is, the force it exerts, the energy stored by moving through it, and the orbits it sustains without any engine at all.

Gravitational field strength g = GM/r²

Every mass is surrounded by a gravitational field. The gravitational field strength g at a point is the gravitational force exerted on each kilogram of mass placed at that point. For a spherical body of mass M, the field strength at distance r from its centre is:

g = G Mr² on formula sheet g gravitational field strength (N kg⁻¹) · G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻² · M mass of the central body (kg) · r distance from its centre (m)

Field strength is a vector quantity. At every point it is directed toward the centre of the source mass, so the field of a planet is radial. The magnitude obeys the inverse-square law: doubling the distance from the centre quarters the field.

Substituting the formula-sheet data for Earth (M = 5.97 × 10²⁴ kg, r = RE = 6.37 × 10⁶ m) gives g = (6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴) ÷ (6.37 × 10⁶)² = 9.81 N kg⁻¹ at the surface. At r = 2RE from the centre the field strength is 9.81 ÷ 4 = 2.45 N kg⁻¹, and at 3RE it is 9.81 ÷ 9 = 1.09 N kg⁻¹. Inside the planet the formula does not apply: the field falls toward zero at the centre, so the graph of g against r has a sharp corner at the surface, as shown in Figure 2.1.

The two units of field strength are equivalent: 1 N kg⁻¹ = 1 m s⁻². The force exerted on a mass m by the field is Fg = mg, so a freely falling object has acceleration a = Fg/m = g. The mass cancels — every object in free fall at the same point accelerates at the same rate, whatever its mass.

Key idea Gravitational field strength at a point is the force per kilogram — and therefore the free-fall acceleration of any object — at that point. N kg⁻¹ and m s⁻² are the same unit.
Connects Every projectile and vertical-circle problem in AOS 1 uses this same field: the constant downward acceleration of 9.8 m s⁻² near the surface is g = GM/r² treated as uniform because the height changes are tiny compared with RE.

Gravitational force and Newton’s third law F = Gm1m2/r²

Any two masses attract each other. Newton’s law of universal gravitation gives the magnitude of the force each exerts on the other:

Fg = G m1m2r² on formula sheet Fg gravitational force (N) · m1, m2 the two masses (kg) · r centre-to-centre separation (m)

The two forces form a Newton’s third law pair: the force exerted on the satellite by Earth and the force exerted on Earth by the satellite are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, as shown in Figure 2.2. They never cancel, because they act on different objects. The universal law and the field picture agree: the force exerted on a mass m at a point where the field strength is g is Fg = mg, since g = GM/r².

Trap A satellite “at an altitude of 630 km” is not at r = 6.30 × 10⁵ m. Altitude h is measured from the surface, but r is measured from Earth’s centre: r = RE + h = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 6.30 × 10⁵ = 7.00 × 10⁶ m. Forgetting to add RE, or adding an altitude in km to a radius in m, is the most common error in this topic.

Worked example — gravitational force on a satellite

Lucia tracks a 750 kg communications satellite in a circular orbit at an altitude of 6.30 × 10⁵ m. She models the satellite as a point mass and uses the formula-sheet data for Earth.

Formula: Fg = G m1m2r² with r = RE + h = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 6.30 × 10⁵ = 7.00 × 10⁶ m.

Substitution: Fg = (6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴ × 750) ÷ (7.00 × 10⁶)²

Answer: Fg = 6.09 × 10³ N, directed from the satellite toward Earth’s centre. By Newton’s third law, the satellite exerts a force of 6.09 × 10³ N on Earth, directed from Earth’s centre toward the satellite.

Gravitational potential energy graph methods

Raising a mass through a gravitational field stores gravitational potential energy. Near the surface, where g is effectively constant at 9.81 N kg⁻¹, the change is:

Eg = mgΔh on formula sheet Eg change in gravitational potential energy (J) · m mass (kg) · g field strength (N kg⁻¹) · Δh change in height (m) — valid only while g is approximately constant

For height changes that are small compared with RE — a thrown ball, a lift, an aircraft — this uniform-field approximation is excellent. For a satellite raised through thousands of kilometres it fails, because g itself decreases with altitude. The general method replaces mgΔh with an area.

The change in gravitational potential energy equals the area under a force–distance graph between the two distances — the same work-as-area rule used for springs and trolleys in earlier units. If the graph instead shows field strength g against distance, its area has units of (N kg⁻¹) × m = J kg⁻¹: it gives the energy change per kilogram, and is multiplied by the mass m to give ΔEg. A field–distance graph is independent of the orbiting object, so one graph serves satellites of every mass.

To estimate an area, count grid squares. First find the energy value of one square — the force spanned by its height multiplied by the distance spanned by its width — then count every square that is at least half under the curve. In Figure 2.3 each square represents 500 N × 5.0 × 10⁵ m = 2.5 × 10⁸ J, and about 12.5 squares lie under the curve between r = 7.0 × 10⁶ m and r = 9.0 × 10⁶ m, so ΔEg ≈ 12.5 × 2.5 × 10⁸ ≈ 3.1 × 10⁹ J. Check the axis units before multiplying: distances are often given in km, and axes often carry scale factors such as ×10⁶.

Satellites in circular orbit geostationary example

A satellite in a stable circular orbit has exactly one force acting on it: the gravitational force exerted on it by the central body. No other force balances it. The satellite accelerates continuously toward the centre while its tangential velocity carries it around rather than down. It travels at constant speed but not constant velocity, because its direction of motion changes at every instant.

The gravitational field supplies exactly the centripetal acceleration of uniform circular motion. For a circular orbit of radius r and period T:

v = 2πrT on formula sheet v orbital speed (m s⁻¹) · r orbital radius (m) · T period (s)
a = v²r = 4π²rT² not on sheet a centripetal acceleration (m s⁻²) — follows from Fnet = mv²/r and v = 2πr/T, which are both on the sheet

Setting the field strength equal to the centripetal acceleration ties the orbit to the central mass: GM/r² = v²/r = 4π²r/T². The satellite’s own mass appears nowhere in this equation. For a given central body, choosing the orbital radius fixes the speed and the period — a bolt and a space station at the same radius orbit identically.

Key idea Orbital speed, period and acceleration depend only on the central mass M and the orbital radius r — never on the mass of the satellite.

Worked example — the geostationary orbit

A geostationary satellite remains above the same point on the equator, so receiving dishes never need to track it. Kenji models the satellite’s motion as a uniform circular orbit in the plane of the equator, travelling in the direction of Earth’s rotation with a period of one day, T = 24 h = 8.64 × 10⁴ s.

Formula: the field supplies the centripetal acceleration, G Mr² = 4π²rT², which rearranges to r³ = GMT²4π².

Substitution: r³ = (6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴ × (8.64 × 10⁴)²) ÷ (4π²) = 7.53 × 10²² m³

Evaluation: r = ∛(7.53 × 10²²) = 4.22 × 10⁷ m, so the altitude is h = r − RE = 4.22 × 10⁷ − 6.37 × 10⁶ = 3.59 × 10⁷ m (about 36 000 km above the surface).

Answer: the orbital speed is v = 2πr/T = (2π × 4.22 × 10⁷) ÷ (8.64 × 10⁴) = 3.07 × 10³ m s⁻¹. Every geostationary satellite, whatever its mass, must occupy this one radius.

Apparent weightlessness

An astronaut “floating” aboard the International Space Station has not escaped Earth’s gravitational field. At the ISS altitude of about 400 km, r = 6.77 × 10⁶ m and g = (6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴) ÷ (6.77 × 10⁶)² = 8.7 N kg⁻¹ — about 89% of the surface value.

The astronaut floats because the station and everything inside it are in free fall together, all accelerating at 8.7 m s⁻² toward Earth’s centre. The floor never presses on the astronaut’s feet, so the normal force is zero: FN = 0 while Fg is large. Apparent weightlessness is the absence of a normal force, not the absence of gravitational force.

Trap “There is no gravity in space” is wrong twice over. The field at ISS altitude is about 89% of its surface value, and without it there would be no orbit at all — the satellite would move in a straight line at constant speed. Weightlessness in orbit means FN = 0; it never means Fg = 0.
Connects Unit 4 returns to these orbits: time signals from GPS satellites must be corrected for the effects of special relativity due to their orbital speed, so the circular-orbit model on this page is the starting point for a measurable relativistic effect.

Quick checks

Check 2.1 Planet X has the same mass as Earth but twice Earth’s radius. Determine the gravitational field strength at its surface. Take Earth’s surface value as 9.81 N kg⁻¹.
Doubling the distance from the centre quarters the field (inverse-square law), so g = 9.81 ÷ 4 = 2.45 N kg⁻¹, directed toward the centre of Planet X.
Check 2.2 A satellite orbits at an altitude of 600 km above Earth’s surface. State the value of r that must be used in g = GM/r².
r is measured from Earth’s centre, not its surface: r = RE + h = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 6.00 × 10⁵ = 6.97 × 10⁶ m.
Check 2.3 The force exerted on the Moon by Earth has magnitude 2.0 × 10²⁰ N. State the magnitude and direction of the force exerted on Earth by the Moon.
2.0 × 10²⁰ N, directed from Earth’s centre toward the Moon — the two forces are a Newton’s third law pair, equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, acting on different objects.
Check 2.4 The area under a gravitational field–distance graph between two orbital radii is 1.2 × 10⁷ J kg⁻¹. Calculate the change in gravitational potential energy of a 300 kg satellite moved between those radii.
The area under a field–distance graph gives the energy change per kilogram, so it is multiplied by the mass: ΔEg = 1.2 × 10⁷ × 300 = 3.6 × 10⁹ J.
Check 2.5 Anika, an astronaut of mass 60 kg, floats inside the International Space Station at an altitude of 400 km. Is the gravitational force acting on her zero? Explain.
No — the field strength there is about 8.7 N kg⁻¹ (89% of the surface value), so the force exerted on Anika by Earth is about 8.7 × 60 ≈ 5.2 × 10² N. She floats because she and the station are in free fall together, so the normal force on her is zero.
