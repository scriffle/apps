6 · Electromagnetic induction

Moving a magnet near a coil of wire creates a voltage from motion alone. This page builds the three ideas behind every power station: magnetic flux, Faraday’s law for the size of the induced emf, and Lenz’s law for its direction.

Magnetic flux ΦB = B⊥A

Every generator on the grid begins with a magnetic field passing through a loop of wire. Magnetic flux ΦB measures how much magnetic field passes through a loop — pictured with field lines, it counts the lines threading the loop’s window. For a loop of area A:

ΦB = B⊥A on formula sheet ΦB magnetic flux (Wb) · B⊥ component of the magnetic field perpendicular to the plane of the loop (T) · A area of the loop (m²)

The subscript on B⊥ carries the geometry. It is the component of the field perpendicular to the plane of the loop — the part that passes straight through the window rather than skimming across it. The flux is therefore a maximum, ΦB = BA, when the field is perpendicular to the plane of the loop, and zero when the field lies parallel to that plane, because no field lines then pass through the loop. At intermediate angles the flux lies between these two values, as shown in Figure 6.1; VCE Physics requires only this qualitative statement, never a calculation at an angle.

The unit of flux is the weber (Wb): 1 Wb = 1 T m². A 0.20 T field directed perpendicular to the plane of a 5.0 cm × 5.0 cm loop (A = 0.050 × 0.050 = 2.5 × 10⁻³ m²) gives ΦB = 0.20 × 2.5 × 10⁻³ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁴ Wb. Areas must be in m² before substituting — converting the side lengths to metres first is the safest route, since 1 cm² is 10⁻⁴ m², not 10⁻² m².

Key idea Flux is the field through the loop’s window: ΦB = B⊥A, maximum when the field is perpendicular to the plane of the loop, zero when the field is parallel to that plane.

Because ΦB = B⊥A, there are exactly three ways to change the flux through a loop: change the field strength B (move a magnet nearer or further, or vary the current in an electromagnet); change the area A inside the field (slide the loop into or out of a field region); or change the orientation (rotate the loop so that B⊥ changes). The third method can reverse the direction of the flux through the loop, and it is the method every generator on the grid uses — the subject of the next page.

Faraday’s law ε = −NΔΦB/Δt

In 1831 Michael Faraday discovered that a changing magnetic flux through a coil induces an emf — a voltage able to drive a current around a closed circuit. The effect appears whenever the flux changes, by any of the three methods above, and it vanishes the instant the flux stops changing. Faraday’s law gives the size of the induced emf:

ε = −N ΔΦBΔt on formula sheet ε induced emf (V) · N number of turns in the coil · ΔΦB change in magnetic flux through one turn (Wb) · Δt time taken for the change (s) · the negative sign records the direction of the induced emf (Lenz’s law, next section)

Everything in Faraday’s law is a rate. The induced emf measures how fast the flux changes — ΔΦB divided by Δt — and does not depend on how large the flux is. A coil threaded by an enormous steady flux has zero emf; a coil whose small flux is destroyed in a millisecond can have a large one. Halving the time taken for the same flux change doubles the emf.

Trap “An emf is induced because there is flux through the coil” — no. Flux alone induces nothing: a magnet resting stationary inside a coil produces a large flux and exactly zero emf. Only the rate of change of flux induces an emf, so every explanation of induction must name the change.

A coil of N turns behaves as N loops in series. The same changing flux threads every turn, and the per-turn emfs add, so doubling the number of turns doubles the emf. In the formula, ΔΦB is the change in flux through one turn; the factor N does the multiplying.

Worked example — coil pulled out of a field

Shanna holds a 200-turn rectangular coil, 5.0 cm × 8.0 cm, with its plane perpendicular to a uniform 0.50 T magnetic field. She pulls the coil completely out of the field in 0.10 s.

Formula: ε = N ΔΦBΔt (magnitude), with ΔΦB = B⊥A = 0.50 × (0.050 × 0.080) = 2.0 × 10⁻³ Wb for each turn.

Substitution: ε = 200 × 2.0 × 10⁻³ ÷ 0.10

Answer: ε = 4.0 V — the average emf while the coil leaves the field. The same withdrawal completed in 0.050 s would give 8.0 V: an identical flux change at twice the rate.

On a graph of flux against time, the induced emf is read from the gradient: ε = −N × gradient of the ΦB–t graph. A straight ramp means a constant emf; a steeper ramp means a larger one; a turning point — flux momentarily at a maximum — means zero emf at that instant. The relationship also runs in reverse: the area under an ε–t graph equals NΔΦB, the total flux change multiplied by the number of turns. Figure 6.2 shows the pair of graphs for a magnet falling through a coil: the emf crosses zero at the very moment the flux peaks, and the exit pulse is taller and narrower than the entry pulse because the accelerating magnet leaves faster than it arrived — yet the two pulse areas are equal.

Trap A ΦB–t graph and its ε–t graph are a gradient pair, never the same shape. Matching the look of one graph to the other — choosing a flux graph shaped like the emf graph — is the classic error. Read the gradient of the flux graph at each instant instead.

Connects A changing magnetic field creating an electric push is the first half of an electromagnetic wave. In Unit 4, changing electric and magnetic fields regenerate each other and travel through empty space as light.

Lenz’s law the minus sign

The negative sign in Faraday’s law is Lenz’s law: the induced emf drives a current whose own magnetic field opposes the change in flux that produced it. The opposition is to the change, never to the field itself. While the flux through a loop is increasing, the induced current’s field points opposite to the applied field, opposing the increase; while the flux is decreasing, the induced field points the same way as the applied field, sustaining it.

Trap “The induced field opposes the magnetic field” is wrong half the time. When the flux is decreasing, the induced current’s field points in the same direction as the applied field — it opposes the decrease. Always state the change being opposed, never the field alone.

Lenz’s law is conservation of energy in disguise. Suppose the opposite: an induced current that reinforced the change. The growing flux would induce a larger emf, driving a larger current, growing the flux further — electrical energy multiplying out of nothing. Opposition is the only direction consistent with energy conservation, and it carries a physical price.

Pushing the north pole of a magnet toward a coil induces a current that turns the near face of the coil into a north pole, so the magnet is repelled, and the work done against that repulsion is the source of the electrical energy. The same opposition is why a generator becomes harder to turn as it supplies more current.

Direction questions yield to a three-step recipe:

  1. Flux direction. Identify the direction of the magnetic flux through the loop — which way the field lines thread the window.
  2. Increasing or decreasing. Decide whether that flux is increasing or decreasing.
  3. Oppose the change. The induced current’s field opposes the change: opposite to the applied field if the flux is increasing, in the same direction if it is decreasing. The right-hand grip rule then converts field into current — the thumb points along the induced field through the coil and the fingers curl in the direction of the induced current.

Applied to a north pole approaching a flat coil face-on: the flux through the coil points away from the magnet and is increasing; the induced field must point back toward the magnet to oppose the increase; with the thumb toward the magnet, the fingers show a current that is anticlockwise as seen from the magnet. Pulling the magnet away instead makes the flux decrease, and every direction reverses.

Key idea The induced emf drives current whose effect opposes the change producing it. Any other direction would manufacture energy from nothing.
Connects A generator is AOS 2’s DC motor run in reverse — the same coil-in-a-field hardware. Feeding current in makes F = nIlB forces spin the coil; spinning the coil pushes current out, and by Lenz’s law the magnetic force on that induced current resists the rotation, so mechanical work in equals electrical energy out.

Quick checks

Check 6.1 A square loop of side 4.0 cm sits with its plane perpendicular to a uniform 50 mT magnetic field. Calculate the magnetic flux through the loop.
Convert first: A = 0.040 × 0.040 = 1.6 × 10⁻³ m² and B = 5.0 × 10⁻² T. Then ΦB = B⊥A = 5.0 × 10⁻² × 1.6 × 10⁻³ = 8.0 × 10⁻⁵ Wb.
Check 6.2 The loop in Check 6.1 is rotated so that its plane is parallel to the field. State the new flux, and describe the flux at orientations between parallel and perpendicular.
Zero — no field lines pass through the loop when the field lies in its plane. At intermediate angles the flux lies between 0 and 8.0 × 10⁻⁵ Wb; VCE Physics requires only this qualitative statement, not a calculation at an angle.
Check 6.3 A strong bar magnet rests stationary inside a 500-turn coil, producing a flux of 3.0 × 10⁻³ Wb through each turn. State the induced emf and explain.
Zero. The induced emf depends on the rate of change of flux, ΔΦB/Δt, not on the size of the flux — while the magnet is stationary the flux is constant, so no emf is induced, however many turns the coil has.
Check 6.4 The flux through each turn of a 20-turn coil decreases steadily from 6.0 × 10⁻³ Wb to zero in 0.50 s. Calculate the magnitude of the induced emf and describe the shape of the ε–t graph over this interval.
ε = NΔΦB/Δt = 20 × 6.0 × 10⁻³ ÷ 0.50 = 0.24 V. The flux–time graph is a straight ramp with constant gradient, so the emf is constant at 0.24 V for the whole 0.50 s.
Check 6.5 The north pole of a bar magnet is pushed toward the face of a flat coil. Determine the direction of the induced current as seen from the magnet, and justify the direction using Lenz’s law.
Anticlockwise. The flux through the coil, directed away from the magnet, is increasing, so the induced current’s field must point back toward the magnet to oppose the increase; by the right-hand grip rule the current is anticlockwise viewed from the magnet — the near face becomes a north pole, repelling the approaching magnet.
