8 · Transformers and transmission

Electricity is generated far from where it is used. This page builds the language for describing an AC supply — rms, peak and peak-to-peak — then the transformer that trades voltage for current, and finally the line losses that make high-voltage transmission the only economical way to move power.

Describing AC: rms, peak and peak-to-peak 230 V RMS · 50 Hz

The coil of an alternator turns at a steady rate, so its output voltage alternates sinusoidally, swinging between +Vpeak and −Vpeak many times each second. A plain time-average of this voltage is zero — every positive half-cycle is cancelled by the negative half-cycle that follows — yet a kettle on an AC supply still boils. Heating does not depend on the direction of the current, so the supply delivers power on both half-cycles. Describing an AC supply therefore requires a value built on power, not on a simple average.

The root-mean-square (RMS) value of an alternating voltage is defined as the constant DC voltage that would develop the same average power in the same resistive component. The RMS current is defined in the same way. The definition says nothing about sine waves; for the sinusoidal output of an alternator, the RMS values work out to be the peak values divided by √2:

VRMS = 1√2 Vpeak   IRMS = 1√2 Ipeak on formula sheet VRMS, IRMS rms values · Vpeak, Ipeak maximum values of the sinusoid — these relations hold for sinusoidal AC

The Australian mains supply is 230 V RMS at 50 Hz. For power purposes every outlet behaves like a 230 V DC supply, but the actual voltage swings between +325 V and −325 V (since 230 × √2 = 325 V) — a peak-to-peak swing of about 650 V — and completes fifty cycles each second, one cycle every 1/50 = 0.020 s.

Three voltages describe the same sinusoid: Vpeak = √2 × VRMS, the peak-to-peak value Vp–p = 2Vpeak, and therefore Vp–p = 2√2 × VRMS. The RMS value is always the smallest of the three, and a stated AC voltage — on a power point or an appliance plate — is an RMS value unless it says otherwise. The same three descriptions, with the same conversions, apply to the current.

Because RMS values are built from power equivalence, the DC power relation transfers directly to AC:

P = VRMS IRMS on formula sheet P average power developed in a resistive component (W) — the formula-sheet relation P = VI applied with rms values; applied with peak values it gives the peak power, which is twice the average
Key idea An RMS value answers one question: what constant DC voltage (or current) would develop the same average power in the same resistive component? For sinusoidal AC, RMS = peak ÷ √2.
Trap A globe is equally bright on a 6.0 V battery and a 6.0 V RMS AC supply. What is the same is the average power — not the average voltage, which is zero for the AC supply, and not the maximum voltage, which is 6.0 × √2 = 8.5 V for the AC supply. Reading the mains label “230 V” as a peak value is the same error in reverse.

Transformers N₁/N₂ = V₁/V₂ = I₂/I₁

A transformer is two separate coils wound on a closed iron core, with no moving parts and no electrical connection between the coils. Alternating current in the primary coil makes it an electromagnet whose magnetic flux alternates with the supply, and the iron core guides almost all of that flux around the loop and through the secondary coil. The flux through every turn of the secondary is therefore changing continuously, so an EMF is induced across it — electromagnetic induction with nothing rotating, as shown in Figure 8.1.

Every turn of the secondary surrounds the same changing flux, so each turn contributes equally to the output: doubling the secondary turns doubles the output voltage. For an ideal transformer — one that loses no power — the voltages are in the ratio of the turns:

N1N2 = V1V2 = I2I1 on formula sheet N1, V1, I1 turns, voltage and current of the primary (input) coil · N2, V2, I2 the same for the secondary (output) coil — the current ratio is inverted relative to the other two

A step-up transformer has more secondary turns than primary turns (N2 > N1) and delivers a higher voltage than it is fed; a step-down transformer is the reverse. Transformers are named for what they do to the voltage, and it is the ratio of the turns, not their actual number, that matters. The output alternates at the same frequency as the input — a transformer rescales the sinusoid; it does not reshape it.

An ideal transformer transfers all of its input power to the output:

V1I1 = V2I2 not on sheet follows directly from the transformer ratio above, which is on the sheet — power in equals power out for an ideal transformer
Trap A step-up transformer is not an amplifier. Raising the voltage by a factor of n lowers the current by the same factor, because the output power can never exceed the input power. The current ratio in the transformer equation is upside-down relative to the other two: the high-voltage side of a transformer always carries the smaller current.

The whole mechanism rests on changing flux, and this is why transformers require AC. If the primary is connected to a battery instead, the steady current produces a constant flux in the core; the flux through the secondary no longer changes, so no EMF is induced and the output is zero. Only at the instant the battery is connected or disconnected — the only moments the flux changes — does a brief pulse appear. A grid that depends on transformers must therefore be an AC grid.

Worked example — inside a phone charger

Ishva’s phone charger steps the 230 V RMS Australian mains supply down to 11.5 V RMS. The transformer inside is modelled as ideal, with 600 primary turns, and the charger supplies 1.2 A RMS from its secondary coil.

Turns: N2 = N1 × V2V1 = 600 × 11.5 ÷ 230 = 30 turns.

Current drawn from the mains: I1 = I2 × V2V1 = 1.2 × 11.5 ÷ 230 = 0.060 A.

Check by power conservation: P2 = 11.5 × 1.2 = 13.8 W and P1 = 230 × 0.060 = 13.8 W — equal, as an ideal transformer requires. RMS values were used throughout: transformer ratios work with RMS, peak or peak-to-peak values, provided one type is used consistently on both sides.

Connects V1I1 = V2I2 is the course’s energy-conservation ledger again. The volt is a joule per coulomb — that is W = qV from AOS 2 — so P = VI counts joules delivered per second, and an ideal transformer must pass every joule on.

Transmission losses the I²R argument

A transmission system has three parts connected in series: a supply, a pair of long lines, and a load — a single machine, a house or a whole town. The two lines are modelled as a single resistor of total resistance Rline, as shown in Figure 8.2. With no transformers in the system, the supply, the lines and the load form one series loop, so a single current Iline flows through all of them.

The lines obey Ohm’s law like any other resistor: driving the current through them costs a potential difference, and dissipates power as heat.

Vdrop = IlineRline   Ploss = Iline²Rline on formula sheet Iline current in the transmission lines (A) · Rline total resistance of the lines (Ω) · Vdrop potential difference across the lines themselves (V) · Ploss power converted to heat in the lines (W)

Both formulas use the line current and the line resistance. The voltage that appears in them is Vdrop, the drop along the lines themselves — never the supply voltage. The supply voltage divides between the lines and the load, so the load receives only the remainder: Vload = Vsupply − Vdrop and Pload = Psupply − Ploss.

Trap The most damaging error in this topic is putting the supply voltage where Vdrop belongs — for example, computing the line current as Vsupply ÷ Rline, or the loss as Vsupply² ÷ Rline. The full supply voltage is never across the lines alone; only Vdrop = IlineRline is. Work from the line current, or from the difference between the voltages at the two ends of the lines — never from the supply voltage directly.

Worked example — one wind turbine, two ways

Shanna’s community energy group runs a small wind turbine that generates 20 kW, which is fed to a dairy 2.0 km away through lines of total resistance 4.0 Ω. All voltages and currents are RMS values, and the turbine supplies its full 20 kW in both arrangements below.

Without transformers, the power leaves the turbine at 500 V. The line current is found from P = VI: Iline = 20 000 ÷ 500 = 40 A. The lines dissipate Ploss = Iline²Rline = 40² × 4.0 = 6.4 × 10³ W = 6.4 kW — 32% of the generated power. The drop along the lines is Vdrop = 40 × 4.0 = 160 V, so the dairy receives only 500 − 160 = 340 V and 20 − 6.4 = 13.6 kW.

With transformers, a step-up transformer at the turbine raises the transmission voltage tenfold, to 5.0 kV, and a step-down transformer at the dairy returns it to a usable level. The same 20 kW now travels at Iline = 20 000 ÷ 5000 = 4.0 A. The loss collapses to Ploss = 4.0² × 4.0 = 64 W — only 0.32% — and the drop to Vdrop = 4.0 × 4.0 = 16 V, so the step-down transformer receives 5000 − 16 = 4984 V and 19.9 kW reaches the dairy.

The numbers display the general rule. The power fed into the lines is fixed, and P = VI, so transmitting at n times the voltage cuts the current by a factor of n; since Ploss = Iline²Rline, the loss falls by a factor of n². Here n = 10: the current fell from 40 A to 4.0 A and the loss fell a hundredfold, from 6.4 kW to 64 W. This is the entire case for high-voltage transmission — the alternative, lowering Rline with thicker wire, means hanging far heavier and more expensive conductors on every tower.

Stepping down at the destination is equally necessary: it returns the voltage to the safe, standard 230 V that appliances are built for — and it raises the current again, so the largest currents in the whole system flow in the local wiring, not in the transmission lines.

Key idea For a fixed power delivery, stepping the transmission voltage up by a factor of n cuts the line current by a factor of n and the line loss by a factor of n². Losses depend on the line current and the line resistance — never compute them from the supply voltage.

Quick checks

Check 8.1 A globe glows at a steady brightness on a 12 V battery. It glows equally brightly on a certain AC supply. State the RMS voltage of the AC supply and calculate its peak voltage.
The RMS value is the DC voltage developing the same average power, so VRMS = 12 V; the peak is Vpeak = √2 × 12 = 17 V.
Check 8.2 The Australian mains supply is 230 V RMS at 50 Hz. Calculate its peak-to-peak voltage and its period.
Vp–p = 2√2 × VRMS = 2√2 × 230 = 6.5 × 10² V (the voltage swings from +325 V to −325 V); the period is T = 1/50 = 0.020 s.
Check 8.3 A transformer has 400 primary turns and 8000 secondary turns, with its primary connected to a 230 V RMS supply. Identify whether it is step-up or step-down and calculate the secondary voltage.
N2 > N1, so it is step-up: V2 = 230 × 8000 ÷ 400 = 4.6 × 10³ V (4.6 kV) — and the secondary current is twenty times smaller than the primary current, since an ideal transformer conserves power.
Check 8.4 Explain why a transformer connected to a battery produces no output voltage once the current has settled.
A steady current in the primary produces a constant flux in the core, so the flux through the secondary does not change — and with no change in flux there is no induced EMF. A transformer works only while the flux is changing, which is why it requires AC.
Check 8.5 A 250 V RMS supply sends 8.0 A RMS through transmission lines of total resistance 5.0 Ω to a workshop. Calculate the voltage across the workshop and the power lost in the lines.
The drop along the lines is Vdrop = IlineRline = 8.0 × 5.0 = 40 V, so the workshop receives 250 − 40 = 210 V; the lines dissipate Ploss = Iline²Rline = 8.0² × 5.0 = 3.2 × 10² W. The 250 V supply voltage is never across the lines alone.
