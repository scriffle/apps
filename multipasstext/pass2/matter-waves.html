12 · Matter waves

An electron is a particle — it has mass, charge and a measurable track. Fired through a crystal, the same electron diffracts like a wave. This page assigns matter its wavelength, λ = h/p, and follows the evidence to the single-particle double-slit experiment.

The de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p

By 1924 light had been caught behaving both ways: it diffracts and interferes like a wave (topic 7), yet it trades energy in photon-sized lumps and carries momentum (topic 8). Louis de Broglie proposed that the symmetry runs in both directions. If a wave carries momentum like a particle, then a moving particle should travel with a wavelength — the de Broglie wavelength:

λ = hp on formula sheet λ de Broglie wavelength (m) · h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s · p = mv momentum (kg m s⁻¹)

Since p = mv, the heavier and faster the object, the shorter its wavelength. Wave behaviour is observable only when the wavelength is comparable to the gap or obstacle that the wave meets — the λ/w ratio of topic 7. That one condition decides why matter waves stayed hidden for so long.

A cricket ball of mass 0.16 kg bowled at 40 m s⁻¹ has momentum p = 0.16 × 40 = 6.4 kg m s⁻¹, so λ = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ 6.4 = 1.0 × 10⁻³⁴ m. No gap this narrow exists: the wavelength is about 10¹⁹ times smaller than the diameter of a proton (about 1.7 × 10⁻¹⁵ m). The ball diffracts, in principle, by an amount no instrument could ever detect.

An electron is different. At 4.0 × 10⁶ m s⁻¹ its momentum is 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 4.0 × 10⁶ = 3.6 × 10⁻²⁴ kg m s⁻¹ and its wavelength is 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ (3.6 × 10⁻²⁴) ≈ 1.8 × 10⁻¹⁰ m — the same scale as the spacing between atomic planes in a crystal. A crystal therefore offers a ready-made diffracting structure at exactly the right scale.

Trap Planck’s constant is given in two unit systems: 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s and 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s. In λ = h/p the momentum is in kg m s⁻¹, an SI unit, so always use h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s. The eV s value belongs with energies expressed in electron-volts, never with momenta in kg m s⁻¹.
Key idea Every moving object has a de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p. Wave behaviour becomes observable only when λ is comparable to the openings available — which, for everyday objects, it never is.

Electron diffraction rings like X-rays

In 1927, three years after de Broglie’s proposal, Davisson and Germer in the United States and G. P. Thomson in Scotland directed beams of electrons at crystalline targets and observed diffraction. In the transmission version of the experiment, an electron beam passes through a thin polycrystalline foil and a set of concentric bright rings appears on a fluorescent screen. The regularly spaced planes of atoms — a few tenths of a nanometre apart — act as the diffracting structure.

Rings are wave evidence. If electrons were particles and nothing more, the beam would strike the screen in a single central patch fading toward the edges. Instead, alternating bright and dark rings appear at exactly the angles where waves scattered by the atomic planes reinforce and cancel.

The decisive comparison is with X-rays, as shown in Figure 9.1. X-rays are electromagnetic waves with wavelengths that match atomic spacings, and they produce ring patterns through the same foils. When the electron gun’s accelerating voltage is chosen so that h/p equals the X-ray wavelength, electrons and X-rays of the same wavelength produce ring patterns with the same dimensions — at equal wavelength, matter diffracts exactly as light does. If two patterns differ in scale, the wavelengths differ — the rings spread further as λ/w grows.

The accelerating voltage is therefore a wavelength dial. From Unit 3, an electron accelerated from rest through a potential difference V gains kinetic energy:

½mv² = qV on formula sheet q = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C · V accelerating voltage (V) · me = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg · combine with p = mv (also on the sheet) to reach momentum
p = √(2mEk) not on sheet Ek = qV kinetic energy (J) — this shortcut follows from Ek = ½mv² and p = mv, which are both on the sheet

Worked example — wavelength from an accelerating voltage

Ishva operates a school diffraction tube in which an electron gun accelerates electrons from rest through a potential difference of 500 V into a thin graphite foil. She models each electron as a free charge accelerated through a uniform field. Ignore relativistic effects.

Formula: the chain is Ek = qV, then p = √(2mEk), then λ = h/p.

Substitution: Ek = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 500 = 8.00 × 10⁻¹⁷ J, so p = √(2 × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 8.00 × 10⁻¹⁷) = 1.207 × 10⁻²³ kg m s⁻¹.

Evaluation: λ = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ (1.207 × 10⁻²³)

Answer: λ = 5.49 × 10⁻¹¹ m — about a quarter of the 2.13 × 10⁻¹⁰ m atomic-plane spacing in graphite, comfortably inside the diffracting range. Checking the assumption: v = p/m = 1.3 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹, which is 4.4% of c, so ignoring relativistic effects is justified. The chain is reversible: a measured wavelength fixes the momentum, the kinetic energy, and hence the accelerating voltage that produced it.

Connects The electron gun is Unit 3 hardware: W = qV set the energy scale there, and the same accelerating voltage now sets a wavelength through p. Same electrons — new identity.

Comparing momenta same λ ⇒ same p

Read λ = h/p in the other direction and it becomes a statement about light: a photon of wavelength λ carries momentum p. The formula sheet lists the relation twice — as photon momentum and as the de Broglie wavelength — because it is one rule that covers both light and matter:

p = hλ on formula sheet p momentum (kg m s⁻¹) · h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s · λ wavelength (m) · applies to photons and to matter alike

It follows that a photon and an electron with the same wavelength carry the same momentum. Their energies are another story. A photon’s energy is:

E = hf = hcλ on formula sheet E photon energy (J, or eV when h = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s is used) · for matter use Ek = p²/2m instead — not on the sheet

At λ = 2.0 × 10⁻¹⁰ m, both a photon and an electron carry p = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ (2.0 × 10⁻¹⁰) = 3.3 × 10⁻²⁴ kg m s⁻¹. The X-ray photon’s energy is 6.2 × 10³ eV, but the electron’s kinetic energy is Ek = p²/2m = 6.0 × 10⁻¹⁸ J = 38 eV — about 165 times less. The reason is speed: the photon delivers its momentum at c, so E = pc, while the electron carries the same momentum at 3.6 × 10⁶ m s⁻¹, about an eightieth of c.

Trap Matching wavelengths matches momenta and diffraction patterns — never energies. An electron and an X-ray photon of equal λ make identical rings and carry identical p, yet their energies differ by two orders of magnitude. Applying E = hf to the electron is a wrong-formula error: hf is photon energy only.

One particle at a time dual nature

Diffraction patterns are made by beams containing enormous numbers of particles, which leaves a loophole: perhaps particles in a dense beam interact with one another, and the fringes are a collective effect. The modern double-slit experiment closes the loophole. The source is turned down until only one photon — or one electron — is in the apparatus at a time, and the arrival point of each is recorded.

Each particle arrives whole, at a single point: one localised dot on the detector, never a faint copy of the full pattern. Arrival is particle-like. But the dots do not land uniformly. As the count grows from dozens to many thousands, as shown in Figure 9.2, they cluster along the bright fringes of the double-slit pattern, and very few land in the dark fringes — with exactly the fringe spacing the wave analysis of topic 7 predicts when λ = h/p is used as the wavelength.

No single particle makes a pattern. With both slits open, each particle behaves as if it passes through both and interferes with itself, and the interference pattern exists only statistically, across many arrivals. Light and matter share this double character — particle-like in arrival, wave-like in distribution — and the single-particle double slit is the cleanest evidence for the dual nature of both.

Key idea One particle, one dot; many particles, a wave pattern. Light and matter arrive as particles but distribute as waves — this is what “dual nature” means.

Quick checks

Check 12.1 Jo, of mass 65 kg, walks at 1.5 m s⁻¹ through a doorway 0.90 m wide. Calculate Jo’s de Broglie wavelength and hence explain why Jo shows no observable diffraction.
p = mv = 65 × 1.5 = 97.5 kg m s⁻¹, so λ = h/p = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ 97.5 = 6.8 × 10⁻³⁶ m. Diffraction is observable only when λ is comparable to the gap width; here λ/w ≈ 10⁻³⁵, so the spreading is immeasurably small.
Check 12.2 The accelerating voltage of an electron-diffraction tube is increased from 500 V to 2000 V. State the effect on the de Broglie wavelength of the electrons and on the ring pattern.
Ek = qV quadruples, so p = √(2mEk) doubles and λ = h/p halves. A smaller λ/w means less spreading, so the rings contract toward the centre of the pattern.
Check 12.3 An electron and an X-ray photon each have a wavelength of 3.0 × 10⁻¹⁰ m. Compare their momenta and their energies.
Momenta are equal: p = h/λ = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ ÷ (3.0 × 10⁻¹⁰) = 2.2 × 10⁻²⁴ kg m s⁻¹ for both. Energies are not: the photon carries E = pc ≈ 6.6 × 10⁻¹⁶ J (4.1 × 10³ eV), while the electron carries only Ek = p²/2m ≈ 2.7 × 10⁻¹⁸ J (17 eV).
Check 12.4 A beam of electrons passing through a thin polycrystalline foil produces concentric rings on a screen, with the same dimensions as the rings produced by X-rays of the same wavelength. Explain why this is evidence for the wave-like nature of matter.
Diffraction rings are produced only by waves; a particle-only model predicts a single central patch. Because the electron rings match those of X-rays — a known wave — of equal wavelength, the electrons must propagate through the crystal as waves of wavelength λ = h/p.
Check 12.5 In a double-slit experiment, electrons are sent through the apparatus one at a time. Describe what is observed on the detection screen, and state what this shows about the nature of the electron.
Each electron arrives as a single localised dot (particle-like arrival), but over many thousands of arrivals the dots build up the interference fringe pattern predicted by the wave model (wave-like distribution). The pattern is statistical — no single electron forms it — which is evidence for the dual nature of matter.
