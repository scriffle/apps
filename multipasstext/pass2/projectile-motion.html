1 · Projectile motion

Once a body leaves the hand, the bat or the launch ramp, gravity is the only force acting on it. The whole of projectile analysis follows from one idea: the horizontal and vertical motions are independent, and only the vertical motion accelerates.

Forces in two dimensions resolving · inclined plane

Newton’s laws apply unchanged when two or more coplanar forces act on a body. The net force is the vector sum of the forces, and the acceleration obeys Fnet = ma in the direction of that net force. When the forces are not collinear, each force is replaced by its components along two perpendicular axes, the components are added separately, and the two axes are then treated as two independent one-dimensional problems.

A force F directed at an angle θ to a chosen axis is resolved into a component F cosθ along that axis and a component F sinθ perpendicular to it. The choice of axes is free, but a sensible choice removes work: the axes are aligned so that as many forces as possible already lie along them.

Fnet = ma on formula sheet Fnet net force (N) · m mass (kg) · a acceleration (m s⁻²) — applied independently along each axis

The inclined plane is the standard two-dimensional force problem. A block of mass m rests on a slope inclined at angle θ to the horizontal. Two forces act on it if the slope is frictionless: its weight mg directed vertically downward, and the normal force N directed perpendicular to the slope surface. Because the block can only accelerate along the slope, the axes are chosen parallel and perpendicular to the slope, and it is the weight that is resolved into these axes.

Perpendicular to the slope the block does not accelerate, so the forces balance: N = mg cosθ. Parallel to the slope the only unbalanced force is the component of weight down the slope, mg sinθ, so the acceleration of a block on a frictionless slope is:

a = g sinθ not on sheet a acceleration down a frictionless slope (m s⁻²) · g 9.8 m s⁻² · θ angle of the slope to the horizontal — follows from Fnet = mg sinθ = ma

The acceleration is independent of the block’s mass. The two limits confirm the result: a horizontal surface (θ = 0) gives N = mg and zero acceleration, and a vertical drop (θ = 90°) gives N = 0 and a = g, recovering free fall. A free-body diagram drawn before any calculation is the surest defence against resolving the wrong force, as shown in Figure 1.1.

Trap The normal force on an inclined plane is N = mg cosθ, not mg. The full weight acts only perpendicular to a horizontal surface; on a slope, only the perpendicular component mg cosθ presses into the surface, while the parallel component mg sinθ drives the block down the slope. Resolve the weight into the slope axes — never the normal force.
Connects The same resolve-into-axes method drives every problem in this area of study: the banked-track and string problems in the circular-motion chapter resolve a normal force or a tension into horizontal and vertical components in exactly this way.

Projectile motion independence of components

A projectile is any body moving freely under gravity alone, once air resistance is ignored. The instant the body is released, the only force acting on it is its weight mg, directed vertically downward. By Newton’s second law its acceleration is therefore g = 9.8 m s⁻² directed vertically downward, constant in both magnitude and direction throughout the flight — at the launch, at the top of the path, and at every point in between.

The power of the model is that the horizontal and vertical motions are independent and can be analysed separately. There is no horizontal force, so the horizontal velocity is constant throughout the flight. The vertical motion is uniformly accelerated free fall, identical to the motion of a body simply dropped from rest if the launch is horizontal. A ball thrown horizontally and a ball dropped from the same height at the same instant strike the ground together, because their vertical motions are identical and unaffected by the horizontal motion.

Key idea Throughout a projectile’s flight the only force is gravity, so the acceleration is a constant 9.8 m s⁻² directed vertically downward. Horizontal velocity is constant; vertical velocity changes at 9.8 m s⁻¹ each second. The two motions are independent and share only the time of flight.

The horizontal and vertical motions are linked by one quantity alone: the time. The same elapsed time appears in both the horizontal and the vertical equations, which is what allows one motion to be solved for the time and that time then fed into the other. The constant-acceleration equations are applied separately to each axis, with acceleration a = g in the vertical equations and a = 0 in the horizontal direction (so horizontally s = uxt):

v = u + at on formula sheet v final velocity (m s⁻¹) · u initial velocity (m s⁻¹) · a acceleration (m s⁻²) · t time (s) — applied to the vertical axis with a = g
s = ut + 12at² on formula sheet s displacement (m) · u initial velocity (m s⁻¹) · a acceleration (m s⁻²) · t time (s)
v² = u² + 2as on formula sheet v final velocity (m s⁻¹) · u initial velocity (m s⁻¹) · a acceleration (m s⁻²) · s displacement (m) — useful for maximum height, where the vertical velocity is zero

A consistent sign convention must be fixed before any substitution and held for the entire problem. Either up is taken as positive (then g = −9.8 m s⁻²) or up is taken as negative (then g = +9.8 m s⁻²). The two choices give the same physical answer; mixing them within one problem does not. A launch velocity v at angle θ above the horizontal is resolved at the outset into ux = v cosθ and uy = v sinθ, and these two components are then carried through the horizontal and vertical equations independently.

Trap A projectile moving upward is still pulled downward. As it rises, the only force on it remains gravity, so the acceleration is 9.8 m s⁻² downward the whole time — even at the very top, where the vertical velocity is momentarily zero. Upward motion does not require an upward force; the upward velocity is simply being reduced by the constant downward acceleration. There is no upward force at any stage of the flight.
Connects The independence of components and the work–energy approach meet in the next chapter: a projectile’s speed at any height can also be found from conservation of mechanical energy, ½mv² + mgh constant, giving the same result as the kinematic method.

Analysing a projectile worked example

A complete projectile problem is solved by resolving the launch velocity into components, analysing each axis separately, and using the shared time of flight to connect them. The worked example below finds the time of flight, the range, the maximum height and the final velocity for a launch at an angle. The parabolic path and its velocity components are shown in Figure 1.2.

Worked example — launch at an angle

Mei kicks a ball from ground level with a speed of 25.0 m s⁻¹ at an angle of 35.0° above the horizontal. The ground is level and air resistance is ignored. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻² and up as positive, so a = −9.8 m s⁻² in the vertical equations. Unrounded intermediate values are retained in the calculator and only final answers are rounded, to three significant figures.

Formula: resolve the launch velocity into components, ux = v cosθ and uy = v sinθ.

Substitution: ux = 25.0 × cos35.0° = 20.5 m s⁻¹; uy = 25.0 × sin35.0° = 14.3 m s⁻¹.

Time of flight. Formula: vertically the ball returns to its launch height, so its vertical displacement is zero, s = uyt + 12at² = 0, giving t = 2uyg.

Substitution: t = (2 × 14.3394) ÷ 9.8.

Answer: t = 2.93 s.

Range. Formula: horizontally there is no acceleration, so R = uxt.

Substitution: R = 20.4788 × 2.9264.

Answer: R = 59.9 m.

Maximum height. Formula: at the top the vertical velocity is zero, so v² = uy² + 2as with v = 0 gives H = uy²2g.

Substitution: H = 14.3394² ÷ (2 × 9.8).

Answer: H = 10.5 m.

Final velocity at landing. Formula: the horizontal component is unchanged, vx = ux; the vertical component is vy = uy + at. The magnitude is v = √(vx² + vy²) and the direction is θ = tan⁻¹(vy/vx) below the horizontal.

Substitution: vx = 20.4788 m s⁻¹; vy = 14.3394 + (−9.8 × 2.9264) = −14.3394 m s⁻¹ (i.e. 14.3 m s⁻¹ downward); v = √(20.4788² + 14.3394²); θ = tan⁻¹(14.3394 ÷ 20.4788).

Answer: v = 25.0 m s⁻¹ at 35.0° below the horizontal. The landing speed equals the launch speed and the landing angle equals the launch angle, confirming the symmetry of a projectile launched and landing at the same height.

Key idea Solve a projectile by axes: resolve the launch velocity into ux and uy; use the vertical equations (with a = g) to find the time and the height; use R = uxt for the range; recombine vx and vy by Pythagoras and trigonometry for the final velocity. The time of flight is the single link between the two axes.
Trap The maximum height is reached when the vertical velocity is zero, not when the speed is zero. At the top of the path the horizontal velocity ux is undiminished, so the ball is still moving and its speed is ux, not zero. Setting the full speed to zero at the top is a common error.
Trap Carry unrounded intermediate values through the calculation and round only the final answer. Substituting a value already rounded to three significant figures — for example using uy = 14.3 m s⁻¹ in the time equation — accumulates rounding error and can shift the reported time, range and height in the final significant figure.

Air resistance (qualitative) asymmetric path

The analysis above ignores air resistance, which is an excellent approximation for dense, slow, compact objects over short flights. For light objects, high speeds or long flights, air resistance is no longer negligible, and the VCE study requires only a qualitative description of its effects.

Air resistance is a force that always opposes the motion — it acts in the direction opposite to the velocity at every instant. Because the velocity is tangential to the path and continually changes direction, the air-resistance force also changes direction throughout the flight, and its magnitude increases with speed. It is therefore not a constant force, and the simple constant-acceleration equations no longer apply.

The consequences are described qualitatively as follows. Air resistance removes energy from the projectile, so both the maximum height and the range are reduced compared with the ideal parabola. The path becomes asymmetric: the descending part of the path is steeper than the ascending part, so the projectile travels less horizontal distance coming down than going up. The horizontal velocity is no longer constant — it decreases throughout the flight, because air resistance has a horizontal component opposing the horizontal motion. The landing speed is less than the launch speed, and the projectile strikes the ground at a steeper angle than it was launched.

As a falling object speeds up, the upward air-resistance force grows until it balances the weight. At that point the net force is zero, the acceleration is zero, and the object continues at a constant terminal velocity. A projectile launched into a long flight tends toward this limiting vertical speed rather than accelerating indefinitely. The qualitative picture is summarised in Figure 1.3.

Trap Air resistance does not act only vertically. It opposes the whole velocity, so it has a horizontal component too — which is why the horizontal velocity is no longer constant and the range is reduced. A response that keeps the horizontal velocity constant while “adding drag” only to the vertical motion is incorrect.
Connects Terminal velocity is the velocity at which the resistive force balances the weight — the same force-balance reasoning used for a charged particle reaching constant speed in a viscous medium, and for the energy-dissipation analyses throughout this area of study.

Quick checks

Check 1.1 A 2.0 kg block rests on a frictionless slope inclined at 30° to the horizontal. State the magnitude of the normal force acting on it. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻².
The normal force balances only the perpendicular component of the weight: N = mg cosθ = 2.0 × 9.8 × cos30° = 17 N. It is not mg = 20 N — the full weight does not press into a sloped surface.
Check 1.2 A ball is thrown vertically upward. At the highest point of its flight, state the magnitude and direction of its acceleration and of the net force on it.
The acceleration is 9.8 m s⁻² directed vertically downward, and the net force is its weight mg directed vertically downward — both unchanged from every other instant of the flight. Gravity is the only force, so even though the velocity is momentarily zero at the top, the acceleration and the net force are still downward.
Check 1.3 A ball is thrown horizontally from a tower at the same instant an identical ball is dropped from rest from the same height. Air resistance is ignored. Which lands first? Explain.
They land at the same time. The horizontal and vertical motions are independent: the thrown ball’s horizontal velocity does not affect its vertical motion, so both balls undergo identical vertical free fall from the same height and reach the ground together. The thrown ball simply lands farther away horizontally.
Check 1.4 Compared with the ideal (no air resistance) case, state two effects of air resistance on the path of a projectile launched at an angle, and explain why the path becomes asymmetric.
Air resistance reduces both the maximum height and the range, and makes the descending part of the path steeper than the ascending part (an asymmetric path). It also reduces the horizontal velocity throughout the flight and gives a landing speed lower than the launch speed. The asymmetry arises because air resistance opposes the velocity at every instant, continually removing energy, so the projectile cannot retrace a symmetric parabola — by the descent it has lost speed and falls more steeply than it rose.
