3 · Momentum and energy

Forces change motion, but two conserved quantities — momentum and energy — let an entire collision or launch be analysed without knowing the force at every instant. This page treats impulse as the area under a force–time graph, classifies collisions as elastic or inelastic by following the kinetic energy, and tracks energy as it transforms among kinetic, elastic and gravitational forms and dissipates to the environment.

Momentum and impulse FΔt = mΔv

The momentum of an object is the product of its mass and its velocity, p = mv. Because velocity is a vector, momentum is a vector: it has the same direction as the velocity. In one dimension this is handled by assigning a positive direction and treating motion in the opposite direction as negative.

A net force changes an object's momentum. Newton's second law in its general form states that the net force equals the rate of change of momentum, so over a time interval Δt the net force produces a change in momentum called the impulse:

FΔt = mΔv on formula sheet F net force (N) · Δt time interval (s) · m mass (kg) · Δv change in velocity (m s⁻¹) · the product FΔt is the impulse (N s) · mΔv is the change in momentum (kg m s⁻¹)

Impulse and change in momentum are the same quantity measured two ways, so 1 N s = 1 kg m s⁻¹. The change in velocity is the final velocity minus the initial velocity, Δv = v − u, and signs must be tracked: a ball that rebounds reverses direction, so its velocity changes sign and the impulse can be larger than the impulse needed merely to stop it.

When the force varies during the interval, F in FΔt is the average force. The impulse is read directly off a force–time graph as the area between the graph and the time axis. This works whatever the shape of the graph: a rectangle for a constant force, a triangle for a force that rises and falls linearly, or a trapezium for a force that ramps up, holds steady and ramps down.

Trap Momentum is a vector. A 2.0 kg trolley moving at 3.0 m s⁻¹ that rebounds at 3.0 m s⁻¹ has not undergone zero change in momentum. Taking the original direction as positive, the momentum changes from +6.0 to −6.0 kg m s⁻¹, so Δp = −6.0 − (+6.0) = −12 kg m s⁻¹. Always assign + and − to opposite directions and keep them consistent throughout a problem.

Worked example — impulse from a force–time graph

Mei pushes a stationary 1.5 kg trolley along a frictionless track. A force sensor records the force she applies as the trapezoidal pulse of Figure 3.1: the force rises linearly from 0 to 40 N over the first 0.10 s, holds at 40 N until 0.30 s, then falls linearly to 0 at 0.40 s. The trolley is at rest before the push.

Formula: FΔt = mΔv, with the impulse FΔt equal to the area under the force–time graph.

Substitution: area = (½ × 0.10 × 40) + (0.20 × 40) + (½ × 0.10 × 40) = 2.0 + 8.0 + 2.0 = 12 N s.

Answer: the impulse is 12 N s, so Δv = FΔt / m = 12 ÷ 1.5 = 8.0 m s⁻¹. The trolley started at rest, so its final velocity is 8.0 m s⁻¹ in the direction of the push.

Conservation of momentum and collisions elastic vs inelastic

An isolated system is one on which no net external force acts. The total momentum of an isolated system is constant: whatever momentum one object gains, another loses by exactly the same amount, because the forces the objects exert on each other are a Newton's third law pair acting for the same time. This is the law of conservation of momentum, and it holds in every collision and explosion, whether or not the objects deform, stick, bounce or shatter.

In one dimension, with a positive direction chosen, conservation of momentum is written:

m1u1 + m2u2 = m1v1 + m2v2 not on sheet m masses (kg) · u velocities before (m s⁻¹) · v velocities after (m s⁻¹) — follows from the conservation of momentum in an isolated system

Kinetic energy is a different matter. Kinetic energy is conserved only in an elastic collision. In a perfectly elastic collision the total kinetic energy after the collision equals the total before it. In an inelastic collision the total kinetic energy after is less than before; the difference is not destroyed but transformed into other forms — heat, sound and the energy of permanent deformation. A collision in which the objects stick together is perfectly inelastic and loses the greatest possible kinetic energy consistent with conserving momentum.

To classify a collision, momentum conservation is used first to find the unknown velocities, then the total kinetic energy Ek = ½mv² is computed before and after. If the two totals are equal the collision is elastic; if the total after is smaller the collision is inelastic, and the shortfall is the energy dissipated to the environment.

Key idea Momentum is conserved in every collision in an isolated system. Kinetic energy is conserved only in an elastic collision. If Ek after is less than Ek before, the collision is inelastic and the missing energy has become heat, sound and deformation.
Trap "Energy is lost" never means energy disappears. Total energy is always conserved. In an inelastic collision it is the kinetic energy that decreases; that energy is accounted for exactly as heat, sound and the work done permanently deforming the colliding objects.

Worked example — classify a one-dimensional collision

On a frictionless air track, cart A of mass 0.80 kg moves at 3.0 m s⁻¹ to the right and collides with a stationary cart B of mass 0.40 kg. The carts couple together and move off as one. Sora takes the right as positive and treats the two carts as an isolated system.

Formula (velocity after): mAuA + mBuB = (mA + mB)v.

Substitution: (0.80 × 3.0) + (0.40 × 0) = (0.80 + 0.40) × v, so v = 2.4 ÷ 1.2 = 2.0 m s⁻¹ to the right.

Formula (classify): compare Ek = ½mv² before and after.

Substitution: Ek before = ½ × 0.80 × 3.0² = 3.6 J; Ek after = ½ × 1.2 × 2.0² = 2.4 J.

Answer: the kinetic energy falls from 3.6 J to 2.4 J, so the collision is inelastic (in fact perfectly inelastic, because the carts stick). The 1.2 J difference is dissipated to the environment as heat, sound and deformation. Momentum, however, is conserved: 2.4 kg m s⁻¹ before and (1.2 × 2.0) = 2.4 kg m s⁻¹ after.

Work and energy W = Fs · Ek = ½mv²

Work is the energy transferred to an object by a force acting through a displacement. For a constant force acting along the line of motion, the work done is the force multiplied by the displacement:

W = Fs not on sheet W work done (J) · F force component along the displacement (N) · s displacement (m) — only the component of force parallel to the displacement does work

When the force varies with position, the work done is the area under a force–distance graph. This area rule is the same one used for impulse on a force–time graph and for elastic energy on a force–extension graph: in each case the area of a force-versus-something graph gives the accumulated effect of the force.

Only the component of force along the displacement does work. A force directed perpendicular to the displacement does zero work, because it has no component in the direction of motion. This is the reason the speed in uniform circular motion is constant: the net force points to the centre, the velocity is tangential, and the two are at right angles, so the centripetal force transfers no energy to the object. It continually changes the object's direction without ever changing its speed.

The energy an object possesses by virtue of its motion is its kinetic energy:

Ek = ½mv² on formula sheet Ek kinetic energy (J) · m mass (kg) · v speed (m s⁻¹)

The net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy: a net force that does positive work speeds the object up, and one that does negative work slows it down. Because kinetic energy depends on the square of the speed, doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy.

Key idea A force perpendicular to the displacement does zero work. The centripetal force in uniform circular motion is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it does no work and the speed stays constant even though the velocity changes direction.
Connects This is why a satellite in a circular orbit, and a car at steady speed on a circular road, keep a constant speed: gravity and friction respectively act toward the centre, perpendicular to the motion, and transfer no energy. The work-as-area rule reappears for springs in the next section.

Springs and elastic energy Es = ½kx²

An ideal spring obeys Hooke's law: the force it exerts is proportional to its extension or compression. Writing the magnitudes, the applied force needed to hold the spring at extension x is:

F = kx not on sheet F force (N) · k spring constant or stiffness (N m⁻¹) · x extension or compression from the natural length (m)

The spring constant k is the gradient of the force–extension graph: a stiffer spring has a steeper line. The graph of applied force against extension is therefore a straight line through the origin, of gradient k.

The elastic potential energy stored in a stretched or compressed spring is the work done in deforming it, which is the area under the force–extension graph. Because that graph is a triangle of base x and height kx, the area is ½ × x × kx:

Es = ½kx² on formula sheet Es elastic potential energy (J) · k spring constant (N m⁻¹) · x extension or compression (m)

The stored energy depends on the square of the extension. Doubling the extension doubles the force but quadruples the stored energy, because the triangle's area grows with both its base and its height.

Trap Es = ½kx² is quadratic in x; it is not kx and not ½kx. The factor of ½ comes from the triangular area under the force–extension graph, and the square means a spring stretched twice as far stores four times the energy, not twice. Do not confuse the force F = kx (the height of the graph) with the energy Es = ½kx² (the area under it).

Worked example — launch speed from a spring

A spring launcher has a spring constant of 250 N m⁻¹. Diego compresses it by 0.080 m, places a 0.20 kg glider against it on a frictionless horizontal track, and releases it. All the stored elastic energy is transferred to the glider as kinetic energy.

Formula: Es = ½kx², then energy conservation Es = Ek = ½mv².

Substitution: Es = ½ × 250 × 0.080² = 0.80 J. Then ½ × 0.20 × v² = 0.80, so v² = 8.0.

Answer: v = √8.0 = 2.8 m s⁻¹, directed along the track in the direction the spring pushes.

Conservation and dissipation of energy Eg = mgΔh

Energy is never created or destroyed; it is transformed from one form to another. The three mechanical forms in this study are kinetic energy Ek = ½mv², elastic potential energy Es = ½kx², and gravitational potential energy, the energy stored by raising a mass through a height in a gravitational field:

Eg = mgΔh on formula sheet Eg change in gravitational potential energy (J) · m mass (kg) · g gravitational field strength (N kg⁻¹) · Δh change in height (m) — valid only while g is approximately constant

This expression is valid only in a uniform field — near Earth's surface, where g = 9.8 N kg⁻¹ is effectively constant over the height changes involved. For the much larger height changes of a satellite, g varies and Eg must instead be found from the area under a force–distance graph, or from the area under a field–distance graph multiplied by the mass (treated in the Fields chapter).

In an isolated system with no dissipative forces, the total mechanical energy — the sum of the kinetic, elastic and gravitational potential energies — is constant. A falling ball converts gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy; a launched spring converts elastic potential energy first into kinetic energy and then, as the object rises, into gravitational potential energy. Following the total is often faster than applying forces and kinematics, because the path between the start and end states does not matter.

A mass hanging on a spring and set oscillating is a continuous energy exchange. At the lowest point the spring is most stretched, so elastic potential energy is greatest and kinetic energy is zero; as the mass rises the spring relaxes and the mass speeds up, so elastic and gravitational stores convert into kinetic energy; at the highest point the kinetic energy is again zero. In an ideal system the total stays constant and the oscillation continues; in a real system a little energy is dissipated each cycle and the oscillation gradually dies away.

Real systems are not perfectly isolated. Friction, drag and the deformation of colliding bodies dissipate mechanical energy to the environment as heat, sound and permanent deformation. When dissipation occurs the mechanical energy decreases, but the total energy — now including the heat and sound produced — is still conserved. Energy accounting closes exactly only when these dissipated forms are included.

Key idea In an isolated system with no dissipation, total mechanical energy (kinetic + elastic + gravitational) is conserved. When friction, drag or deformation act, mechanical energy is dissipated to the environment as heat, sound and deformation, and the total energy is still conserved once those forms are counted.

Worked example — energy conservation in a spring launch

The launcher of the previous example is now held vertically. The same spring (k = 250 N m⁻¹) is compressed by 0.080 m and a 0.20 kg ball is placed on top. The ball is released and rises freely. Priya finds the maximum height the ball reaches above its release point, neglecting air resistance, and takes g = 9.8 N kg⁻¹.

Formula: with no dissipation the elastic potential energy stored becomes gravitational potential energy at the highest point, where the ball is momentarily at rest: Es = mgΔh.

Substitution: Es = ½ × 250 × 0.080² = 0.80 J, so 0.80 = 0.20 × 9.8 × Δh.

Answer: Δh = 0.80 ÷ (0.20 × 9.8) = 0.41 m above the release point. Air resistance, which dissipates energy as heat and sound, would reduce this height.

Connects The same energy bookkeeping governs a projectile: a ball thrown upward trades kinetic energy for gravitational potential energy on the way up and back on the way down, reaching the same speed at any given height (in the absence of air resistance). Throughout the flight the only force is gravity, so the acceleration is g downward even while the ball is rising. Air resistance dissipates energy and lowers both the maximum height and the range.

Quick checks

Check 3.1 A 0.16 kg ball travelling at 5.0 m s⁻¹ strikes a wall and rebounds straight back at 4.0 m s⁻¹. Taking the original direction as positive, calculate the impulse delivered to the ball.
Impulse = Δp = mΔv = m(v − u) = 0.16 × (−4.0 − 5.0) = 0.16 × (−9.0) = −1.4 N s. The impulse is 1.4 N s directed away from the wall. The velocity reverses sign, so the change is found from −4.0 − (+5.0), not 4.0 − 5.0.
Check 3.2 A force–time graph for a 2.0 kg object initially at rest is a triangle: the force rises from 0 to 30 N over 0.40 s, then falls back to 0 at 0.80 s. Determine the object's final speed.
Impulse = area = ½ × 0.80 × 30 = 12 N s. Then Δv = FΔt / m = 12 ÷ 2.0 = 6.0 m s⁻¹. Starting from rest, the final speed is 6.0 m s⁻¹.
Check 3.3 In a collision the total kinetic energy before is 50 J and after is 50 J. In a second collision the total is 50 J before and 38 J after. Classify each collision and state what happens to any missing energy.
The first is elastic: kinetic energy is conserved. The second is inelastic: kinetic energy falls by 12 J, which is dissipated to the environment as heat, sound and deformation. Momentum is conserved in both.
Check 3.4 A car travels at constant speed around a flat circular roundabout. The friction force provides the centripetal force. State how much work this force does on the car over one lap, and explain.
Zero work. The centripetal (friction) force points to the centre, perpendicular to the car's velocity at every instant, so it has no component along the displacement. A force perpendicular to the motion does no work, which is why the speed stays constant. There is no outward "centrifugal" force; the inward friction is the only horizontal force.
Check 3.5 A spring of constant 400 N m⁻¹ is compressed by 0.050 m. It is then compressed by 0.100 m. By what factor does the stored elastic potential energy increase, and what is its value at 0.100 m?
Es = ½kx² is quadratic, so doubling the compression quadruples the energy — a factor of 4. At x = 0.100 m, Es = ½ × 400 × 0.100² = 2.0 J (compared with 0.50 J at 0.050 m).
