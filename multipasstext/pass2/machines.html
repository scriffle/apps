8 · Motors & accelerators

Two machines apply this area of study: the DC motor, which turns the force on a current-carrying coil into continuous rotation, and the particle accelerator, which uses electric fields to increase the speed of charged particles and magnetic fields to steer them.

The simple DC motor split-ring commutator

A DC motor transforms electrical energy into energy of rotation. The simplest version is a rectangular coil of n turns mounted on an axle between the poles of a permanent magnet and connected to a DC supply. Figure 5.1 shows the standard starting position, with the plane of the coil parallel to the uniform field.

Consider the two sides of the coil that run perpendicular to the field, labelled AB and CD in Figure 5.1. They carry equal currents in opposite directions, so the field exerts equal and opposite forces on them, each of magnitude

F = nIlB on formula sheet F force exerted on the conductor (N) · n number of turns · I current (A) · l length of conductor in the field (m) · B magnetic field strength (T) · valid when the current is perpendicular to B

In the position shown, one force acts vertically upward and the other vertically downward, and their lines of action are separated by the full width of the coil: this pair produces the maximum torque about the axle. The other two sides, BC and DA, carry current parallel or antiparallel to the field and experience no magnetic force at all.

After a quarter turn the plane of the coil is perpendicular to the field. The two forces are unchanged in magnitude, but they now act in line with the coil, directly toward or away from the axle, so the torque is zero. This position is the dead spot — and at the same instant the gaps in the commutator pass under the brushes, momentarily cutting the current. A coil placed at rest here would never start; a spinning coil is carried through by its own momentum.

If the current direction never changed, the torque beyond the dead spot would act in the opposite rotational sense and the coil would swing back, oscillating about the perpendicular position instead of rotating. The split-ring commutator prevents this.

The commutator is a conducting ring split into two halves, fixed to the axle, with each half connected to one end of the coil; stationary brushes press against the rotating ring to carry current in from the supply. Every half revolution each brush crosses the gap and swaps from one half-ring to the other, reversing the current through the coil. Reversed current means reversed forces, so the torque continues to act in the same rotational direction.

A split-ring commutator is not the same component as a pair of slip rings. Slip rings are continuous: each end of the coil keeps its own ring permanently, so the current through the coil never reverses. Fitted to a DC motor, slip rings produce exactly the failure described above — oscillation about the perpendicular position. Slip rings belong to alternators, the AC generators of Area of Study 3.

Key idea The split-ring commutator reverses the coil current every half revolution, as the coil passes through the zero-torque position, so that the torque always acts in the same rotational direction.
Trap A motor and a generator are different machines. A motor uses the force exerted on a current-carrying conductor by a magnetic field (F = nIlB) to produce rotation; a generator uses rotation to induce a current. Explaining DC motor operation in terms of electromagnetic induction, flux or EMF is a category error.

Worked example 1 — forces on a motor coil

Lucia builds a simple DC motor for a class demonstration. Her square coil ABCD has 40 turns of side length 5.0 cm and carries a current of 1.5 A in a uniform magnetic field of 0.20 T, with the plane of the coil parallel to the field, as shown in Figure 5.1. Calculate the magnitude and direction of the force exerted on side AB by the magnetic field, and state the magnitude of the force exerted on side BC.

Force on AB. The current in AB is perpendicular to the field, so F = nIlB applies. Substituting, with the side length converted to metres: F = 40 × 1.5 A × 0.050 m × 0.20 T = 0.60 N. The force exerted on side AB by the field is 0.60 N, directed vertically upward by the right-hand slap rule; the force exerted on side CD is 0.60 N vertically downward.

Force on BC. The current in BC runs parallel to the field, so the magnetic force exerted on it is zero. Sides BC and DA contribute no force and no torque; they complete the circuit between the force-carrying sides.

Connects In Area of Study 3 the same coil-in-field geometry runs in reverse: turned by an external torque, the coil becomes a generator. The split-ring commutator that keeps a motor turning one way becomes the component that keeps a DC generator's output voltage one way, and slip rings reappear as the AC alternative.

What changes the torque qualitative

The study design asks only for the qualitative effect of three quantities on the torque of a simple motor. All three act through F = nIlB: anything that increases the force on the coil sides increases the torque.

  • Current I — a larger current means a larger force on every turn. In practice the current is raised by increasing the supply voltage or by lowering the resistance of the coil.
  • External field B — stronger magnets exert a larger force on the same current.
  • Number of turns n — each extra turn is one more conductor for the field to act on, so the force scales directly with n.

Distinguish carefully between the force and the torque as the coil rotates. The force on each force-carrying side keeps the same magnitude nIlB at every angle, because n, I, l and B do not depend on the coil's orientation. The torque is what varies: maximum when the plane of the coil is parallel to the field, zero when it is perpendicular.

Trap Force is not torque. Over one revolution the force on a force-carrying side keeps the constant magnitude nIlB, reversing direction only at each commutation; the torque rises and falls as the lever arm changes. Graphed against time, the force magnitude is a flat line, not a sine curve.

Particle accelerators includes synchrotrons

Every particle accelerator in this course is built from two ingredients. A uniform electric field accelerates a charged particle in a straight line, increasing its kinetic energy; a uniform magnetic field bends the particle's path, changing its direction without changing its speed.

The straight-line stage is the electron gun of the electric fields topic, or its scaled-up version, the linear accelerator (linac). A charge q accelerated from rest through a potential difference V gains kinetic energy W = qV not on sheet, which the formula sheet writes in its from-rest form:

½mv² = qV on formula sheet m mass of the particle (kg) · v speed gained from rest (m s⁻¹) · q charge (C) · V accelerating potential difference (V)

The bending stage is the physics of the magnetic fields topic. A particle moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field experiences a force of constant magnitude F = qvB on formula sheet, always at right angles to its velocity, so it travels in a circle of radius

r = mvqB on formula sheet r radius of the circular path (m) · m mass (kg) · v speed (m s⁻¹) · q charge (C) · B magnetic field strength (T) · valid for v ≪ c

Because the magnetic force is perpendicular to the velocity at every instant, it does no work on the particle. Magnetic fields change direction; only electric fields change speed.

Worked example 2 — electron gun into a magnetic field

Kenji operates a teaching accelerator. Electrons are accelerated from rest through a potential difference of 2.5 kV, then enter a region of uniform magnetic field of strength 1.5 mT directed perpendicular to their velocity. You may ignore relativistic effects. Determine the speed of the electrons as they leave the gun and the radius of their circular path in the magnetic field, taking me = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg and qe = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C from the formula sheet.

Speed. Rearranging ½mv² = qV gives v = √(2qV/m). Substituting: v = √((2 × 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C × 2.5 × 10³ V) / (9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg)) = √(8.78 × 10¹⁴ m² s⁻²) = 2.96 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹. The electrons leave the gun at 3.0 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹ — below one-tenth of the speed of light, slow enough for the classical treatment.

Radius. Convert the field strength first: 1.5 mT = 1.5 × 10⁻³ T. Then r = mv/qB = (9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg × 2.96 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹) / (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C × 1.5 × 10⁻³ T) = 0.11 m. The electrons follow a circular arc of radius 0.11 m, and the magnetic force changes their direction without changing their speed.

The Australian Synchrotron in Clayton combines these two ingredients on a large scale, as shown in Figure 5.2. An electron gun feeds a linac, where electric fields raise the electrons' kinetic energy in repeated W = qV stages. A booster ring then brings them to a final energy of 3.0 GeV before they are injected into the 216 m storage ring, where dipole magnets supply the uniform magnetic fields that bend the beam around the circuit.

The booster ring contains the one piece of synchrotron physics the study design names. As the electrons' momentum mv grows, r = mv/qB requires the radius of their circular path to grow too — unless B grows in step. A synchrotron therefore increases the strength of its bending field in synchrony with the particles' momentum, holding the radius constant so that the beam stays inside the one ring of magnets. This synchronisation gives the machine its name.

Connects At 3.0 GeV the storage-ring electrons travel at more than 99.999% of the speed of light, where the classical relation r = mv/qB (valid for v ≪ c) no longer applies cleanly. Unit 4 examines why accelerator designers must build special relativity into machines like this one — and why charges forced around a circle radiate the intense synchrotron light the facility exists to produce.

Satellites and storage rings: the same circle synthesis

This area of study returns to uniform circular motion. A satellite in orbit and an electron in a storage ring are both solutions of the same equation, Fnet = mv²/r on formula sheet. The physics differs in exactly one place — which force supplies the centripetal force.

FeatureSatellite in circular orbitCharged particle in a uniform magnetic field
The moving objectA satellite of mass m — a spacecraft, or the MoonA particle of charge q and mass m — an electron in a storage ring
Centripetal force supplied byThe gravitational force, Fg = GMm/r² on formula sheetThe magnetic force, F = qvB
Circular-motion statementGMm/r² = mv²/rqvB = mv²/r
What fixes the radiusv² = GM/r — at each radius only one orbital speed is possibler = mv/qB — the radius grows with momentum and shrinks with charge and field strength
Depends on the object's mass?No — m cancels, so every satellite at a given radius shares one speedYes — at the same speed and charge, a more massive particle moves in a larger circle
Work done by the forceNone — the force is perpendicular to the velocity, so the speed is constant although the velocity is notNone — the force is perpendicular to the velocity, so the speed is constant although the velocity is not

Two rows are significant. Neither force ever changes the speed of the object it steers, because both act perpendicular to the velocity at every instant: each object moves at constant speed but not constant velocity. The mass row is the one real asymmetry — the satellite's mass cancels out of its orbit, while the particle's mass remains in r = mv/qB.

Key idea A satellite and a storage-ring electron solve the same equation, Fnet = mv²/r. Gravity supplies the centripetal force for one, the magnetic force for the other, and neither force changes the object's speed.

Quick checks

Check 5.1 A motor coil sits with its plane parallel to a uniform magnetic field. Two of its sides run parallel to the field. State the magnitude of the force exerted on these sides by the field, and give the reason.
Zero. F = nIlB applies only to current that runs perpendicular to the field; a current parallel or antiparallel to B experiences no magnetic force.
Check 5.2 A motor coil reaches the position where its plane is perpendicular to the field, so the torque is zero. Explain why a spinning motor does not stop there.
The rotating coil's momentum carries it through the dead spot; once past, the commutator has reversed the current, so the torque resumes in the same rotational direction.
Check 5.3 Magnus replaces the split-ring commutator of a DC motor with a pair of slip rings. Describe the resulting motion of the coil.
The coil oscillates about the position where its plane is perpendicular to the field, instead of rotating continuously — without commutation the current never reverses, so beyond that position the torque acts back toward it every half cycle.
Check 5.4 The current in a simple motor's coil is halved and the number of turns is doubled, with the field and coil dimensions unchanged. State the effect on the maximum torque.
No change. The force on each side is proportional to the product nI in F = nIlB, and doubling n while halving I leaves nI — and therefore the force and the torque — unchanged.
Check 5.5 In the Australian Synchrotron's booster ring the electrons' momentum increases. Use r = mv/qB to explain why the magnetic field strength must increase at the same time.
With B fixed, a growing momentum mv means a growing radius, and the beam would spiral out of the ring; increasing B in proportion to mv holds r constant so the beam stays inside the same ring of magnets.
