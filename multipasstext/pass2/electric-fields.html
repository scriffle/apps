6 · Electric fields

A charged particle in an electric field experiences a force without contact, exactly as a mass does in a gravitational field. This page covers the radial field of a point charge, the uniform field between parallel plates, and the energy a field transfers to the charges it accelerates.

Point charges: field and force inverse square

Every charge fills the space around it with an electric field. The field strength E at distance r from a point charge Q is the force the field would exert on each coulomb of charge placed there, measured in newtons per coulomb (N C⁻¹).

E = k Qr² on formula sheet E field strength (N C⁻¹) · k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N m² C⁻² · Q source charge (C) · r distance from the charge (m)

The direction is fixed by a positive test charge: the field at any point is in the direction of the force exerted on a small positive charge placed at that point. The field of a positive charge therefore points radially outward, and the field of a negative charge points radially inward — the same shape as the gravitational field around a point mass, as shown in Figure 3.1. A gravitational field has only the inward form, because mass carries one sign and attracts only. Use the magnitude of Q in the formula and attach the direction from the sign of the charge.

Because E depends on 1/r², doubling the distance reduces the field strength to one quarter and tripling it to one ninth. As a worked case, at r = 0.30 m from a point charge Q = +2.0 μC: E = kQ/r² = (8.99 × 10⁹ × 2.0 × 10⁻⁶) ÷ (0.30)² = 2.0 × 10⁵ N C⁻¹, directed radially away from the charge.

Two point charges exert forces on each other along the line joining their centres. The magnitude of each force is given by Coulomb's law:

F = k q1q2r² on formula sheet F force each charge exerts on the other (N) · q1, q2 the two charges (C) · r centre-to-centre separation (m)

Like charges repel and unlike charges attract. The two forces are a Newton's third law pair: the force exerted on q1 by q2 is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force exerted on q2 by q1, even when one charge is much larger than the other.

Key idea The electric field at a point is the force per coulomb that would be exerted on a positive test charge at that point. A negative charge experiences a force opposite to the field direction.

Uniform fields between parallel plates ideal-field assumption

Two parallel metal plates connected to a power supply, one positive and one negative, produce a uniform electric field in the gap: the field has the same magnitude and direction at every point. The field lines run straight from the positive plate to the negative plate, parallel and equally spaced, as shown in Figure 3.2. Real plates have curved fringing fields near their edges; throughout this course, assume the field between the plates is uniform.

E = Vd on formula sheet E field strength (V m⁻¹) · V potential difference between the plates (V) · d plate separation (m)

The units V m⁻¹ and N C⁻¹ are identical; either may be used for electric field strength. The field exerts a force on any charge placed in the gap:

F = qE on formula sheet F force exerted on the charge by the field (N) · q charge (C) · E field strength (N C⁻¹)

Because the field is uniform, the force is the same at every point in the gap, so the charge undergoes constant acceleration — the electric parallel of a mass in the uniform gravitational field near Earth's surface. The force exerted on a positive charge by the field is in the field direction, from the positive plate toward the negative plate; the force exerted on a negative charge by the field is in the opposite direction.

For plates separated by 25 mm with 5.0 kV across them: E = V/d = (5.0 × 10³) ÷ (2.5 × 10⁻²) = 2.0 × 10⁵ V m⁻¹. The force exerted on an electron in the gap by the field is F = qE = (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹) × (2.0 × 10⁵) = 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁴ N, directed toward the positive plate.

Work, energy and the electron-volt the d trap

A charge that crosses the gap experiences a constant force over the whole distance, so the field does work on it. When a charge q moves through a potential difference V, the work done by the field is

W = qV not on sheet W work done on the charge by the field (J) · q charge (C) · V potential difference crossed (V) — the formula sheet carries this relation only in the form ½mv² = qV below

This is the Unit 1 definition of potential difference read in reverse: one volt is one joule per coulomb, so a charge of q coulombs crossing V volts gains qV joules. Note carefully what the equation does not contain: the plate separation d.

Trap "Wider plates give the charge more distance to accelerate, so more kinetic energy." This is wrong. At fixed voltage, doubling d halves the field (E = V/d) and therefore halves the force (F = qE); the charge travels twice the distance under half the force, so the work W = Fd is unchanged. The kinetic energy gained in crossing the gap depends only on q and V — never on d.

If the charge starts from rest and only the electric force acts, all of the work appears as kinetic energy. This combined form is the one printed on the VCAA formula sheet:

½mv² = qV on formula sheet m particle mass (kg) · v speed gained from rest (m s⁻¹) · q charge (C) · V accelerating potential difference (V)

Energies of single particles are inconveniently small in joules, so this area of study also uses the electron-volt: 1 eV is the energy gained by one electron moving through a potential difference of one volt, so 1 eV = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. Conversion runs both ways: an electron accelerated through 2.5 kV gains 2.5 × 10³ eV = (2.5 × 10³) × (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹) = 4.0 × 10⁻¹⁶ J; conversely, an energy of 8.0 × 10⁻¹⁶ J is (8.0 × 10⁻¹⁶) ÷ (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹) = 5.0 × 10³ eV. For any particle carrying one elementary charge, the energy gained in eV is numerically equal to the accelerating voltage in volts.

Connects Unit 4 will use W = qV as a measuring instrument: in the photoelectric effect, the stopping voltage that brings the fastest photoelectrons to rest reads off their kinetic energy directly in electron-volts.

Accelerating charges: the electron gun parabola vs circle

Every particle accelerator, including the Australian Synchrotron, begins with an electron gun. A heated filament, the cathode, releases electrons; a potential difference V0 between the cathode and an anode accelerates them across an evacuated gap; the beam emerges through a small aperture in the anode, as shown in Figure 3.3.

The exit speed follows from ½mv² = qV. For V0 = 2.5 kV: v = √(2qV0/m) = √((2 × 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 2.5 × 10³) ÷ (9.11 × 10⁻³¹)) = 3.0 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹, about 0.10c. You may ignore relativistic effects; this area of study assumes v is much less than c.

Once moving, a charge can be deflected. In a uniform electric field, a charge entering at right angles to the field lines experiences a constant force perpendicular to its initial motion, so it follows a parabolic path — the exact analogue of a projectile in a uniform gravitational field. In a uniform magnetic field, the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so the charge follows a circular path at constant speed: the direction of motion changes but the kinetic energy does not.

This separation of functions is the accelerator model the study design requires: uniform electric fields provide linear acceleration, because they do work and increase speed, while uniform magnetic fields provide direction change, because they do no work. The two stages are assembled into the full accelerator pipeline on the motors and accelerators page.

Quick checks

Check 3.1 The electric field strength at a distance of 2.0 m from a point charge is 36 N C⁻¹. What is the field strength at 6.0 m from the same charge?
4.0 N C⁻¹ — the distance has tripled and E ∝ 1/r², so the field falls by a factor of 3² = 9: 36 ÷ 9 = 4.0 N C⁻¹.
Check 3.2 An electron is placed in a region where the electric field points east. State the direction of the force exerted on the electron by the field.
West — the field direction is defined by the force on a positive test charge, and the electron's charge is negative, so the force is opposite to the field.
Check 3.3 Two parallel plates 8.0 mm apart have a potential difference of 400 V across them. Calculate the field strength between the plates.
E = V/d = 400 ÷ (8.0 × 10⁻³) = 5.0 × 10⁴ V m⁻¹ — the separation must be converted to metres before dividing.
Check 3.4 The plate separation in an electron gun is doubled while the accelerating voltage is unchanged. What happens to the kinetic energy an electron gains in crossing the gap?
It is unchanged — W = qV contains no d. Doubling d halves the field and the force, but the electron travels twice as far, so the work done on it is identical.
Check 3.5 A proton enters a uniform electric field at right angles to the field lines; an identical proton enters a uniform magnetic field at right angles to the field lines. Describe the shape of each path.
A parabola in the electric field (constant force, like a projectile in uniform gravity) and a circle in the magnetic field (the force stays perpendicular to the velocity, so the speed is constant while the direction changes).
