4 · The field idea

Gravitational, electric and magnetic forces act without contact. The field model explains how: every point in space carries a vector, and an object placed at that point experiences the corresponding force. This topic builds the model that the next four topics use.

What a field is KK 2.1

A field is a region of space in which an object experiences a non-contact force. More precisely, a field is a physical quantity that has a value at every point in space. A temperature map of Victoria is a scalar field — one number at each point. The three fields in this area of study — gravitational, electric and magnetic — are vector fields: at every point the field has both a magnitude and a direction.

The value at a point is revealed with a test object: a small mass for the gravitational field, a small positive charge for the electric field, a compass needle for the magnetic field. The test object must be small enough that it does not disturb the field being measured. Field strength is then the force exerted on the test object by the field, per unit of the property the field acts on.

g = Fgm not on sheet g gravitational field strength (N kg⁻¹) · Fg gravitational force exerted on the test mass (N) · m mass of the test object (kg)
E = Fq on formula sheet E electric field strength (N C⁻¹, equivalent to V m⁻¹) · F electric force exerted on the test charge (N) · q test charge (C) — the sheet gives the rearranged form F = qE

Each field has a direction convention. The gravitational field at a point is in the direction of the force exerted on a test mass by the field — always toward the source mass. The electric field is in the direction of the force exerted on a positive test charge by the field. The magnetic field is in the direction in which the north pole of a compass needle points.

The familiar g = 9.8 N kg⁻¹ from Units 1&2 is a field strength: near Earth's surface, the gravitational force exerted on each kilogram is 9.8 N, and the unit N kg⁻¹ states exactly that. Magnetic field strength B is measured in tesla (T) and is defined through the force exerted on a moving charge, treated in 7 · Magnetic fields.

Key idea A field has a value — magnitude and direction — at every point in space, whether or not a test object is present. The test object reveals the field; it does not create it.

Field diagrams and field-line rules

Drawing a separate arrow at every point is impossible, so vector fields are drawn as field lines — continuous curves whose direction at every point is the field's direction there. Four rules govern every correct field diagram.

  • Direction. Every line carries an arrowhead. The line's direction at a point is the field's direction there: toward a mass; away from a positive charge and toward a negative charge; from N to S outside a magnet.
  • Density shows strength. Where lines are closer together, the field is stronger. Parallel, equally spaced lines represent a uniform field.
  • Lines never cross. A crossing point would give the field two directions at one point, but the field is a single vector at each point.
  • Where lines start and end. Electric field lines start on positive charges and end on negative charges. Gravitational field lines only ever end, on masses. Field lines meet the surface of a spherical mass or charged conductor at right angles.

As shown in FIG 1.1, the field patterns around a point mass and around point charges are radial. Around any point source the same set of lines spreads over a larger and larger area with distance, so the line spacing grows and the field weakens. This is the basis of the inverse-square laws of topics 5 and 6.

Trap Field lines are a representation, not physical objects. A test mass placed between two drawn lines still experiences a force: the field fills all of space, and the number of lines drawn is a matter of convention.

Comparing the three fields KK 2.2 · 2.3

The three fields share common features — a vector at every point, a force exerted on the appropriate kind of object, and strength shown by line density — but differ in their sources and in whether they can repel. The table summarises the comparison required by the study design.

FieldSourceActs onAttract or repelMonopoles or dipoles
Gravitational g (N kg⁻¹)massmassattract onlymonopoles only, of a single kind; every mass is a sink
Electric E (N C⁻¹)electric charge, positive or negativeelectric charge, moving or stationaryboth — like charges repel, unlike charges attractmonopoles of both signs; dipoles when paired
Magnetic B (T)moving charge (currents) and permanent magnetsmoving charges, current-carrying conductors, magnetic materialsboth — like poles repel, unlike poles attractdipoles only — an isolated pole has never been observed

A monopole is a source or a sink of field lines: the lines point away from it or toward it, but not both. A dipole is a source and a sink paired together, with lines running from one to the other. Gravity has monopoles of one kind only, because masses only attract. Electric charge comes in two signs, so electricity has both monopoles and dipoles; magnetism has dipoles only.

As shown in FIG 1.2, the field of an electric dipole and the external field of a bar magnet have the same shape. The difference lies in what can be separated: the two charges of an electric dipole can be pulled apart into two monopoles, but cutting a bar magnet in half produces two smaller dipoles, never an isolated pole.

Trap Cutting a bar magnet in half does not give one north piece and one south piece. Each half is a complete dipole with its own N and S — this continues down to the level of individual electrons.
Key idea Field lines point away from sources and toward sinks. Gravity has sinks only. Electricity has sources and sinks. Magnetism has both at every point, inseparably.

Sources of magnetic fields KK 2.4

Magnetic fields come from two kinds of source: permanent magnets and electric currents. In 1820 Ørsted noticed that a compass needle deflects near a current-carrying wire — the first evidence that moving charge creates a magnetic field. For scale, Earth's magnetic field in Melbourne is about 6.0 × 10⁻⁵ T (60 μT).

A bar magnet produces the dipole field of FIG 1.2: external lines from N to S, densest at the poles. A straight wire produces circular field lines centred on the wire, with no poles at all. The direction comes from the right-hand grip rule: grip the wire with the right hand, thumb pointing along the conventional current; the fingers curl in the direction of the magnetic field. The circles space out with distance from the wire, showing the field weakening.

Bending the wire into a loop makes the circular field of every segment pass the same way through the centre, so the field there is concentrated; the two faces of the loop act as N and S poles. A solenoid — many loops side by side — combines these contributions: inside, the field is strong and uniform; outside, it matches a bar magnet's field. The grip rule adapts: curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of the current around the coil, and the thumb points along the field inside, toward the N end. As shown in FIG 1.3, the N end is where the field lines exit.

Three-dimensional fields are drawn on flat pages using the convention × for a vector into the page and • for a vector out of the page. This notation is practised throughout 7 · Magnetic fields.

Trap The grip rule uses conventional current — the direction of positive-charge flow, from the + terminal through the circuit to the −. Applying it with the thumb along the electron flow predicts a field direction that is reversed by 180°.

Static or changing, uniform or non-uniform KK 2.5

Every field can be classified on two independent axes. A field is static if its magnitude and direction at each point do not change over time, and changing otherwise. A stationary bar magnet, a fixed charged sphere and a solenoid carrying a constant current all produce static fields; a rotating magnet, or a coil carrying alternating current, produces a changing field.

A field is uniform in a region if it has the same magnitude and the same direction at every point in that region — drawn as parallel, equally spaced lines. It is non-uniform if either varies from point to point. The field between oppositely charged parallel plates is uniform; the radial field around a point mass or point charge is non-uniform. As shown in FIG 1.4, Earth's gravitational field is non-uniform on the planetary scale, yet within a laboratory the lines are so nearly parallel that the field is treated as uniform at 9.8 N kg⁻¹.

Key idea Uniform/non-uniform describes variation across space; static/changing describes variation over time. The two classifications are independent, and every field carries one label from each pair.
Connects The static/changing distinction becomes central in Unit 3 AOS 3: only a changing magnetic field induces an emf in a coil. A generator is constructed to produce that change.
Connects Unit 4 extends the field idea further: a changing electric field produces a changing magnetic field and vice versa, and the self-sustaining pair travels through empty space as light, a field with no source attached.

Quick checks

Check 1.1 On the Moon's surface, a 2.0 kg rock experiences a gravitational force of 3.2 N. Calculate the Moon's surface gravitational field strength.
g = Fg/m = 3.2 N ÷ 2.0 kg = 1.6 N kg⁻¹ — field strength is force per kilogram of test mass, directed toward the Moon's centre.
Check 1.2 Two field lines in a student's diagram cross each other. Explain why the diagram must be incorrect.
The field is a single vector at each point; at a crossing the field would have two directions at once, which is impossible.
Check 1.3 A diagram shows field lines pointing radially outward from a sphere. Explain why this cannot represent a gravitational field, and state one field it could represent.
Masses only attract, so gravitational field lines always point toward a mass — outward lines would mean gravitational repulsion. The diagram could represent the electric field of a positive point charge.
Check 1.4 A long straight wire lies in the plane of the page and carries conventional current toward the top of the page. State the direction of the magnetic field at a point in the plane of the page to the right of the wire.
Into the page — gripping the wire with the right thumb pointing up the page, the fingers curl into the page on the wire's right side.
Check 1.5 A solenoid carries a constant current. Identify the magnetic field inside the solenoid as static or changing, and as uniform or non-uniform.
Static and uniform — the constant current means the field does not change over time, and inside the solenoid the lines are parallel and equally spaced, so magnitude and direction are the same at every point.
