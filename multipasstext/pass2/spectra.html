16 · Atoms and spectra

Line spectra identify atoms uniquely. This topic explains the origin of the lines: the energy of a bound electron is quantised, and every spectral line is a photon carrying exactly the difference between two allowed energies.

Why quantisation was revolutionary qualitative

By 1911 Rutherford’s scattering experiments had shown that an atom is a tiny positive nucleus with electrons moving around it. Classical physics then makes two predictions, both of which are contradicted by observation.

An electron in a circular orbit is continuously accelerating, and topic 12 established that accelerating charges produce electromagnetic waves — so the orbiting electron should radiate energy continuously, spiral inward, and collapse into the nucleus within a fraction of a nanosecond. While spiralling it would pass through every orbital frequency, radiating a continuous range of colours. Real atoms are stable, and the light they emit contains only sharp, discrete lines.

The resolution was quantisation: the energy of an atom cannot take any value on a continuous range, but is restricted to a fixed set of allowed values, its energy levels. An electron occupying an allowed level does not radiate; the atom emits or absorbs energy only when the electron jumps between levels. This was significant because in classical physics an orbit — a planet around the Sun, a charge around a nucleus — can have any energy. Quantisation replaces that continuous range with a fixed set of permitted levels, and so explains why matter does not collapse.

Topic 15 supplies the reason the levels exist. A bound electron is a matter wave with de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p, and a wave confined to a closed path must close on itself smoothly. The wave survives only if a whole number of wavelengths fits around the circumference of its path:

nλ = 2πr not on sheet qualitative condition for an electron standing wave · n = 1, 2, 3, … · λ de Broglie wavelength of the electron (m) · r radius of the electron’s path (m)

For any other wavelength, each successive lap of the loop arrives out of step with the last, and the wave destructively interferes with itself — no such state can persist. This is the standing-wave condition from topic 12: a string fixed at both ends supports only the wavelengths that fit its length, so it produces only discrete frequencies. A bound electron is a standing wave on a closed loop, so only discrete wavelengths fit, which means only discrete momenta (p = h/λ) and therefore only discrete energies. The model is qualitative — it explains why levels must exist, rather than depicting the atom — but its conclusion is exact: atomic energies take fixed values.

Key idea A bound electron behaves as a standing wave. Only patterns with a whole number of de Broglie wavelengths can exist, so the energy of an atom is quantised, and a quantised atom is stable.
Connects The wavelength that must fit around the nucleus is the same de Broglie wavelength from topic 15 — the electron that diffracted through a crystal there is here a closed standing wave, and its wave character accounts for the stability of the atom.

Energy levels and transitions ΔE = hf = hc/λ

An energy-level diagram represents the allowed energies of an atom as horizontal lines on a vertical energy scale. The lowest level is the ground state, the most stable configuration of the atom; every level above it is an excited state. The highest level corresponds to ionisation: an electron given this much energy or more escapes the atom entirely — for hydrogen, 13.6 eV from the ground state. Levels are labelled in electron-volts because atomic energies expressed in joules are very small numbers: 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.

Two labelling conventions appear, as shown in Figure 13.1. VCAA examination diagrams often place the ground state at 0 eV with energies climbing to ionisation at 13.6 eV. Many reference books instead place ionisation at 0 eV, so every bound level is negative and the ground state lies at −13.6 eV — the negative sign indicates that a bound electron requires energy to be added before it can escape. The two conventions describe identical physics, because only the differences between levels are measurable.

When an electron drops from a higher state to a lower one, the atom emits a single photon. The photon’s energy equals the difference in energy between the two states:

ΔE = hf = hcλ on formula sheet ΔE energy difference between the two states = photon energy (J or eV) · h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s = 4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV s · f photon frequency (Hz) · λ photon wavelength (m) · c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹

The same equation runs upward: an electron is promoted to a higher state only by absorbing a photon whose energy exactly equals the gap. On a level diagram, a downward arrow is an emission and an upward arrow is an absorption. Large gaps produce high-frequency, short-wavelength photons; small gaps produce low-frequency, long-wavelength photons. A photon of 13.6 eV or more (λ ≤ 9.1 × 10⁻⁸ m, deep ultraviolet) ionises ground-state hydrogen — and because a free electron may carry any kinetic energy, the spectrum above the ionisation level is continuous, not discrete.

Trap The photon energy is the difference between two states, never the energy of a single level. A hydrogen electron falling from the n = 3 level (12.1 eV) to the n = 2 level (10.2 eV) emits a 1.9 eV photon — not a 12.1 eV photon. The difference between the two levels must be taken before applying ΔE = hf.

Worked example — one transition, one line

Ishva examines the hydrogen energy-level diagram, as shown in Figure 13.1, in which the n = 2 and n = 3 levels lie 10.2 eV and 12.1 eV above the ground state. An electron drops from n = 3 to n = 2. Calculate the frequency and the wavelength of the emitted photon.

Formula: ΔE = hf, so f = ΔE/h with ΔE = 12.1 − 10.2 = 1.9 eV.

Substitution: f = 1.9 ÷ (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵) = 4.6 × 10¹⁴ Hz.

Answer: the emitted photon has wavelength λ = hc/ΔE = (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ 1.9 = 6.5 × 10⁻⁷ m — a red spectral line. Using h in eV s avoids unit conversion while the energies are in eV; in joules the photon energy is ΔE = 1.9 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 3.0 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.

Key idea Each spectral line corresponds to one transition between two specific states: ΔE = hf = hc/λ. Down the ladder is emission; up the ladder is absorption of exactly the gap energy.
Connects The electron-volt used here was defined in U3 electric fields as the energy gained by one electron crossing one volt — the same unit defined by W = qV applies to atomic energy differences.

Emission and absorption spectra metal vapour lamps

An emission spectrum is produced by a hot or electrically excited gas. In a discharge tube, free electrons accelerated by the applied voltage collide with gas atoms and promote bound electrons into excited states. The excited electrons fall back toward the ground state — sometimes directly, sometimes in several steps — and each downward step emits one photon of one specific energy.

Viewed through a spectroscope, the light is a set of sharp bright lines on a dark background. The lines are discrete because the atom has only fixed levels, so only certain energy differences are available; no transition exists between the lines, so no light is emitted there.

Each element has its own set of levels, so each element produces a distinct pattern of lines that identifies the element in a laboratory flame or in the atmosphere of a star. The number of possible lines can be read off the diagram. With electrons able to occupy n levels, every pair of levels is a possible downward transition:

N = n(n − 1)2 not on sheet N maximum number of emission lines · n number of energy levels involved — e.g. the five levels of Figure 13.1 allow 5 × 4 ÷ 2 = 10 distinct lines

A metal vapour lamp is an application of an emission spectrum. In a sodium street light, an electric arc vaporises sodium and the current excites the vapour by collision; nearly all of the light is then emitted in one dominant transition, 2.10 eV down to the ground state, giving λ = hc/ΔE = (4.14 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 3.0 × 10⁸) ÷ 2.10 = 5.9 × 10⁻⁷ m — an orange-yellow output. Concentrating the output into one narrow visible line makes these lamps efficient. Mercury vapour lamps operate by the same mechanism but emit a different line pattern, the blue-white set by mercury’s level spacing.

An absorption spectrum is the same line pattern shown as dark lines rather than bright. When white light — a continuous spectrum from a hot filament or a star’s surface — passes through a cool gas, the gas absorbs only those photons whose energy exactly matches a gap above an occupied level. The absorbing atoms quickly re-emit, but in random directions, so the forward beam is depleted at exactly those wavelengths: dark lines on a continuous background, at the same positions as the element’s corresponding emission lines, as shown in Figure 13.2.

Trap Absorption is all-or-nothing: a photon of 11.0 eV is not absorbed by ground-state hydrogen, because no level lies 11.0 eV above the ground state — a photon cannot deposit part of its energy and continue on. A cool gas has almost every atom in the ground state, so its absorption spectrum shows only transitions starting from the ground state — usually fewer lines than the same element’s emission spectrum.

Quick checks

Check 13.1 Using the standing-wave model, explain why a bound electron in an atom can have only certain energies.
A bound electron is a matter wave on a closed path, so a whole number of de Broglie wavelengths must fit the circumference (nλ = 2πr); any other wavelength destructively interferes with itself and cannot persist. Discrete wavelengths mean discrete momenta (p = h/λ) and therefore discrete energies.
Check 13.2 In hydrogen, the n = 4 level lies 12.8 eV above the ground state and the n = 2 level lies 10.2 eV above it. Calculate the energy of the photon emitted in the n = 4 → n = 2 transition, in eV and in joules.
The photon carries the difference between the two states: ΔE = 12.8 − 10.2 = 2.6 eV. In joules, 2.6 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 4.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.
Check 13.3 The atoms of a certain gas have four energy levels. Determine the maximum number of different lines in the gas’s emission spectrum.
Every pair of levels is a possible downward transition: N = n(n − 1)/2 = 4 × 3 ÷ 2 = 6 lines.
Check 13.4 A photon of energy 11.0 eV passes through cool hydrogen gas, whose first two excited states lie 10.2 eV and 12.1 eV above the ground state. State whether the photon can be absorbed and explain your answer.
It cannot be absorbed. Absorption requires the photon energy to match a level difference exactly, and no level lies 11.0 eV above the ground state — the photon cannot hand over part of its energy, so it passes through.
Check 13.5 Sodium vapour absorbs light at 5.9 × 10⁻⁷ m and then re-emits it. Explain why the absorption spectrum nevertheless shows a dark line at this wavelength.
The re-emitted photons leave in random directions, so only a tiny fraction continue toward the observer. The forward beam is depleted at 5.9 × 10⁻⁷ m, which appears as a dark line against the continuous background.
