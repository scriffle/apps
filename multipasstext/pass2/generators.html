7 · Generators and alternators

Turning a coil steadily in a constant magnetic field keeps the flux through it changing, so an emf is induced for as long as the coil turns. The remaining design choice is how the rotating coil is connected to the external circuit: slip rings deliver AC; a split-ring commutator delivers DC.

The spinning coil the 90° offset

A generator is a coil of N turns mounted on an axle between the poles of a magnet and turned by an external torque, such as a steam turbine, a wind turbine or a hand crank. It is the simple DC motor of Area of Study 2 with the direction of energy conversion reversed: a motor converts electrical energy into rotation; a generator converts rotation into electrical energy. Figure 7.1 shows the two standard forms.

As the coil rotates, the magnetic flux through it changes continuously: greatest when the plane of the coil is perpendicular to the field and the coil presents its full area to the field lines, zero when the plane is parallel to the field and no field lines thread the coil. In the maximum-flux position:

ΦB = B⊥A on formula sheet ΦB magnetic flux (Wb) · B⊥ magnetic field perpendicular to the area (T) · A area of the coil (m²) · 1 Wb = 1 T m²

Uniform rotation carries the coil smoothly between these orientations, so the flux rises and falls sinusoidally between +B⊥A and −B⊥A. Faraday’s law turns that changing flux into an emf:

ε = −N ΔΦBΔt on formula sheet ε induced emf (V) · N number of turns · ΔΦB change in magnetic flux (Wb) · Δt time interval (s) · the minus sign is Lenz’s law: the induced emf opposes the change

The emf at any instant is set by the gradient of the flux–time graph, not by the size of the flux. Two positions in each rotation therefore behave in the opposite way to a first expectation based on the size of the flux. When the flux is at a maximum — coil plane perpendicular to the field — the flux is momentarily not changing at all: the gradient is zero, so the emf is zero. When the flux is zero — coil plane parallel to the field — the flux is changing at its fastest, because the field lines are about to stop threading one face of the coil and start threading the other: the gradient is steepest, so the emf is at its peak.

The emf trace is therefore a sinusoid with the same period as the flux but shifted by a quarter of a cycle (90°): every emf peak sits directly below a flux zero, and every emf zero below a flux maximum. Figure 7.3 sets one full rotation against both graphs.

Key idea The induced emf follows the gradient of the flux: ε is a maximum where ΦB is zero and changing fastest, and ε is zero where ΦB is a maximum and momentarily unchanging.
Trap “Maximum flux gives maximum emf” states the relationship the wrong way around. At the maximum-flux position the coil presents a full but momentarily unchanging area to the field, so ΔΦB/Δt = 0 and the emf is zero at that instant. An emf requires the flux to be changing — it does not require the flux to be large.

Worked example — average emf over a quarter turn

Magnus hand-cranks a generator whose rectangular coil has 50 turns, each 8.0 cm × 5.0 cm, in a uniform magnetic field of 0.25 T, at a steady 10 Hz. Calculate the magnitude of the average emf induced as the coil rotates from the maximum-flux position to the zero-flux position.

Formula: the magnitude of the average emf is εavg = N ΔΦBΔt, where ΔΦB is the full flux change and Δt is the time for one quarter of a revolution.

Flux change: ΦB = B⊥A = 0.25 × (0.080 × 0.050) = 1.0 × 10⁻³ Wb at the maximum-flux position, falling to zero a quarter turn later, so ΔΦB = 1.0 × 10⁻³ Wb.

Time: T = 1/f = 0.10 s, and a quarter turn takes Δt = T/4 = 0.025 s — not the full period.

Answer: εavg = 50 × 1.0 × 10⁻³ ÷ 0.025 = 2.0 V. This is an average over the quarter turn; the instantaneous emf varies through the rotation, peaking at the zero-flux instant.

Connects This is the coil-in-field geometry of the simple DC motor run in reverse. In Area of Study 2 a current placed in the field produced rotation via F = nIlB; here rotation in the field produces an emf via Faraday’s law — one machine, two directions of energy flow.

Alternators: slip rings preserve the AC slip rings

An alternator is an AC generator. Each end of the rotating coil is joined to its own continuous metal ring — a slip ring — mounted on the axle, and a stationary carbon brush presses against each ring, as shown in Figure 7.1(a). The slip rings maintain continuous electrical contact between the rotating coil and the stationary external circuit, so current can be drawn from the coil without the connecting wires twisting.

Because each coil end keeps its own ring permanently, the connections never swap and the terminals follow the coil’s emf exactly: when the emf reverses every half revolution, the output reverses with it, and the alternator delivers the unaltered sinusoid — alternating current. Almost all grid electricity is generated this way, by turbine-driven alternators turning at a rate locked to the 50 Hz of the Australian mains. On any diagram, the recognition cue is the count: two separate rings means slip rings, and therefore AC output.

DC generators: the commutator as rectifier split-ring commutator

Replace the two slip rings with a single ring split into two insulated halves — a split-ring commutator, each half connected to one end of the coil — and the same machine delivers DC, as shown in Figure 7.1(b). Every half revolution the brushes cross the gaps and swap from one half-ring to the other, timed exactly to the coil passing the maximum-flux position — the instant the emf is zero and about to reverse direction. Each time the coil’s emf reverses, the connections reverse with it, so the polarity at the output terminals never changes: the negative half-cycles of the sinusoid are flipped upward, as shown in Figure 7.2.

The output is direct current, but it is not steady: it rises from zero to the peak value and falls back twice per revolution. The commutator rectifies the output — forces it to keep one sign — but does nothing to smooth it.

The same split-ring commutator appears in the DC motor of Area of Study 2, doing the opposite job: in the motor it reverses the supply current through the coil every half revolution so the torque keeps one rotational direction; in the generator it reverses the coil’s connection to the circuit so the output keeps one polarity. A generator’s rotation is produced by the external torque, not by its commutator.

Key idea One component, two roles: the split-ring commutator reverses the current through a motor’s coil, and rectifies the output of a generator. Slip rings preserve AC; a split ring produces varying DC.
Trap A split-ring commutator does not produce a constant voltage. Its output still varies continuously between zero and the peak value, twice per revolution — it is DC only in the sense that the sign never reverses. Describing the commutator’s job in a generator as “keeping the coil turning” imports the motor explanation into the wrong machine.

Reading the output trace f, T, Vp–p

An oscilloscope across the output terminals draws the emf–time trace, and four quantities describe it. The amplitude is the peak voltage Vpeak, the greatest value the emf reaches. The peak-to-peak voltage Vp–p is the full vertical span from the bottom of a trough to the top of a crest, so Vp–p = 2Vpeak for a sinusoid. The period T is the time for one complete cycle — for a single rotating coil, exactly one revolution. The frequency f is the number of cycles each second:

f = 1T not on sheet f frequency (Hz) · T period (s) · for a single coil, the frequency of the trace equals the rotation frequency

When the alternator drives a current, the current alternates in step with the emf and is read the same way: peak current Ipeak and peak-to-peak current Ip–p = 2Ipeak. Figure 7.3 sets one revolution of the coil against the flux and emf graphs with the trace measurements marked. Reading is direct: crests at +6.0 V and troughs at −6.0 V, with successive crests 40 ms apart, give an amplitude of 6.0 V, Vp–p = 12 V, T = 0.040 s and f = 1 ÷ 0.040 = 25 Hz — the coil turns 25 times each second.

Consider the coil driven twice as fast. Two quantities change together. Each revolution takes half the time, so the period halves to 20 ms and the frequency doubles to 50 Hz. At the same time, every flux change now happens in half the time: ΔΦB is unchanged while Δt is halved, so by ε = −NΔΦB/Δt the emf doubles at every point on the trace — the amplitude rises from 6.0 V to 12 V and Vp–p from 12 V to 24 V.

The reverse interpretation is equally important: a trace whose amplitude has doubled at an unchanged period cannot result from faster rotation, which would also have shortened the period. An amplitude-only change must come from doubling the number of turns N, the field strength B or the coil area A — each scales the flux swing without touching the timing.

Key idea Rotation speed sets both the timing and the size of the output: doubling it doubles both the frequency and the amplitude. An amplitude change at constant frequency must come from N, B or A instead.

Photovoltaic cells and the inverter DC, then AC

A photovoltaic (PV) cell generates electricity without any motion at all: it converts light energy directly into electrical energy. There is no coil, no magnet and no changing flux. Light absorbed in thin layers of treated semiconductor frees electrons, and a built-in electric field at the junction between the layers drives them around the external circuit in one direction only: the output is DC for as long as light falls on the cell. A single cell produces only a small voltage, so a solar panel connects many cells, and a rooftop array many panels.

How the layers establish that junction field is semiconductor physics beyond the scope of this course, and the inverter’s internal circuitry is excluded for the same reason; only the input–output behaviour of each device is required.

Households run on the grid’s standard supply: 230 V rms AC at 50 Hz — alternating, because the grid was built around alternators, and because transformers operate only on AC. A rooftop solar system therefore feeds its panels into an inverter, the device that converts the panels’ DC into mains-compatible AC, which can then power the household’s appliances or be exported to the grid.

Connects Light is able to free electrons from a material because it delivers its energy in discrete packets — photons, as treated in Unit 4. The photoelectric effect is the laboratory demonstration of the physics on which every PV cell relies.

Quick checks

Check 7.1 An alternator coil rotates uniformly in a uniform magnetic field. State the value of the induced emf at the instant the magnetic flux through the coil is at its maximum, and explain why.
Zero. The emf follows the rate of change of flux, not its size: at maximum flux the gradient of the flux–time graph is momentarily zero, so ε = −NΔΦB/Δt = 0 at that instant.
Check 7.2 Anika inspects a generator diagram and sees a single ring split into two halves connecting the coil to the brushes. State whether the output is AC, constant DC or varying DC, and name the component.
Varying DC. The component is a split-ring commutator: it swaps the coil’s connections to the circuit at each zero of the emf, so the polarity never reverses — but the magnitude still rises and falls between zero and the peak twice per revolution.
Check 7.3 The oscilloscope trace from an alternator shows a peak voltage of 3.0 V and a period of 50 ms. Calculate the peak-to-peak voltage and the frequency of rotation of the coil.
Vp–p = 2 × 3.0 = 6.0 V, and f = 1/T = 1 ÷ 0.050 = 20 Hz — one cycle of the trace is one revolution of the coil.
Check 7.4 The rotation rate of the alternator in Check 7.3 is doubled. Determine the new peak voltage and the new period.
Both change: the same flux change now occurs in half the time, so the peak voltage doubles to 6.0 V, and each revolution takes half as long, so the period halves to 25 ms (f = 40 Hz).
Check 7.5 Kenji installs rooftop solar panels on a house whose appliances run on 230 V rms AC at 50 Hz. Name the device that must sit between the panels and the household circuits, and state its function.
An inverter. The panels produce DC, and the inverter converts that DC into mains-compatible AC — its internal circuitry is not required, only this input–output role.
