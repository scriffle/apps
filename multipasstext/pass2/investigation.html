18 · The scientific investigation

A student-designed practical investigation generates primary data on fields, motion or light, using one continuous independent variable, and is assessed as a scientific poster. This chapter sets out how to design the investigation, judge the quality of its measurements, process and graph the data, and communicate a defensible conclusion in the prescribed conventions.

Designing the investigation aim · variables · methodology

A scientific investigation begins with a clearly stated investigation question: a specific, answerable question about the relationship between two measurable quantities. The question becomes the title of the poster. From it follow the aim, a statement of what the investigation sets out to determine, and the relevant physics concepts and key definitions, identified from the study design so that the design rests on established theory.

A hypothesis is a testable, falsifiable prediction of the relationship between the variables, expressed before data are collected and justified by the background physics. A hypothesis specifies a direction or form: for example, that the period of a pendulum increases with its length, or that the magnetic field strength decreases with distance from a magnet. A prediction states the expected outcome of the specific procedure.

Variables

An investigation in this area of study tests how one quantity depends on another, so three classes of variable are identified before any measurement is taken.

  • The independent variable is the quantity the investigator deliberately changes. The study design requires one continuous independent variable: a variable that can take any value across a range, such as length, distance, angle, mass or time, rather than a category. A continuous independent variable allows a graph to be drawn and a gradient to be found.
  • The dependent variable is the quantity that is measured in response, and which is expected to change as the independent variable is changed.
  • The controlled variables are the quantities held constant so that they cannot affect the dependent variable. Identifying and fixing the controlled variables is what allows the measured change in the dependent variable to be attributed to the independent variable.
Key idea The investigator changes the independent variable, measures the dependent variable, and holds every controlled variable constant. With one continuous independent variable, the dependent variable is measured at a series of values across a range so that a relationship can be graphed.

Methodology and method

The methodology is the overall type of scientific investigation chosen; the method is the specific step-by-step procedure carried out. For an investigation with one continuous independent variable, the appropriate methodology is a controlled experiment: the independent variable is varied across a range while all controlled variables are held constant, and the dependent variable is measured at each value. The method specifies the equipment, the values of the independent variable to be tested, the number of repeated measurements at each value, and the instruments used.

The method generates primary data, data collected first-hand by the investigator. Primary data may be quantitative (numerical measurements, such as a time in seconds or a field strength in millitesla) or qualitative (observed properties that are not numerical, such as the colour of a fringe or whether a surface is reflective). An investigation with a continuous independent variable relies on quantitative primary data, with qualitative observations recorded where they support the analysis.

Health, safety and ethical guidelines

Before data collection, relevant health, safety and ethical guidelines are applied. A risk assessment identifies hazards and the controls that reduce them: eye protection where projectiles or lasers are used, safe handling of masses and electrical supplies, and limits on laser power and exposure. Ethical conduct requires honest recording of all results, including those that do not match the hypothesis, and acknowledgement of all sources and assistance.

The logbook

All primary data, observations, procedures and changes to the method are recorded in a logbook at the time they occur. The logbook is dated and kept in chronological order; it authenticates the primary data as the investigator’s own first-hand work, and provides the record from which results are later processed and reported.

Connects The physics being investigated is drawn from Units 3 and 4: a fields investigation may measure field strength against distance (topic 5, topic 7), a motion investigation may measure acceleration or period, and a light investigation may measure fringe spacing or intensity (topic 13). The design conventions on this page apply to whichever context is chosen.

Measurement quality accuracy · precision · error

Every measurement is judged against the true value: the value that would be found if the quantity could be measured perfectly. The true value cannot be known exactly, but the language of measurement quality describes how close a result is likely to be to it, and how the result was obtained.

Accuracy and precision

Accuracy is a qualitative description: a measurement is accurate if it is close to the true value. Accuracy is described as more accurate or less accurate; it is never stated as a number. Precision is how close repeated measurements are to each other, that is, the spread of the readings. Precision gives no indication of closeness to the true value, so it is separate from accuracy. A set of readings may be precise but inaccurate if they agree closely with one another yet are all far from the true value.

Trap Accuracy and precision are not synonyms and must not be used interchangeably. Precision is the agreement among repeated measurements; accuracy is closeness to the true value. A clustered set of readings is precise; whether it is also accurate depends entirely on where the true value lies. State accuracy qualitatively (more or less accurate) and never as a number.

Resolution

The resolution of an instrument is the smallest change in the measured quantity that produces a perceptible change in the reading. The resolution of a millimetre ruler is 1 mm; the resolution of a stopwatch reading to 0.01 s is 0.01 s. Resolution is a property of the scale and is not the same as the uncertainty of a measurement made with the instrument.

Error and uncertainty

A measurement error is the difference between a measurement result and the true value. Uncertainty is different: it is the interval within which the true value is expected to lie. A result written as a mean plus or minus an uncertainty, such as 20 ± 1 mm, states that the true value is expected to lie between 19 mm and 21 mm. On a graph this interval is drawn as an uncertainty bar.

Trap Resolution is not uncertainty. The resolution of a millimetre ruler is 1 mm, but by VCE convention its measurement uncertainty is half the smallest scale increment, ±0.5 mm. Stating the uncertainty as the full increment, or confusing the two terms, is a common error.

Random and systematic error

Two kinds of error affect measurements in different ways.

  • Random error consists of unpredictable variations that cause a spread of readings. Random error affects precision. It arises from limits of reading the instrument and from small uncontrolled fluctuations. It is reduced by repeating measurements and averaging, because the variations tend to cancel.
  • Systematic error causes readings to differ from the true value in a consistent direction, by the same amount each time. Systematic error affects accuracy. It arises from calibration, from a fault in the instrument such as a zero error, or from the method, such as parallax in reading a scale. Averaging does not reduce systematic error, because every reading is shifted the same way.
Trap Averaging reduces random error only. Systematic error affects all readings equally, so taking more readings and averaging them does not remove it. A systematic error is removed by finding and correcting its cause, for example by zeroing the instrument or eliminating parallax.

Repeatability, reproducibility and validity

Repeatability is the closeness of agreement between successive measurements made under the same conditions: the same observer, instrument, method and location, over a short time. Repeatability evaluates precision. Reproducibility is the closeness of agreement when conditions are changed: a different observer, instrument, method or location. Reproducibility checks claimed precision and can uncover systematic error, so it links to accuracy.

An investigation is valid if it investigates what it sets out to investigate. Validity is threatened if controlled variables are allowed to change, so that the change in the dependent variable can no longer be attributed to the independent variable, or by observer bias, in which the observer’s expectations influence the readings.

Key idea Random error scatters readings and harms precision; it is reduced by repeating and averaging. Systematic error shifts every reading the same way and harms accuracy; averaging does not fix it. Repeatability and reproducibility evaluate precision; validity is whether the experiment tests what it claims to test.

Recording and processing data significant figures · uncertainty

Significant figures

Calculated results are stated to a number of significant figures consistent with the precision of the data. Two rules govern calculations.

  • When values are added or subtracted, the answer keeps the fewest decimal places of the values used.
  • When values are multiplied or divided, the answer keeps the fewest significant figures of the values used.

Rounding is carried out only at the final step. Intermediate results are kept to extra figures so that rounding does not accumulate.

Trap The two significant-figure rules are not interchangeable. Addition and subtraction are governed by decimal places; multiplication and division are governed by significant figures. Rounding at each intermediate step, rather than only at the end, introduces avoidable error.

Uncertainty of a measurement

By VCE convention, the uncertainty of a reading taken with an analogue or digital instrument is half its smallest scale increment. A millimetre ruler has a smallest increment of 1 mm, giving an uncertainty of ±0.5 mm. A stopwatch reading to 0.01 s has an uncertainty of ±0.005 s. A result is reported as a mean plus or minus an uncertainty:

measurement = mean ± uncertainty not on sheet the true value is expected to lie within the interval mean ± uncertainty

The percentage uncertainty expresses the uncertainty as a fraction of the measurement, which allows the relative quality of different measurements to be compared:

percentage uncertainty = uncertaintymeasurement × 100 not on sheet a dimensionless percentage; smaller percentage uncertainty indicates a relatively more precise measurement

Worked example — stating a length with its uncertainty

A length is measured with a millimetre ruler as 152 mm. The smallest scale increment is 1 mm, so the uncertainty is half of this, ±0.5 mm.

Result: the length is 152.0 ± 0.5 mm. The true value is expected to lie between 151.5 mm and 152.5 mm.

Percentage uncertainty: 0.5152.0 × 100 = 0.33%, stated to two significant figures.

Worked example — a significant-figure calculation

A trolley travels 1.27 m in 0.42 s, and the average speed is required. The calculation is a division, so the answer keeps the fewest significant figures of the values used. The value 1.27 m has three significant figures and 0.42 s has two, so the answer is stated to two significant figures.

Speed = 1.270.42 = 3.0238… = 3.0 m s⁻¹. Rounding is applied only at this final step.

Key idea State every result as a mean ± uncertainty, with the uncertainty equal to half the smallest scale increment. Apply decimal places when adding or subtracting and significant figures when multiplying or dividing, and round only at the final step.

Graphs, linearisation and the gradient line of best fit · gradient

A graph reveals the relationship between the independent and dependent variables. The independent variable is plotted on the horizontal axis and the dependent variable on the vertical axis. Each axis is labelled with its quantity and unit, and scaled so that the data fill the available area. Each plotted point carries uncertainty bars: a vertical bar shows the uncertainty in the dependent variable and a horizontal bar shows the uncertainty in the independent variable. The true value for each point is expected to lie within its bars.

Line of best fit

When the relationship is linear, a line of best fit is drawn so that it passes as close as possible to all the points, with points scattered evenly above and below it. The line of best fit should pass within the uncertainty bars of the points. Several errors are to be avoided: the line must not be drawn through only the first and last points, must not be forced through a chosen point or through the origin, and must not be extended beyond the range of the data.

Trap A line of best fit is not drawn through the first and last points only, is not forced through the origin, and is not extrapolated beyond the data. If a line that is expected on theoretical grounds to pass through the origin does not, that displacement is evidence of a systematic error.

Linearising data

When the relationship between the raw variables is not a straight line, the data are linearised: one variable is transformed so that a plot of the transformed data is a straight line. Both the variable and its axis unit must be transformed. If a quantity is expected to depend on the square of the time, the values of time are squared and the axis is labelled with the squared quantity and its unit, for example “time² (s²)”. The gradient of the linearised graph then equals a physical quantity.

The gradient

The gradient of a straight-line graph is the rise divided by the run, read from two points that lie on the line and are chosen far apart, not from points taken from the data table:

gradient = riserun = ΔyΔx not on sheet read Δy and Δx from two points on the line of best fit, far apart; account for any ×10ⁿ scale factors on the axes

The units of the gradient are the units of the vertical axis divided by the units of the horizontal axis. Any ×10ⁿ scale factor printed on an axis must be included when reading values. The gradient is not adjusted to match a known constant.

Trap Read the gradient from two widely spaced points that lie on the line of best fit, never from points in the data table that may not lie on the line. Include any ×10ⁿ axis scale factors, give the gradient its correct units (vertical-axis unit divided by horizontal-axis unit), and do not adjust the gradient to force agreement with an expected value.

Worked example — linearising a pendulum graph

The period T of a simple pendulum is related to its length L by T = 2π√(L/g), so a plot of T against L is a curve. Squaring gives T² = 4π²gL, which is linear in L. The values of T are squared and plotted on the vertical axis, labelled “period² (s²)”, against L in metres on the horizontal axis.

Two points on the line of best fit, chosen far apart, are read as (0.20 m, 0.80 s²) and (1.00 m, 4.0 s²). The gradient is 4.0 − 0.801.00 − 0.20 = 3.20.80 = 4.0 s² m⁻¹, with units of vertical-axis unit divided by horizontal-axis unit.

The gradient equals 4π²g, so g = 4π²gradient = 39.54.0 = 9.9 m s⁻², stated to two significant figures. The gradient of the linearised graph has yielded a physical quantity.

Key idea Linearise by transforming the variable and its axis unit together, so that the gradient of the straight line equals a physical quantity. Read the gradient as rise over run from two widely spaced points on the line of best fit, give it the correct units, and include any axis scale factors.

Evidence, conclusions and the poster scientific poster

The nature of evidence

Primary data constitute evidence that bears on the hypothesis. Evidence is organised, analysed and evaluated to find patterns; the gradient of linearised data, the trend of the line of best fit, and the spread of the points relative to their uncertainty bars are all read as evidence. A conclusion states how strongly the evidence supports the hypothesis, after the causes of uncertainty, the assumptions made, and the limitations of the data and method have been considered.

Evidence can support or refute a hypothesis, model or theory, but it cannot establish one with certainty. A conclusion therefore states that the evidence supports, partially supports or does not support the hypothesis. The words “proves” and “disproves” are not used, because a finite set of measurements cannot prove a general claim.

Trap A conclusion never states that the evidence “proves” or “disproves” the hypothesis. The correct expressions are that the evidence supports, partially supports, or does not support the hypothesis. Evidence that partially supports a hypothesis matches the predicted trend over part of the range, or matches the trend but not the predicted value.

Limitations and improvements

The evaluation identifies the limitations of the investigation, the assumptions on which it relied, and the causes of uncertainty in the data, then proposes improvements that would address them. A limitation is a specific weakness, such as a restricted range of the independent variable or a controlled variable that could not be held perfectly constant; an improvement is a concrete change that would reduce its effect.

Science communication conventions

The investigation is reported using the conventions of science communication: correct terminology, symbols, equations and standard abbreviations; consistent units; and results stated to an appropriate number of significant figures. Quantities are written with their units in upright form, such as m s⁻¹, and uncertainties with the ± symbol.

The scientific poster

The investigation is assessed as a scientific poster of no more than 600 words. At its centre is a single Communication statement: one sentence that answers the investigation question. This statement occupies 20–25% of the poster area. The poster carries the prescribed sections.

  • Title — the investigation question.
  • Student name.
  • Introduction — the aim, the hypothesis or prediction, and the background physics.
  • Methodology and methods — the methodology together with the data-generation and data-analysis methods.
  • Results — the data presented to reveal trends, including the central one-sentence Communication statement.
  • Discussion — interpretation and evaluation of the data; limitations and improvements; the relationship to the physics; and the link back to the hypothesis.
  • Conclusion — an answer to the question, its limitations, and implications or further work, introducing no new information.
  • References and acknowledgements.

Communication on a poster is succinct: the word limit requires that only essential material is included, and all references and sources of assistance are acknowledged. The key findings and their implications are explained clearly enough that a reader can judge how the evidence answers the question.

Key idea The poster answers the investigation question in a single central Communication statement, supported by results that reveal the trend and a discussion that evaluates the evidence. The conclusion states that the evidence supports, partially supports or does not support the hypothesis, within a limit of 600 words and the prescribed sections.

Quick checks

Check 15.1 A student records five timings of a pendulum that agree closely with one another but all fall about 0.3 s below the accepted value. State whether the measurements are precise, accurate, or both, and justify your answer.
The measurements are precise but not accurate. Precision is the closeness of repeated measurements to each other; the close agreement shows high precision. Accuracy is closeness to the true value; because all readings fall consistently below the accepted value, they are not close to the true value and are therefore inaccurate. The consistent offset indicates a systematic error.
Check 15.2 A stopwatch has not been zeroed and adds 0.2 s to every timing. State whether this is a random or systematic error, state which of accuracy and precision it affects, and explain whether repeating the measurement and averaging would remove it.
It is a systematic error, because it shifts every reading by the same amount in the same direction. It affects accuracy, not precision, because the readings are all displaced from the true value but remain as close to each other as before. Averaging does not remove a systematic error, since every reading is shifted equally; the error is removed only by correcting its cause, here by zeroing the stopwatch.
Check 15.3 A distance of 0.84 m is travelled in 0.30 s. Calculate the average speed and state it to the correct number of significant figures, explaining the rule used.
Speed = 0.84 ÷ 0.30 = 2.8 m s⁻¹. The calculation is a division, so the answer keeps the fewest significant figures of the values used. Both 0.84 and 0.30 have two significant figures, so the answer is stated to two significant figures, 2.8 m s⁻¹, with rounding applied only at this final step.
Check 15.4 The kinetic energy of a falling mass is expected to depend on the square of its speed. A graph of energy against speed is a curve. Describe how the data should be linearised, including what happens to the axis, and state what the gradient of the linearised graph represents in terms of how it is read.
Plot energy against speed²: square each speed value and relabel the horizontal axis “speed² (m² s⁻²)”, transforming both the variable and its unit. A graph of energy against speed² is then a straight line. The gradient is read as rise over run from two widely spaced points that lie on the line of best fit (not from table points), with units of the vertical-axis unit divided by the horizontal-axis unit, and equals a physical quantity (here half the mass).
Check 15.5 A student writes: “The results prove that field strength is inversely proportional to distance.” Rewrite the statement using the correct conclusion language, and state one reason the original wording is unacceptable.
A correct form is: “The evidence supports the hypothesis that field strength is inversely proportional to distance,” or “partially supports” if only part of the range agrees. The word “proves” is unacceptable because a finite set of measurements, each carrying uncertainty, cannot establish a general relationship with certainty; evidence can only support, partially support or not support a hypothesis.
