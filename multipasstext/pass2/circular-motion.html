2 · Circular motion

An object moving at constant speed around a circle is accelerating at every instant, because its direction of motion is always changing. The whole topic reduces to a single task: identify which real force, directed toward the centre, supplies that acceleration.

Uniform circular motion Fnet = mv²/r

An object moves in uniform circular motion when it travels around a circle of fixed radius at constant speed. The speed is constant, but the velocity is not: velocity is a vector, and its direction changes continuously. At every point the velocity is tangential — directed along the circle, perpendicular to the radius. Because the velocity is always changing direction, the object is accelerating, even though its speed never changes.

The acceleration points toward the centre of the circle. It is called the centripetal acceleration (“centre-seeking”), and for an object of speed v moving on a circle of radius r its magnitude is:

a = v²r on formula sheet a centripetal acceleration (m s⁻²), directed toward the centre · v speed (m s⁻¹) · r radius of the circular path (m)

The acceleration is radial — perpendicular to the velocity at every instant. By Newton’s second law a mass cannot accelerate unless an unbalanced force acts on it, so a net force must point toward the centre. This net force is the centripetal force:

Fnet = mv²r on formula sheet Fnet net force toward the centre (N) · m mass (kg) · v speed (m s⁻¹) · r radius (m)

“Centripetal force” is not a new kind of force and never appears in a free-body diagram as a separate arrow. It is the name given to the resultant of the real forces — gravity, tension, friction, the normal force — once they are added together. In every problem the first task is to identify which real force, or which component of a real force, points toward the centre and therefore supplies mv²/r.

For a vehicle driving around a flat circular road, that force is friction between the tyres and the road, directed horizontally toward the centre of the bend. For an object whirled on a horizontal string, the force is the horizontal component of the tension in the string. In each case the net force, the acceleration and the radius all point along the same line, toward the centre.

Key idea In uniform circular motion the velocity is tangential, while the acceleration and the net force both point toward the centre and have magnitude v²/r and mv²/r. The speed is constant; the velocity is not.
Trap There is no outward “centrifugal force” in this physics. Nothing pushes the object away from the centre. The outward feeling a passenger reports is the seat or door pushing them inward to keep them on the circle, plus their own inertia carrying them straight on. Never draw an outward force arrow, and never set the centripetal force equal to an imagined outward force.
Connects The centripetal force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it does zero work (work = force × displacement along the force). That is precisely why the speed in uniform circular motion stays constant — the net force changes the direction of motion but never the kinetic energy.

Worked example — friction on a flat bend

A 1200 kg car rounds a flat circular bend of radius 45 m at a constant speed of 15 m s⁻¹. Determine the magnitude of the friction force the road must provide, and the minimum coefficient of friction that makes this possible. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻².

Formula: the friction force is the only horizontal force, so it supplies the centripetal force, Fnet = mv²r.

Substitution: Fnet = (1200 × 15²) ÷ 45 = (1200 × 225) ÷ 45

Answer: Fnet = 6.0 × 10³ N, directed horizontally toward the centre of the bend.

For the road to supply this, the available friction μmg must be at least 6.0 × 10³ N. On a flat road the normal force is N = mg = 1200 × 9.8 = 1.18 × 10⁴ N, so the minimum coefficient is μ = Fnet/N = 6.0 × 10³ ÷ 1.18 × 10⁴ = 0.51. If the actual friction available is less than this, the car cannot hold the bend and slides outward along a tangent.

Banked tracks v = √(rg tanθ)

A road or track is banked when its surface is tilted toward the centre of the bend. Banking allows a vehicle to round the bend without relying on friction. On a frictionless banked surface the only two forces acting on the vehicle are its weight mg, directed straight down, and the normal force N, directed perpendicular to the tilted surface. Because the surface is tilted by angle θ to the horizontal, the normal force is also tilted by θ from the vertical.

Resolve the normal force into horizontal and vertical components. The horizontal component, N sinθ, points toward the centre of the bend and supplies the centripetal force. The vertical component, N cosθ, points upward and balances the weight, because there is no vertical acceleration. Writing both conditions:

N sinθ = mv²r not on sheet horizontal: the inward component of N supplies the centripetal force
N cosθ = mg not on sheet vertical: the upward component of N balances the weight (no vertical acceleration)

Dividing the first equation by the second eliminates N and m, since sinθ/cosθ = tanθ:

tanθ = v²rg not on sheet derived by dividing the two equilibrium equations

Rearranging gives the centripetal acceleration and the design speed — the single speed at which a vehicle rounds the frictionless bank perfectly:

ac = g tanθ not on sheet centripetal acceleration on a frictionless bank at the design speed
v = √(rg tanθ) not on sheet design speed (m s⁻¹) · r radius (m) · g = 9.8 m s⁻² · θ bank angle — derived from Fnet = mv²/r

At the design speed the result depends only on r, g and θ — never on the mass of the vehicle. A truck and a motorcycle negotiate the same frictionless bank at the same design speed.

Key idea On a frictionless banked track the horizontal component of the normal force, N sinθ, alone supplies the centripetal force, while the vertical component N cosθ balances the weight. This gives ac = g tanθ and the design speed v = √(rg tanθ).
Trap The normal force on a slope is not equal to mg. On a bank it is N = mg/cosθ, which is larger than mg. Resolve N into horizontal and vertical components and write a separate equation for each direction; do not assume the normal force balances the weight directly. At the design speed there is no friction — only the components of N act. Above the design speed the vehicle tends to slide up the bank and friction points down the slope; below it the vehicle tends to slide down and friction points up the slope.

Worked example — design speed of a banked bend

A test track is banked at 20° to the horizontal. A bend on the track has radius 80 m. Determine the design speed at which a vehicle rounds this bend with no reliance on friction, and the centripetal acceleration at that speed. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻².

Formula: at the design speed the horizontal component of the normal force alone supplies the centripetal force, giving v = √(rg tanθ) and ac = g tanθ.

Substitution: ac = 9.8 × tan20° = 9.8 × 0.364 = 3.57 m s⁻², and v = √(80 × 9.8 × tan20°) = √(80 × 9.8 × 0.364) = √285

Answer: v = 16.9 m s⁻¹ ≈ 17 m s⁻¹, with a centripetal acceleration of 3.6 m s⁻² directed horizontally toward the centre of the bend. The mass of the vehicle does not enter the calculation.

Vertical circular motion top and bottom only

When an object moves in a vertical circle — a ball on a string, a bucket of water swung overhead, a vehicle crossing a hump or dipping through a valley — gravity acts the same way throughout (straight down), but its relationship to the centre of the circle changes from point to point. The analysis is required only at the two extreme points, the highest point and the lowest point, where the net force, the weight and the tension (or normal force) all lie along the vertical line through the centre.

At the top of the circle the centre is directly below the object. Both the weight mg and the string tension T point downward — toward the centre — so they add. Newton’s second law toward the centre gives:

Fnet = T + mg = mv²r not on sheet at the highest point: both T and mg point toward the centre (downward)

At the bottom of the circle the centre is directly above the object. The tension points upward toward the centre while the weight points downward away from it, so they subtract. Taking toward the centre (upward) as positive:

Fnet = T − mg = mv²r not on sheet at the lowest point: T points toward the centre (up), mg points away (down)

The tension at the bottom is therefore greater than at the top for the same speed, because at the bottom the tension must both support the weight and supply the centripetal force.

As the object slows near the top, less tension is needed. The minimum speed at the top is the speed at which the string just goes slack, so the tension falls to zero and gravity alone supplies the centripetal force. Setting T = 0 in the top equation:

mg = mv²r  ⟹  vmin = √(gr) not on sheet minimum speed at the top, where T = 0 and gravity alone is centripetal · independent of mass

Below this speed the object cannot maintain the circular path at the top and falls inward. For a vehicle the supporting surface provides a normal force N in place of tension. Crossing a hump (a convex crest), the centre is below the vehicle, so mg − N = mv²/r, giving N = m(g − v²/r): the road pushes up less than the weight, and the driver feels lighter. In a dip (a concave valley) the centre is above, so N − mg = mv²/r, giving N = m(g + v²/r): the road pushes up harder than the weight, and the driver feels heavier. The normal force falls to zero, and the vehicle leaves the road, when v² = gr over the crest.

Key idea At the top of a vertical circle both T and mg point toward the centre, so T + mg = mv²/r. At the bottom they oppose, so T − mg = mv²/r. The string just goes slack at the top when v = √(gr).
Trap The sign of the weight switches between the two points because the centre is in a different direction, not because gravity changes. Gravity is always mg straight down. At the top “down” is toward the centre, so the weight adds to the tension; at the bottom “down” is away from the centre, so the weight subtracts. Always decide which way the centre lies before writing Fnet = mv²/r, and never assume the minimum-speed condition applies anywhere except the top.

Worked example — tension at the top of a vertical circle

A 0.20 kg ball on the end of a string of length 0.80 m is swung in a vertical circle. Determine the minimum speed the ball can have at the highest point while the string stays taut, and the string tension at the highest point when the ball moves there at 4.0 m s⁻¹. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻².

Formula (minimum speed): at the top the string is just slack, so T = 0 and mg = mv²/r, giving vmin = √(gr).

Substitution: vmin = √(9.8 × 0.80) = √7.84

Answer: vmin = 2.8 m s⁻¹. Below this speed the string goes slack and the ball falls inward.

Formula (tension at 4.0 m s⁻¹): at the top both forces point toward the centre, so T + mg = mv²/r, giving T = mv²r − mg.

Substitution: T = (0.20 × 4.0²) ÷ 0.80 − 0.20 × 9.8 = 4.0 − 1.96

Answer: T = 2.0 N, directed along the string toward the centre. Because 4.0 m s⁻¹ exceeds the minimum speed of 2.8 m s⁻¹, the tension is positive and the string stays taut.

Satellites as circular motion r = RE + altitude

A satellite — whether a natural one such as the Moon or an artificial one such as the International Space Station — can be modelled as an object in uniform circular motion. It travels around the central body at constant speed on a path of fixed radius, so everything established for circular motion applies: the velocity is tangential, and the acceleration of magnitude v²/r points toward the centre of the orbit. The single force that supplies this centripetal acceleration is gravity, but the quantitative link between that force and the orbit — Newton’s law of universal gravitation and the relationship between orbital radius and period — belongs to Area of Study 2 and is developed in the Fields chapter. Here the satellite is treated purely as a case of circular motion.

The radius used in Fnet = mv²/r and a = v²/r is the orbital radius measured from the centre of the central body, in metres:

r = RE + altitude not on sheet r orbital radius from Earth’s centre (m) · RE = 6.37 × 10⁶ m · altitude measured from the surface, converted to metres

The orbit is independent of the satellite’s mass. Both a = v²/r and the gravitational acceleration depend only on the orbital radius and the central mass, so two satellites of different mass sharing the same orbit have the same speed, period and acceleration. A spanner released by an astronaut keeps pace with the station beside it.

A satellite in a circular orbit is accelerating, even though its speed is constant, because its direction of motion is always changing. The acceleration is explained by Newton’s second law: an unbalanced force — gravity — acts toward the centre, and an unbalanced force produces an acceleration in its own direction. It is not explained by the first law (which describes motion when forces are balanced) or the third law (which concerns the equal and opposite force the satellite exerts on Earth).

Key idea Model a satellite as uniform circular motion of radius r = RE + altitude (in metres). The orbit is independent of the satellite’s mass. The satellite accelerates because an unbalanced force — gravity — acts toward the centre: this is Newton’s second law.
Trap Two recurring mark-losers. First, the orbital radius is not the altitude: an altitude of 400 km gives r = RE + h = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 4.00 × 10⁵ = 6.77 × 10⁶ m, not 4.00 × 10⁵ m. Always add RE and convert the altitude from km to m. Second, “why is a satellite in a circular orbit accelerating?” is answered by Newton’s second law — an unbalanced (net) force causes acceleration toward the centre — never the first or third law.
Connects The reason gravity supplies exactly mv²/r, and how the orbital radius fixes the period, comes from Newton’s law of universal gravitation in Area of Study 2. The full orbit derivation, geostationary satellites and apparent weightlessness are developed there — see Satellites in circular orbit in the Fields chapter.

Worked example — acceleration of the International Space Station

The International Space Station orbits at an altitude of 400 km with an orbital period of 92 min. Modelling its motion as uniform circular motion, determine the orbital radius, the orbital speed and the centripetal acceleration. Take RE = 6.37 × 10⁶ m.

Formula: r = RE + altitude, then v = 2πrT and a = v²r.

Substitution (radius): r = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 4.00 × 10⁵ = 6.77 × 10⁶ m, with the altitude converted from 400 km to 4.00 × 10⁵ m. The period is T = 92 × 60 = 5.52 × 10³ s.

Substitution (speed): v = (2π × 6.77 × 10⁶) ÷ (5.52 × 10³) = 7.71 × 10³ m s⁻¹

Answer: a = v²/r = (7.71 × 10³)² ÷ (6.77 × 10⁶) = 8.8 m s⁻², directed toward Earth’s centre. This equals the gravitational field strength at that altitude, which is why gravity alone sustains the orbit — and the result does not depend on the station’s mass.

Quick checks

Check 2.1 A car rounds a flat circular bend at constant speed. State the direction of its acceleration and name the real force that supplies the centripetal force.
The acceleration is directed toward the centre of the bend (radially inward, perpendicular to the velocity). The centripetal force is supplied by friction between the tyres and the road, directed horizontally toward the centre. There is no outward force.
Check 2.2 A 900 kg car travels at 12 m s⁻¹ around a flat bend of radius 30 m. Calculate the net (centripetal) force required.
Fnet = mv²/r = (900 × 12²) ÷ 30 = (900 × 144) ÷ 30 = 4.3 × 10³ N, directed horizontally toward the centre of the bend.
Check 2.3 A track is banked at 15° and a bend has radius 60 m. Calculate the design speed at which a vehicle rounds the bend with no friction. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻².
At the design speed the horizontal component of the normal force alone is centripetal, so v = √(rg tanθ) = √(60 × 9.8 × tan15°) = √(60 × 9.8 × 0.268) = √158 = 12.6 ≈ 13 m s⁻¹. The mass does not enter the calculation.
Check 2.4 A 0.30 kg ball is swung in a vertical circle of radius 1.2 m. Calculate the minimum speed it can have at the highest point while the string stays taut, and explain why the weight is added to the tension there. Take g = 9.8 m s⁻².
At the top the string is just slack, so T = 0 and gravity alone is centripetal: vmin = √(gr) = √(9.8 × 1.2) = √11.76 = 3.4 m s⁻¹ (independent of mass). The weight is added to the tension at the top because the centre of the circle is directly below the ball, so both mg (straight down) and T point toward the centre: Fnet = T + mg = mv²/r.
Check 2.5 A satellite orbits at an altitude of 550 km. State the orbital radius to use in a = v²/r, and name the Newton's law that explains why the satellite is accelerating. Take RE = 6.37 × 10⁶ m.
The radius is measured from Earth’s centre: r = RE + altitude = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 5.50 × 10⁵ = 6.92 × 10⁶ m (the altitude must be converted from 550 km to 5.50 × 10⁵ m and added to RE). The satellite accelerates because an unbalanced force — gravity — acts toward the centre, which is Newton’s second law, not the first or third.
