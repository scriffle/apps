14 · Relativity and mass–energy

At speeds approaching the speed of light, the classical rules for time, length and energy give wrong answers. This topic states Einstein’s two postulates, quantifies their consequences through the Lorentz factor, and ends with the exchange rate between mass and energy: E0 = mc².

Where classical mechanics fails v → c

Classical mechanics rests on assumptions nobody thought to test for two centuries: time passes at the same rate for every observer, lengths are the same for everyone who measures them, velocities add directly, and kinetic energy ½mv² can grow without limit. At everyday speeds every experiment agrees. As speeds approach c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹, all four fail — an accelerator can supply energy to an electron indefinitely while the electron’s speed never reaches c.

Light raised the sharpest problem: topic 6 established that light travels at c — but relative to what? Classical velocity addition says an observer rushing toward a light source should measure more than c. Nineteenth-century physics answered with the ether, a hypothetical medium through which light travels at c; Earth’s orbital motion through it should produce an “ether wind”, with light travelling at different measured speeds in different directions.

In 1887 Michelson and Morley split one light beam along two perpendicular interferometer arms and recombined the reflections to form an interference pattern. If the ether wind existed, light travelling along the wind would take a different time from light travelling across it, so rotating the apparatus should shift the fringes by a predicted, measurable amount. The observed shift was zero — at every orientation, time of day and season: the null result.

The null result shows that the measured speed of light is the same in all directions regardless of Earth’s motion, and provides no evidence that the ether exists. It supports the central claim of special relativity — that c is the same for all observers — which Einstein adopted in 1905 as a postulate.

Key idea Michelson and Morley expected the ether wind to produce a fringe shift when their interferometer rotated; they observed no shift under any conditions; the null result supports the postulate that the speed of light is the same for all observers, independent of the motion of the source or the observer.

The two postulates and inertial frames foundations

A frame of reference is the perspective from which positions, times and motions are measured. An inertial frame of reference does not accelerate: it moves at constant velocity. Constant speed is not enough — a car rounding a bend at steady speed is changing direction, so its frame is non-inertial. Special relativity makes claims about inertial frames only.

Einstein built the theory on two postulates:

Postulate 1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames of reference. No experiment performed inside a smoothly moving laboratory can reveal whether the laboratory is “really” moving.

Postulate 2. The speed of light in a vacuum, c, has the same value for all observers in inertial frames, regardless of the motion of the light source or of the observer.

The second postulate is the break with classical physics. Classically, if a train moving at 0.50c turns on its headlights, a trackside observer should measure the light at 1.50c; the postulate says the driver and the trackside observer both measure exactly c. Two observers in relative motion can only agree on the speed of the same light beam if they disagree about distances and time intervals — absolute time and absolute length must be abandoned.

Trap “Inertial” means constant velocity, not constant speed. An electron circulating in the Australian Synchrotron storage ring at constant speed is continuously accelerating toward the centre, so its frame is non-inertial — the standard time-dilation and length-contraction formulas are written for inertial frames.

Proper time, proper length and the Lorentz factor t = γt₀ · L = L₀/γ

An event is something that happens at a particular point in space at a particular time — a muon is created, a clock ticks. Inertial observers in relative motion disagree about the time between two events and the length of an object; each comparison is anchored by a privileged measurement called the proper quantity.

Proper time t0 is the time interval between two events measured in the frame of reference in which both events occur at the same point in space. Both beats of a traveller’s heart happen at the traveller’s chest, so the traveller measures proper time; every other observer measures a longer, dilated time. Proper length L0 is the length of an object measured in the frame of reference in which the object is at rest. Observers moving relative to the object measure a shorter, contracted length — contracted only along the direction of relative motion.

Every relativity problem starts with two questions, asked before any formula: in which frame do the two events occur at the same point in space (that observer measures t0), and in which frame is the object — or the distance being traversed — at rest (that observer measures L0)? The amount of dilation and contraction is set by the Lorentz factor:

γ = 1√(1 − v²/c²) on formula sheet γ Lorentz factor (no unit; γ ≥ 1 always) · v relative speed of the two frames (m s⁻¹) · c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹
t = γt0 on formula sheet t dilated time interval (s), measured in a frame where the two events occur at different points · t0 proper time (s) — t ≥ t0 always
L = L0γ on formula sheet L contracted length (m), measured by an observer moving relative to the object · L0 proper length (m) — L ≤ L0 always

Since v < c for any massive object, γ is never less than 1: γ = 1 at rest, and at 0.10c it is still only 1.005, which is why classical mechanics serves at everyday speeds. At 0.50c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.25) = 1.15; at 0.90c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.81) = 2.29; at 0.99c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.9801) = 7.09. As v → c the denominator approaches zero and γ grows without bound, as shown in Figure 11.1.

Trap Both observers’ measurements are correct in their own frames — a contracted length and a dilated time are real measurements, not illusions. Proper time does not belong to “the stationary observer” by default, and proper time and proper length usually belong to different observers in the same problem: a muon’s creation and decay both happen at the muon, so t₀ is measured in the muon’s frame of reference, while the atmosphere it falls through is at rest relative to Earth, so L₀ is measured in the Earth frame. Locate the events and the object first; only then assign γ.
Key idea Identify the proper quantity before reaching for a formula: t₀ belongs to the frame where both events occur at the same point in space, and L₀ belongs to the frame where the object is at rest. Then t = γt₀ stretches time and L = L₀/γ shrinks length, by the same factor γ ≥ 1.

Muons, accelerators and GPS three test cases

Three real systems show these predictions operating; each is analysed with the two proper-quantity questions above.

Worked example — the muon, calculated both ways

Cosmic rays striking the upper atmosphere create muons about 5.0 km above Shanna’s sea-level detector, travelling downward at 0.995c. A muon at rest has an average lifetime of 2.2 μs. Shanna models each muon as travelling in a straight line at constant speed until it decays.

Classically, the muon travels 0.995 × 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ × 2.2 × 10⁻⁶ s = 6.6 × 10² m in one lifetime — about an eighth of the way down — so almost none should survive the journey. Yet sea-level detectors record muons in abundance. At 0.995c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.995²) = 10.0, and each frame resolves the puzzle in its own way.

In the Earth frame: the muon’s creation and decay both occur at the muon, which moves relative to Earth, so the 2.2 μs lifetime is proper time; Shanna measures the dilated lifetime t = γt0 = 10.0 × 2.2 μs = 22 μs. In 22 μs the muon travels 2.985 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ × 2.2 × 10⁻⁵ s = 6.6 km — more than the 5.0 km of atmosphere. The muon arrives.

In the muon’s frame: the muon is at rest, its 2.2 μs lifetime is the proper time, and the atmosphere moves upward past it at 0.995c. The atmosphere is at rest relative to Earth, so 5.0 km is a proper length of the Earth frame; the muon measures it contracted to L = L0/γ = 5.0 × 10³ ÷ 10.0 = 5.0 × 10² m, crossed in only 1.7 μs — less than one lifetime. The muon arrives.

The frames disagree about times and distances but agree on the observable fact: the muon arrives. One frame’s time dilation is the other frame’s length contraction, as shown in Figure 11.2.

Accelerator lengths

A linear accelerator 3.2 km long is at rest in the laboratory, so the laboratory measures its proper length. Electrons travelling along it at 0.998c (γ = 15.8) measure the beamline contracted to L = 3.2 × 10³ ÷ 15.8 = 2.0 × 10² m, and the machine’s accelerating structures must be designed to work for particles that experience that shorter length.

GPS time signals

A GPS receiver finds its position from the travel times of timing signals broadcast by satellites, so satellite clocks must agree with ground clocks to extraordinary precision. Each satellite orbits at about 3.9 × 10³ m s⁻¹, giving γ = 1 + 8.5 × 10⁻¹¹ — tiny, but over one day (86 400 s) the orbiting clock, ticking proper time aboard the satellite, falls behind ground clocks by about 7.3 μs. Light travels 2.2 km in 7.3 μs, so an uncorrected system would drift by kilometres each day; GPS clocks are deliberately corrected for their orbital speed.

Connects The satellites being corrected here are the circular orbits of topic 2 — the orbital radius fixes the speed v, and v fixes γ, so the relativistic correction is set by the same orbit mechanics that put the satellite there.

Mass–energy E₀ = mc²

Relativity’s final step rewrites energy. A stationary object is not energetically empty: its mass is itself a store of energy, the rest energy:

E0 = mc² on formula sheet E0 rest energy (J) · m mass (kg) · c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ — the energy an object has by virtue of its mass alone

When the object moves, its total energy is the rest energy plus kinetic energy:

Etotal = Ek + E0 = γmc² on formula sheet Etotal total energy (J) · Ek kinetic energy (J) · γ Lorentz factor at the object’s speed
Ek = (γ − 1)mc² on formula sheet Ek relativistic kinetic energy (J) — the difference between total energy and rest energy; valid at every speed

At low speeds (γ − 1)mc² agrees with ½mv², which is why the classical formula succeeds at everyday speeds. Near c the two expressions diverge. For an electron at 0.99c (γ = 7.09), the classical formula gives ½mv² = ½ × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × (2.97 × 10⁸)² = 4.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ J, but Ek = (7.09 − 1) × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 9.00 × 10¹⁶ = 5.0 × 10⁻¹³ J — more than twelve times larger. As v → c, γ → ∞: the kinetic energy required grows without limit, so no massive object can reach the speed of light.

Trap γmc² is the total energy, not the kinetic energy. Kinetic energy is the part above the rest energy: Ek = (γ − 1)mc². The classical ½mv² is not an acceptable approximation near c — at 0.99c it understates the kinetic energy by a factor of about twelve.

Worked example — the Sun’s mass budget

The Sun radiates 3.8 × 10²⁶ W from nuclear fusion in its core: hydrogen nuclei fuse into helium, and the products have slightly less mass than the reactants. Anika models the radiated energy as converted mass and calculates the mass converted each second.

Formula: each second the Sun radiates E = 3.8 × 10²⁶ J, so E0 = mc² rearranges to m = E0/c².

Substitution: m = 3.8 × 10²⁶ ÷ (3.00 × 10⁸)² = 3.8 × 10²⁶ ÷ 9.00 × 10¹⁶

Answer: m = 4.2 × 10⁹ kg every second — about four million tonnes of mass leaving as sunlight, every second, consuming well under 0.1% of the Sun’s mass over its lifetime.

Annihilation and creation

Mass-to-energy conversion is total in positron–electron annihilation — the positron is the electron’s antiparticle, with the same mass. When the two meet at negligible speed, both vanish and their entire rest energy emerges as two gamma-ray photons: 2mec² = 2 × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 9.00 × 10¹⁶ = 1.64 × 10⁻¹³ J in total, 8.2 × 10⁻¹⁴ J ≈ 0.51 MeV per photon. The photons leave in opposite directions because the total momentum before annihilation is approximately zero. PET medical scanners locate tumours by detecting these back-to-back pairs.

Particle accelerators run the conversion in reverse: collisions at large γ transform kinetic energy into the rest energy of new matter, routinely creating particles more massive than the ones that collided — nuclear transformations budgeted by E0 = mc² in both directions.

Key idea Mass and energy are interchangeable at the fixed rate E₀ = mc². The Sun converts mass to radiation, annihilation converts it totally to photon pairs, and accelerators convert kinetic energy back into the mass of new particles.
Connects The radius formula r = mv/qB used for the synchrotron in topic 5 carries the quiet caveat v << c. The Australian Synchrotron’s electrons break that caveat by design — their energy is thousands of times their rest energy — so the machine’s magnetic fields must be engineered for the relativistic energies on this page, not the classical formula.

Quick checks

Check 14.1 A space probe travels past Earth at 0.60c. Show that the Lorentz factor for the probe in Earth’s frame of reference is 1.25, and state the value of γ when the probe’s speed relative to the observer is zero.
γ = 1/√(1 − 0.60²) = 1/√0.64 = 1/0.80 = 1.25. At rest, v = 0 gives γ = 1 — the minimum possible value, where all relativistic formulas reduce to their classical forms.
Check 14.2 Kenji travels in a spacecraft at 0.90c relative to Earth and measures the time between two of his own heartbeats. Magnus, observing from Earth, measures the time between the same two heartbeats. State who measures the proper time and justify your answer.
Kenji measures the proper time: both events (the two heartbeats) occur at the same point in space in his frame — at his own chest. In Magnus’s frame the heartbeats occur at different points along the flight path, so Magnus measures the dilated time t = γt₀ = 2.29t₀.
Check 14.3 Protons travel along a 250 m beamline at a speed for which γ = 2.5. Identify the observer who measures the proper length of the beamline and calculate its length in the protons’ frame of reference.
The beamline is at rest in the laboratory, so a laboratory observer measures the proper length L₀ = 250 m. In the protons’ frame the beamline is contracted: L = L₀/γ = 250 ÷ 2.5 = 1.0 × 10² m.
Check 14.4 Muons created 5.0 km above the ground, travelling at 0.995c with a lifetime at rest of 2.2 μs, reach sea-level detectors in abundance. Explain why, using measurements made in the muon’s frame of reference. No calculations are required.
In the muon’s frame of reference the muon is at rest, its 2.2 μs lifetime is the proper time, and the atmosphere moves past it at 0.995c. The atmosphere is at rest relative to Earth, so its 5.0 km depth is contracted in the muon’s frame to L₀/γ — about 5.0 × 10² m — which passes by in less than one lifetime, so the ground reaches the muon before it decays.
Check 14.5 An electron and a positron, each of mass 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg and moving at negligible speed, annihilate. Calculate the total energy released and explain why the two photons produced travel in opposite directions.
Both rest energies are released: E = 2mc² = 2 × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × (3.00 × 10⁸)² = 1.64 × 10⁻¹³ J. The total momentum before annihilation is approximately zero, so momentum conservation requires the two photons to carry equal and opposite momenta — they leave in opposite directions.
