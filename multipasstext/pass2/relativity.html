17 · Relativity and mass–energy

As speeds approach the speed of light, the classical rules for time, length and energy give incorrect results. This topic states Einstein’s two postulates, quantifies their consequences through the Lorentz factor, and concludes with the equivalence of mass and energy: E0 = mc².

Where classical mechanics fails v → c

Classical mechanics assumes that time passes at the same rate for every observer, that lengths are identical for all observers, that velocities add directly, and that the kinetic energy ½mv² may grow without limit. At everyday speeds these assumptions are consistent with experiment. As speeds approach c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹, all four assumptions fail: an accelerator can supply energy to an electron indefinitely while the electron’s speed never reaches c.

Light travels at c, as established in topic 12, but classical physics could not specify relative to what. Classical velocity addition implies that an observer moving toward a light source should measure a value greater than c. Nineteenth-century physics proposed the ether, a hypothetical medium through which light propagates at c; Earth’s orbital motion through this medium should produce an “ether wind”, causing the measured speed of light to differ with direction.

In 1887 Michelson and Morley split one light beam along two perpendicular interferometer arms and recombined the reflections into an interference pattern. An ether wind would cause light travelling along the wind to take a different time from light travelling across it, so rotation of the apparatus should shift the fringes by a predicted, measurable amount. The observed shift was zero at every orientation, time of day and season: the null result.

The null result establishes that the measured speed of light is the same in all directions regardless of Earth’s motion, and provides no evidence for the ether. It supports the central claim of special relativity, that c is the same for all observers, which Einstein adopted in 1905 as a postulate.

Key idea Michelson and Morley predicted that an ether wind would produce a fringe shift when their interferometer rotated; they observed no shift under any conditions; the null result supports the postulate that the speed of light is the same for all observers, independent of the motion of the source or the observer.

The two postulates and inertial frames foundations

A frame of reference is the set of coordinates from which positions, times and motions are measured. An inertial frame of reference does not accelerate; it moves at constant velocity. Constant speed is not sufficient: a car rounding a bend at steady speed changes direction, so its frame is non-inertial. Special relativity applies to inertial frames only.

The theory rests on two postulates:

Postulate 1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames of reference. No experiment performed within an inertial frame can determine the frame's absolute motion.

Postulate 2. The speed of light in a vacuum, c, has the same value for all observers in inertial frames, regardless of the motion of the light source or of the observer.

The second postulate is the break with classical physics. Classically, if a train moving at 0.50c turns on its headlights, a trackside observer should measure the light at 1.50c; the postulate states that the driver and the trackside observer each measure exactly c. Two observers in relative motion can agree on the speed of the same light beam only if they disagree about distances and time intervals. Absolute time and absolute length are therefore abandoned.

Trap “Inertial” means constant velocity, not constant speed. An electron circulating in the Australian Synchrotron storage ring at constant speed accelerates continuously toward the centre, so its frame is non-inertial. The standard time-dilation and length-contraction formulas apply to inertial frames.

Proper time, proper length and the Lorentz factor t = γt0 · L = L0/γ

An event is something that happens at a particular point in space at a particular time, such as the creation of a muon or one tick of a clock. Inertial observers in relative motion disagree about the time interval between two events and about the length of an object. Each comparison is anchored by a privileged measurement called the proper quantity.

Proper time t0 is the time interval between two events measured in the frame of reference in which both events occur at the same point in space. Both beats of a traveller’s heart occur at the traveller’s chest, so the traveller measures proper time; every other observer measures a longer, dilated time. Proper length L0 is the length of an object measured in the frame of reference in which the object is at rest. Observers moving relative to the object measure a shorter, contracted length, contracted only along the direction of relative motion.

Every relativity problem begins with two questions, asked before any formula is applied: in which frame do the two events occur at the same point in space (that observer measures t0), and in which frame is the object, or the distance being traversed, at rest (that observer measures L0)? The amount of dilation and contraction is set by the Lorentz factor:

γ = 1√(1 − v²/c²) on formula sheet γ Lorentz factor (no unit; γ ≥ 1 always) · v relative speed of the two frames (m s⁻¹) · c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹
t = γt0 on formula sheet t dilated time interval (s), measured in a frame where the two events occur at different points · t0 proper time (s) — t ≥ t0 always
L = L0γ on formula sheet L contracted length (m), measured by an observer moving relative to the object · L0 proper length (m) — L ≤ L0 always

Since v < c for any massive object, γ is never less than 1: γ = 1 at rest, and at 0.10c it is still only 1.005, so classical mechanics is sufficient at everyday speeds. At 0.50c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.25) = 1.15; at 0.90c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.81) = 2.29; at 0.99c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.9801) = 7.09. As v → c the denominator approaches zero and γ increases without bound, as shown in Figure 11.1.

Trap Both observers’ measurements are correct in their own frames; a contracted length and a dilated time are real measurements, not illusions. Proper time does not belong to the stationary observer by default, and proper time and proper length usually belong to different observers in the same problem: a muon’s creation and decay both occur at the muon, so t0 is measured in the muon’s frame of reference, while the atmosphere through which it falls is at rest relative to Earth, so L0 is measured in the Earth frame. The events and the object must be located first; only then is γ assigned.
Key idea The proper quantity is identified before any formula is applied: t0 belongs to the frame in which both events occur at the same point in space, and L0 belongs to the frame in which the object is at rest. Then t = γt0 stretches time and L = L0/γ shrinks length, by the same factor γ ≥ 1.

Muons, accelerators and GPS three test cases

Three real systems demonstrate these predictions in operation; each is analysed using the two proper-quantity questions above.

Worked example — the muon, calculated both ways

Cosmic rays striking the upper atmosphere create muons approximately 5.0 km above a sea-level detector, moving downward at 0.995c. A muon at rest has an average lifetime of 2.2 μs. Each muon is modelled as travelling in a straight line at constant speed until it decays.

Classically, the muon travels 0.995 × 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ × 2.2 × 10⁻⁶ s = 6.6 × 10² m in one lifetime, approximately an eighth of the distance, so almost none should reach the detector. Detectors at sea level nevertheless record muons in abundance. At 0.995c, γ = 1/√(1 − 0.995²) = 10.0, and each frame resolves the result in its own way.

In the Earth frame: the muon’s creation and decay both occur at the muon, which moves relative to Earth, so the 2.2 μs lifetime is the proper time. The dilated lifetime is t = γt0 = 10.0 × 2.2 μs = 22 μs. In 22 μs the muon travels 2.985 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ × 2.2 × 10⁻⁵ s = 6.6 km, which exceeds the 5.0 km of atmosphere. The muon arrives.

In the muon’s frame: the muon is at rest, its 2.2 μs lifetime is the proper time, and the atmosphere moves upward past it at 0.995c. The atmosphere is at rest relative to Earth, so 5.0 km is a proper length in the Earth frame; in the muon frame it is contracted to L = L0/γ = 5.0 × 10³ ÷ 10.0 = 5.0 × 10² m, crossed in 1.7 μs, which is less than one lifetime. The muon arrives.

The frames disagree about times and distances but agree on the observable fact: the muon arrives. The time dilation of one frame corresponds to the length contraction of the other, as shown in Figure 11.2.

Accelerator lengths

A linear accelerator 3.2 km long is at rest in the laboratory, so the laboratory measures its proper length. Electrons travelling along it at 0.998c (γ = 15.8) measure the beamline contracted to L = 3.2 × 10³ ÷ 15.8 = 2.0 × 10² m, and the accelerating structures must be designed to operate for particles that experience this shorter length.

GPS time signals

A GPS receiver determines its position from the travel times of timing signals broadcast by satellites, so satellite clocks must agree with ground clocks to extremely high precision. Each satellite orbits at approximately 3.9 × 10³ m s⁻¹, giving γ = 1 + 8.5 × 10⁻¹¹. This is small, but over one day (86 400 s) the orbiting clock, which records proper time aboard the satellite, falls behind ground clocks by approximately 7.3 μs. Light travels 2.2 km in 7.3 μs, so an uncorrected system would drift by kilometres each day; GPS clocks are corrected for their orbital speed.

Connects The satellites corrected here are the circular orbits of topic 5: the orbital radius fixes the speed v, and v fixes γ, so the relativistic correction is set by the same orbital mechanics that placed the satellite in orbit.

Mass–energy E0 = mc²

The final result of relativity concerns energy. A stationary object is not energetically empty: its mass is itself a store of energy, the rest energy:

E0 = mc² on formula sheet E0 rest energy (J) · m mass (kg) · c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ — the energy an object has by virtue of its mass alone

When the object moves, its total energy is the sum of the rest energy and the kinetic energy:

Etotal = Ek + E0 = γmc² on formula sheet Etotal total energy (J) · Ek kinetic energy (J) · γ Lorentz factor at the object’s speed
Ek = (γ − 1)mc² on formula sheet Ek relativistic kinetic energy (J) — the difference between total energy and rest energy; valid at every speed

At low speeds (γ − 1)mc² agrees with ½mv², which is why the classical formula succeeds at everyday speeds. Near c the two expressions diverge. For an electron at 0.99c (γ = 7.09), the classical formula gives ½mv² = ½ × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × (2.97 × 10⁸)² = 4.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ J, while Ek = (7.09 − 1) × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 9.00 × 10¹⁶ = 5.0 × 10⁻¹³ J, which is more than twelve times larger. As v → c, γ → ∞: the kinetic energy required increases without limit, so no massive object can reach the speed of light.

Trap γmc² is the total energy, not the kinetic energy. The kinetic energy is the part above the rest energy: Ek = (γ − 1)mc². The classical ½mv² is not an acceptable approximation near c; at 0.99c it understates the kinetic energy by a factor of about twelve.

Worked example — the Sun’s mass budget

The Sun radiates 3.8 × 10²⁶ W from nuclear fusion in its core. Hydrogen nuclei fuse into helium, and the products have slightly less mass than the reactants. The radiated energy is modelled as converted mass, and the mass converted each second is calculated as follows.

Formula: each second the Sun radiates E = 3.8 × 10²⁶ J, so E0 = mc² rearranges to m = E0/c².

Substitution: m = 3.8 × 10²⁶ ÷ (3.00 × 10⁸)² = 3.8 × 10²⁶ ÷ 9.00 × 10¹⁶

Answer: m = 4.2 × 10⁹ kg each second, equivalent to about four million tonnes of mass radiated as sunlight every second. This consumes well under 0.1% of the Sun’s mass over its lifetime.

Annihilation and creation

Mass-to-energy conversion is total in positron–electron annihilation. The positron is the antiparticle of the electron and has the same mass. When the two meet at negligible speed, both are annihilated and their entire rest energy is emitted as two gamma-ray photons: 2mec² = 2 × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 9.00 × 10¹⁶ = 1.64 × 10⁻¹³ J in total, which is 8.2 × 10⁻¹⁴ J ≈ 0.51 MeV per photon. The photons travel in opposite directions because the total momentum before annihilation is approximately zero. PET medical scanners locate tumours by detecting these back-to-back pairs.

Particle accelerators run the conversion in reverse: collisions at large γ transform kinetic energy into the rest energy of new matter, creating particles more massive than the ones that collided. These are nuclear transformations governed by E0 = mc² in both directions.

Key idea Mass and energy are interchangeable at the fixed rate E0 = mc². The Sun converts mass to radiation, annihilation converts mass entirely to photon pairs, and accelerators convert kinetic energy into the mass of new particles.
Connects The radius formula r = mv/qB used for the synchrotron in topic 8 carries the condition v << c. The electrons of the Australian Synchrotron do not satisfy this condition by design, since their energy is thousands of times their rest energy, so the magnetic fields of the machine must be engineered for the relativistic energies on this page rather than the classical formula.

Quick checks

Check 14.1 A space probe travels past Earth at 0.60c. Show that the Lorentz factor for the probe in Earth’s frame of reference is 1.25, and state the value of γ when the probe’s speed relative to the observer is zero.
γ = 1/√(1 − 0.60²) = 1/√0.64 = 1/0.80 = 1.25. At rest, v = 0 gives γ = 1 — the minimum possible value, where all relativistic formulas reduce to their classical forms.
Check 14.2 Kenji travels in a spacecraft at 0.90c relative to Earth and measures the time between two of his own heartbeats. Magnus, observing from Earth, measures the time between the same two heartbeats. State who measures the proper time and justify your answer.
Kenji measures the proper time: both events (the two heartbeats) occur at the same point in space in his frame — at his own chest. In Magnus’s frame the heartbeats occur at different points along the flight path, so Magnus measures the dilated time t = γt0 = 2.29t0.
Check 14.3 Protons travel along a 250 m beamline at a speed for which γ = 2.5. Identify the observer who measures the proper length of the beamline and calculate its length in the protons’ frame of reference.
The beamline is at rest in the laboratory, so a laboratory observer measures the proper length L0 = 250 m. In the protons’ frame the beamline is contracted: L = L0/γ = 250 ÷ 2.5 = 1.0 × 10² m.
Check 14.4 Muons created 5.0 km above the ground, travelling at 0.995c with a lifetime at rest of 2.2 μs, reach sea-level detectors in abundance. Explain why, using measurements made in the muon’s frame of reference. No calculations are required.
In the muon’s frame of reference the muon is at rest, its 2.2 μs lifetime is the proper time, and the atmosphere moves past it at 0.995c. The atmosphere is at rest relative to Earth, so its 5.0 km depth is contracted in the muon’s frame to L0/γ — about 5.0 × 10² m — which passes by in less than one lifetime, so the ground reaches the muon before it decays.
Check 14.5 An electron and a positron, each of mass 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg and moving at negligible speed, annihilate. Calculate the total energy released and explain why the two photons produced travel in opposite directions.
Both rest energies are released: E = 2mc² = 2 × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × (3.00 × 10⁸)² = 1.64 × 10⁻¹³ J. The total momentum before annihilation is approximately zero, so momentum conservation requires the two photons to carry equal and opposite momenta — they leave in opposite directions.
