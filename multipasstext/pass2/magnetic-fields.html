7 · Magnetic fields

A magnetic field exerts a force on moving charge — but only on motion that crosses the field. This topic covers the force law for free charges and for currents, the circular paths that follow, and the notation for working in three dimensions.

Force on a moving charge F = qvB

A magnetic field exerts no force on a stationary charge. Only a moving charge experiences a force, and only the part of the motion that crosses the field counts. Two cases cover everything in this course: velocity perpendicular to the field, and velocity parallel to it.

F = qvB on formula sheet F force on the charge (N) · q charge magnitude (C) · v speed perpendicular to the field (m s⁻¹) · B magnetic field strength (T)

The field strength B is measured in tesla (T). The formula applies when the velocity is perpendicular to the field. When the charge moves parallel — or antiparallel — to the field, the force is exactly zero. A proton fired along a field line travels in a straight line at constant speed.

Trap No crossing, no force. A charge moving along the field direction experiences zero magnetic force, however fast it moves and however strong the field. The same applies to a wire whose current runs along the field (see Force on a current-carrying wire). Applying F = qvB without first checking the angle between v and B is a common error.

The direction of the force comes from the right-hand slap rule, as shown in FIG 4.1. Hold the right hand flat with the thumb at right angles to the fingers: thumb along the velocity of a positive charge, fingers along the field — the palm then faces in the direction of the force. The force is always perpendicular to both the velocity and the field.

For a negative charge, reverse the result. Either apply the slap rule and flip the force, or point the thumb against the electron's motion: a negative charge moving left is equivalent to a positive charge moving right. This reversal is part of every electron-beam problem.

Because v, B and F are mutually perpendicular, one of the three usually points along the line of sight in a diagram. A field into the page is drawn as a grid of × symbols and a field out of the page as • symbols; the convention is set out in Into and out of the page.

Worked example. Lucia studies a beam in which each proton travels at 4.0 × 10⁶ m s⁻¹ at right angles to a uniform 0.25 T magnetic field. The magnitude of the force exerted on one proton by the field is F = qvB = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 4.0 × 10⁶ × 0.25 = 1.6 × 10⁻¹³ N, directed perpendicular to the velocity as given by the slap rule.

Circular paths r = mv/qB

A force of constant magnitude that is always perpendicular to the velocity is exactly the condition for uniform circular motion, met in Unit 3 AOS 1. A charge moving across a uniform magnetic field therefore follows a circle: the magnetic force supplies the centripetal force. Setting qvB equal to mv²/r and rearranging gives the radius.

qvB = mv²r   ⇒   r = mvqB r = mv/qB on formula sheet r radius of the circular path (m) · m mass (kg) · v speed (m s⁻¹) · q charge magnitude (C) · B field strength (T)

Four consequences follow directly. A faster particle sweeps a wider circle (r ∝ v), and a stronger field bends a tighter circle (r ∝ 1/B). A more massive particle, with more inertia, turns on a wider circle (r ∝ m). Reversing the sign of the charge reverses the force, so electrons and protons that enter the same field with the same velocity circulate in opposite senses.

The magnetic force does no work on the charge. Work requires a force component along the motion, and the magnetic force never has one: it is perpendicular to the velocity at every instant. The speed is constant and the kinetic energy is constant; only the direction of motion changes, as shown in FIG 4.2. A magnetic field can steer a charged particle but cannot change its speed — any change of speed must come from an electric field.

Key idea A uniform magnetic field changes the direction of a moving charge, never its speed. Speed, kinetic energy and radius all remain constant; the velocity only turns.

Worked example. Anika fires an electron at 2.0 × 10⁷ m s⁻¹ into a uniform 1.5 mT field, perpendicular to the field. Ignore relativistic effects. Convert the field first: 1.5 mT = 1.5 × 10⁻³ T. Then r = mv/qB = (9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 2.0 × 10⁷) ÷ (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 1.5 × 10⁻³) = 7.6 × 10⁻² m; the electron circles with a radius of 7.6 cm at a speed that never changes.

Trap Millitesla. Field strengths are routinely quoted in mT: convert before substituting, as in 2.5 mT = 2.5 × 10⁻³ T. Substituting the raw number makes the force 1000 times too large and the radius 1000 times too small, and the resulting wrong answer looks plausible.

Force on a current-carrying wire F = nIlB

A current is charge in motion, so a wire carrying current across a magnetic field experiences a force: the field acts on every drifting charge, and the charges, unable to leave the metal, transfer the force to the wire. For a straight conductor at right angles to a uniform field:

F = nIlB on formula sheet F force on the conductor (N) · n number of conductors (turns of a coil) · I current (A) · l length of conductor in the field (m) · B field strength (T)

n counts how many conductors sit side by side in the field: for a coil of 20 turns, n = 20 and the force is 20 times the force on a single wire. Only the length actually inside the field contributes to l (note the lowercase l, as on the formula sheet).

The slap rule gives the direction, with the thumb now pointing along the conventional current: thumb along I, fingers along B, palm facing along F, as shown in FIG 4.3. The zero-force case returns here in full: if the current runs parallel or antiparallel to the field, the force is zero. In a rectangular coil lying in a field, the two sides whose current crosses the field experience forces; the two sides whose current runs along the field experience none. That single fact is the key to the DC motor in the next topic.

Worked example. Magnus tests a flat coil of 20 turns. One side of the coil is 0.050 m long, carries a current of 1.5 A and lies at right angles to a uniform 0.30 T field. The magnitude of the force exerted on that side by the field is F = nIlB = 20 × 1.5 × 0.050 × 0.30 = 0.45 N. The opposite side carries the same current the opposite way, so it experiences an equal force in the opposite direction — a pair of forces that can rotate the coil.

Connects This section is the motor principle: a magnetic field makes a current-carrying coil move. Unit 3 AOS 3 runs it in reverse — moving a coil through a field drives a current — and that reversal, electromagnetic induction via changing magnetic flux, is how every generator in the grid works.

Into and out of the page × and • notation

Magnetic-force problems are three-dimensional: v (or I), B and F are mutually perpendicular, so all three cannot lie on the page at once. The standard convention places one of them along the line of sight and pictures it as an arrow: seen from behind, the crossed fletching of the tail — ×, into the page; seen approaching, the point — •, out of the page. Every diagram that uses the symbols should carry a key, for example "× = field into the page".

A reliable routine for any direction question: first, read the field direction off the symbols. Second, point the flat right hand's thumb along the velocity of positive charge — reversed for an electron — or along the conventional current. Third, align the fingers with the field; the palm now faces along the force.

For example, an electron travels up the page through a field that points out of the page (•). For a positive charge moving up the page, the slap rule gives a force to the right; the electron's negative charge reverses this, so the force exerted on the electron by the field is to the left of the page.

Quick checks

Check 4.1 A proton travels at 5.0 × 10⁵ m s⁻¹ at right angles to a uniform 2.0 mT magnetic field. Calculate the magnitude of the force exerted on the proton by the field.
Convert first: 2.0 mT = 2.0 × 10⁻³ T. Then F = qvB = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 5.0 × 10⁵ × 2.0 × 10⁻³ = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁶ N. Substituting 2.0 instead of 2.0 × 10⁻³ gives an answer 1000 times too large.
Check 4.2 A second proton travels at the same speed parallel to the same field. State the magnitude of the magnetic force exerted on this proton by the field.
Zero. F = qvB applies only to the component of velocity perpendicular to the field; a proton moving parallel to B has no such component, so it experiences no force at any speed.
Check 4.3 An electron moves to the right across the page through a magnetic field directed into the page. Determine the direction of the force exerted on the electron by the field.
Toward the bottom of the page. For a positive charge the slap rule (thumb right, fingers into the page) gives a force up the page; the electron's negative charge reverses this to down the page.
Check 4.4 An electron follows a circular path of radius r in a uniform magnetic field. The field strength is then doubled. State what happens to the radius of the path and to the electron's speed.
The radius halves and the speed is unchanged. r = mv/qB, so r is inversely proportional to B; the magnetic force remains perpendicular to the velocity, does no work, and therefore cannot change the speed.
Check 4.5 A rectangular loop WXYZ carries a current in a uniform magnetic field that lies in the plane of the loop, parallel to sides WX and YZ. Identify the sides of the loop that experience a magnetic force.
Only XY and ZW. The current in WX and YZ runs parallel or antiparallel to the field, so the force on those sides is zero; in XY and ZW the current crosses the field at right angles, giving a force F = nIlB on each, in opposite directions.
