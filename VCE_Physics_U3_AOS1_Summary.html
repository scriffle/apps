Constants & Conventions

g9.8 m s−2 (near Earth)
G6.67 × 10−11 N m2 kg−2

Take a direction as positive and keep it consistent. Vectors: resolve into ⊥ components. "Net" = vector sum of all forces. Mass scalar (kg); weight is a force (N).

Newton's Three Laws

1st (inertia): an object stays at rest or constant velocity unless acted on by a non-zero net force. Constant velocity ⇔ Fnet = 0.

2nd: net force = mass × acceleration. Acceleration is in the direction of Fnet.

Newton's 2nd law (vector) Fnet = ma F (N), m (kg), a (m s−2)

3rd: for every force there is an equal, opposite reaction force. The pair acts on two different bodies (e.g. foot pushes ground back, ground pushes foot forward).

Action–reaction pairs NEVER act on the same object, so they never cancel each other in one body's free-body diagram.

Solving force problems: (1) draw a free-body diagram of EACH object. (2) choose +/− axes (along motion). (3) resolve forces into components. (4) apply Fnet=ma per axis. (5) solve simultaneously for connected bodies.

Inclined Plane

For a slope at angle θ, resolve weight into components parallel and perpendicular to the surface:

Weight components on incline F∥ = mg sinθ    F⊥ = mg cosθ

Frictionless slope: a = g sinθ down the slope. Normal force N = mg cosθ (< mg).

2.0 kg block, 30° frictionless ramp. a = g sinθ = 9.8 × sin30° = 4.9 m s−2 down the slope; N = mg cosθ = 2.0 × 9.8 × cos30° = 17 N.

Connected Bodies

Treat the whole system to find acceleration (one Fnet=ma using total mass), then isolate one body to find the internal tension.

3.0 kg and 2.0 kg masses over a frictionless pulley (Atwood). a = (m1−m2)g / (m1+m2) = (1.0)(9.8)/5.0 = 2.0 m s−2. Tension: T = m2(g+a) = 2.0(11.8) = 24 N.

Projectile Motion

Horizontal and vertical motions are INDEPENDENT and share only time t. Horizontal: a = 0, vx constant. Vertical: a = g = 9.8 m s−2 down.
Horizontal (x)Vertical (y)
Accel0g down
Velocityvx = u cosθ (const)vy = u sinθ − gt
Displ.x = vxty = uyt − ½gt2

At maximum height vy = 0 (vx unchanged). Path is symmetric (no air resistance): speed on landing = launch speed.

Projectile method: split u into ux=u cosθ, uy=u sinθ. Use vertical SUVAT to get time (e.g. to max height vy=0, or to ground y=0). Substitute t into horizontal x=vxt for range. Final speed: v=√(vx²+vy²).
Ball launched 20 m s−1 at 30°. ux=17.3, uy=10.0. Time up: t=uy/g=1.02 s → flight 2.04 s. Max height = uy²/2g = 5.1 m. Range = ux×2.04 = 35 m.
Air resistance (qualitative): acts opposite to velocity → reduces range & max height, makes the descent steeper than the ascent (asymmetric path), and lowers landing speed below launch speed.

Uniform Circular Motion

Constant speed, continuously changing direction → velocity changes → there IS acceleration, directed toward the centre (centripetal). The net force points to the centre.

Centripetal force & acceleration Fnet = mv2/r    a = v2/r
v = 2πr/T    a = 4π2r/T2 r radius (m), T period (s)
Centripetal force is NOT a new force — it is the NET of real forces (gravity, normal, tension, friction) resolved toward the centre. There is no outward "centrifugal" force.
SituationProvides Fc

Object on string (horizontal)Horizontal component of tension
Banked track (design speed)Horizontal component of normal force
Satellite / MoonGravity

Banked Tracks

On a track banked at θ, the horizontal component of N supplies Fc. At the design speed no friction is needed:

Design speed (no friction) tanθ = v2/(rg)
Track banked 20°, r = 50 m. Design speed v = √(rg tanθ) = √(50 × 9.8 × tan20°) = √178 = 13 m s−1.

Vertical Circles

Apply Fnet = mv²/r toward the centre at the top and bottom only.

Top & bottom of loop Top: N + mg = mv2/r
Bottom: N − mg = mv2/r
Minimum speed at the top (N = 0, gravity alone supplies Fc): vmin = √(gr). Below this the object leaves the track.

Satellites as Circular Motion

Gravity is the only force on a satellite → it is the centripetal force. (Full field treatment: AOS2.)

Orbital motion a = v2/r = 4π2r/T2

An orbiting astronaut feels "weightless": both they and the craft accelerate toward Earth at the same rate (g at that altitude), so N = 0.

Momentum

Momentum is mass × velocity — a vector in the direction of motion.

Momentum p = mv p (kg m s−1), m (kg), v (m s−1)

Impulse

Impulse = change in momentum = force × time it acts. Equal to the area under a force–time graph.

Impulse–momentum theorem Impulse = FΔt = Δp = mΔv = m(v − u)
Same Δp can be delivered by a large force over a short time OR a small force over a long time. Crumple zones, airbags and bending knees extend Δt → reduce the peak force (and injury).
A 0.15 kg ball hits a wall at 12 m s−1 and rebounds at 8.0 m s−1. Taking "away from wall" as +: Δp = m(v−u) = 0.15(8.0 − (−12)) = 3.0 kg m s−1. If contact = 0.020 s, F = Δp/Δt = 150 N.

Conservation of Momentum

In an isolated system (no external net force) total momentum is conserved: Σpbefore = Σpafter. Apply per direction.
1-D collisions / explosions m1u1 + m2u2 = m1v1 + m2v2
Collision method: assign +/− to directions. Write Σp before = Σp after. Solve for the unknown velocity. For "stick together" (perfectly inelastic) use a single combined mass (m1+m2)v on the right.
A 1000 kg car at 20 m s−1 hits a stationary 1500 kg car; they lock together. v = (1000×20)/(2500) = 8.0 m s−1.

Elastic vs Inelastic

MomentumKinetic energy
ElasticConservedConserved
InelasticConservedNOT conserved
Perfectly inelasticConservedMax KE lost (stick)
Momentum is conserved in EVERY collision in an isolated system. Kinetic energy is only conserved in elastic collisions. "Lost" KE becomes heat, sound and deformation.
To test if a collision is elastic: calculate total ½mv² before and after. Equal → elastic. Less after → inelastic (the difference is the energy dissipated).

Work Done by a Force

Work W = Fs (force ∥ displacement)
W = area under a force–distance graph W (J), F (N), s (m)

Work transfers energy. Force ⊥ to motion (e.g. centripetal, normal) does zero work. Use the area method for a varying force.

Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy (low speed) Ek = ½mv2

Net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy (work–energy theorem).

A 1200 kg car braking from 25 to 15 m s−1: ΔEk = ½(1200)(15²−25²) = ½(1200)(−400) = −2.4 × 105 J dissipated by the brakes.

Elastic Potential Energy (springs)

An ideal spring obeys Hooke's Law — restoring force proportional to extension/compression x.

Hooke's Law & spring energy F = −kx    Es = ½kx2 k spring constant (N m−1), x extension (m)
Es = area under the force–extension graph = ½kx² (a triangle of base x, height kx). The − sign in F = −kx shows the force opposes the displacement.

Gravitational PE (near Earth)

GPE change Eg = mgΔh valid where g ≈ constant; also = area under force–distance graph

Energy Conservation

In an isolated system total energy is conserved. Mechanical energy (Ek + Eg + Es) is constant when no energy is dissipated. Otherwise: Estart = Eend + Edissipated (heat + sound + deformation).
Energy method: choose a reference height. Write total energy at A = total energy at B (+ losses). Cancel mass where it appears throughout (e.g. drops, ramps). Solve for the unknown.
A 0.50 kg ball dropped 1.8 m (no air resistance): ½mv² = mgh → v = √(2gh) = √(2×9.8×1.8) = 5.9 m s−1. (Mass cancels.)
Spring launcher: a 0.20 kg ball is pushed onto a spring (k = 800 N m−1) compressed 5.0 cm. ½kx² = ½mv² → v = x√(k/m) = 0.05√(800/0.20) = 3.2 m s−1.

Common Exam Traps

  • Centripetal force is the net of real forces, not an extra force; nothing points outward.
  • At a projectile's peak vy = 0 but vx (and total v) is not zero.
  • Momentum is a vector — assign signs to directions before adding.
  • KE is a scalar and is only conserved in elastic collisions; momentum is conserved in all.
  • Normal force ≠ weight on an incline or in a lift accelerating vertically.
