Aim, Question & Hypothesis

TermWhat it is
AimWhat the investigation sets out to find/test
QuestionThe specific, answerable research question
HypothesisA testable, falsifiable prediction linking the variables
A good hypothesis is testable and predicts a relationship, e.g. "If the pendulum length increases, then the period will increase." It names the independent and dependent variables and predicts the direction of the effect.
Weak: "Springs are stretchy." Strong: "As the load on the spring increases (IV), the extension (DV) will increase in direct proportion, provided the elastic limit is not exceeded." — testable, directional, names both variables.

Variables

TypeRole
Independent (IV)The one you deliberately change. SAC requires ONE continuous IV.
Dependent (DV)The one you measure in response
ControlledKept constant so they don't affect the DV
Continuous variables can take any value in a range (length, time, voltage) — these allow a line graph and a gradient. Controlling variables is what makes the test fair (valid).

Methodology & Method

Methodology = the overall type of investigation. Method = the specific step-by-step procedure, equipment and data to be generated. Repeat trials to improve reliability.

MethodologyExample
Controlled experimentVary one IV, measure DV
ModellingPhysical or mathematical model
SimulationComputer model of a system
FieldworkData gathered in a real setting
Design checklist: state aim & hypothesis → identify IV (continuous), DV, controlled variables → choose equipment & range of IV values → plan repeat measurements → identify risks (safety) and ethical issues → record everything in a logbook.

Accuracy vs Precision

TermMeaning
AccuracyHow close a measurement is to the TRUE value
PrecisionHow close repeated measurements are to EACH OTHER (spread)
ResolutionThe smallest change an instrument can detect
RepeatabilityAgreement when the SAME person/equipment repeats it
ReproducibilityAgreement under CHANGED conditions (different person/method/equipment)
Accurate but not precise = centred on the true value but scattered. Precise but not accurate = tightly grouped but off-target (a sign of systematic error). The two are independent.

Error vs Uncertainty

Error = the difference between a measurement and the true value. Uncertainty = the range within which the true value is expected to lie (e.g. 20 ± 1 mm means 19–21 mm).

Error typeFeature / fix
RandomScatter both ways; reduce by averaging more repeats → improves precision
SystematicConsistent shift one way (e.g. zero offset, miscalibration); reduce by calibrating → improves accuracy
Averaging repeats reduces RANDOM error but does NOTHING to systematic error. A systematic error shifts every reading the same way and only calibration/technique fixes it. An outlier is a value far from the rest.

Validity & Safety

An investigation is valid if it actually tests the hypothesis: only the IV is changed, all other variables are controlled, and the method measures what it claims to. Identify and manage safety risks (e.g. electrical, projectiles) and ethical guidelines before starting.

Graphing Conventions

  • Independent variable on the x-axis, dependent on the y-axis.
  • Label both axes with quantity and unit; use a sensible, even scale.
  • Plot points, then a line (or curve) of best fit — do not just join the dots.
  • Show uncertainty bars where a numerical uncertainty is given.
The line of best fit balances points above and below; it averages out random scatter. Points far off the trend are outliers and should be identified (and may be excluded with justification).

Linearisation & Gradient

Plotting data so it forms a straight line (y = mx + c) makes the relationship easy to test and the gradient physically meaningful.

Straight-line form y = mx + c m = gradient, c = y-intercept
For a pendulum T = 2π√(L/g): plotting T against √L gives a straight line through the origin with gradient 2π/√g. The gradient therefore yields g — its physical significance.
Always state what the gradient REPRESENTS physically (it usually equals a constant or combination of constants), not just its number. A non-zero intercept can reveal a systematic error.

Nature of Evidence

Evidence either supports or refutes a hypothesis, model or theory. A single result does not "prove" a theory; repeated, reproducible data that survives testing builds confidence. Note assumptions and limitations of the data, methodology and method.

Evaluating a conclusion: does the data answer the question? Is the trend consistent with the hypothesis? How big is the scatter (precision)? Any systematic offset (accuracy)? What are the limitations and how could precision be improved / uncertainty reduced?

Significant Figures & Units

  • Quote a result to a number of significant figures that reflects the precision of the measurements.
  • Use correct SI units and standard abbreviations throughout.
  • Keep representations (symbols, equations, vector diagrams) conventional.
Measuring g = 9.81 vs accepted 9.80 m s−2: percentage difference = |9.81−9.80|/9.80 × 100 = 0.10% — close to the true value, so accurate. A small spread across repeats would also make it precise.

The Logbook

The logbook records all planning, raw data, risk management and preliminary analysis. It authenticates that the data is the student's own primary data and is assessed alongside the poster.

Scientific Poster (≤ 600 words)

The investigation is communicated as a structured scientific poster. Standard sections:

SectionContents
Title & questionThe research question
IntroductionBackground physics, aim, hypothesis
Methodology & methodHow the data was generated
ResultsTables, graphs (with uncertainty)
DiscussionAnalysis, trends, limitations, improvements
ConclusionAnswer to the question, with reference to the hypothesis
ReferencesAcknowledged sources
The 600-word limit excludes tables, graphs, captions, references and acknowledgements. A central visual (graph) usually carries the key finding. Communicate succinctly and acknowledge all sources.
