Kinetic Energy: KE = ½mv² ▼

What you need to know:

Energy of motion. Depends on mass and velocity squared. Double speed = quadruple energy.

Why it matters:

Why crashes get worse with speed. Why it's harder to stop from high speed. The v² is crucial.

▼ Why?

Because work done accelerating equals ∫F·ds = ∫ma·vdt = ∫mv·dv = ½mv². The integral creates the square.

▼ But really why?

Energy is frame-independent (sort of). The ½ makes the energy transform properly between reference frames.

▼ But really actually why?

It's what's conserved when forces are conservative. Nature picks this specific combination of m and v to conserve.

▼ So, actually.

Speed kills quadratically.

Model for all oscillations. Approximates any system near equilibrium. Leads to simple harmonic motion.

Near equilibrium, all forces are approximately linear. Taylor expansion: F ≈ F₀ + (dF/dx)x + ... First term vanishes at equilibrium.

▼ But really why?

Atoms in solids act like springs. Stretch bonds = restoring force. Hooke's law emerges from quantum mechanics.

▼ But really actually why?

Nature likes quadratic potentials near equilibrium. U = ½kx² gives F = -dU/dx = -kx. Simplest stable potential.

▼ So, actually.

Springs = linear approximation to everything.

Force is momentum flow rate. Total momentum transferred = rate × time. Like flow × time = volume.

Equilibrium
Torque: τ = r⊥F ▼

What you need to know:

Torque = perpendicular distance × force. Measures turning effect. Longer lever arm = more torque.

Why it matters:

Why door handles are far from hinges. Why wrenches have long handles. Leverage multiplies force.

▼ Why?

Torque is to rotation what force is to translation. It's the twisting influence that causes angular acceleration.

▼ But really why?

Angular momentum L = r × p. Torque = dL/dt = r × F. Cross product captures the perpendicular nature.

▼ But really actually why?

Rotational symmetry leads to angular momentum conservation. Torque breaks this symmetry, causing angular momentum change.

▼ So, actually.

Torque = force with leverage.

Static Equilibrium ▼

What you need to know:

For no motion: ΣF = 0 (no translation) AND Στ = 0 (no rotation). Both conditions needed.

Why it matters:

How bridges stand. Why buildings don't topple. Engineering is applied equilibrium.

▼ Why?

Objects can translate and rotate independently. Need to prevent both motions for true stillness.

▼ But really why?

6 degrees of freedom in 3D: 3 translational, 3 rotational. Equilibrium constrains all six.

▼ But really actually why?

Stability requires all derivatives of energy to vanish. First derivative = 0 gives force balance. Higher derivatives give stability conditions.

▼ So, actually.

Balance everything or fall.

Concepts Used to Model Motion
Vectors vs Scalars ▼

What you need to know:

Scalars have magnitude only (speed, distance, time). Vectors have magnitude AND direction (velocity, displacement, acceleration, force).

Why it matters:

Direction changes everything. Moving 5m east then 5m west gives you 10m distance (scalar) but 0m displacement (vector). Speed tells you how fast; velocity tells you how fast AND where.

▼ Why?

Because physics happens in 3D space. Things don't just have amounts—they point somewhere. Force without direction is meaningless.

▼ But really why?

Space has no preferred direction. Up isn't special. Every direction is equivalent, so we need to specify which way we mean.

▼ But really actually why?

Because change requires comparison. Motion is change in position relative to something else. Relativity demands we specify direction.

▼ So, actually.

No absolute positions = must specify direction.

Short Answer 1: A boat travels 12 m at 30° north of east, then 8.0 m due south. Calculate the magnitude of the boat's displacement.
▼ Show Solution 
Step 1: Break into components
First displacement: 12 m at 30° north of east
x₁ = 12 cos(30°) = 12 × 0.866 = 10.4 m east
y₁ = 12 sin(30°) = 12 × 0.5 = 6.0 m north

Second displacement: 8.0 m south
x₂ = 0 m
y₂ = -8.0 m (negative because south)

Step 2: Find total displacement components
x_total = x₁ + x₂ = 10.4 + 0 = 10.4 m east
y_total = y₁ + y₂ = 6.0 + (-8.0) = -2.0 m (south)

Step 3: Calculate magnitude
|displacement| = √(x_total² + y_total²)
|displacement| = √(10.4² + (-2.0)²)
|displacement| = √(108.16 + 4.00)
|displacement| = √112.16
|displacement| = 10.6 m

Answer: 11 m (2 sig figs)

Motion Equations: v = u + at ▼

What you need to know:

For constant acceleration: velocity changes linearly with time. Start with initial velocity u, add acceleration × time.

Why it matters:

This is THE fundamental equation. Everything else in kinematics comes from this. It says acceleration is the rate of velocity change.

▼ Why?

Because acceleration means "velocity change per second." After t seconds, you've added a×t worth of velocity to what you started with.

▼ But really why?

This assumes acceleration is constant. Real acceleration varies, but we approximate with constant chunks. Calculus handles the varying case.

▼ But really actually why?

Because force causes acceleration (F=ma), and we're assuming constant force. Constant force → constant acceleration → linear velocity change.

▼ So, actually.

Constant push = steady speed increase.

Short Answer 2: A rocket accelerates upward from rest at 15 m/s² for 8.0 s. What is its maximum height if it then coasts upward until stopping?
▼ Show Solution 
Phase 1: Powered ascent (a = +15 m/s²)
u₁ = 0, a₁ = 15 m/s², t₁ = 8.0 s

Final velocity: v₁ = u₁ + a₁t₁ = 0 + 15(8.0) = 120 m/s
Height gained: s₁ = u₁t₁ + ½a₁t₁² = 0 + ½(15)(8.0)² = 480 m

u₂ = 120 m/s, v₂ = 0, a₂ = -9.8 m/s²

0² = 120² + 2(-9.8)s₂
0 = 14400 - 19.6s₂
s₂ = 14400/19.6 = 735 m

Total height = s₁ + s₂ = 480 + 735 = 1215 m

Short Answer 3: Two cars start 100 m apart. Car A (behind) starts from rest with acceleration 3.0 m/s². Car B (ahead) travels at constant 15 m/s. When does A catch B?
▼ Show Solution 
Car A: s_A = u_At + ½a_At² = 0 + ½(3.0)t² = 1.5t²
Car B: s_B = 100 + u_Bt = 100 + 15t

When A catches B: s_A = s_B
1.5t² = 100 + 15t
1.5t² - 15t - 100 = 0

Using quadratic formula:
t = [15 ± √(225 + 600)]/3
t = [15 ± √825]/3
t = [15 ± 28.7]/3

t = 43.7/3 = 14.6 s (positive solution)

Check: s_A = 1.5(14.6)² = 320 m
   s_B = 100 + 15(14.6) = 319 m ✓

Motion Equations: s = ut + ½at² ▼

What you need to know:

Displacement has two parts: distance from initial velocity (ut) plus extra distance from acceleration (½at²).

Why it matters:

Shows why things fall in parabolas. The t² term creates the curve. Without acceleration, motion is straight (just ut).

▼ Why?

Because velocity increases linearly, but distance is the accumulation of all those velocities. Integration of (u + at) gives ut + ½at².

▼ But really why?

The ½ appears because average velocity during acceleration is halfway between start and end velocities. It's the area of a triangle.

▼ But really actually why?

Because position is the integral of velocity, and velocity is the integral of acceleration. Each integration adds a power of t.

▼ So, actually.

Distance = old speed contribution + acceleration's growing contribution.

Motion Equations: v² = u² + 2as ▼

What you need to know:

Links velocity change directly to distance, eliminating time. Useful when you don't know or care about time.

Why it matters:

Energy in disguise! Multiply by ½m and you get kinetic energy change equals work done (Fs).

▼ Why?

Derived by eliminating time from v=u+at and s=ut+½at². The algebra gives this time-independent relationship.

▼ But really why?

Because energy is conserved. Work done by force (Fs = mas) must equal kinetic energy change (½mv² - ½mu²).

▼ But really actually why?

Space and time interweave. This equation shows their deep connection—velocity change over distance is fundamental, time is secondary.

▼ So, actually.

Speed² change = acceleration × distance. Energy hiding in plain sight.

Forces and Motion
Newton's First Law (Inertia) ▼

What you need to know:

Objects keep doing what they're doing unless a net force acts. No force = no acceleration.

Why it matters:

Destroys the intuition that motion needs force. Motion is natural; change needs explanation.

▼ Why?

Because velocity is relative. There's no universal "stopped." Constant velocity is indistinguishable from rest.

▼ But really why?

Space has no friction. Nothing in empty space opposes motion. Earth experience misleads—friction isn't fundamental.

▼ But really actually why?

Symmetry. Laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames. No frame is special, so constant velocity can't be special.

▼ So, actually.

Motion is default. Force changes motion.

Binary Question 1: True or False: An object moving at constant velocity has forces acting on it.
▼ Model Answer

True. Forces CAN act on an object moving at constant velocity, but the NET force must be zero. For example, a car moving at constant velocity on a highway has driving force forward balanced by air resistance and friction backward. The forces are present but balanced.

Binary Question 2: True or False: In the absence of friction, a rolling ball will eventually stop.
▼ Model Answer

False. Newton's First Law states that objects in motion stay in motion unless acted upon by a net external force. Without friction (or air resistance), there is no force to slow the ball down, so it will continue rolling indefinitely at constant velocity.

Binary Question 3: True or False: Inertia is a force that keeps objects moving.
▼ Model Answer

False. Inertia is NOT a force. It is a property of matter that describes an object's resistance to changes in motion. Objects don't need a force to keep moving - they need a force to CHANGE their motion. This is a common misconception.
