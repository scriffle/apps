Step 10: Critical Proofreading

Understand LLM limitations and verify all generated content

⚠️ CRITICAL WARNING: LLM-generated content requires careful human review.

While LLMs are powerful tools, they have significant limitations that can affect assessment quality. You MUST carefully proofread and verify all generated content before using it with students.

Why Proofreading is Essential

  • Factual Errors: LLMs can confidently generate incorrect information, false facts, or outdated data
  • Ambiguous Questions: Questions may have multiple valid interpretations or unclear wording
  • Invalid Distractors: Distractors may not reflect genuine misconceptions or may be technically correct
  • Bias and Sensitivity: Content may contain cultural bias, insensitive examples, or inappropriate contexts
  • Difficulty Mismatch: Questions may be too easy, too hard, or not match the intended cognitive level
  • Curriculum Misalignment: Content may not align with specific curriculum standards or teaching emphasis
  • Answer Key Errors: Explanations may be incorrect even when the answer is right (or vice versa)

What to Verify

Factual Accuracy

  • Check every fact, figure, date, formula, and definition against authoritative sources
  • Verify scientific principles, mathematical formulas, and technical terminology
  • Confirm historical events, dates, and contexts are accurate
  • Cross-reference with textbooks, curriculum documents, and subject experts

Question Quality

  • Ensure each question has ONE clearly correct answer
  • Check for ambiguity - can the question be interpreted multiple ways?
  • Verify all options are grammatically parallel and similar in length
  • Confirm the cognitive level matches what you requested
  • Test each question yourself - is it fair and answerable?

Distractor Validity

  • Verify distractors are plausible but definitely incorrect
  • Check that distractors reflect real student misconceptions, not arbitrary wrong answers
  • Ensure no distractor could be argued as correct under any reasonable interpretation
  • Confirm distractors are at appropriate difficulty (not obviously wrong)

Cultural Sensitivity and Bias

  • Check for stereotypes, cultural assumptions, or insensitive content
  • Ensure examples are inclusive and accessible to all students
  • Verify names, contexts, and scenarios represent diverse backgrounds
  • Remove content that may disadvantage or alienate certain student groups

Answer Key Verification

  • Work through each question independently before checking the answer key
  • Verify explanations are accurate and help students understand
  • Check that misconception descriptions for distractors are valid
  • Ensure learning hints are pedagogically sound

Proofreading Process

  1. Initial Read: Read all questions without looking at answers. Note any confusion or ambiguity.
  2. Answer Independently: Try to answer each question yourself based on curriculum knowledge.
  3. Check Against Key: Compare your answers to the generated answer key. Investigate any discrepancies.
  4. Verify Facts: Look up any facts, figures, or principles you're unsure about.
  5. Test Distractors: For each distractor, ask: "Why would a student choose this?" Does it reflect a real misconception?
  6. Colleague Review: Have another teacher review questions, especially in unfamiliar topics.
  7. Student Pilot: If possible, test questions with a small group before full deployment.

Common LLM Errors to Watch For

  • "Hallucinated" facts: Plausible-sounding but entirely invented information
  • Outdated information: Facts that were true but have changed
  • Subtle conceptual errors: Misunderstandings of complex topics that sound convincing
  • Overgeneralization: Statements that are mostly true but have important exceptions
  • Mixing up similar concepts: Confusing related but distinct ideas
  • Formula errors: Mathematical or scientific formulas with subtle mistakes
  • Citation fabrication: References to studies or sources that don't exist

When to Reject or Heavily Revise Questions

  • Any factual error that can't be easily corrected
  • Questions where multiple answers could be defended as correct
  • Distractors that are actually correct or equally valid
  • Content that is culturally insensitive or potentially harmful
  • Questions that don't align with your curriculum or teaching emphasis
  • Overly complex or confusing wording that obscures the learning objective

Documentation

As you proofread, document your changes:

  • Note which questions were modified and why
  • Keep track of patterns in errors (helps refine future prompts)
  • Record factual corrections with sources
  • Save rejected questions with explanations (for future reference)

Remember: YOU are responsible for the quality and accuracy of assessment materials used with students. The LLM is a powerful assistant, but you are the expert educator who must verify and validate all content.

Step 11: Devil's Advocate Review

Use LLM to critically review generated content for errors and issues

After your own proofreading, use this prompt to have the LLM critically review its own work. The LLM will act as a devil's advocate, looking for factual errors, ambiguities, bias, and poor student experience.

Why This Step Helps

  • Catches errors you might have missed
  • Identifies ambiguous wording or multiple valid interpretations
  • Flags potential factual errors for you to verify
  • Evaluates student experience and fairness
  • Provides a second perspective on question quality

Important: This review is NOT a substitute for your own verification. Use it as an additional check, but YOU must still verify factual accuracy and make final decisions about question quality.
