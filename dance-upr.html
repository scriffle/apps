Evidence Base

The practices in this resource are informed by extensive research demonstrating that supportive, relationship-based teaching approaches develop technical excellence while protecting dancer wellbeing. Key findings include:

  • Ballet dancers have 3x higher eating disorder risk than general population (16.4% prevalence)
  • 54% of dance students experience depression and/or anxiety symptoms
  • Empowering motivational climates improve retention by 45-60% while maintaining technical standards
  • Improvisation classes show 29:1 positive-to-negative feedback ratio vs nearly 1:1 in traditional ballet
  • Higher rates of positive feedback lead to more effective learning and higher intrinsic motivation

What is UPR?

Unconditional Positive Regard (UPR) is a concept from humanistic psychology describing an attitude of warmth, acceptance, and respect toward another person‚Äîregardless of their behaviour or performance in any given moment.

In educational settings, UPR means separating the person from their actions: a dancer can make mistakes, struggle, or have difficult days while still being regarded as worthy and capable of growth.

Why UPR in Dance Education?

Dance training has historically relied on authoritarian methods‚Äîsometimes including harsh criticism, body shaming, and psychological pressure. While some dancers survived these approaches, research increasingly shows:

  • Supportive teaching environments produce equivalent or better technical outcomes
  • Psychological safety enables risk-taking essential for artistic growth
  • Dancers in positive environments have longer, healthier careers
  • Trauma-informed approaches reduce eating disorders, anxiety, and dropout

UPR doesn't mean abandoning standards‚Äîit means pursuing excellence through support.
