Magnetic Flux

Magnetic flux = the amount of magnetic field passing through an area. Maximum when the field is ⊥ to the area; zero when the field is parallel to the area (lies in the plane).

Magnetic flux ΦB = B⊥ A ΦB (weber, Wb), B (tesla, T), A (m²)
Only the component of B perpendicular to the area contributes. As a coil rotates, the angle between B and the area changes, so the flux through it continuously changes — this is what drives a generator.

Electromagnetic Induction — Faraday's Law

A changing magnetic flux through a coil induces an emf (voltage). Larger emf from a faster rate of change of flux and more loops.

Induced emf (Faraday + Lenz) ε = −N ΔΦB/Δt N loops, ΔΦB/Δt rate of change of flux
emf depends on the RATE of change of flux, not the flux itself. A steady (unchanging) flux induces NO emf. emf = N × gradient of a flux–time graph.
A 50-turn coil's flux falls from 4.0×10−3 Wb to 1.0×10−3 Wb in 0.020 s: |ε| = N ΔΦ/Δt = 50 × (3.0×10−3)/0.020 = 7.5 V.

Lenz's Law (the minus sign)

The induced current flows in the direction that OPPOSES the change in flux that produced it. This is a direct consequence of conservation of energy.
Lenz's law method: (1) find the direction of the original flux and whether it is increasing or decreasing. (2) The induced current creates a flux to oppose that change (opposes an increase, supports a decrease). (3) Use the right-hand grip rule to get the current direction in the coil.
A magnet's N pole pushed toward a coil increases flux → the coil's near face becomes a N pole to repel it → induced current opposes the approach. Pulling it away reverses the induced current.

Generators & Alternators

A coil rotating in a magnetic field experiences continuously changing flux → an alternating (sinusoidal) emf is induced.

OutputConnection
AlternatorACSlip rings
DC generatorPulsating DCSplit-ring commutator
Same machine, different output connection. Slip rings keep each coil end on its own ring → AC. A split-ring commutator flips the connection every half turn → the output stays one-directional (DC).

When is the emf maximum?

emf is greatest when the coil sides cut field lines fastest — when the coil plane is parallel to the field (flux = 0 but changing fastest). emf is zero when the plane is perpendicular to the field (flux maximum, momentarily not changing).

Flux and emf are a quarter-cycle (90°) out of phase: emf is maximum exactly when flux is zero, and emf is zero when flux is maximum.

Photovoltaic (Solar) Cells

Convert light energy directly into electrical energy, producing DC. A home needs AC, so the DC output is passed through an inverter to convert it to AC at mains voltage and frequency.

PV cell → DC. Inverter → converts DC to AC for household use and feed-in to the grid. (Semiconductor and inverter circuit details are not required.)

RMS Values

The root-mean-square (rms) value of an AC voltage/current is the equivalent steady DC value that delivers the same average power in a resistor.

RMS ↔ peak Vrms = Vp/√2    Irms = Ip/√2
Pavg = Vrms Irms
Quoted mains voltage (e.g. 240 V) is an rms value. Its peak is Vp = √2 × 240 ≈ 339 V, and peak-to-peak ≈ 679 V.
Mains at 50 Hz: period T = 1/f = 1/50 = 0.020 s = 20 ms per cycle. If peak current Ip = 5.0 A then Irms = 5.0/√2 = 3.5 A.
A 240 Vrms supply across a 60 Ω heater: Irms = 240/60 = 4.0 A; P = VrmsIrms = 240 × 4.0 = 960 W.

Transformers

Two coils on a shared iron core. AC in the primary makes a changing flux that induces an emf in the secondary (induction needs CHANGING flux, so transformers work on AC only, not DC).

Ideal transformer ratios N1/N2 = V1/V2 = I2/I1 N turns, V voltage, I current (1 = primary, 2 = secondary)
Ideal transformer: power in = power out (V1I1 = V2I2). Step UP voltage → step DOWN current (and vice versa). Note current ratio is INVERTED relative to the turns ratio.
Step-up: N1 = 100, N2 = 2000, V1 = 240 V. V2 = 240 × (2000/100) = 4800 V. If I1 = 10 A, then I2 = 10 × (100/2000) = 0.50 A.
Transformer method: write the ratio N1/N2 = V1/V2. Solve for the unknown voltage. For current, remember it is INVERTED: I2/I1 = N1/N2, or just use V1I1 = V2I2 (ideal, power conserved).

Power Transmission & Losses

Power lines have resistance R. Power lost as heat in the lines depends on the current they carry:

Transmission line loss Ploss = I2R    Vdrop = IR I = transmission-line current, R = line resistance
Transformers step the voltage UP for transmission → for the same delivered power P = VI, a higher V means a LOWER current I → much smaller I²R loss. The voltage is stepped down again near homes.
Transmission problem method: (1) find the line current from delivered power and transmission voltage (I = P/V). (2) Ploss = I²R. (3) voltage drop along the line = IR; voltage delivered = Vsupply − IR. Halving the current quarters the power loss.
Deliver 100 kW at 10 kV over lines of R = 5 Ω: I = 100000/10000 = 10 A; Ploss = 10² × 5 = 500 W (0.5%). At 1 kV instead: I = 100 A, Ploss = 100²×5 = 50 kW (50%).

Common Exam Traps

  • emf depends on the rate of change of flux — constant flux gives zero emf.
  • Transformers need AC; they do nothing with steady DC (no changing flux).
  • Transformer current ratio is inverted: step up V ⇒ step down I.
  • Use I²R (not V²/R with supply voltage) for transmission-line loss.
  • Mains voltages are rms; multiply by √2 for the peak.
  • emf is maximum when flux is zero (coil plane parallel to field).
