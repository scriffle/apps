Generative AI in Your Classroom

For Victorian teachers navigating the ChatGPT era

The Mass Production Analogy

Think of ChatGPT and similar tools of generative AI as mass production for words. Just as calculators eliminated the effort of long division but didn't replace mathematical thinking, AI tools like ChatGPT eliminate the effort drafting, proofing and other basic writing tasks, however but can't replace a demonstration of genuine understanding or critical thinking.

The Current Reality

Your students already have access to these tools. The tools are free, they work on all devices, and they can produce a passable essay on Ancient Egypt or a book report on Tomorrow When the War Began in seconds. Denying this reality is like trying to uninvent the internet.

What's Actually Happening

Generative AI is mass production for intellectual effort. It can quickly generate customised content that would have taken hours to research, organise and write. Despite problems it is often correct, and difficult to distinguish from polished work.

However, like any mass-produced item, the output is standardised, derivative, and lacks the genuine student insight that comes from actual engagement with ideas.

The Immediate Challenge

Traditional homework and assessment methods are now no longer representative of a student's learning progress. Any task that asks students to produce text or content that could be generated by AI such as:

  • Basic essays
  • Comprehension responses
  • Information summaries
  • Canva presentations

...no longer reliably measures student learning.

The Opportunity

This forces teachers and students back to what actually matters. For example:

  • Can students evaluate AI-generated content?
  • Can they identify errors and gaps?
  • Can they use AI as a starting point and then improve upon it?
  • Can they explain their reasoning in person?

These become the start of new markers of understanding.

Your Realistic Starting Position

You don't need to become an AI expert. You need to acknowledge that students will use these tools, design assessments that remain meaningful despite AI availability, and help students understand both the capabilities and limitations of these systems.

It is however, helpful to be able to model rigorous and ethical learning-focussed use of generative AI to your students.

Assessment Challenges in the AI Era

The Core Challenge

The fundamental issue isn't that students will cheat—it's that our traditional measures of learning no longer measure what we think they measure. When any student can produce polished work instantly, we need to rethink what we're actually assessing.

Subject-Specific Challenges

English Challenges
  • Authentic voice becomes harder to identify
  • Literary analysis can be instantly generated
  • Creative writing loses personal connection
  • Reading comprehension can be faked

Solution Focus: Emphasise in-class discussion, personal connections to texts, and handwritten reflections.

Mathematics Challenges
  • Students may submit correct answers without understanding
  • Working out can be generated to look authentic
  • Problem-solving processes can be mimicked
  • Conceptual understanding harder to verify

Solution Focus: Live problem-solving, multiple solution methods, and error analysis tasks.

Science Challenges
  • Lab reports can be generated from data
  • Scientific explanations may contain subtle errors
  • Hypothesis development can be outsourced
  • Research skills become invisible

Solution Focus: Hands-on practicals, anomaly explanations, and live data analysis.

Humanities Challenges
  • Historical arguments can be instantly constructed
  • Source analysis can be AI-generated
  • Geographic data interpretation automated
  • Critical thinking hard to authenticate

Solution Focus: Local case studies, current events, and Socratic seminars.

New Assessment Approaches

1. Process-Focused Assessment

Instead of marking final products, assess the journey:

  • Learning logs: Students document thinking at each stage
  • Version control: Track changes and improvements
  • Mistake journals: Students identify and learn from errors
  • Reflection videos: Students explain their process

Implementation tip: Use Teams or Google Classroom to timestamp submissions.

2. Live Demonstration

Make thinking visible in real-time:

  • Think-alouds: Students narrate problem-solving
  • Socratic questioning: Deep-dive Q&A sessions
  • Peer teaching: Students explain to classmates
  • Skills demonstration: Practical application

Implementation tip: Schedule 5-minute conference slots during class.

3. Authentic Context

Create assessments AI can't complete:

  • Local investigations: Study your specific school/suburb
  • Personal connections: Link to family history
  • Current events: Use yesterday's news
  • Classroom-specific: Reference in-class discussions

Implementation tip: Build tasks around excursions and unique class experiences.

Assessment Types: Traditional to AI-Ready

Each traditional assessment type can be adapted for the AI era. Click on each to see specific strategies.

1. Essays

Traditional: Take-home essay on set topic

AI-Ready Adaptations:

  • In-class handwritten components
  • Essay conferences where students defend arguments
  • Personal connection requirements
  • Process portfolio showing development
2. Research Projects

Traditional: Term-long research on chosen topic

AI-Ready Adaptations:

  • Mandatory primary data collection
  • Daily research logs
  • Live research demonstrations
  • Personal interviews required
3. Multiple Choice

Traditional: Standard test format

AI-Ready Adaptations:

  • Add justification boxes
  • Confidence ratings
  • Diagram interpretation
  • Sequential reasoning chains
4. Presentations

Traditional: Prepared speech with slides

AI-Ready Adaptations:

  • Mandatory Q&A component
  • Surprise topic variations
  • No-notes sections
  • Peer questioning rounds
5. Group Projects

Traditional: Collaborative assignment

AI-Ready Adaptations:

  • Individual video logs
  • Live problem-solving sessions
  • Rotating documentation roles
  • Specific contribution tracking
6. Practical Tasks

Traditional: Skills demonstration

AI-Ready Adaptations:

  • Already strong—increase frequency
  • Add verbal explanations
  • Peer teaching elements
  • Self-assessment videos
7. Exams

Traditional: Closed-book test

AI-Ready Adaptations:

  • Open-book, no-device format
  • Error correction tasks
  • Problem-solving stations
  • Novel scenario application
8. Creative Tasks

Traditional: Original creation

AI-Ready Adaptations:

  • Document process via video
  • Class-specific constraints
  • Live creation sessions
  • Defence of choices

School Policy Framework: Supporting AI Integration

1. Vision & Values

Our Commitment

We recognise AI as a transformative tool that's here to stay. Our role is to help students use it wisely, ethically, and in ways that enhance rather than replace learning. We support our teachers in exploring AI's potential while maintaining educational integrity.

2. Teacher Support & Development

Professional Learning

  • Regular workshops on AI tools and pedagogy (once per term)
  • Subject-specific collaboration time for AI integration
  • Peer observation and sharing opportunities
  • Access to premium AI tools for all staff
  • Protected time for experimentation and learning

Resource Development

  • Shared library of AI-enhanced materials
  • Quality assurance guidelines (not restrictions)
  • Attribution practices for transparency
  • Collaborative refinement of resources
  • Recognition that curation takes time and skill
3. Classroom Integration

Modelling Good Practice

Teachers demonstrate:

  • How to craft effective prompts
  • How to identify AI limitations and errors
  • When AI helps versus hinders learning
  • Ethical use and attribution
  • Critical evaluation skills

Student Skill Development

We explicitly teach:

  • AI literacy as part of digital citizenship
  • Prompt engineering as a communication skill
  • Critical evaluation of AI output
  • Ethical use and academic integrity
  • When to use and not use AI tools
4. Assessment Approaches

Three-Tier System

Rather than banning or allowing, we categorise assessments:

Tier 1: Human-Only

  • Supervised conditions
  • Handwritten components
  • Live demonstrations

Purpose: Verify fundamental skills

Tier 2: AI-Assisted

  • Specific tools allowed
  • Process documentation required
  • Attribution mandatory

Purpose: Learn to work with AI

Tier 3: AI-Integrated

  • Full access encouraged
  • Focus on evaluation and improvement
  • Higher-order thinking emphasis

Purpose: Prepare for real-world

5. Student Guidelines

Acceptable Use

Students may use AI for:

  • Brainstorming and idea generation
  • Grammar and spell checking
  • Creating practice materials
  • Research assistance (with verification)
  • First draft development (with attribution)

Learning-Focused Boundaries

Students understand that:

  • AI output requires verification
  • Attribution is always required
  • Personal voice and thinking matter
  • Some tasks specifically exclude AI
  • Learning requires personal effort

Support Not Surveillance

We:

  • Assume positive intent
  • Focus on learning outcomes
  • Provide guidance before consequences
  • Celebrate good AI use examples
  • Learn from mistakes together
6. Equity & Access

Ensuring Fairness

  • School provides basic AI access for all
  • Alternative assessment options available
  • Support for home technology gaps
  • Parent information sessions
  • Regular equity impact reviews
7. Implementation & Review

Gradual Rollout

  • Start with willing early adopters
  • Share successes and learnings
  • Adjust based on experience
  • Build confidence gradually
  • Maintain flexibility

Continuous Improvement

  • Term reviews of practices
  • Student and teacher feedback
  • Academic outcome monitoring
  • Policy updates as needed
  • Responsive to AI developments

Guiding Principles Throughout

Learning First: AI serves learning, not vice versa

Transparency: Open about AI use by all

Growth Mindset: Mistakes are learning opportunities

Collaboration: We figure this out together

Student Agency: Build independent learners

Ethical Foundation: Integrity matters most

Flexibility: Policies evolve with understanding

Celebration: Recognise innovative practice

Moving Forward Together

This framework isn't about restricting AI use or catching cheaters. It's about preparing students for a world where AI is ubiquitous while ensuring they develop genuine understanding and critical thinking skills.

We're all learning together. No one has all the answers yet. What matters is that we approach this transition thoughtfully, supportively, and with our students' long-term success in mind.

Next Steps for Teachers
  1. Experiment with one AI tool this week
  2. Try one new assessment approach
  3. Share what works with colleagues
  4. Ask students about their AI use
  5. Remember: perfect is the enemy of good
For School Leadership
  1. Provide time and resources for experimentation
  2. Celebrate innovative attempts (even failures)
  3. Support collaborative planning time
  4. Ensure equity of access
  5. Model learning mindset

Remember

The goal isn't to AI-proof our classrooms but to create learning experiences so engaging, relevant, and human that AI becomes a tool rather than a substitute for thinking.

This is the beginning of an ongoing conversation. The landscape will continue to evolve, and so will our approaches. What remains constant is our commitment to authentic learning and student growth.
