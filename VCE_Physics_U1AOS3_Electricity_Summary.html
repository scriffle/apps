5A: Charge & Current

Atomic Model & Conductors

  • Metals: outer electrons form a "sea" of free electrons, not bound to any nucleus — free to drift through the lattice
  • Conductors: materials with free charges (metals, saltwater)
  • Insulators: no free electrons (plastic, rubber)
  • Semiconductors: conduct partially or under certain conditions
CONCEPT: Conventional Current vs Electron Flow
  • Electrons drift toward the positive terminal
  • Conventional current (I) flows positive → negative (opposite to electrons)
  • Convention set ~70 years before electrons discovered (1897)
  • Energy transfer is the same regardless of which direction we label

Drift Velocity

Electrons drift very slowly (~0.1 mm s⁻¹) due to collisions with lattice ions. However, the drift begins everywhere simultaneously when the circuit is completed — the electric field propagates at near light speed.

Current
I = Q / t
I = current (A)  |  Q = charge (C)  |  t = time (s)
1 A = 1 C s⁻¹  |  Rearranged: Q = It

Key Values

  • Elementary charge: e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
  • 1 coulomb = 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons
  • 1 mA = 10⁻³ A  |  1 μC = 10⁻⁶ C
⚠ EXAM TRAP: Complete Circuit Required

Current flows only in a complete (closed) loop. An open switch or broken wire = open circuit = zero current everywhere. Current doesn't "build up" at a break.

Ammeter

  • Measures current in amperes (A)
  • Connected in series (all current flows through it)
  • Ideal ammeter has zero resistance

5B: Energy & Potential Difference

What is Potential Difference?

The energy difference per coulomb of charge between two points. A battery does work separating charges (chemical → electrical energy), creating a potential difference that drives current.

Potential Difference
V = E / Q
V = potential difference (V)  |  E = energy (J)  |  Q = charge (C)
1 V = 1 J C⁻¹  |  Rearranged: E = QV
Electrical Energy
E = VIt
E = energy (J)  |  V = potential difference (V)  |  I = current (A)  |  t = time (s)
Derived: E = QV and Q = It → E = VIt
CONCEPT: Gravitational Analogy

Battery = person lifting bowling balls to height
Height = potential difference (V)
Balls = coulombs of charge
Falling through liquid = energy converted at load
Total energy = (number of balls) × (height) = Q × V

Voltmeter

  • Measures potential difference in volts (V)
  • Connected in parallel across the component
  • Ideal voltmeter has infinite resistance
⚠ EXAM TRAP: Ammeter vs Voltmeter
  • Ammeter: SERIES, low R (ideally 0 Ω) — must not restrict current
  • Voltmeter: PARALLEL, high R (ideally ∞ Ω) — must not draw current from circuit
  • Swapping them damages meters and gives meaningless readings

DC vs AC

  • DC: constant polarity, charge flows one direction (batteries)
  • AC: polarity alternates, charge oscillates back and forth
  • Australian mains: 230 V AC, 50 Hz

Common Battery Voltages

1.5 V (cell) | 3 V | 9 V | 12 V (car) | 300–500 V (EV)

5C: Resistance — Series Circuits

Resistance & Ohm's Law

Resistance (R) = how much a component opposes current flow. Measured in ohms (Ω).

Ohm's Law
V = IR   →   R = V / I
V = potential difference (V)  |  I = current (A)  |  R = resistance (Ω)

I–V Graphs

FeatureOhmicNon-ohmic
I–V shapeStraight line through originCurved
ResistanceConstant (R = V/I same everywhere)Changes (e.g. ↑ with temperature)
ExampleLab resistorFilament bulb, diode
Gradient= 1/R (constant)= 1/R (varies along curve)
⚠ EXAM TRAP: I–V Graph Gradient

Gradient of I–V graph = 1/R, NOT R. A steeper line = lower resistance. To find R at any point on a non-ohmic curve, use R = V/I at that point (NOT the gradient).

Series Circuit Rules

PropertySeries Rule
CurrentSame everywhere: I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃
VoltageDivides: Vsupply = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
ResistanceAdds: Req = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
Series Equivalent Resistance
Req = R₁ + R₂ + … + Rn
Total resistance always larger than any individual resistor
CONCEPT: Why Voltage Drops in Series

Each resistor converts electrical energy → other forms. Larger R converts more energy per coulomb → larger voltage drop. The drops must add up to the supply voltage (energy conservation).

Voltage Dividers

Voltage Divider
Vout = Vin × R₂ / (R₁ + R₂)
Output voltage across R₂ is proportional to R₂'s share of total resistance.
Equivalently: Vout/Vin = R₂/Req
💡 STRATEGY: Voltage Divider Problems
  1. Identify R₁ and R₂ (Vout is always across R₂)
  2. Find Req = R₁ + R₂
  3. Apply Vout = Vin × R₂/(R₁ + R₂)
  4. Check: Vout must be less than Vin

Example: 12 V battery, need 5 V out, R₂ = 1.0 kΩ.
Req = 12 × 1.0/5 = 2.4 kΩ → R₁ = 2.4 − 1.0 = 1.4 kΩ

5D: Resistance — Parallel Circuits

Parallel Circuit Rules

PropertyParallel Rule
VoltageSame across all branches: V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃
CurrentDivides: Isupply = I₁ + I₂ + I₃
ResistanceReciprocals add: 1/Req = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + …
Parallel Equivalent Resistance (general)
1/Req = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + … + 1/Rn
Total resistance always smaller than the smallest individual resistor
Two Resistors in Parallel (shortcut)
Req = (R₁ × R₂) / (R₁ + R₂)
"Product over sum" — only works for exactly 2 resistors
Special case: two identical resistors → Req = R/2
⚠ EXAM TRAP: Parallel Resistance Calculation
  • Don't forget to flip back: 1/Req = 0.5 means Req = 2 Ω, not 0.5 Ω
  • The "product over sum" shortcut only works for 2 resistors — use the general formula for 3+
  • Adding more parallel branches always decreases total resistance
CONCEPT: Why Parallel R is Lower

More parallel branches = more pathways for current. Think of opening extra checkout lanes at a supermarket — the "resistance" to getting through drops, total flow (current) increases.

Current Division in Parallel

Current splits inversely proportional to resistance: lower R branch carries more current. At each junction, total current in = total current out.

Combined Series-Parallel Circuits

💡 STRATEGY: Solving Combined Circuits
  1. Identify which components are in series and which in parallel
  2. Simplify parallel groups first → replace with single Req
  3. Then add series resistances: Rtotal = Rseries + Rparallel eq
  4. Find total current: I = V/Rtotal
  5. Work back to find V and I for individual components

Example: Two 10 Ω in parallel (= 5 Ω) + 10 Ω in series = 15 Ω total

I–V Graphs: Series vs Parallel

  • Series combination: shallower gradient (higher R)
  • Parallel combination: steeper gradient (lower R)
  • To find Req graphically: read V and I at a point, then R = V/I

5E: Electric Power

Power = Rate of Energy Transfer
P = E / t
P = power (W)  |  E = energy (J)  |  t = time (s)  |  1 W = 1 J s⁻¹
Electrical Power (3 forms)
P = VI   =   I²R   =   V² / R
P = VI — use when V and I are known (most common)
P = I²R — best for power loss in wires (Joule heating)
P = V²/R — best for comparing devices at same voltage
⚠ EXAM TRAP: Power Misconceptions
  • "100 watts per hour" is WRONG — power is already a rate (joules per second). A 100 W device uses 100 W whether it's on for 1 ms or 10 hours.
  • kW ≠ kWh: kW is power (rate), kWh is energy (amount). E = P × t.
  • Always convert time to seconds when using P = E/t.

Choosing the Right Power Formula

KnownFormulaUse Case
V and IP = VIGeneral circuit power
I and RP = I²RHeat loss in cables/wires
V and RP = V²/RComparing appliances at same V

Power in Series vs Parallel

EXPLAIN TEMPLATE: Why Parallel Uses More Power

Parallel circuits have lower Req than series → draw more current from supply → more power consumed (P = V²/R — same V, lower R = higher P). Components are brighter/hotter but battery drains faster.

Example: 1 kΩ and 3 kΩ globes with 24 V supply:
Parallel: Req = 750 Ω → P = 24²/750 = 0.77 W
Series: Req = 4000 Ω → P = 24²/4000 = 0.14 W

Power Loss in Cables

Use P = I²R. Lower cable resistance = less wasted energy as heat. Example: 10 A through 1 Ω cable loses 100 W; through 5 Ω cable loses 500 W.

Why Homes Use Parallel Circuits

Series advantage: a single master switch can control everything (e.g. power board main switch).

💡 STRATEGY: Multi-Step Circuit Problems
  1. Draw the circuit — label all known values
  2. Find Req — simplify parallel groups first, then series
  3. Find total I — use I = Vsupply/Req
  4. Find individual V's — series: V = IR for each; parallel: V same across branches
  5. Find individual I's — parallel: I = V/R for each branch
  6. Find power — choose P = VI, I²R, or V²/R as appropriate
  7. Check: do V's add up? Do I's add up at junctions? Is Ptotal = Vsupply × Itotal?
