Cells & Organisms ‚Äî Overview

Key Concepts

  • Organism: something that is living
  • Biotic: living; made up of at least one cell
  • Abiotic: not living
  • Cell: basic unit of life
  • All cells share four common features

Cell Theory

  • Living things are made up of at least one cell
  • The cell is the basic unit of life
  • Every cell comes from a pre-existing cell
Remember: VCE Biology uses biotic/abiotic instead of MRSGREN criteria. All organisms share four essential cell components.

Biotic vs Abiotic

Biotic (Living)

  • Made up of at least one cell
  • Contains genetic material, cytosol, ribosomes, plasma membrane
  • Examples: plants, animals, bacteria, fungi, protists

Abiotic (Non-living)

  • Not made of cells
  • Does not meet cell theory criteria
  • Examples: rocks, water, sunlight, air, minerals
Tricky cases: Bones (once part of living organism, now abiotic), picked vegetables (dead cells, abiotic), viruses (no cells, abiotic)

MRSGREN vs VCE Criteria

Junior science used MRSGREN (Movement, Reproduction, Sensitivity, Growth, Respiration, Excretion, Nutrition). VCE Biology focuses on cellular structure instead.

Cell Theory

Three fundamental principles:

  1. All living things are made up of at least one cell ‚Äî cells are the fundamental building blocks of life
  2. The cell is the basic unit of life ‚Äî smallest unit that can perform all life processes
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells ‚Äî cells divide to produce new cells
Cell theory is one of the most important principles in biology. It unifies our understanding of all living organisms.

Historical Context

Cell theory developed through contributions from multiple scientists including Robert Hooke (discovered cells), Anton van Leeuwenhoek (observed microorganisms), and others in the 1600s-1800s.

Four Features Common to All Cells

Whether prokaryote or eukaryote, all cells contain:

1. Genetic Material

Contains hereditary information with genes that code for proteins

2. Cytosol

Liquid inside cells; 80% water plus salts and organic molecules; site of many reactions

3. Ribosomes

Non-membrane-bound organelles; site of protein synthesis

4. Plasma Membrane

Phospholipid bilayer that separates interior from exterior; controls what enters and leaves

These four features are found in bacteria, plants, animals, fungi, and all other living cells!

Genetic Material

Function

Contains hereditary information, including genes that code for proteins

Key Points

  • Carries instructions for making proteins
  • Passed from parent cells to daughter cells
  • Contains DNA (in most organisms)
  • Prokaryotes: DNA in nucleoid region (not membrane-bound)
  • Eukaryotes: DNA in nucleus (membrane-bound)
Why it matters: Genetic material allows organisms to reproduce, pass on traits, and respond to environmental changes through evolution

Cytosol

Function

The liquid inside cells; site of many cellular reactions

Composition

  • 80% water
  • Dissolved salts
  • Organic molecules (sugars, amino acids, etc.)

Key Points

  • Fills space between organelles
  • Medium for chemical reactions
  • Does NOT include the organelles themselves
  • Also called cytoplasm in some contexts
Think of it as: The "ocean" inside the cell where molecules float and reactions occur

Ribosomes

Function

Site of protein synthesis ‚Äî where proteins are assembled

Structure

  • Non-membrane-bound organelles
  • Made of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and proteins
  • Consist of two subunits (large and small)

Location

  • Free-floating in cytosol
  • Attached to endoplasmic reticulum (in eukaryotes)
  • Found in ALL cell types
Essential role: Without ribosomes, cells cannot make proteins, which means no enzymes, no cell structures, no life!

Plasma Membrane

Function

Separates interior from exterior environment; selectively controls what enters and leaves the cell

Structure

  • Phospholipid bilayer (two layers)
  • Embedded proteins for transport
  • Fluid mosaic model

Key Roles

  • Maintains cell integrity
  • Controls passage of substances
  • Cell recognition and communication
  • Provides barrier between cell and environment
Selective permeability: The membrane allows some substances through while blocking others ‚Äî crucial for maintaining cell conditions

Different Cell Types ‚Äî Same Four Features

Prokaryotes

  • Example: Bacteria
  • No nucleus
  • Genetic material in nucleoid
  • Smaller and simpler
  • Still has all four features!

Eukaryotes

  • Examples: Animals, plants, protists, fungi
  • Have nucleus
  • Membrane-bound organelles
  • Larger and more complex
  • Still has all four features!
Universal features: Despite huge differences between bacteria and animals, all cells share genetic material, cytosol, ribosomes, and plasma membrane

Check-in Questions

  1. Define 'organism'
  2. Which of the following are biotic? Bones, the Sun, water, the ocean, vegetables that have been picked
  3. What is the function of the cytosol?
  4. What are the four features common to all cells?
Reveal Answers
  1. Organism: Something that is living; made up of at least one cell
  2. Biotic: None of these! Bones (dead), Sun (not cellular), water (abiotic), ocean (contains biotic but itself is water), picked vegetables (dead cells)
  3. Cytosol function: Liquid inside cells (80% water, salts, organic molecules); site of many cellular reactions
  4. Four features: Genetic material, cytosol, ribosomes, plasma membrane

Section Questions 1-4

  1. Recall the four cell features common to all organisms
  2. State the function of each of the four common cell features
  3. Define 'abiotic'
  4. Compare an abiotic factor with a biotic factor
Complete Answers
  1. Four features: genetic material, cytosol, ribosomes, plasma membrane
  2. Functions:
    • Genetic material: contains hereditary information, genes code for proteins
    • Cytosol: liquid inside cells, site of many reactions
    • Ribosomes: site of protein synthesis
    • Plasma membrane: separates interior/exterior, controls what enters/leaves
  3. Abiotic: Not living; does not contain cells
  4. Comparison: Biotic factors are living and made of cells; abiotic factors are non-living and not made of cells

Application Questions

Q5: Is tree bark biotic or abiotic?

Answer & Explanation

Answer: Tree bark contains both biotic and abiotic components

Explanation: The outer dead layers are abiotic (no longer cellular), but inner living cells (cambium) are biotic. Context matters!

Q6: Provide examples of abiotic and biotic factors affecting an apple tree's survival

Sample Answers

Abiotic factors: Sunlight, water, soil minerals, temperature, air quality, pH of soil

Biotic factors: Bacteria in soil, fungi (helpful or harmful), insects (pollinators or pests), herbivores, competing plants

Q7: Recall the cell theory

Complete Answer
  1. Living things are made up of at least one cell
  2. The cell is the basic unit of life
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells
